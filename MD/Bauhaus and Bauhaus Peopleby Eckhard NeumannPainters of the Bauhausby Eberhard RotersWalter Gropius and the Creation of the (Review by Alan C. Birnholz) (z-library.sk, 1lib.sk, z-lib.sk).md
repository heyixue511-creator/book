For although one is ideological and the other antiideological, both are dedicated to using theatre, language itself, as a means of freeing the spectator to make his own decisions on the basis of his own feelings and thoughts. His major purpose is, after all, to cut through preconceptions about the nature and the effects of a writer's polemic aims on his work. NEUMANN, ECKHARD, ed. Bauhaus and Bauhaus People. N.Y.: Van Nostrand Reinhold, 1970, pp. 256, 42 ills., \$13.50.

ROTERS, EBERHARD. Painters of the Bauhaus. N.Y.: Praeger, 1969, pp. 215, 91 ills., \$18.50.

SCHLEMMER, OSKAR. Man. Edited by Heimo Kuchling. The M.I.T. Press, 1971, pp. 159, 81 ills., \$8.95.

Bauhaus and Bauhaus People is a collection of forty-eight reminiscences of the Bauhaus. Each essay is preceded by a rather prosaic biographical sketch of the author, and a number of interesting photographs rounds out this generally "those were the good old days" anthology. Some of the essays are worth serious consideration. Paul Citroen, for example, provides insight into the Mazdaznan cult which fluorished under Johannes Itten during the early years of the Bauhaus. "The Bauhaus Style-A Myth" by

Walter Dexel debunks the common view of the Bauhaus' ubiquitous impact in the 1920s. T. Lux Feininger points out some of the Bauhaus' pedagogical innovations. And Hannes Beckmann discusses the nature of Albers' approach to the Vorkurs. The remaining essays are crippled by their severe brevity, superficiality, and/or unwillingness to deal with highly complex issues. Often the author's bias is clear enough as, to take one example, when Lou Scheper writes that the Bauhaus was "so basically different from the other academies before and after" (p. 113), a view which Franciscono corrects in Walter Gropius and the Creation of the Bauhaus in Weimar in stating "the curriculum seems to have been little different outwardly from that of a conventional academy" (p. 163). But for the most part, the reader is left with a disturbing sense of issues being drastically simplified. Moreover, no effort is made by Neumann to indicate degrees of authenticity of the various authors as Franciscono does, for example, in his "warn[ing] against the extremely unreliable anecdotes of Lothar Schreyer" (p. 163, note 34). The concept of an anthology of recollections by the Bauhdusler is certainly laudable, but this book fails to provide it.

Equally disappointing is Roters' Painters of the Bauhaus. Individual chapters deal with Gropius, Feininger, Itten, Muche, Schreyer, Schlemmer, Klee, Kandinsky, Moholy-Nagy, Bayer, and Albers, with some minor figures handled with dispatch in the conclusion. The lack of an index symbolizes the overall unscholarly and confusing nature of this book. We can accept Roters' statement about Itten that "meditation lies at the core of his conception of artistic experience." But we are left hanging in air when Roters adds that Itten's "earlier works reveal their meaning most fully when they are observed with the conscious mind 'switched off' " (p. 52), since Roters never attempts to tell us what that meaning is, much less how to switch off our conscious minds. Or, to cite the chapter on Lionel Feininger, the artist's work and teaching at the Bauhaus receive remarkably scant attention, and there is no analysis of his relationship to other artists at this time. In the chapter on Schlemmer, Roters chooses to focus on the artist's ballets and puppets, but not on his paintings. All too often Roters substitutes quotations of other authors for his own analyses. In short, Roters has taken a number of artists who shared an association with the Bauhaus and proceeded to discuss their careers in the most general terms with little regard for their work at the Bauhaus itself.

some of the same points discussed by Roters. Itten's departure from the Bauhaus in 1923 and his replacement as director of the Vorkurs by Moholy-Nagy have usually been considered a major turning point in the history of the Bauhaus. Roters provides only three sentences in his examination of the reasons for Itten's resignation. "In 1923, Itten left the Bauhaus, following disagreements with Gropius. The latter felt he could no longer take responsibility for Itten's activities. Itten, on his part, found that the Bauhaus was no longer a suitable field for him" (p. 54). Franciscono, to the contrary, rightly devotes a good deal of attention to the factors leading to Itten's departure from the school. Chapter six, "Johannes Itten, the Bauhaus Preliminary Course, and the Bases of Design," opens with the observation that Itten "offered the most serious challenge of the early years to Gropius' ideas" (p. 173). Franciscono then links Itten's efforts in the Vorkurs to bring out his students' hidden, inner talents to "the liberal Rousseau-Pestalozzi-Froebel-Montessori reform tradition of child education" (p. 180), to the freer discipline of such art teachers as Obrist and Cizek (particularly the latter), and finally to the impact on Itten of certain oriental ideas. The important discussion, which helps to illuminate Itten's goals in the Vorkurs, then allows a number of conclusions regarding his eventual resignation. A struggle for power within the Bauhaus between Itten and Gropius is considered by Franciscono but held to be of relatively minor importance. Franciscono goes on to examine the clash between Itten's concern with various cults and the pragmatism of the Bauhaus. But "more fundamental," Franciscono maintains, were the "serious differences" between Itten's and Gropius' philosophies, since "Itten had no commitment to the goal of a practical handicraft training for the artist" (pp. 215-216). Hence it was not a case, as Roters argues, of "disagreements" in 1923 causing Itten to leave; instead, "the break between Itten and the Bauhaus came already at the end of 1921" (p. 216), with the following semesters taught by Itten only after he was persuaded to stay on for his students' sake.

Roters also echoes the traditional interpretation of the split in the Bauhaus at the midtwenties when he writes of "the constructivefunctional character of Dessau as opposed to the metaphysical trend of the Weimar period" (p. 188). Though Franciscono admits that after Itten left "the climate of the Bauhaus-including the attitudes of the students-became appreciably more hardheaded, and the character of the design work more geometric and normative" (p. 239), he stresses the significant continuities that bind, rather than separate, the Weimar and Dessau Bauhaus. Both documentation, which Franciscono amply cites in the text and in the appendices, and the works themselves "make it evident that the ideal of a sober, geometrically controlled design"-what is meant by Sachlichkeit--"was ingrained in the Bauhaus from the start" (p. 237).

This ideal becomes the focal point for Franciscono's examination of the formation of the Bauhaus. Contending that at its inception the ideas of the Bauhaus were, in fact, really Gropius's ideas, Franciscono observes that after the war "like many German artists at the time, Gropius became at least partially persuaded that modern large-scale industrial organization had itself been a major cause of the war and that the hope for the future lay in the revival of skilled hand labor" (p. 21). But in the same speech from which Franciscono excerpts this statement "Gropius remained convinced of the necessity, for good or ill, of including industry in his plans for [design] education" (p. 23). Similarly, Gropius' vision of the recreation of the medieval Bauhhutten, customarily "regarded as the paradigmatic symbol of Gropius's postwar, so-called backward-looking 'expressionism'," occurs "within the context of an almost total absorption in the issues of industrial design and the creation of a modern style based on industry" (p. 37). Gropius' Sommerfield House of 1920, "which has always been held up as the prime indication of Gropius' 'expressionist' turn of mind during this period," (p. 40) undergoes an analogous reinterpretation. Thus, just as his ideas remained consistent into the era of the "constructive-functional" Dessau Bauhaus, so too "Gropius remained surprisingly within the limits of his prewar aesthetic" in the immediate postwar period (p. 39). Franciscono stresses, however, the significance of Gropius' movement in the years after the war to a concern with "feeling" (Empfindung) as the source of the artist's inspiration and to an estimation of art, in Gropius' words, as "nothing other than the sensible embodiment of the transcendental", "a crystallized longing for God" (p. 69). (Cf. W. Pehnt, "Gropius the Romantic," Art Bulletin 53 [1971], 379-392: "Between 1918 and 1922, when Gropius, in theory and in practice, gave highest priority to form and expression, he was doing no differently than he had in the period before World War I....")

Franciscono then proposes a number of factors which effectively prohibited the Bauhaus from engaging in meaningful industrial activity prior to 1922: 1) a severely restricted budget prevented the necessary expansion to a viable industrial program; 2) there were numerous problems of establishing the proper relationship of the Formlehre to shopwork; 3) the desire to have students develop a correct sense of form necessarily postponed practical application of their work; 4) there existed some difficulty in convincing students of the desirability of collaborating with industry; 5) many of the faculty, a good part of which was held over from the old Hochschule fur bildende Kunst, retained a belief in the notion of painting and sculpture as the highest form of art, an attitude which conflicted with Gropius' emphasis on the crafts. These considerations "forced [the Bauhaus] to emphasize picture-making and theory" (p. 163), and it is to Gropius' great credit, Franciscono declares, that he remained so eminently practical that he could accept these temporary digressions from his principal objectives in the conviction that, with time, the Bauhaus would achieve its destiny. Hence Gropius permitted the pursual of work on easel painting, though he warned "against endangering the foundation of the Bauhaus, which requires from every single pupil the complete mastery of a craft, by the creation of special places for individuals that would release them from handiwork" (p. 170).

The rest of Walter Gropius and the Creation of the Bauhaus in Weimar is equally filled with penetrating insights. If one of Franciscono's principal contentions is the adherence by Gropius after the war of his earlier recognition of the importance of industry, so too did Gropius retain his prewar faith in the significance of the artist's own individual talent. Chapter two, "Gropius and the 1914 Werkbund Controversy," places Gropius in basic accord with van de Velde in defending the essential freedom of the artist in opposition to Muthesius' concept of Typisierung. Franciscono also examines the relationship of the early Bauhaus to the Arbeitsrat fur Kunst, founded by Gropius, Bruno Taut, and Adolf Behne. Unlike the other founders of the Arbeitsrat, however, Gropius remained concerned with the practical applications of art. In chapter four, "The First Bauhaus Program and the Arbeitsrat fur Kunst," Franciscono analyzes the significant differences in theory between these two organizations despite their apparent agreement on so many issues.

Franciscono's analysis of the early Bauhaus clearly demonstrates, as he states in the conclusion, that "from the beginning it was a tangled skein of various and even conflicting motives and tendencies, nowhere more in evidence, as this study has tried to show, than in the person of Gropius himself" (p. 240). Franciscono deserves to be commended for skillfully guiding the reader to an understanding of what the early Bauhaus really was. The text is well complemented by helpful appendices and a bibliography of pertinent sources.

The publication of Oskar Schlemmer's notes for his course on man from 1928 and 1929 may be seen in a new perspective when we keep Franciscono's remarks in mind. In these notes, which were never worked into a completed state, Schlemmer asserted "it is essential for the new life that man should be understood as a cosmic being. His conditions of existence, his relationships with the natural and artificial environment, his mechanism and organism, his material, spiritual, and intellectual image; in short, man as a bodily and spiritual being is a necessary and important subject of instruction" (p. 25). Man, for Schlemmer, was "the measure of all things".

The tremendous scope Schlemmer envisioned for this course is illustrated by the proposed syllabus which lists such subjects for discussion as the history of man's origin, various artistic theories used to represent man, his physiognomy, man in his spatial environment, food, and the "philosophy of life" (p. 33). These subjects were then divided into three parts: the formal ("which is mainly concerned with drawing" and with man's relationship to his environment), the scientific (physiology), and the philosophical. "In all three parallel subjects importance will be attached to the making of illustrative, easily remembered, drawn and painted schemata and diagrams, so that, for example, even processes of thought, scales of feeling, will be presented in line and color" (p. 26).

Upon examination of the numerous drawings reproduced in Man we quickly realize how traditional one of the most innovative members of the Bauhaus truly was. "The Bauhaus," Schlemmer believed, "should seek out, collect, and conserve the possible laws-old and new" (p. 17), an attitude wholly in accord with the continuity of thought at the Bauhaus that Franciscono emphasizes. This book forms a welcome companion volume to the recently reissued Die Buiihne im Bauhaus, which presents Schlemmer's thoughts on the theater, and it points up once again the need for a new study of Schlemmer to replace Hans Hildebrandt's monograph of 1952.
