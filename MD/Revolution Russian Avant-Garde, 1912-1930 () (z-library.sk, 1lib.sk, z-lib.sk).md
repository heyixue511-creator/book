Midwest Landscape #53 (1962) is among the 40 photographs by Art Sinsabaugh that will be on view in the third-floor Steichen Center through October 31. Selected by John Szarkowski, Director of the Department of Photography, ART SINSABAUGH: LANDSCAPES includes not only Sinsabaugh's photographic studies of the Midwest (both town and country) but also more recent work that was done in New England. "The gods are changing from day to day ... everything has been jumbled together in a pandemonium. Everyone tries to scream as loudly as possible to appear as modern as possible. "

-Russian art critic, 1911

In the years surrounding the Revolution of 1917, Russia was a chaotically creative center for new ideas in art. The world of art, like that of society and the political order, seemed ripe for revolution. Movements rapidly succeeded one another: Cubo-Futurism, Suprematism, Constructivism. REVOLUTION: Rus-SIAN AVANT-GARDE, 1912-1930 is a survey of drawings, paintings, sculpture, prints, and photographs in the Museum's Collection. It is being directed by Magdalena Dabrowski, Curatorial Assistant in the Department of Drawings. Featured will be the work of Malevich, Rodchenko, Gabo, Tatlin, Popova, and El Lissitzky, among others. Also on view in the exhibition will be documentary material relating to the Russian avant-garde, much of it initially assembled by Alfred H. Barr, Jr., the Museum's Founding Director. In 1928, the year before the Museum's establishment, the young Barr, along with his friend Jere Abbott, later to be the Museum's first Associate Director, went to the Soviet Union to study the post-Revolutionary painting, theater, and cinema.

William S. Lieberman, Director of the Department of Drawings, notes in his brief introduction to the exhibition: "As the result of the pioneering interest of its first director, Alfred H. Barr, Jr., The Museum of Modem Art was able to acquire a substantial and unique collection of paintings, sculpture, drawings, and prints that illustrate crucial points in the Russian artistic evolution during the second and third decades of this century. These holdings have been considerably augmented during the past few years, most recently by The Lauder Foundation's gift of two watercolors by Vladimir Tatlin, the only examples of his work held in a public collection in the West."

While culturally backward as a whole, Russia in the first decade of this century did have a small avantgarde that in many respects was better informed about development in the West-particularly the work of Matisse and Picasso-than were many of its contemporaries in Paris. In addition, the Futurist Marinetti had made a lecture tour of Russia and reproductions of Cubist paintings filled the art magazines. Cubism and Fauvism were certainly strong early influences on the work of Kasimir Malevich, whose Suprematism promulgated the ideal of a purely abstract painting. "In 1913," Malevich explained, "trying desperately to liberate art from the ballast of the representational world, I sought refuge in the form of the square. " It was in that year Malevich painted his radically innovative black square on a white background, a step toward his absolute, pure painting White on White, which will be included in the exhibition. The square, like the rectangle, the circle, the triangle, and the cross, was an element of Suprematist art.

## WE DECLARE UNCOMPROMISING WAR ON ART!

-ALEKSEI GAN, 1922

Vladimir Tatlin, whose name along with that of Alexander Rodchenko and El Lissitzky is closely associated with Constructivism, believed in an art that would join with life, an art that would incorporate contemporary technology, massproduction, and industrial materials. Tatlin created the first assembled works in abstract forms and exhibited them in 1915 as free, geometric constructions in space. The two recently acquired Tatlin drawings are Study for a Counter-Relief (1914) and Study for Board No. I (1917).

In the years after the 1917 Revolution it seemed that Constructivism, which dates from 1919-20, might be accepted as the art most appropriate to a forward-looking proletarian revolution. But the currents of official opinion soon turned against the artists. Lenin saw their efforts as a sort of "infantile leftism" and their art as inaccessible to the masses. The Constructivists were largely out of favor by the late Twenties. Several emigrated to Germany while others continued to live in the Soviet Union and do their work under conditions of relative obscurity. The brothers Naum Gabo and Antoine Pevsner did much of their most interesting work after leaving Russia. Works such as Pevsner's 1924-26 Torso combine geometric abstraction and industrial materials with abstract form.

"This exhibition," Magdalena Dabrowski notes, "shows the different stages in the transition from representational painting to the nonrepresentational, and from two-dimensional pictorial composition to the three-dimensional 'construction.' It provides an opportunity to present the Museum's holdings almost in their entirety, portraying the most important aspects of this pictorial revolution." O □

# Film Programs

## HAPPY BIRTHDAY MICKEY MOUSE!

The world's most popular animated personality is fifty years old this year. He made his screen debut on November 18, 1928 as star of the first sound cartoon, Steamboat Willie, at the Colony Theatre in New York. The Museum's Department of Film will salute Mickey on weekends beginning November 18 through December 17, featuring many rare cartoons courtesy of Walt Disney Productions.

## Cineprobe

Babette Mangolte's The Camera: Je (La Camera: 1), 1977, inaugurates the eleventh season of CINEPROBE on Monday, October 16 at 6:00. CIN-EPROBE, the Museum's series dedicated to independent and/or avantgarde film, offers filmmakers an opportunity to show their work and a forum in which to discuss it with the audience after the screening. This year, as in past seasons, CINEPROBE will bring to New York a number of filmmakers who have made fine work that may not be well-known in this city. CINEPROBE, made possible by grants from the Jerome Foundation, the National Endowment for the Arts, and the New York State Council on the Arts, is supervised by Adrienne Mancia and Larry Kardish of the Film Department.

## Rediscovering RKO

Katherine Hepburn lives up to the title of John Cromwell's 1934 Spitfire, one of the more than 100 films in the forthcoming series, REDISCOVERING RKO. Opening December 21, the program, which includes many films not seen since their original release, explores the adventurous spirit of the great RKO Studio. Adrienne Mancia of the Film Department is assembling the series with the assistance of Larry Kardish.
