Indeed, as Morris's own archival findings reveal, Vietnam probably sought to undermine and coerce the Pol Pot regime as early as 1976 when Le Duan told Soviet interlocutors that Nuon Chea, Cambodia's "Brother Number Two," was "our man and a personal friend" (p. 96). He added: "I can promise you that with Kampuchea all will be in order. Sooner or later it will be with Vietnam. The Khmers do not hav another way out" (emphasis added). Two years later (p. 109), Duan told the Soviet Ambassador to Hanoi that Sao Phim, the secretary of Cambodia's Eastern Zone (who had recently killed himself, unbeknownst to Duan) was also "our man," just as Pol Pot and his colleagues had suspected earlier in the year when they had inaugurated a brutal purge of the Zone.

In the following chapter, "Vietnam and the Communist World" (pp. 119-42), and those that follow, Cambodia drops out of the picture and Morris's analysis of Vietnamese behavior sometimes seems doctrinaire. He might have suggested, for example (p. 121) that Vietnam's support of "Stalin" in the mid-1960s was influenced at least in part by a wish to please the Chinese. Similarly, on page 140, he seems baffled that the Vietnamese should have taken an ambiguous stance toward the Sino-Soviet split when they were receiving aid from both countries and were engaged in a full scale war. Discretion, under those conditions, was surely the better part of valor.

I had mixed feelings about chapter 6, "North Vietnam's Tilt Toward the Soviet Union, 1968-1975" (pp. 143-66). On the one hand, the "tilt" seems to have little connection with Vietnam's decision to invade Cambodia. On the other, the chapter reflects Morris's enterprising research and provides many interesting insights, even though they mesh poorly with the first half of the book. I have similar mixed feelings about the next two chapters, "The Collapse of Vietnamese-Chinese Relations" (pp. 167-96) and "The Emergence of the Soviet-Vietnamese Alliance" (pp. 196-218).

Chapter 9, "The Consequences of the Vietnamese Invasion" (pp. 219-28), however, struck me as scrappy and indecisive. In view of the book's title, I would have welcomed more military information about the invasion itself, and a more detailed examination of the Vietnamese protectorate (1979-1989) that followed. The book's concluding chapter, "History and Theory" (pp. 2 2 9 ^ 4 ) , although appealingly presented, seems similarly sketchy and off-hand. The theories that Morris opposes might have been more clearly explained and he might have defended his own theoretical position with greater vigor.

In summary, I found Why Vietnam Invaded Cambodia invaluable for the wealth of insights it draws from the Soviet archives. This book is helpful as a narrative of the relations among Marxist-Leninist states at a particular point in their histories, and disappointing in terms of the ambitious theoretical agenda that Morris hints at in his subtitle, sets out in the book's opening pages, and fails to defend later on.

Drawn from the precincts of anthropology, history, and literature, and succinctly introduced by Henk Schulte Nordholt's opening remarks on the volume's themes, the papers gathered here offer absorbing and exceedingly well-written reflections on the cultural politics of clothes and "outward appearances" as modernity took Indonesia into its grasp. As Schulte Nordholt puts it, "Modernity did not walk barefoot through Indonesia's history: it wore shoes" (p. 21). It also garbed itself in—among other things—uniforms, haircuts, jackets, shirts, trousers, batiks, sarongs, kebayas, the Muslim jubbah, neckties, celluar phones, veils, hats, dress codes, designed interiors and landscapes, photographs, hygiene programs, radio broadcasts, light bulbs, revolution, nationalist heroes, postage stamps, street names, vaccinations, advertising and political slogans, canned food, dolls, new codes of etiquette and sexual behavior, debates over shaking hands, newspapers, clocks, overseas education, Benny Goodman recordings, and signs of a reawakened Muslim piety. Outward Appearance takes us into modernity's vast, walk-in closet, and lets us look over its Indonesian collection. Any reader is sure to find something to catch the mind and eye here.

All the papers in this book help us see how individuals, social groups, and state institutions made sense of each other in outward signs of modern identity. All are grounded in ethnographic fieldwork, or rely on historical work with photographs, manuals, diaries, novels, poems, travel accounts, government archives, newspapers, and other what-nots of Indonesian life. Kees van Dijk looks at the ever changing triangulation between Western, Muslim, and "indigenous" influences on the way Indonesians have dressed themselves since the time of the VOC, and on the way ideas about political authority and affiliation, about progress, and about nationhood and governance could be debated and negotiated through clothes. Jean Gelman Taylor examines photographs to show how the colonial state promoted differences between men and women, and between notions of the public and the private. Ideas about clothes and bodies are deeply shaped by an unfolding political history, and Taylor links colonial antecedents to the postcolonial impulse to garb men with power and Western-style suits and women with "traditional" costume and political subordination. Rudolf Mrazek, too, discusses the politics of clothes, but by way of a look at the "Indonesian dandy" in the late colonial period. The dandy Mrazek has in mind "was a 'native' who borrowed Dutch clothes to place himself in a 'modern' colonial society" (pp. 131—32). Dutch anxiety about—and dismissal of—the incongruously dressed native reflected concern about hygiene and order as well as a colonial impulse to orientalize colonial subjects; the hybrid dandy, meanwhile, was a boundary-crosser who, however elusive, could neither profoundly disturb the neatness of things nor straightforwardly serve as model for radical nationalist yearnings. Elsbeth Locher-Scholten continues the volume's emphasis on clothing, but extends her study to look at both women's fashion and food and the ways they simultaneously demarcated white identities and promoted Westernization in colonial society. Highly educated, expatriate women, or totok, emerged as trendsetters around matters of fashion and food, and helped define the late colonial order and its exclusion of indigenous influences.

William Frederick, James Danandjaja, and Teuro Sekimoto put focus on clothing in the postcolonial era. Frederick uses fresh archival material to explore the diverse identities and styles of the revolutionary youth, or pemuda, from 1945-1949; he shows that the majority of pemuda did not conform in appearance to the canonized and romantic image of the young, long-haired freedom fighter. Danandjaja offers an insider's recollections of dressing up for school, boy scouts, office, and civil ceremony, and treats readers to a brief history of the sandal and its demonization during the

Suharto years. Sekimoto, meanwhile, depicts how people present themselves as constituents in a vast state machinery when donning uniforms for Independence Day celebrations; an interest in displaying civic-minded neatness, progressiveness, and discipline is manifest not only in uniforms, but also in the public decorations, projects, and programs organized for the day's commemorations.

Two essays concern the appearance of national martyrs, heroes, and villains in the state's production of its foundational myths. Klaus Schreiner investigates the criteria and bureaucratic procedures for designating citizens as national or revolutionary heroes, and for promulgating their names, biographies, and images through publications, artwork, street names, commemorations, and so forth. The late Jacques Leclerc—to whose memory the book is dedicated—left a set of notes and drafts about the demonic portrayals of leftist terror at the New Order's monument Pancasila Sakti; those portrayals cast violence and disorder as female qualities, and order and stability as male qualities.

The remaining two essays take us into households and a consideration of modernity at two points during the last century. Henk Maier reflects upon an image of modern, electrified, domestic bliss and calm featured in a 1940 magazine advertisement for Philips light bulbs. Combining visual and textual analysis, Maier's article is an extremely evocative and well-crafted essay on the anxieties, ambiguous dreams, contradictions, and dazzling force of late colonial modernity. Closing the volume is Lizzy van Leeuwen's brilliant and unsparing portrait of daily life for the wife and daughters of a Jakartan fertilizer tycoon during the 1990s. This is the most revealing and devastating ethnographic portrayal of the Indonesian superrich that I know.

Outward Appearances is a marvelous read and an exceptional collection of studie on material culture and social history. It is of sure importance to all Indonesianists, and to any scholar interested in Asian modernities.
