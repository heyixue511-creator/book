It is important to point out, however, that (as revealed by commercial initiatives such as those of Wanamaker, see note 29 above) the marketing of Paris as 'a woman's city' involved interests other than those of women (or indeed, those of the French). The promotion of Paris as a feminized city of high fashion was fundamental to economic concermns of other countries. In the case of the United States, an identification with this version of 'Paris' was vital to the sale of goods, both American and French, the massproduced as well as the luxurious.
