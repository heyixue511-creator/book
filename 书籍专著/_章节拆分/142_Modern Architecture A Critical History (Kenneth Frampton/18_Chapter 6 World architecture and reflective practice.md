# Chapter 6 World architecture and reflective practice

The idea of reflective practice is an alternative to the traditional epistemology of practice. It leads to new conceptions of the professionalclient contract, the partnership of research and practice, and the learning systems of professional institutions. Now I would like to suggest how it might also lead us to think differently about the roles of professionals in public policy and about the place of the professions in the larger society. The idea of reflective practice leads, in a sense both similar to and different from the radical criticism. to a demystification of professional expertise. It leads us to recognize that for both the professional and the counterprofessional. special knowledge is embedded in evaluative frames which bear the stamp of human values and interests. It also leads us to recognize that the scope of technical expertise is limited by situations of uncertainty, instability, uniqueness, and conflict. When research-based theories and techniques are inapplicable, the professional cannot legitimately claim to be expert. but only to be especially well prepared to reflect-inaction.

Donald A. Schön

The Reflective Practitioner, 1983

## Finland

In recent years Finland has seen two main trends in architectural development: on the one hand a painstaking elaboration and inflection of Aaltoesque organicism, on the other a Constructivist swerve away from the pervasive influence of Aalto. As far as the Aalto legacy is concerned, the main problem has been how to pursue the Finnish organic tradition while keeping one's distance from the idiosyncratic tropes of the master. This predicament seems to have led to a geometrically inflected, stripped organicism as we find it, say, in Kaija and Heikki Siren's Otaniemi Church (1957) and more recently in Kapy and Sino Paavilainen's Olari Church (1976). A similar line is also evident in Juha Leiviskä's St Thomas's Church at Oulu (1975) and in his Myyrmäki Church (1985), both being as much indebted to the middle-generation expressionist architect Reima Pietila as to Aalto himself. Beyond this, however, Leiviskå's later syncopated formal assemblies remind one of certain strands in late Neo-Plastic art, such as these appear, say, in the relief sculpture of the French artist Jean Gorin. The primary drive in Leiviska's architecture is the modulation of light, which no doubt explains his personal affinity for Erik Bryggman's Turku Chapel of 1941. At the same time Leiviska's hypersensitivity to the existing context is clear from his description of a newspaper plant designed in 1987 for the town of Västerás in Sweden, situated between a historical centre and a newer, brasher, business quarter. A clash would have been unavoidable had it not been for Leiviska's capacity to shift the scale from one building to the next. As he puts it:

Most of the Vasterås site was of great archaeological importance. A normal construction process would have meant extensive. lengthy and costly excavations and study. Thus my building only touches the ground in a few places. On this precious ground there are five pairs of pillars and all the levels and spaces, even the small spatial units on the side of the old centre. hang from the main supports, like washing on a line.

This ability to employ structural suspension as a sensitive device by which to reconcile modern development with traditional fabric tends to confirm Leiviskå's indifference to the vicissitudes of fashion.

Many reasons besides prosperity must be invoked to account for the manifestation of so much talent in such a small country. In the first place, there is the evident influence of one of the most rigorous architectural schools in the world, the Helsinki Technical University school, with its traditions going back to the leadership of Nystrom (see p. 193). ## France

While the revival of architecture in France dates in many respects from the student revolt of May 1968, the modernization of the country under Charles de Gaulle seems to have been the ultimate catalyst that eventually brought the French State to intervene in the field of architecture, both theoretically through governmentsponsored research and practically through the initiation of large-scale public commissions. This was accompanied in 1968 by a total reorganization of the centralized and moribund Ecole des Beaux Arts into a series of 'unités pédagogiques', not only in Paris but also in Versailles, Clermont-Ferrand, Bordeaux and Marseilles. This decentralization transformed French architectural education and led, as a result, to the creation of a number of rigorous unités such as the UP 8 and UP 3 in Paris, respectively led by the engagé architects Henri Ciriani and Henri Gaudin. New curatorial institutions also came into being during this period, including the Institut Français d'Architecture, established under the presidency of Valéry Giscard d'Estaing.

The 1962 international competition for the Centre Pompidou in Paris inaugurated an expanding programme of government-sponsored construction that has culminated in the Grands Travaux' realized in Paris and elsewhere in France in the 1980s, under the presidency of François Mitterand. Among the numerous embellishments recently made to the French capital, prominence is given, at the two ends of the city's great axis, to a glass pyramid for the Louvre completed in 1988 by the American architect I. M. Pei and an office building in the form of a giant arch completed at La Défense in 1989 to the designs of the Danish architect Johan Otto von Spreckelsen. Other important realized competition designs dating from the first half of the 1980s include Bernard Tschumi's Par de a Villette (see above, p. 33) and the new Opéra de la Bastille designed by the littleknown Canadian architect Carlos Ott. To this array of prominent cultural amenities must be added the Musée d'Orsay, installed within the restored shell of the Gare d'Orsay by the Italian architect Gae Aulenti. This unsentimental contextual approach is evident in all of Ciriani's work, from the crescent-shaped housing built at Lognes near Evry (1986) to an archaeological museum realized at Arles in 1 991. The selection of Barcelona as the site for the 1992 Olympics has had the effect of expanding the city plan, thereby leading not only to a totally new residential quarter for 10,000 people to serve in the frst instance as he Olympic Village, but also to the creation of a coastal parkway extending from this quarter in the north to the sports park at Montjuich in the south.

357 Bonell and Rius, Horta Velodrome, Vall d'Hebron, Barcelona, 1984.

Called upon to design sports facilities for the Olympics, a number of Catalan architects seem to have had recourse to the tradition of the grands constructeurs. This is most evident perhaps in Esteban Bonell and Francesc Rius's basketball arena at Badalona (1991) and in their earlier velodrome built at Vall d'Hebron in 1984. Both are surely among the most elegant stadiums erected anywhere in the last fifty years. The strength of the velodrome parti depends upon an opposition between the pure form of the bounding circle and the elliptical trajectory of the banked track and further, of course, on an intimate correspondence between function, volume, material and structure. This last, so characteristic of the Spanish modern tradition at its best, is explicitly alluded to in Bonell's description of the project:

The outer enclosing walls are built out of perforated bricks up to a certain height and faced with white tiles at the top. In these are set the small apertures that provide ventilation and illumination for the areas intended for public use (toilets, bars, etc.).

The floors of the public areas and the stands are made out of artificial stone and artificial granite. In the changing room area, internal dividing walls are built out of unplastered blocks of reinforced concrete. . . . Each material is used according to its own logic of construction. This is intended to improve the visual quality of the finished product. Thus in the brick walls there are several courses made out of the same kind of brick but of a different color. On the pillars of reinforced concrete the joints and marks left by forms are retained as an element of visual interest.

From Antonio Vázquez de Castro's Caño Roto housing in Madrid (1961) to J. A. Coderch's new urban block type realized in the Sarria district of Barcelona in 1974, the public and private housing programs of the 1960s, 70s and 80s compelled Spanish architects to evolve new residential types and to refine still further their already consummate skills in the organization of domestic space. The typological approach of the Italian Tendenza, as proselytized through the Madrid magazine 2C Ciudad, had a decisive influence on the normative housing types developed during this period, from Francisco Barrionuevo's perimeter blocks in Seville (1 980) to the high-rise slabs built in the Palomeras district of Madrid by Estanislao Pérez Pita and Jerónimo

Junquera (1983). This pursuit of Rationalist paradigms also occurred in institutional buildings, such as the Ikastola (school) at Fuenterrabia of 1978 by Miguel Garay and José Ignacio Linazasoro and the minimalist Colegio San Fermin, Madrid (1986), by Alberto Campo Baeza. This last, in retrospect, seems uncannily close, in terms of modulated light and minimalist form, to the work of Tadao Ando.

One needs to comment in passing on the beneficial role played by the vestigial guild system operating throughout Spain under the anachronistic title of the colegios. These institutions have effectively guaranteed the profession a level of protection that hardly exists in any other country. In Spain, where no building may be erected without an architect, the regionallybased colegios exercise an influence over the entire industry. having jurisdiction not only in respect of building permits but also over professional fees, from which they take a percentage. This virtual monopoly enables them to patronize local architectural culture in many secondary ways, through exhibitions and lectures and above all through subsidizing magazines. The current Spanish lead in architectural criticism and reportage largely derives from this patronage, reflected in a number of outstanding publications such as Quaderns d'Arquitectura i urbanisme (Barcelona), Arquitectura (Madrid) and Arquitectónica (Vasco Navarro). Not only has this cultivation and implicit sense of learning embodied in the colegios assured Spanish architects of their prestige: it has also encouraged them to practise their art in terms of an immediate reality rather than look with nostalgia towards some inaccessible future or equally remote past.

## Japan

While Japanese megalopoli seem to be as chaotic and alienating as any in the West, Japan has created an exceptionally rich architectural culture in the years that have elapsed since the completion of Kenzo Tange's Tokyo City Hall in 19 a building that inaugurated a more civic phase in the reconstruction that followed the devastation of the Second World War. Directly or indirectly, the Japanese Government has played a prominent role in the rebuilding of the country, from the massive housing programmes of the Japanese Housing Authority started in 1945 to the latest State subsidies now being linl in the middle of Osaka Bay, together with a new terminal structure designed by Renzo Piano (ill 342). In this instance, half of the funds are being supplied by the Government while the other half comes from the local municipality. As this example would indicate, the role of local patronage has been just as important as that of the State. Similar undertakings include the vast Makuhari Exhibition Centre in Chiba Prefecture (1989) by Fumihiko Maki and the Tokyo Forum, under construction in 1991 to the designs of the New York-based Argentine architect Rafael Vinoly. One should note that the Tokyo Regional Administration was a major partner in financing both of these developments. Throughout this period the patronage of the public sector has been complemented by private industrial and corporate commissions, which, however paternalistic, have tended to be more socially conscious and civic-minded than is commonly the case with such agencies in the West.

Another predisposing factor affecting the quality of Japanese architectural production has been the unique capacity of the Japanese building industry to combine craft production with both rationalized industrial systems and high levels of self-sustaining research dedicated to the evolution of new materials and methods. This corporate zaibatsu approach to building, wherein many enterprises have their own architectural and engineering offices, is complemented on an ideological level by a number of influential magazines such as Shinkenchiku, Kenchiku Bunka, A + U. Telescope, GA and SD, and by the cultural activities of the major universities. While Post-Modern architectural fashions are as prevalent here as in any other advanced industrial nation, often with rather deleterious results, the country is still able to produce a considerable number of works that are conceived and executed at the highest level and often imbued with a critical as well as a poetic aspect.

Typical of this last is the work of Kazuo Shinohara, who has always assumed a somewhat disdainful attitude towards the normative building styles sponsored by the State, distancing himself successively not only from the Neo-Corbusian practice pioneered by Tange and Mayekawa but also from the post-war revival of the Japanese domestic tradition with which he was partially involved during the early 1 960s. In the numerous private houses that he has built since then, Shinohara has subtly alluded, like the writer Kobe Abe, to the chaos and violence lying beneath the surface of an all too homogeneous and seemingly tranquil technological society. His strongest and most public work to date, the metal-clad Centennial Hall erected in 1988 opposite the main entrance of the Tokyo Institute of Technology, epitomizes Shinohara's juxtaposition of dissonant forms with loosely applied off-the-peg technology. This work adequately represents his all too ambivalent attitude towards the functionalist legacy of the modern movement, a position revealed with great clarity in his essay Towards Architecture' of 1981:

I was highly impressed by a photograph of the elevation of one of the most important fighter planes used in the United States Naval Air Force. the Tomcat. To enable this craft to perform its special combat functions, all possible technical expertise and skill has been lavished on its production. But seen in elevation, the plane lacks the smooth and streamlined form one generally associates with aircraft. .   Another example of this same kind of clumsiness of desig  e Scrafat carried man moon. Although it has lost ts absolute authority. the association of form with function is by no means without effect today. However, half a century of technological development has altered the conditions for this fundamental architectural theme. In the two examples mentioned above, I discovered a solution to the problem but the machine objects in my analogy are not the mechanical bodies themselves but only parts of them. . . . New technological products have a freshness of shape and nourish our concepts of function and form. Where the architects of the twenties made analogous use of the machine as a whole. I prefer to employ only a part and even then not always to make visual analogies.

A similar critical impulse can be detected beneath the high-tech aestheticism of Shinohara's most distinguished followers, Kazunari Sakamoto, Itsuko Hasegawa and Toyo Ito. Ito's own residence in Nakano, a space-framecovered courtyard house known as the Silver Hut (1984), ironically combines 'high technology' with a reference to primitive nomadic tented architecture.

Nothing could be further from this ironic mode than the work of Fumihiko Maki who, aside from his aesthetic interests, has always been preoccupied with the urban potential of what he calls 'group form', as defined in his 1964 essay, 'Some Thoughts on Collective Forms'

We have long deplored the separation of architecture and planning. Perhaps the static, compositional methods of the past have been completely outmoded now by the rapid demands of a new technology and a new social organization. One makes static compositions of individual buildings. and only subsequently can they become aspects of the grain of the City. The vital image of group form, on the other hand, derives from the dynamic equilibrium of generative elements and not a composition of stylized and finished objects.

While Maki's concept of 'metabolic' urban form was as much indebted to Team X structuralism as to Metabolism, the specific syntax of his approach to formal fragmentation derives, in the last analysis, from the Graduate School of Design at Harvard University with which Maki was associated as both student and teacher between 1954 and 1968. This methodological connection perhaps explains the ease with which he has since been able to pass from the aggregate form of his Previ housing built in Lima, Peru, in 1972 to the cubistic collage of his Wacoal Media Centre realized in Tokyo in 1985.

Maki's architectural manner changed greatly in the 1980s as he moved away from the somewhat decorative syntax employed in his Hillside Terrace Apartments, Tokyo (1966-79) to the unprecendented structural boldness of his Fujisawa Gymnasium and Makuhari Exhibition Centre of 1987 and 1989 respectively. Departing from the model of Tange's stadiums, Maki turned to metal trusswork and stainless steel sheets in order to achieve the long-span shell forms, covered with thin metal scales, that roof over the hyperbolically profiled earthwork of the Fujisawa Gymnasium. As Serge Lalat remarked, this lightweight exterior shell form resulted in an equally luminous interior.

The great vault of the roof has an unsupported span of 80 meters [262 feet]. It is made up of a network of steel H girders which support a stainless steel envelope 0.4 millimeters [4 in.] thick. Inside, the large arena. creates a dilated interior space in which the wall surfaces recede from the center of the building. Four swaths of light, taut and dynamic, divide the membrane of the main hall into self-contained curved surfaces whose imaginary continuations enlarge and stretch the space.

Maki's fragmentary aesthetic moves today in two different but complementary directions:

![](../../Modern Architecture A Critical History (Kenneth Frampton)/images/7df164db144d1cb79c3f6f274d598ac3fa105434af785b68ec52a3acbdc46589.jpg)

towards taut orthogonal volumes, enclosed hi m sh al extremities into paper-thin louvers or gridded matrices, as in the Tepia Pavilion, Tokyo (1990), and towards metal-skinned, faceted shell forms that paradoxically seem to have more in c ith th ohon than particular oriental precedent.

The other architect laureate in Japan today is unquestionably Tadao Ando, whose recent landscaped pieces are surely among the finest architectural works built anywhere in the 1980s, above all his Chapel-on-the-Water at Tomamu, Hokkaido (1985-88), and his Children's Museum at Himijei, Hyogo Prefecture (1990). We have already come across his ideas in the chapter on Critical Regionalism. In these later works, the blank concrete walls that have become the hallmark of his style are used to integrate monumental public buildings with their immediate surroundings and, at the same time, to extend the foreground into the distance, using the traditional Japanese device of shakkei or borrowed scenery. Both the chapel and the museum open onto large expanses of water, the surfaces of which are kept moving by gravitational feeds descending over a series of shallow weirs. These works evoke the traditional Japanese concept of oku, a procedure whereby a series of auspicious sites may be conceptually linked over great distances, just as the traditional tokonoma or domestic shrine could be associated with the peak of a sacred mountain via a series of shrines and tori. Every tectonic feature in the Chapel-on-the-Water - the sliding wall, the free-standing cross and the reflecting pool that lies outside its volume - serves to engender that sense of oku whereby the artificial is mystically integrated into the surrounding natural environment. As Ando put it in 1989, 'My goal has not been to commune with nature as it is but rather to change the meaning of nature through architecture. I believe that when that happens, man will discover a new relationship with nature.'

## Postscript

From a human, economic and ecological standpoint, there is perhaps no area of human activity that is more in need of a new relationship with nature than our current mode of haphazard suburban development; the random wasteful subdivision, the strip, the shopping mall and the untold acres of relatively open landscape lost each year to urban sprawl. Despite the fact that the concept of low-rise, high-density has been widely acknowledged as a desirable model for late 20th-century ex-urban residential development, there has been precious little settlement carried out according to these principles. It is disturbing to think that low-rise, high-density was first extensively posited as a reformist strategy for neo-capitalist urban expansion over aquarter of a century ago in SergeChermayeff and Christopher Alexander's book Community Privacy963) and that since then very little legislation has been brought to bear to check the environmental pollution that spontaneously results from universal automobile ownership and rampant land speculation.

That low-rise, high-density housing is both practical and eminently livable has been more than adequately demonstrated in a number of situations since the end of the 1950s, most notably perhaps in Atelier 5's Siedlung Halen built outside Berne, Switzerland, in 1960 and in t later Thalmatt Siedlung 985) designed y the same architects. The piecemeal pursuit of this co-operative pattern has by no means been restricted to Switzerland, as we may judge from Roland Rainer's garden city of Puchenau, near Linz in Austria, built in two phases between 1969 and 1978, and from Neave Brown's exemplary inner-city Fleet Road Estate built in London in 1975. Further afield and closer in each instance to an indigenous, low-rise carpethousing tradition, one may cite Kamran Diba's Shushtar New Town, Iran (1978), and Tadao Ando's Rokko hillside housing, near Kobe, Japan, completed in 1983. More recently still we may look to Alvaro Siza's Malaguiera district at Evora in Portugal, built as a series of twostorey housing enclaves developed between 1975 and 1989.

Aside from these isolated examples, the spontaneous urbanization of the world is such that it is estimated that there will be close to fifty cities with populations of well over ten million by the year 2000, some two-thirds of which will be located in the Third World. Our somewhat effete debates as to an appropriate architecture for our time pale into insignificance beside the apocalyptic prospect of this megalopolitan explosion, comparable in its negative ecological impact to the destruction of the rain forests and the depletion of the ozone layer. There is, of course, no way in which either the building industry or the architectural profession can serve urban migrations of this magnitude.

For all ts relative permanence, building has no choice but to exist in its own historical moment. It has as its task the realization of the here and now. This means that its object can no longer be the idealized projections of the Enlightenment but rather must become the embodiment of habitable places. In a society mesmerized by consumerism, balanced eco-ontological conditions can perhaps only be achieved through the strategy of creating discontinuous enclaves; that is to say bounded fragments wherein a certain cultural and ecological symbiosis can prevail in defiance of the surrounding chaos.

The loss of the finite city as a significant cultural object no doubt partially accounts for the deliquescence of avant-garde thought and for the recognition that architecture can no longer intervene at a global scale. The void left by our incapacity to realize clearly defined urban domains, together with the institutions which they once embodied, was hidden until relatively recently by the optimistic illusions of operational planning, a quasi-positivistic discipline whose effectiveness in managing the consumer economy was contingent upon maintaining a certain indifference towards cultural form. Against this, the urban enclave now asserts tel s  feasible alternativ stray given the recent bankruptcy of planning as a projective practice.

The enclave, however, is only a potential what' at a moment when architecture has to reposition itself in order to maintain some sense of continuity and depth in relation to the overall context. In order to arrive at a viable general method for the entire practice of architecture, the 'how' must be accorded equal status. There is, as Hans Sedlmayr has pointed out, a moment when place and production are fused together to yield that quality of character from which we receive our identity. As Christian Norberg-Schultz wrote in his study of the work of Portoghesi and Gigliotti, Alla ricerca dell'architettura perduta (1975),

Whereas spatial organization may be described without referring to a particular technical solution, character cannot possibly be separated from the process of making. That is the meaning of Mies van der Rohe's well-known statement Gon the detailsThetenical evoltion the last hundred yearsis therefore more than a technicalrevolution. In fact, modern technology does not only serve to solve quantitative and economic problems but, if properly understood. may help us to substitute for the devalued motifs of historicism forms which give our environment character and thereby make it become a real place.

The veil that photo-lithography draws over architecture is not neutral. High-speed photographic and reproductive processes are surely not only the political economy of the sign but also an insidious filter through which our tactile environment tends to lose its responsiveness. When much of modern building is experienced in actuality, its photogenic quality is denied by the poverty and brutality of its detailing. Time and again an expensive and ostentatious display of either structure or form results in the impoverishment of intimacy; in that which Heidegger has recognized as the loss of 'nearness'. How rarely do we encounter a modern work where the inflection of a chosen tectonic penetrates into the innermost recesses of the structure, not as a totalizing force but as the declension of an articulate sensibility. That modern society still possesses a capacity for such inflection finds confirmation in the finest work of Aalto. Seen in this light, the art of architecture becomes a kind of 'reality reserve', as Ignasi de Solà-Morales put it, a place where man can still find material and spiritual repose; a kind of enclave capable of withstanding as other the destructive onslaught of technological modernization.
