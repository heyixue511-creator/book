# WILLIAM MORRIS AND THE ARTS AND CRAFTS MOVEMENT LINDA PARRY

WITH 100 FULL-COLOR PLATES

In the history of the decorative arts, there are few designers who have achieved such lasting impact as William Morris (1834-96). Craftsman, artist and political theorist, Morris not only created his own beautiful patterns, but inspired subsequent generations to follow the ideals of the Arts and Crafts Movement based on his teachings. Art, as he saw it, should not be the preserve of the rich, but should be accessible to everybody, and he sought to counter the worst effects of mass-production by encouraging the development of handicrafts of all kinds. Beginning with embroidery some of which he executed himself, he moved on to designing patterns for wallpapers, printed and woven textiles, carpets and tapestries, always immersing himself in the relevant manufacturing techniques so that his designs would be appropriate for their purpose. His characteristic use of native hedgerow plants and of oriental and historic patterns as source material produced designs which are still used for home furnishing to this day.

Linda Parry's comprehensive introduction to the beautiful color illustrations in this book examines Morris himself; the company, Morris and Co., which he set up; the work of his immediate successors, including his daughter May and the designs produced by some of the leading members of the Arts and Crafts Movement during the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, among them founder member Walter Crane and influential artist Charles Voysey.

A separate section, by Gillian Moss, covers the progress of the Movement in the United States. Here, too, there was an emphasis on traditional crafts, on the decoration of the home, and on the use of the simple and the familiar as inspiration for patterns. Eventually, however, the term 'Arts and Crafts' acquired overtones of amateurishness, and Morris' ideals came to be reflected in the work of individual craftspeople rather than in a living and cohesive movement.

A delight to the eye of anybody who appreciates the delicacy and subtleties of William Morris' distinctive style, this book will have a particular appeal for students of design and the history of art. Through the wealth of examples it is possible to trace the evolution of a whole school of design, as well as to examine the genius of individual designers.
