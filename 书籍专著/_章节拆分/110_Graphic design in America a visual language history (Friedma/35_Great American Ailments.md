Since the early 1960s (until 1989 under the watchful eye of Louis Silverstein) The New York Times has gradually been redesigned. As both the audience and substance of this and all American daily papers are in a constant state of flux, flexible yet concise design is a paramount need. One of Silverstein's most significant moves was the inclusion of more and larger photographs in a newspaper that in the past had used fewer than most. In this 1975 example from the Op-Ed page, the image of the rifle that many presume killed President Kennedy creates a powerful lead-in to the accompanying critical assessment of the Warren Commission Report.
