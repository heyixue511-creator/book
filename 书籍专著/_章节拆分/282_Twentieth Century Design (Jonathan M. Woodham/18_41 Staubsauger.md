The New York World's Fair of 1939/4o, centred on the theme of 'Design for Tomorrow', provided an unrivalled opportunity for industrial designers to harness themselves to the marketing ambitions of the larger American corporations since, as Walter Dorwin Teague remarked in 1939, they were 'supposed to understand public taste and be able to speak in the popular tongue, and because as a profession they are bound to disregard traditional forms and solutions and think in terms of today and tomorrow'. The main themes of the World's Fair echoed the major developments which had been taking place in the 1930s: transportation, science, and communications.

## Contemporary critiques of industrial culture

A number of historians have drawn attention to the critics of the ethos of mass-production in contemporary America.1° Some critics looked back to the past as representing a simpler, more moral existence, contrasting modern capitalist commercial society with a rural way of life. The photographer Doris Ulman, for example, through her early 1930s images of the communities of the Appalachian mountains focused attention on the values inherent in 'folk' culture, sustained by craftsmen and women working within their own cultural traditions. However, other more strident voices launched more direct attacks on contemporary industrial culture in the United States.

Henry Ford, whose assembly-line techniques were increasingly widely applied in factories throughout the industrialized world, was a particular target of those who saw life in the USA as 'shaped by the spiritual dictatorship of machinery, warfare and magazines and newspapers of large circulation'.11 For example, in Brave New World of 1932 Aldous Huxley attacked wide-spread faith in the concepts of the large-scale and standardization which he saw as major ingredients of what he feared would become a loveless and emotionally arid society, a society in which Fordism was the dominant religion, with Ford replacing God and Ford's autobiography the Bible. The cinema also carried a number of anti-technological messages in films such as Fritz Lang's chilling vision of Metropolis of 1926, a hostile, futurist city in which its inhabitants were enslaved and dominated by technology; many of the sets were directly inspired by the Manhattan skyline which Lang had seen on a visit to New York in 1924. Over a decade later, in Chaplin's Modern Times of 1936, the relentless drive for ever-increasing efficiency in the production line (which Chaplin had experienced at first-hand at the Ford Factory in Detroit) eventually resulted in the insanity of the worker.

There were also critics within the wider reaches of the design world, perhaps the most articulate of whom was Richard Buckminster Fuller.12 Very much opposed to what he saw as the complicity of industrial designers in the superficial styling and built-in obsolescence of an everincreasing range of consumer goods, he had sought increasingly to propose design solutions that derived maximum human advantage from the minimum use of materials and energy. He wrote on this 'Dymaxion' theme and produced prototypes for his highly revolutionary Dymaxion cars (from 1933) and bathrooms (from 1930) [142], although for a variety of reasons these failed to find acceptance with manufacturers. Unwilling to compromise his beliefs, he remained highly sceptical of the grandiose claims of the burgeoning industrial-design profession to be able to take on everything from a fountain pen to an ocean liner. Fuller viewed industrial design as a close adjunct of advertising and marketing and, reflecting on such an outlook in the late 195os, he declared;

industrial design is a very tarnished affair . . . I assure you that no aircraft company will let an industrial designer through its engineering front door. Industrial designers are considered to be pure interior and exterior decorators. And yet, I've listened to industrial designers assert that they designed the steamship, United States. If you were to exhibit schematically all the items that the industrial designers created or he Unitd States, you would have sailing down New York Harbour an array of window curtains, chairs, paint clouds and bric-a-brac floating in space, with nothing to really hold it together. To assert that these men designed the United States could not have been more dishonest.13

## The impact of European design

American interest in European design in the interwar years was bolstered by a number of European immigrant designers including Joseph Urban and Paul Frankl from Austria, Gustav Jensen from Denmark, Peter Muller-Munk from Germany, George Sakier, William Lescaze, and Raymond Loewy from France, and Paul Lazlo from Hungary. Furthermore the American Designers' Gallery and Contempora Inc. were both established in the later 192os in order to promote modern decorative arts and design in the United States, much along the lines of European groups such as the Wiener Werkstätten and the Werkbunds in Austria and Germany or the Société des Artistes Décorateurs in France. Although the activities of such American propagandist organizations were seriously curtailed by the Wall Street Crash of 1929 they did much to foster a new climate for modern design in the United States in the years leading up to the Second World War. ## Paris 1925: luxury, elegance, and commerce

As has been seen in connection with the New York World's Fair of 1939/40, international exhibitions were important vehicles for the promotion of national interests, whether propagandist or economic, most particulary for the host nation, whose industries and national displays assumed the greatest significance. The Paris Exposition des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels of 1925 was very much a matter of French national prestige since its explicit aim was to re-establish her preeminence as the leading force in the decorative arts. Even before the outbreak of hostilities in 1914 France's economic status in this respect had been increasingly called into question as a result of the growing international industrial competitiveness of other countries.

For various reasons—most particularly the intervention of the First World War—the idea of mounting an international exhibition to follow up the 19oo Paris Exposition Universelle was not realized until 1919 when it featured as part of the French postwar programme of industrial development and economic recovery. Sponsored by the

Ministries of Commerce and Fine Arts, the projected postwar exhibition was intended to encourage the establishment of a modern design vocabulary that would blend traditional French expertise in the design of luxury goods with the realities of modern mass-production technology. None the less, the kind of modernism embraced by the vast majority of French products and room settings was far from the ideas being discussed by Muthesius and others at the Deutscher Werkbund before the First World War. Apart from the austere modernism of Le Corbusier's Pavillon de l'Esprit Nouveau (discussed in Chapter 2, see 28) a quality of unbridled luxuriousness and élitism characterized a significant proportion of the French exhibits, many of which were out of the financial reach of the everyday consumer. As a result the exhibition engendered a considerable amount of criticism. None the less, as will be discussed below, many of the stylistic characteristics on display, subsequently known as Art Deco, were widely influential in the United States, and made their presence firmly felt in many fields of design in Britain and elsewhere.

One of the pavilions at the Exposition which attracted most attention was Jacques-Emil Ruhlmann's Pavilion of a Wealthy Collector [44]. Even the title confirmed Ruhlmann's close links with high fashion, high prices, exclusivity, and a premium on craftsmanship rather than industry. Lacquered furniture by Dunand, decorative iron railings by Edgar Brandt, a blue and pink carpet by Gaudissard, and the celebrated exoticism of Jean Dupas's oil painting The Parakeets all sustained such an impression.

As at the Paris 19oo international exhibition, some of the most memorable displays a quarter of a century later were located in the pavilions of the great Parisian department stores, all of which were situated on favourable sites. Their presence in the French section together with the enticing Rue des Boutiques on the Pont Alexandre III, helped sustain the desired image of Paris as a city of shopping.14 This street contained forty shop displays of haute couture, fashion accessories, perfumes, and other luxury goods.

The lavish displays of the Parisian department stores were driven by their desire to capitalize on a growing public interest in contemporary design. This spirit of commercial entrepreneurship had already brought about the establishment of their own design ateliers: 'Primavera' (under the directorship of René Guilleré and his wife Charlotte Chaucet-Guilleré) had been established at Printemps in 1912, La Maitrise (under Maurice Dufrène) at Galeries Lafayette in 1921, Pomone (under Paul Follot) at Bon Marché in 1923, and Studium Louvre (under Étienne Kohlmann and Maurice Matet) at the Magasins du Louvre in 1924 [45].

## Paris 1925 and the United States

Although not a participant at the 1925 Paris Exposition des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels,15 the United States appointed a commission of over a hundred representatives drawn from a wide range of industries and institutions to visi it and report on the state o contemporary European design. Charles Richards, Director of the American Association of Museums, headed the commission and subsequently organized a touring exhibition of designs drawn from the 1925 exhibition which was shown in nine museums in the USA, commencing with the Metropolitan Museum in New York. Drawing on diverse sources such as Fauvism, Cubism, Purism, and African art, the Art Deco style was characterized by fat, geometric abstract motifs used in a decorative manner, often deriving from sunbursts, lightning flashes, fountains, flowers, and other organic forms. Such abstract forms soon became fashionable, and the style was actively promoted by leading stores throughout the country, many of whom also mounted exhibitions.16 In New York, artists such as Erté and Raoul Dufy designed for the Amalgamated Silk and Onondaga Companies, and firms such as Cheney Brothers and F. Schumacher & Co marketed French textiles alongside other ranges.

Architecture and interiors, particularly in terms of detailing such as metalwork, lettering and signage, lighting and the geometric patterning of floor and wall surfaces, brought the Deco style very much to the public eye in the major cities of North America. In New York, William Van Alen's Chrysler Building of 1928-31, with its dramatic stainless steel cladding and roofline, external geometric frieze of wheels, radiator caps, and abstracted eagles, and striking lobby interior typified most vividly a stylistic trend which manifested itself throughout Manhattan. In Washington many Government buildings, particularly in the Federal Triangle, also adopted the style, which was equally widespread in the West, as in Los Angeles, or in the south, as in Miami Beach.

The Deco influence was also widely seen in interiors connected with the leisure and entertainment industries. The Rockefeller Center complex in New York provided an almost unprecedented opportunity for the exploration of the Deco style, particularly in the interiors of Radio City Music Hall (RCMH). The elaborative decorative schemes were overseen by Donald Deskey who did much to link European design with practice in the United States, having studied in Paris in the early 192os and visited the 1925 Exposition.
