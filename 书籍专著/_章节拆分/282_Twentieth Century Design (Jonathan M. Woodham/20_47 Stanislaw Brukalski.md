## Wider patterns of taste: historical diversity

Not only was film instrumental in the promotion of luxurious Art graphical origins, ranging from ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome through to the Middle Ages, the Renaissance and the Elizabethan era, and onwards to the present day. American cinemas themselves also took on a plethora of styles in their highly striking interiors, often competing for effect with the elaborate celluloid fantasies seen on screen.17

Stylistic diversity was also reflected in the homes, furniture, and furnishings of the majority of Americans. Although they willingly embraced notions of contemporaneity in the streamlined forms of their domestic appliances, kitchen equipment, and automobiles, admired the strikingly modern designs for public transport systems as seen in aircraft such as the Douglas DC-3, trains such as Dreyfuss's Twentieth Century Limited or Loewy's Greyhound bus, they generally looked to traditional styles in the decoration of their homes. The ubiquitous mail-order catalogues of the period unequivocally underline this affirmation of the traditional in the home. Manufacturers were generally conservative in their outlook, drawing on the past for commercial survival in the present, and consumers could purchase a variety of styles including Tudor, Queen Anne, Chippendale, and Louis XVI.

In their 1945 book Tomorrow's House: A Complete Guide to the Home-Builder, the modernist propagandists George Nelson and Henry Wright lamented the sentimental attachment of home-owners o the past. Considering the Home as a Psychological Fact they wrote:

The period styles in which many houses were built in the 192os and 3os reflected a similar spirit of retrospection, although taking on a richer diversity and choice. As will be considered in the following chapter, a number of these styles were identified as specifically American in their origins and were promoted by a number of organizations intent on lessening dependency on European models through the establishment of a distinctive American style. For example, the restoration of Williamsburg in 1926, funded by the Rockefellers, reinforced interest in the Colonial style [133]; the Spanish Colonial style, deriving from traditions rooted in the Southwest, was also popular. ## Commerce and consumerism in Britain

Historians of design have often played down the popular and commercially successful in the interwar years, following the line of contemporary design reformers who have suggested that cheap historicist or modernistic mass-produced items were often purchased as a result of a lack of education inmatters of taste. Yet for the consumer or user their forms and decoration were often laden with meaning and associations, whether or not Pevsner in his Enquiry into Industrial Art of 1937 condemned them as part of the 9o per cent of industrial products that he adjudged to be 'artistically objectionable', whether or not they were designed by a named designer or simply copied from, or variants on, long-standing precedents. With the exception of museums such as the Geffrye Museum in London or site museums such as Ironbridge Gorge or Beamish examples of this kind are rarely collected by museums, let alone located in the context of 'ordinary' rooms. Such objects are generally seen as artistically deficient rather than culturally significant; documentation concerning their use by consumers is hard to come by; often material concerning their design, production, and distribution has often simply been thrown away. However, research²0 into such areas has been slowly developing, with a steady stream of publications about methodology and possible approaches.2²1 However, highly commercial styles often signify more than the fact that they are ephemeral trends and, in the case of Tudor, Elizabethan, and Jacobean formulae, can be seen to embrace notions of national identity in design, as will be discussed in Chapter 4. Similarly, the floral patterns adorning countless tea-cups and furnishing textiles may be interpreted as communicators of an essential' Englishness rather than summarily dismissed as hackneyed designs.

## Other sites of commercial display: England and France

Barometers of commercial production and consumer interest in Britain may be found in trade catalogues and periodicals, as well as the range of home-making magazines which grew considerably after the war. The vast range of exhibits at the annual British Industries Fairs (which commenced on an annual basis in 1915 as a manufacturers' 'shopwindow') and the displays and show houses at the increasingly popular Daily Mail Ideal Home Exhibitions are also revealing. The latter commenced in 1908, with 160,o00 visitors in its first year, and nearly 700,000 even in the depression of the early 193os. In the 1950s and 196os there were regularly more than 1 million visitors, tailing off in the later 197os and early 1980s to an average of about 900,000. Many of the exhibitions were widely reported in the media, and catalogues and related ephemera, including visual material, are extant.22
