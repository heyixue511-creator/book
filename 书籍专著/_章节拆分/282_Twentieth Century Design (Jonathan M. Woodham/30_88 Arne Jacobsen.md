## 88 Arne Jacobsen

Cutlery, AJ pattern, produced by A. Michelsen, Copenhagen, 1957

The bold yet refined simplicity of this Danish cutlery is an elegant reconsideration of everyday artefacts and typifies what has become known as Scandinavian Modern design. Jacobsen's minimalist design created a furore when it was first introduced but has subsequently become a design 'classic'. The organic character, particularly of the spoon, allies it to essays in sculptural form design in Italy, France, and elsewhere.

However, there was also a strong desire amongst German designers to establish themselves on a sounder professional footing than had been the case in the interwar years. As one means of persuading commerce and industry of their economic worth designers placed an increased emphasis on the need to adopt a more systematic and scientific analysis of design problems. This also signalled a clear ideological shift away from the völkisch characteristics associated with the Third Reich. As will be investigated further in Chapter 7, this scientific approach was particularly associated with the development of the curriculum at the highly influential Hochschule für Gestaltung, where teaching commenced in 1953, as well as with products made by Braun in which notions of 'functional beauty' were emphasized. For many years the dominant trend amongst manufacturers was to place a premium on design, a trend sustained by official and industrially supported organizations promoting 'good design'. The Rat für Formgebung (German Design Council), the Neue Sammlung Gallery in Munich, the Deutscher Werkbund [92, 93], the Institut für Neue Technische Form at Darmstadt, and Industrieform at Essen ll sought to stress the economic and aesthetic benefits of rational design.
