# INTRODUCTION by Mark Crispin Miller

## I.

The history of America since the Civil War is, in large part, a history of conquest by commercial advertising. The decades have seen more and more of it, appearing in more places than it ever was before, with more and more of it more brilliantly designed, and more efficiently compressed, and more ingeniously disguised as something else. Thus more and more of it has passed into our hearts and minds without our seeing it or knowing it (a feature of the most effective propaganda); and its appeals and promises have been devised with ever more sophistication, and with ever greater subtlety implanted deep inside our heads. In short, the modern history of America is, in large part, the history of an ever-rising flood of corporate propaganda—and also of our various responses to it, as We the People have obscurely struggled to reverse it, or resist it, or to live our lives in spite of it, or have simply let it carry us away.

For over a century, there have been many works diversely critical of that commercial tide—books and articles and films assailing its unsightliness, intrusiveness, false claims, deceptive offers, moral crudeness, idiotic language, poisonous effects on public health, sly approaches to small children, non-stop sexist fantasy, casual racism, creed of selfishness and catastrophic impact on the world's ecology, etc. It is a highly edifying literature; and yet very few of those quixotic works have moved the public. In 1927, crusading journalist Stuart Chase and engineer Frederick J. Schlink came out with Your Money's Worth: A Study in the Waste of the Consumer's Dollars, a powerful exposé of the countless little frauds routinely perpetrated on Americans by corporate manufacturers, whose ads hid more than they revealed. (In 1925, Chase had taken aim directly at commercial advertising in his first book, Tragedy of Waste.) The book was a political sensation, starting a new chapter in the history of mass activism. "The Uncle Tom's Cabin of the consumer movement," as one admirer later put it, Chase and Schlink's book led to the founding, in 1929, of Consumer Research, Inc., the first group of its kind, and a crucial stimulus for later, bolder organizing.

And yet no popular critique of advertising moved the public, or changed their view of that essential corporate craft, as deeply or enduringly as Vance Packard's The Hidden Persuaders. Although it had no obvious political effects (nor did its author call for any), this anomalous exposé encouraged a new mass attentiveness to all of modern marketing, the ads included—a critical alertness, or heightened wariness, that is still perceptible (albeit less prevalent) today.

Not that advertising had been much esteemed or trusted prior to the publication of this book. From the Twenties, and especially since the Great Depression, "Madison Avenue" had been about as popular as smoker's cough. What Packard did was educate, and thereby heighten, that common animus, by illuminating what he called "the depth approach"that had overtaken corporate marketing since World War II. "Large-scale efforts are being made, often with impressive success," Packard notes, correctly, in his opening paragraph,

to channel our unthinking habits, our purchasing decisions, and our thought processes by the use of insights gleaned from psychiatry and the social sciences. Typically these efforts take place beneath our level of awareness; so that the appeals which move us are often, in a sense, "hidden."

Although it had no obvious political effect, The Hidden Persuaders made a difference in the public's concept of commercial suasion. Formerly—i.e., from the Twenties into the mid-Fifties—advertising had been generally conceived as a coercive din, drummed up everywhere to browbeat, addle and/or mesmerize the masses into squandering their hard-earned wages on a lot of glittering junk. Such "bunk" or "ballyhoo" was the obvious and abrasive work of "hucksters," highpaid bunko artists working in the circus mode of P.T. Barnum, and yet advantaged by a range of marvelous new propaganda media—from neon to rotogravure to radio to cinema, etc.—that Barnum never had a chance to use. Thus, before the coming of Vance Packard's exposé, the public, insofar as they thought much about it, tended to see advertising as an endless con, all half-truths and hyperbole and outright lies. That casually cynical perception was reflected back in various satiric bits and censures in the post-war years: Cary Grant in Mr. Blandings Builds His Dream House, agonizing over copy for WHAM Ham (1948, based on Eric Hodgins' novel of two years before); Lucy getting plastered doing several takes of a TV commercial for Vitameatavegamin, a bogus tonic spiked with alcohol (1952); Roy Norr's "Cancer by the Carton," the famous article assailing the tobacco companies for glamorizing toxic wares (which Reader's Digest published twice in 1952); and so on.

As Hodgins' (gentle) barb makes clear, it was not just the public that saw advertising as an overload of bunk. Many in the advertising industry also regarded their profession as a giant rip-off. Hence the definitive self-contempt of such literary heroes as Mr. Blandings, who, as one of many "hard-working, highly competent, deeply miserable men who wrote advertising copy," Hodgins writes, "loathed his calling with a deep, passionate intensity."2 (Having labored as a writer at, editor for, and publisher of Fortune, Hodgins had known many ad men.) What made that sort of work despicable, especially for anyone with democratic notions, was its treatment of Americans en masse according to the mechanistic tenets of old-fashioned crowd psychology—an angry inside view that would from time to time receive a national hearing.

"Remember, two things make good advertising," counsels Evan Evans, the monstrous hand-soap magnate in The Hucksters, Frederic Wakeman's best-selling novel of 1946 (and the basis of a Clark Gable vehicle released by MGM in 1947)

"One, a good simple idea. Two, repetition. And by repetition, by God, I mean until the public is so irritated with it, they'll buy your brand because they bloody well can't forget it. All you professional advertising men are scared the death of raping the public; I say the public likes it, if you got the know-how to make 'em relax and enjoy it."3

ington Hill, the bullying, amoral founder/president of American Tobacco—his crude take on advertising is, unfortunately, on the money (so to speak), as Vic Norman, the novel's hero, fully understands. Based on, and speaking for, the novel's author—who had worked for several years at Foote, Cone & Belding in New York—Norman ends up telling Evans off and moving on to less degraded, less degrading labor.

All such satire and polemic, even at its most indignant, greatly understated the threat posed by advertising, particularly since the war; for those responses all cast advertising as overt solicitation: easy to perceive and, therefore, easy to resist, if you just keep your wits about you and deploy your common sense. Anyone thus armed will not be moved by the false promises of advertising, nor led into temptation by its other blatant stimuli, however expertly concocted they may be: the motto that you can't forget, the jingle that won't leave your head, or the endless hints that you're too fat or scrawny, pale or pimply, vulgar, flakey-headed, dull or stinky, or just ludicrously "out-of-date." As advertising is, in short, a vast unwanted overture, those who always try to see it coming will see through it every time, because its many faults—its triviality, its foolishness, its wild hyperbole, and even its incessant bald-faced lies—are all explicit evils, plain as day to anyone with open eyes.

The Hidden Persuaders shook up that complacent view, by baring the dark side of advertising; or, to put it more precisely, by demonstrating that the advertising industry had lately started up, and now was seeking to perfect, a whole new science of allurement—one based not on crowd psychology, with its crude model of collective stimulus/response, but on much subtler notions of the mind, derived primarily from psychoanalysis, with insights also gleaned from sociology and cultural anthropology.4 Although Packard did not make the point explicitly, that giant step beyond behaviorism—or, as it were, from Pavlov to Freud & Co.—mooted the blithe theory that one might withstand the siren song of advertising just by hunkering down into a constant posture of defensive skepticism. While that stance might have helped defeat the coarsest tactics of the past, it could not shield the skeptic from the more exquisite and oblique devices used increasingly throughout the last half-century. As this book revealed, the industry was trying to hone its probing apparatus to so fine a point that the staunchest skeptic would not see it coming; and its long needles would now reach so deeply into him, without his even knowing it, that he might buy just as the apparatus meant him to, while quite convinced that such "decisions" were his own. Thus advertising, at its most effective, was no longer comparable to that blunt rape so gleefully invoked by Evan Evans, but far more like a masterful seduction: unsuspected, irresistible, and leaving the seduced with an insatiable and highly profitable appetite for more, and more, and more.

The influence of Packard's book was evident throughout the culture. In the movies, for example, advertising men now sometimes figured as ambiguous protagonists of a new kind: not tormented idealists, like Vic Norman in The Hucksters, but cool manipulators gifted with a sinister attractiveness. "You're very clever with words," Eve Kendall (Eve Marie Saint) says to adman Roger Thornhill (Cary Grant) in Hitchcock's North by Northwest (1959), as they stand necking shakily in her sleeper on the westbound train. "You can probably make them do anything for you: Sell people things they don't need; make women who don't know you fall in love with you." ("I'm beginning to think I'm underpaid," Thornhill charmingly replies.) The Doris Day/Rock Hudson comedy Lover Come Back (1962) is another notable post-Packard view of advertising, with ad man Jerry Webster (Hudson) almost managing to compromise the steely chastity of his top advertising rival, Carol Templeton (Day), by very ably posing as his virginal antithesis, the earnest bumpkin Dr. Linus Taylor. (Both of those films end with their smooth anti-heroes overcoming their professional seductiveness for love and marriage.) In entertainment, Packard's view continued to resound throughout the century, with horrifically manipulative ad men at the heart of clever films like Bruce Robinson's How to Get Ahead in Advertising (1989) and Jan Egleson's A Shock to the System (1990, based on Simon Brett's novel published in 1984). On television, meanwhile, Packard's influence showed also on ABC's thirtysomething... (1987-91), which featured the satanic ad man Miles Drentell (David Clennon), a terrifying whiz at playing games with other people's minds.

Off-screen, meanwhile, The Hidden Persuaders inadvertently gave rise to a persistent myth about the prevalence of "subliminal" appeals, or "implants": words or images that make us hungry, thirsty, scared or (mostly) horny, even though the naked eye cannot detect them. Although Packard does refer to an alleged attempt to use "implants" on moviegoers at a theater in New Jersey (to get them out to the concession stand to buy refreshments), he treats the claim with proper journalistic skepticism; nor does his book at any other point suggest that advertisers had deployed "subliminal" techniques. Nevertheless, due, no doubt, to its suggestive title, The Hidden Persuaders is often misidentified as the first book to expose the use of advertising "implants"—a thesis that was then pursued, relentlessly, by Wilson Brian Key, whose Subliminal Seduction (1973) was the first of several works asserting that the images of advertising, among many others, have been stealthily inscribed with countless imperceptible profanities and breasts, to which the masses had presumably responded with robotic unanimity. Thus the theory of "subliminalism" actually owes far less to Vance Packard than it does to Ivan Pavlov and his kind. Although the advertising industry was keenly interested in such invisible suggestion (and still conducts sporadic research on the possibility), they never could come up with an effective means of doing it, and have therefore largely dropped it.

Meanwhile, advertising has, since Packard's day, continued to deploy "the depth approach," albeit not just with the quasi-Freudian methods noted in these pages. For the past half-century, the industry has been refining and augmenting its machinery for tracking, stalking, driving all the rest of us—a long, hard push necessitated not just by the advertisers' ever-growing need to sell us even more (and more, and more), but also by our ever-growing unresponsiveness to all that subtle "motivation" by the corporate juggernaut; for we are only human after all, and there is only so much suasion we can take. The scope, relentlessness and fiendish ingenuity of advertising/marketing today are captured brilliantly in two incisive Frontline documentaries——"The Merchants of Cool" (2001) and "The Persuaders" (2003)—whose producers, Rachel Dretzin and Barak Goodman (with the help of author Douglas Rushkoff), have carried on Vance Packard's legacy.

## II.

Published in April of 1957, The Hidden Persuaders soon became a huge success, shattering the fondest expectations of its author and his publisher. Packard had allowed himself to hope his book might sell as many as 12,000 copies—a coup that would enable him to realize his dream of living as an independent author. (Since 1942 he had been working full-time as a staff writer for American Magazine, which had recently folded.) Within a month, buoyed by many excellent reviews ("One of the most startling books of the season," noted The Atlantic Monthly, "[i]t deserves to be widely read"), The Hidden Persuaders was on the New York Times non-fiction best-seller list, reaching the top on August 4, and staying up there till the last week of September (except for the week of August 18, when Jim Bishop's The Day Christ Died was briefly number one). Packard's book topped other national lists for a full year, and kept on selling for decades thereafter. By 1975, there were three million copies of the book in print.

Meanwhile, Packard's next two books also soared straight into the commercial heavens. The Status Seekers (1959), which brightly debunked the great myth of America's "classless society," was also on national best-seller lists for a year. This book was followed shortly by The Waste Makers (1960), a detailed survey of the vast threat posed by surplus production—or, in Packard's words, "the pressures to make us more wasteful, imprudent, and carefree in our consuming habits."5 Although The Waste Makers topped the Times best-seller list for a few weeks only, it was on other lists for some six months. Thus had The Hidden Persuaders introduced a trilogy that was extraordinary, in part, for its mass appeal. "With these three books," writes David Horowitz, "Packard achieved what few if any other American nonfiction authors had done before or since—had three different books in the number one position on the best-seller list within four years."6

As surprising as it was to Packard, the broad appeal of his trilogy—and of this book in particular—is even more surprising now, as we can see from a quick retrospective of the other cultural goods on sale in 1957/58. Packard's exposé was a "frightening report on how manufacturers, fund-raisers, and politicians are attempting to turn the American mind into a kind of catatonic dough that will buy, give, or vote at their command," as The New Yorker put it. As that book was selling, so was Grace Metalious's Peyton Place (both the novel and the movie, starring Lana Turner), Art Linkletter's Kids Say the Darndest Things (both the TV program and the book), Jean Kerr's Please Don't Eat the Daisies, and 'Twixt Twelve and Twenty by Pat Boone (whose biggest hit ever, "Love Letters in the Sand," topped the charts in June and July). On the big screen there was Auntie Mame, Cat on a Hot Tin Roof, and also Gigi, South Pacific and Old Yeller; and theaters were still screening Giant (1956) with the late James Dean. On the small screen there was Gunsmoke and The Ed Sullivan Show (both on CBS since 1955), and, debuting in the fall, Leave It to Beaver (CBS), Perry Mason (CBS), Wagon Train (NBC), Maverick (ABC) and Have Gun Will Travel (CBS). Meanwhile, teens were going crazy over Elvis Presley's "Teddy Bear," Debbie Reynolds's "Tammy," "I'm Walking" by Ricky Nelson, and Jerry Lee Lewis's "Whole Lotta Shakin' Goin' On" (which, on the night of July 28, brought the house down on The Steve Allen Show).

In that campy Hall of Fame there seems to be no place for Packard's anxious trilogy. (Although he came out with another seven books before his death in 1996, those first three are his most influential.) This is another way of saying that The Hidden Persuaders and its two sequels seem to have been published prematurely, as they apparently belong not to the Fifties but the Sixties—the decade of Silent Spring, The Other America, Unsafe at Any Speed and other great analyses of major ills that too few affluent Americans had even noticed. That Packard's status is anomalous, his place in history unsure, is evident in his peculiar absence from the pertinent surveys of his time and place. In David Halberstam's The Fifties, a suitably gigantic overview, Packard's name comes up just once: not for anything he wrote, but for a speech he gave in 1959, noting his decision to start writing books instead of articles—a revelation that inspired the young Betty Friedan to write an entire volume on "the feminine mystique."7 Packard's writings also go unmentioned in most studies of the contemporary critical response to the Eisenhower Era's culture of consumption. While such retrospectives duly note the academic works of C. Wright Mills and David Riesman, and such best-sellers as William O. Whyte's Organization Man and Sloan Wilson's The Man in the Gray Flannel Suit, we find only passing mention, if any, of the decade's most renowned critiques of corporate commercialism.

For several reasons, we should now re-examine Packard's place in the history of U.S. consumer culture, and include his most important work in our considerations of what might be called the intellectual resistance to that culture. First of all, we might enrich our understanding of the Fifties by taking note of Packard's healthy sales and positive celebrity—clear signs that the American public was, in Eisenhower's second term, awakening from the collective stupor that enabled, and/or was intensified by, Joe McCarthy's meteoric reign. Not that anticommunism suddenly became unpopular post-1955. In 1956, the year before The Hidden Persuaders was first published, G-Man Don Whitehead's The F.B.I. Story, published by Random House, had been a national best-seller (and inspired the film released by Warner Bros. in 1959, with James Stewart and Vera Miles). And in June of 1958, when Packard's book was still on the best-seller lists, Henry Holt brought out J. Edgar Hoover's Masters of Deceit, which was an immediate smash hit (although Hoover had, in fact, not really written it).

According to such works, the only "masters of deceit" worth any serious concern were all those stealthy agents dedicated to the imperceptible creation of "a Soviet America." ("Basic communist strategy dictates that noncommunist hands, knowingly or unknowingly, under communist guidance, must further the influence of the communist world," notes "Hoover.")s The lingering appeal of such mythology makes Packard's book, and its tremendous popularity, all that much more remarkable. Packard claimed, implicitly, that We the People were routinely and successfully manipulated not by agents of the Kremlin but by "manufacturers, fund-raisers and politicians" right here in the USA. To say so back then took a certain boldness; and the startling resonance of Packard's argument, moreover, may suggest that more Americans were sophisticated than we may imagine in this very knowing new millennium, when our conception of the Fifties is based largely on Grease, Happy Days and Nick at Nite.

A re-examination of this book may also help illuminate this moment; for the transcontinental shopping mall that is America today (and not only in America) was implicit in the trend of marketing research that Packard studied and exposed. Indeed, such is the primary interest of The Hidden Persuaders for us here and now. On the one hand, the book is an invaluable historical resource: a veritable gold mine of information as to how and why all sorts of products-cigarettes, instant coffee, margarine, milk (and dried milk), beer (and light beer), cars (and tires), Ry-Krisp, soup, Father's Day, and even politicians, churches and communities—were represented as they were back in the late mid-morning of American consumer culture. More importantly, however, Packard's book is a frank, if courteous, indictment of a massive corporate drive against ourselves as thinking citizens, on behalf of a commercial project radically opposed to our true interests, and (as Packard's later work makes clear) ecologically impossible.

## III.

The book has certain weaknesses, which some reviewers noted at the time. Writing as a journalist, and desperate to support his family by doing so, Packard opted for a lively, wide-eyed style that was, perhaps, more appropriate to his commercial needs than to his critical responsibilities. The book is largely anecdotal: a long barrage of striking or disquieting examples, with Packard not distinguishing among them, and sometimes lumping disparate methods or techniques together, under the distended rubric of "M.R."9 Some of those examples, furthermore, may well have been exaggerated, if not bogus. Packard based his claims primarily on what M.R.'s own top practitioners had told him; and since those businessmen had every reason to appear infallible, one must take their testimony with a grain of salt. Packard himself makes this very point in Chapter 22—-a late acknowledgement that seems to come up only as an afterthought. The academic reader may well wish that Packard had been cooler and more skeptical. (This one also wishes that the book included endnotes.)

More substantively, Packard's critics also faulted him for the softness of his analysis. First, his conception of the mass response to advertising is deterministic, as if people would desire and buy exactly as M.R.'s mad scientists intended. There are certain natural and/or circumstantial limits to just how successful any propaganda drive might be, however expertly it may be crafted—a fact that Packard overlooks or understates, whether out of mere naiveté or his own commercial urge to simplify his story.

Moreover, Packard's view of advertising is completely ahistorical and apolitical. Although he had been something of a leftist in his youth (like most everybody else back in the Thirties), Packard was no radical. His critique has no systemic basis. Packard sees the problem of covert "persuasion" as a mere unsettling fad within the marketing establishment, and not as an inevitable outgrowth of the economic system. His animus against M.R. was founded mainly on a deep albeit vague nostalgia for the rugged individualism that he recalled from his own early years as a "backwater farm boy" from Bradford County, Pennsylvania. There he had imbibed the Jeffersonian ideals that, more than any conscious theory, economic or political, underlay not only his critique of marketing research but all his works from first to last. That world-view his critics often found uncomfortably naive (although Christopher Lasch—another inconvenient figure— later pointedly extolled him for it).10 Packard's critics also noted his ambivalence toward M.R., by which he seemed to be as fascinated as he was appalled; and, as well, they scored him for the social narrowness of his analysis, which focused only on those citizens who could afford to be of any interest to the marketing establishment. In Packard's view, there seemed to be no poor people (a charge leveled at a number of his books).

There was justice to the criticism (although some of the attacks, especially by academic sociologists, were driven also by professional resentment, as they envied him his national readership). Packard certainly did not, or would not, perceive that M.R., even at its most fanciful, was neither an anomalous nor sudden trend but an organic outgrowth of U.S. corporate capitalism after World War II. While the researchers' quasi-Freudian methodology was something new, the fundamental impetus behind their "depth approach" had long since electrified the American economy. Thoughtful ad men had for years perceived themselves as experts at "consumption engineering"—a phrase devised in 1930 by Earnest Elmo Calkins, a brilliant copywriter and one of advertising's top apologists. "Goods fall into two classes," Calkins wrote in Printer's Ink, "those we use, such as motor cars or safety razors, and those we use up, such as toothpaste or soda biscuit. Consumption engineering must see to it that we use up the kind of goods we now merely use." Calkins then elaborated with a passage that uncannily anticipated Packard's qualms, as expressed not only in this book but throughout the best-selling trilogy that made his name:

Would any change in the goods or the habits of people speed up their consumption [sic]? Can they be displaced by newer models? Can artificial obsolescence be created? Consumption engineering does not end until we can consume all we can make.11

That such a plan must have\~—and has had—catastrophic consequences for our health and happiness (not to mention our democracy) Calkins et al. surely could not know, and never would accept. By 1930, there was nothing odd about his notion of "consumption engineering," which had in fact increasingly absorbed the corporate advertisers and their agencies since the boom times just after the Great War. In 1921, J. Walter Thompson hired psychologist John Watson, one of the founders of behaviorism, in order to exploit his expertise, while also lending him a bully pulpit for his own campain to pitch behaviorism as a great new tool for corporate commerce. Watson prospered at the agency, remaining there for over twenty years; but it was not his academic background that, in the end, enabled his success. Although his training helped the agency make certain valuable discoveries—for example, he ran the first blind taste test in the history of marketing research, thereby demonstrating that the top four cigarette brands all tasted the same—Watson learned a lot more after joining Thompson than he had in the academy. "I saw I would have to more or less junk my psychological training," he wrote later. In the trenches of the advertising business, he discovered, the "consumption engineer" depended not on erudition or theoretical acuity but on a certain visceral sense of what might work to sell the masses on this wristwatch or that coffee or whatever else the advertiser wanted to unload. "It is all a matter of 'instinctive' judgment," Watson told a colleague.12

Such practical psychology was commonplace throughout the advertising industry between the two world wars.13 Thus there was some rudimentary science underlying that great food of what Americans perceived as simple "bunk." Back then, some observers noted the predominance of that yahoo psychology (which had a later champion in Frederic Wakeman's Evan Evans). "The technicians in charge of this work," Thorstein Veblen wrote in 1923, referring to the rising craft of "sales-publicity," "are by way of being experts and experimenters in applied psychology, with a workmanlike bent in the direction of what may be called creative psychiatry."14 A decade later, in his cogent Marxist overview Our Master's Voice: Advertising, ex-ad man James Rorty echoed Veblen's point, out of his own professional experience, confirming that "the advertising man is, in fact, a journeyman psychologist." Unlike the later advocates of Motivation Research, who came to prominence after World War II, those early "journeymen" were not sophisticated analysts. "If there is a 'science' of advertising," Rorty wrote, its "formula is very simple. It is this: 'Figure out what they want, promise 'em everything, and blow hard."15 And yet the fact that such psychology was primitive does not detract from its historical importance; for Motivation Research, although infinitely more ingenious and far better educated than the studies of those earlier "technicians," was still but a continuation, and elaboration, of that rough and ready "science.

If Packard had treated M.R. not as an isolated trend, but as the latest chapter in the history of "consumption engineering," The Hidden Persuaders would have been a very different book: more magisterial and, in its implications, far more radical than this amusing and uncertain exposé. On the other hand, so broad and (necessarily) dense a volume, although it may have made a bigger academic splash, certainly would not have moved as broad a readership as Packard reached in his heyday; and so a more sophisticated study would have been less radical, potentially, in its effects, since many fewer people would have even heard of it, much less read it. Although naive by current academic standards, The Hidden Persuaders packed a startling punch not just in 1957 but for many years—as we can see from the intense reaction of the advertising industry, whose champions trashed the book obsessively for decades. Even after advertising had drawn many other public critics, this book was still the ad men's primary target, as if it were itself the source of every subsequent critique.

The barbs began to fly as soon as Packard's book became the nation's top best-seller in non-fiction. (The ad men organized an even more ferocious drive against The Waste Makers three years later.) Advertising Age and Printer's Ink, predictably, began the lashing with attacks by Walter Weir of Donahue & Coe, who called The Hidden Persuaders "malicious," accusing Packard of portraying M.R.'s practitioners as "a cross between Harold Lloyd and the witch in Walt Disney's Snow White," and dismissing the book itself as "propaganda that walks around hidden in the trusted and respected apparel of a book."16 Most of the industrial attacks were thus abusive and defensive, charging repetitiously that Packard's whole book was a pack of lies (a claim that might have been a bit less ludicrous if advertising men weren't making it).

Packard's name resounds contemptuously throughout the writings of the industry's foremost apologists. In The Huckster's Revenge: The Truth About Life on Madison Avenue (1959), Fred Manchee of B.B.D.O.—the agency that sold "Ike" Eisenhower—ridicules the notion of "a deep, dark plot to hoodwink you into buying things against your wil,"17 and otherwise jeers Packard every chance he gets (and takes some shots at Frederic Wakeman, too, as well as others critical of advertising). In With All Its Faults: A Candid Account of Forty Years in Advertising (1969), Foote, Cone & Belding's Fairfax Cone—author of "With men who know tobacco best . . . it's Luckies two to one!"—pooh-poohs The Hidden Persuaders as "a lively story" put across with dubious pizzazz by "the rip-roaring Mr. Vance Packard."18 Most scornful of them all, perhaps, is The Trouble with Advertising: A View from the Inside (1985), by Foote, Cone & Belding's John O'Toole, who would later serve as president and chief executive of the American Association of Advertising Agencies. "Much has been written about the insidious powers of advertising. There are none," O'Toole declares.

Paranoids like Vance Packard, author of The Hidden Persuaders, have made fortunes peddling the fiction that advertising is some arcane force that causes you to act against your will. It's hogwash.19

Some reactions were less bilious, as certain cooler heads atop the advertising world came out with subtler apologias, meant not to smear Vance Packard but to counter his dystopian view with a more benign impression of their trade. These books are classics of their kind. Rosser Reeves's Reality in Advertising (1962) is a snappy, jolly and incisive little manual on the art, or craft, of grinding out effective ads: a process that requires a thorough understanding of the Unique Selling Proposition (USP)—and not a lot of stealthy probing into anyone's unconscious. "The waste maker, the builder of status symbols, the clever manipulator of the masses," etc., is a myth cooked up by "the new economists [e.g., John Kenneth Galbraith] and such writers as Vance Packard." "There are no hidden persuaders," he declares in no uncertain terms, in a chapter called "The Freudian Hoax."20 Much impressed by Reeves' book, David Ogilvy sat down to write Confessions of an Advertising Man (1963), which tacitly refutes the sinister idea of "hidden persuaders" by offering, as Reeves does, a lot of lessons based entirely on long practical experience and common sense; and also by foregrounding Ogilvy himself as a breezy faux-aristocratic character and conscientious semi-artist, for whom copywriting is a literary process. Such a classy guy would never waste his time with any fancy Freudian ideas—nor, it is implied, would any of his colleagues.21

Here was a powerful (if oblique) one-two punch, for Ogilvy and Reeves personified two very different schools of national advertising. The undisputed master of the hard sell, Rosser Reeves was famous for devising pithy slogans that were literally unforgettable, and evidently irresistible, as they sold millions upon millions of M&M's, Rolaids, Viceroys, Certs, loaves of Wonder Bread and tubes of Colgate Toothpaste, among other items that Americans "used up." On the other hand, David Ogilvy excelled at subtler, more ironic pitches, for goods that were, or that were meant to seem, luxurious, including Rolls-Royce, Hathaway shirts, Schweppes, Dove Beauty Bar and Puerto Rico. That those two titans both attempted to discredit Packard's take on advertising surely comforted their colleagues all throughout the industry; and yet such unanimity, and the long duration (and intellectual weakness) of the anti-Packard propaganda, also indicate that his book was attacked so loudly not for its defects, but because it hit too close to home.

## IV.

There is much painful truth throughout The Hidden Persuaders, which, for all its faults, remains essential fare for those who want to grasp the evolution, and follow the trajectory, of this culture. The book is frightening in its prescience. Studying the national bazaar, and some of its chief planners, half a century ago, Packard saw the start of trends and practices that have by now wrought terrible destruction on our lives, our minds, our politics, our culture and our planet. In 1957, Packard was already rightly shocked by Richard Nixon's smooth appropriation of advertising tactics ("the techniques of generating the appearance of public demand and the technique of winning people's hearts with carefully simulated candor"); by "the psycho-seduction of children" (e.g. pressuring little girls to get home permanents, or Miss Frances brightly pushing pills on "Ding Dong School" one day in 1955); by the use of marketing research to fill churches and sharpen sermons (a trend notable in 1954); and by much more, including canned laughter, planned communities, total advertising, and other bad ideas that have by now advanced far, far beyond the author's worst nightmares.

Indeed, today's news often reads like a horrific palimpsest of bulletins on the breakdown of the great "consumer culture" that the advertisers made for us, or forced on us. Once upon a time a viable republic, and the world's greatest agricultural and industrial producer, this nation has become a nervous continental rabble of consumers and would-be consumers. As most productive work has been transferred abroad, we are now routinely tantalized with images of goods devised in other, poorer countries; and, as there's no longer any civic space for us, our very ballots having been erased, we are now endlessly distracted, or (barely) placated, by a daily spectacle of "politics," in which We the People figure not at all.

We put up with this because we have been educated not to rule ourselves as citizens, but only to expect, or hope, to make enough to buy a Mac and shop on-line; and the media continues such instruction, as our "free press" is not dedicated to the preservation of our freedoms (as the First Amendment tacitly demands) but, with very few exceptions, grimly services its own prodigious parent companies and other major advertisers. Thus "the liberal media" today is little other than a vehicle for corporate propaganda, and just when we most badly need the sort of press the Framers had in mind. Such a press might long ago have warned us that a nation built for shopping cannot possibly endure as a democracy—or, for that matter, last at all, as it must necessarily bring on those catastrophic ills that are at last converging on us. For our toxic air and water, our tasteless crops and doubtful meats, our ever-rising cancer rates, the "obesity epidemic" striking down our children, the poisoned imports sent to us from China and, of course, for global warming, we can now belatedly thank Earnest Elmo Calkins, Rosser Reeves, David Ogilvy and all the other geniuses of advertising, and, especially, the corporations that retained them.

Now that we have come so far, we are, paradoxically, less likely than Vance Packard's readers were to see that there's a fundamental problem here—a failure which, in fact, is yet another modern (or post-modern) adaptation that the author here foretold: "Eventually—say by A.D. 2000—perhaps all this depth manipulation of the psychological variety will seem amusingly old-fashioned." If we are now inured to such manipulation, we should, perhaps, return to Packard not for easy laughs, but for a clear reminder of what's actually been happening from his time (actually from long before his time) up through today. "Motivation research" is, per se, beside the point. The aim of all the powerful interests at issue here, in both this introduction and this book, is not, finally, to "persuade" us, any more than it's to free us, or to make us happy, or empower us. For their intent is not to grapple with your will, as any true "persuader" must, but to replace it with their own imperatives—and that is simply wrong, as Packard notes in his last lines:

The most serious offense many of the depth manipulators commit, it seems to me, is that they try to invade the privacy of our minds. It is this right to privacy in our minds—privacy to be either rational or irrational—that I believe we must strive to protect.

If we see that modest statement as "amusingly old-fashioned," the war against ourselves is over, and we've lost. If, on the other hand, we take his point, the war continues, and we have some chance.
