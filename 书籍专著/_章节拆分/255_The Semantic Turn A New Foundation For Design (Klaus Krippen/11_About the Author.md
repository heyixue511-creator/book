# About the Author

Klaus Krippendorff (Ph.D., University of Illinois, Urbana, 1967) is Professor of Communication and Gregory Bateson Term Professor for Cybernetics, Language, and Culture at the University of Pennsylvania’s Annenberg School for Communication. He has published in journals of communication, sociological methodology, cybernetics, and system theory. He authored Information Theory, Structural Models for Qualitative Data, a Dictionary of Cybernetics, edited Communication and Control in Society, and coedited The Analysis of Communication Content, Developments in Scientific Theories and Computer Techniques. His Content Analysis, an Introduction to Its Methodology, has been translated into four languages and most recently appeared in a second expanded edition. He is a member of the editorial boards of several academic journals and an elected fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science (AAAS), the International Communication Association (ICA), Netherlands Institute for Advanced Studies (NIAS), and the Society for Science of Design Studies in Japan.

Klaus is also an award-winning designer (a graduate of the Ulm School of Design, 1961). Occasionally teaching design and consulting with industry, he has coedited journal issues on product semantics for Innovation and Design Issues, edited Design in the Age of Information, a Report to the National Science Foundation (NSF), and led international workshops on design semantics. His contributions have been published in various books and journals on design and related fields. Here, he is advocating a human-centered approach to design, moving meaning and interactivity into the center of design considerations.

Klaus’ current interest is fourfold: With epistemology in mind, he inquires into the role of language and discourse in the social construction of realities. As a critical scholar, he explores the conditions of entrapment and emancipation. As a second-order cybernetician, he works with recursive constructions of self and others; and as a designer, he attempts a new synthesis of technology and human concerns, proposing a redesign of design.
