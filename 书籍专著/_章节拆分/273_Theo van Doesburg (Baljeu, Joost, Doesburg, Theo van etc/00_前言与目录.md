Theo Van Doesburg is one of the seminal figures in modern art. Both artist and theoretician, his immense energy and fierce idealism encompass an entire range of artistic activity: painting, architecture, writing, philosophy, typography and design. It was, however, as co-founder and editor of the magazine De Stijl that he stamped his ideas and personality most forcibly upon the art world between 1917 and his premature death in 1931.

This study contains a selection from Van Doesburg's key writings, reproduced for the first time in this book. It is prefaced by an analysis of his life and work, with particular emphasis on his relationships with other artists of his day and their effect upon his work. The author shows how Van Doesburg, as editor of De Stijl, worked tirelessly to promote the cause and define the purpose of art - building his theories on his own unique grasp of the problems and possibilities facing the artists of his time.

The Dada movement of Theo Van Doesburg's time helped to promote a spirit of experimentation and a general artistic revolt against conventional bourgeois taste. Along with other avant-garde artists Van Doesburg rejected traditional forms and advanced new ones in an atmosphere of fierce debate. He often found himself out of sympathy with his contemporaries, even Mondrian, his collaborator on De Stijl, and was openly in dispute with the Bauhaus and Le Corbusier over the future of architecture.

However, while involved in controversy, Van Doesburg was also a great synthesizer, sympathetic to - and influenced by - many artists, including the Futurists, the Russian Constructivists, Lissitzky, Kandinsky, and Van der Leck. Significant in themselves, Van Doesburg's ideas take on a greater meaning when viewed in conjunction with his painting and architectural projects. He never allowed his theories of a universal, profound form of expression to become divorced from his art, and his canvases have a pure and serene clarity which reflects this principle.

This is an essential monograph for all serious students of modern art: scholarly, well documented, and beautifully illustrated with examples of Van Doesburg's paintings and designs.

Joost Baljeu is himself a practising artist and architect, whose constructions and architectural projects have been widely exhibited in Europe and America. He teaches art history in his native Holland and lectures frequently there and abroad. He edited and founded the constructivist magazine Structure. This is his third book.
