Two recent typefaces in the Mannerist tradition. Poetica (above) is a chancery italic based on sixteenth-century models. It was designed by Robert Slimbach and issued by Adobe in 1992. Galliard (below), designed by Matthew Carter, was issued by Linotype in 1978. It is based on letterforms cut in the sixteenth century by Robert Granjon.

Mannerist art is Renaissance art to which subtle exaggerations of length, angularity or tension, for example  have been added. Mannerist typographers, working chiefly in Italy and France early in the sixteenth century, began the practice of using roman and italic in the same book, and even on the same page  though rarely on the same line. It was also during the Mannerist period that sloped roman capitals were first added to the italic lower case.

There are many fine sixteenth-century examples of Mannerist typefaces, including roman titling fonts with long, delicate extenders, chancery italics with even longer and often ornamented extenders, and text faces with short extenders but increased tension in the forms. Digital interpretations of a number of these faces have recently been made. Two significant examples  one ornate and one restrained  are shown overleaf.
