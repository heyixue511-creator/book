## Photographic Constructivism

In painting and in graphics, Rodchenko was a minimalist. He made compositions from points and lines or flat surfaces, presenting them as elements of visual thinking, refined to the simplicity and universality of a mathematical formula. This kind of minimalism may be found in his photographs, too. Rather than betray a lack of feeling, or poor handling of modern techniques, the limitations imposed on the means and forms produce the very effect that Rodchenko was looking for. It was less his concern to show us a building, rather to demonstrate the principle of foreshortened perspective. It was not part of an automobile as such which interested him, rather the idea of its structure and technical organization; not so much the high dive as to fix an image of man air-borne for an instant. His concentrated will and creative consciousness compel us to look again at an ordinary house or a pioneer with a bugle. In each and every photo that Rodchenko took we can feel the energy in the visual expression.

In the Twenties, artists and theorists of >production art< considered Constructivism not merely as a style but as the artist's working method for the sake of production in any medium, whether it be graphics, textiles, wood or metalwork. Theatre was also thought of as a form of production; Constructivist architects would design their projects in view of functional and social need. The impact of Constructivism extended to music and literature, so it is unsurprising that photographers should have also been attracted by its geometric and structural qualities and by its minimalism of means.
