Henry Cole, after he had become a fgure of authority in the art word, would quite often be attacked as an untrained ignoramus in artistic matters. Indeed, even one of the most zealous defenders of his posthumous reputation, Sir Henry Trueman Wood, said that 'he had no aesthetic judgment and no artistic power'' Did Cole really know nothing about art? How did he become involved in it?

At least he began with some capacity for the co-ordination of hand and eye, as is witnessed by his calligraphic abilities at school. We have seen, too, that he took drawing lessons from David Cox and Charles Wild in the 182os, and was encouraged by Elizabeth Palgrave. We shall see that in due course he took up etching. He believed that his creative capacities included an artistic element, 'some instinct which, although not sufficiently cultivated to make me an artist, I have always felt I possessed'.2

As to his capacity to appreciate art, we know that from early adulthood he visited art galleries.³ In the winter of 1827-8 he attended lectures on anatomy, perspective, painting and sculpture at the Royal Academy, and would have continued with this course the following year, but found that the lecturers just repeated themselves annually.4 By this time he had evidently developed architectural and antiquarian interests, because he tells us that the Dean of Westminster gave him permission to draw in the abbey 'as early as 1827'.5 There is also some evidence of artistic sensibility in his early travel journals. He made a tour in Scotland in 1833, sending back to his fiancée, Marian, conscientious descriptions of the terrain, assessing it as it presented itself to 'the discerning eye of the Artist', and dropping in references to Richard Payne Knight, high priest of the cult of the picturesque. Townscape as well as landscape attracted his criticism, as when in Edinburgh he asserted that 'throughout the New Town . . . instances of bad taste are perpetually forcing themselves on the attention'.6 The following year he and Marian, now married, travelled to Cornwall. 'In respect of the Picturesqueness of the County . . ', commented Cole, 'I should not give it a very strong character . .. . The grandeur or quaintness or boldness of every scene pleases but they do not furnish sufficiency for the pencil to work upon.' Nonetheless, he and Marian did make a number of sketches.7 In Yorkshire in 1835, Cole was assiduous in architecture-spotting, assigning columns and tracery to their correct periods. He also continued to lay down the law on taste. Of Beverley Minster he said: 'This relique of the catholic Church is preserved in good repair . . . and excepting the abominable whitewash, which has been daubed throughout the interior, the reparations and attentions of modern times are not altogether in bad taste.' After admiring a little medieval church at Hemingborough, he disparaged modern churches: 'They are erected upon a lathe & Plaster sentiment of hypocrisy - wanting firmness, consistency & feeling - and the physical materials are of corresponding flimsiness and rottenness.'\*8 From his journals, then, one can see that Cole had some knowledge of the history of art and architecture, and cultivated aesthetic responses, albeit at a fairly commonplace level.

As a tyro journalist, Cole wrote as a critic of music and drama, but not, it seems, of art, until early in 1838 he embarked on a substantial article for the London and Westminster Review on contemporary wood engraving - a well-researched survey that is still informative to present-day scholars. Wood engraving, a more subtly expressive technique than traditional woodcut because it used finer tools on harder wood, was pioneered by Thomas Bewick $\left( \mathbb { I } 7 5 3 \mathrm { - } \mathbb { I } 8 2 8 \right)$ While Bewick worked for most of his life in his native Northumberland, the new technique of wood engraving necessarily moved to London, the centre of publishing. Here Robert Branston $\left( \mathrm { I } 7 7 8 \mathrm { - } \mathrm { I } 8 2 7 \right)$ stood at the head of a 'London school' of wood engravers. It was his disciples whom Cole studied, still quite a small group of craftsmen (and some women). In May 1838 and through the summer, Cole went to see engravers such as John Jackson, Orrin Smith and John Thompson $\left( \mathrm { I } 7 8 \varsigma \mathrm { - } \mathrm { I } 8 6 6 \right)$ , the final name being generally regarded as the leading exponent of wood engraving in the 1830s.

By the autumn, Cole had become firm friends with Thompson, and with his family. Thompson was 53 in 1838, and had two sons somewhat younger than Cole: Charles Thurston, born in 1816 and Richard Anthony, born in 1819. Thurston was to marry Marian Cole's sister Charlotte in 1857, and, moving over from wood engraving, in which he had been trained, into photography, joined Cole's South Kensington Museum as its principal photographer. Richard also joined the museum when it opened in South Kensington in 1857, and ended his working life there as an assistant director. John Thompson lived in Kensington, partly because it was convenient for Chiswick, where the printer with whom he mostly dealt, Charles Whittingham, had his press. Whittingham also was soon to become one of Cole's important collaborators. As the Coles lived in Kensington at various addresses, the Thompsons were near enough to be neighbours, and the families constantly visited each other.

Callcott introduced Cole to his wife Maria, who became an important influence during the short time Cole knew her before her death. Quite suddenly, he found himself part of an artistic peer group, and a group that looked to Prince Albert as its leader.

Cole's first formal engagement with art was in a literary mode: he wrote guidebooks to historic and artistic sights in or near London. The first was to Hampton Court, published in 1841 (fig.4). The Queen had allowed the public to visit Hampton Court since 18 3 8 and Cole already knew it well, having studied the building during holidays spent nearby at Shepperton and Weybridge. He decided that the pictures on display there had already been well described by other authors, so he concentrated on the building, and the restorations being done at that time by Thomas Willement, who was the best restoration architect of the day, famous as an expert on heraldry, and the first to design stained-glass windows in a believable medieval style. Cole became friendly with him, and was invited to Willement's house in London where he admired his collection of medieval artefacts, or, as Cole was still heedless enough to call them, 'knick knackeries'. Later on he visited Davington Priory, Willement's house in Kent, soon after he bought it.19 Although the two men did not become close friends, acquaintance with Willement provided Cole not only with a link to architectural history but also, as will be seen, with useful contacts in other directions.

Cole's Handbook was declared to be 'By Felix Summerly Esq. With embellishments engraved on wood by ladies'. The nom de plume was adopted because convention precluded a public servant from publishing under his own name. No one knows why he chose 'Felix Summerly', but it suggests happiness in the summertime and is typical of Cole and his ebullient and cheerful personality. The 'ladies' who made the illustrations were not just females, but ladies of rank: Lady Callcott and Lady Palgrave. Among Cole's guidebooks was a slim pamphlet on the Temple Church (fig.5), where restorations by Thomas Willement had just been completed. For the floors of the church, Willement had bought tiles from Herbert Minton, the most innovative and successful china manufacturer in the world, based at Stoke-on-Trent. These tiles, a re-creation of a medieval art form, 'were made after the models of the ancient and most interesting pavement in the Chapter House of Westminster Abbey'.25 Willement introduced Cole to Minton (plate 2 1), and their collaboration and friendship persisted throughout Cole's career. Their first joint project was to bring out an educational toy, consisting of small coloured tiles, made by Minton, using a process recently invented by Richard Prosser for which Minton owned half the patent.26 Cole called it the 'Tesselated Pastime', and he wrote a booklet to go with it, in which a juvenile Felix Summerly instructs two little girls in the 'principles', as he significantly calls them, of flat pattern making. The toy came out in time for Christmas 1843. Lord Langdale told Cole that his daughter, who was about six years old, "was delighted' with the toy and the booklet, 'which he had to read 6 times a day'.27 This pleasingly responsive girl was later to marry the Count Teleki, and to be a benefactress of the South Kensington Museum.

The guidebook to Hampton Court appeared in a second and expanded edition in 1843. Here Cole upgraded the historical information, making the publication more scholarly. It received a somewhat critical review in the Athenaeum, which deprecated the increased amount of 'antiquarian information', preferring the earlier, chattier tone. 'Popular Guide Books should be brief. We do not object to Felix Summerly's gossiping and suggestive style; indeed we think it a great merit; it helps people to a pleasant idea, and gives its humanizing moral to inanimate things.'28 For once in his career as an art publicist, Cole was criticised for being academic rather than populist. In a publicity flier for a later handbook, he claimed that what he wanted to provide for his readers was 'the guidance of a good natured and intelligent friend'. The friend would be willing to match his reader's pace, loitering or hurrying as occasion demanded, but he would also be authoritative, supplying 'judicious guidance' that would help the reader to a 'correct' view.29

Most children's books of the time, claimed Elizabeth Rigby - a periodical essayist of some weight, who was to marry Sir Charles Eastlake - contained 'an insipid tale [that] goes feebly wriggling through an unmerciful load of moral, religious, and scientific preaching'. Such books, she said regretfully, 'have been the most abundant and characteristic product of the present day, not intended, we are assured, for school use, but, we speak equally from experience and observation, with no charm for any other'. 37 Cole's friend Thackeray was equally severe on the books that he had had as a child:

Abominable attempts were made in those days to make useful books for children, and cram science down their throats as calomel used to be administered under the pretence of a spoonful of currant-jelly. Such picture-books as we had were illustrated with the most shameful, hideous, old wood-cuts which had lasted through a century . . . Some painted pictures there were in our time likewise, but almost all of the very worst kind; the hideous distortions of Rowlandson, who peopled the picture-books with bloated parsons in periwigs, tipsy aldermen and leering salacious nymphs, horrid to look at.38

Cole set out to produce books that, in both text and illustrations, would appeal to the imagination of children. He wanted both to amuse his own children and to reform children's literature.

His publishers, up to 1843, had been Charles Knight, George Bell and Wood, Hugh Cunningham and, for pamphlets, Henry Hooper. Of these, Bell and Wood, who were publishing the guidebooks, seemed the most likely to be interested in a series of children's books. But when he approached George Bell he found no support, because Bell's partner, Wood, had absconded, leaving him short of working capital. Bell did, however, continue to publish the guidebooks. In March 1843, Cole had the good fortune to meet a new publisher: the 25-year-old, red-haired Joseph Cundall,39 who had just taken over a shop in New Bond Street, known as The Juvenile Library. He had already written some children's books and published others. With him Cole embarked on a long series of children's books that he called 'The Home Treasury'.40

Cole paid great attention to the appearance of his books, both outside and inside. They were small for small hands to handle. The bindings were decorative in themselves, with designs related to the contents of the book.He took the trouble to go to the British Museum to look at covers and bindings that he could use as models. Though he wrote some of the stories himself, he roped in others, and Marian, Charles Cole and Harriett Mill all wrote for him. Marian, in the thin disguise of 'Mrs Felix Summerly', was acknowledged as the author of The Mother's Primer (plate 17). Charles Whittingham the Younger of Chiswick, one of the best printers in the country and a friend of John Thompson, printed the books. John Mill encouraged Cole by saying that 'Felix Summerly would be as notorious as the Brothers Grimm'.41

The old tales retold in 'The Home Treasury' included Little Red Riding Hood, Beauty and the Beast, Jack and the Bean Stalk, Cinderella, Jack the Giant Killer, Sleeping Beauty and Dick Whittington, plus the less well known Sir Hornbook, Reynard the Fox, and Chevy Chase. There was also a volume of nursery rhymes. The longest text was Cole's version of Berthold Niebuhr's Heroic Tales of Ancient Greece.

Cole announced that all 'The Home Treasury' books 'will be illustrated, but not after the usual fashion of children's books, in which it seems to be assumed that the lowest kind of art is good enough to give first impressions to a child. In the present series, though the statement may perhaps excite a smile, the illustrations will be selected from the works of Raffaelle, Titian, Hans Holbein, and other old masters. Some of the best modern artists have kindly promised their aid in creating a taste for beauty in little children'.42 Cole's triumph was to persuade the fashionable painters who were now his friends to do the illustrations. So Mulready, Redgrave, Horsley, Webster, Townsend, Linnell and his sons, and Cope all worked for him, together with Frederick Tayler (1802-89), who specialised in hunting scenes, and Henry Corbould (1787-1844). The pictures were lithographed and hand-coloured. Cole himself coloured the illustrations in the set of these books that he sent to the Queen for her children, not trusting anyone else. The series also included several books whose illustrations were drawn from the work of the Old Masters. Holbein's Bible Events, Raffaelle's Bible Events and Albert Durer's Bible Events had illustrations based on work by these three artists. An Alphabet of Quadrupeds included illustrations after Paul Potter, Teniers and Rembrandt, among others. 'Pictures by the Old Masters' illustrated A Century of Fables. In The Little Painter's Portfolio were to be found illustrations after Giotto, 'S. Del Piombo' and Holbein.43

Cole's series of books was an immediate success. Already in November 1843 a reviewer in the Athenaeum could greet the latest volumes with enthusiasm:

Another bouquet of flowers, immortals' as the florists would call them for they are not subject to change, and will be fresh and fragrant at Christmas, or on New Year's Day, or at Easter, or at any other and all other holiday seasons - for which our young friends are indebted to Felix Summerly. We earnestly hope that these little volumes will be welcomed in every family: the paths of learning may thus be made paths of pleasure, and children's libraries become lustrous with beauty, pleasurable as well as profitable. The illustrations are greatly superior to what is usually met with, even in the best works of the class.44

It would be interesting to know who wrote this cordial review. Since Cole himself was writing regularly for the journal at the time, it is quite possible that he was the reviewer of his own work. However, there were other reviews, no less ardent. Thornton Leigh Hunt, for example, hailed the books as reaching a new level of good taste.

This is a novelty in infant literature; a novelty as important as it is daring. It is due to Felix Summerly, a name synonymous with taste. All the 'common sense' of the kingdom will be ready to declare that art is thrown away on children 'who cannot understand it'. Does 'common sense' itself understand it? No. Why not? Because it has not taste. Because in infancy its taste was never cultivated; because children's books were filled with glaring daubs of red and green, which were considered 'good enough' ... In bestowing our hearty approbation . . . let us not forget to thank the eminent artists for their good nature in thus lending their great talents to a task hitherto performed by the humblest draughtsman. Think of Mulready, Cope, Horsley, F. Tayler, Webster, Redgrave, and the rest illustrating children's books! In a country like England where 'appearances' are so highly prized, we can understand the sneers of ambitious mediocrity when hearing of such 'degradation'. But the above artists are superior to any fantastic pride. They thought  and justly  that their talents were not ignobly employed, in endeavouring to introduce a new element in early education.

In 'The Home Treasury' we see Cole at his most engaging, directing his overflowing vitality into a project that was both creatively satisfying to himself and sympathetic to the developing pleasures of his children.

Having embarked on publishing reproductions of classic art in his children's books, Cole was not slow to seize an opportunity to do it for adults also. On 3 November 1843, through the good offices of John Linnell, he met Henry Josi, keeper of prints and drawings at the British Museum, to look at the original woodblocks of Dürer's Small Passion. Josi had bought 35 (out of 37) of these for the museum in 1839. Josi 'said he thought I might have casts if I chose to apply'. Stereotypes were indeed made for Cole, from which he printed an edition of Dürer's designs. For The Passion of Our Lord Jesus Christ, pourtrayed by Albert Durer Cole wrote a 16-page preface, a serious excursion into art history, with a characteristic aside on the virtues of the (relatively) new technology of stereotyping.46 He published the book not under the name 'Felix Summerly' but under his own name - 'Henry Cole, An Assistant Keeper of the Public Records' - because, having laboured long over it, he regarded it as a work of scholarship. The book had an impressive imprint, being issued in 1844 by a consortium of superior publishers: Cundall, Pickering and Bell in London, with Parker of Oxford and Deighton of Cambridge.

Another publication for adults that Cole arranged was So much of the Diary of Lady Willoughby as relates to her Domestic History, and to the Eventful Period of the Reign of Charles the First. This, while purporting to be a seventeenth-century diary, was in fact a new work by Hannah Rathbone, a member of the well-known Liverpool Quaker family. Richard Rathbone, her husband, asked Cole to arrange its publication, anonymously, by Longmans. Whittingham designed a special pseudoseventeenth-century typeface for it, and the book appeared in an archaic-style binding in the autumn of 1844, though it could also be bought bound more cheaply in half leather or paper. It caused a great stir and several authors, including John Murray, were thought to be the inventors of it. As a critic, even in these early days, he seldom failed in confidence, as may be seen in three papers on house decoration which he wrote for the Athenaeum in 1843. Was he again drawing on personal experience, this time the experience of setting up his home with Marian in Notting Hill Square? At any rate, an interest in colour and the theory of colour was very much in the air. David Hay of Edinburgh, the principal interior decorator of the day, had written the standard work in the late 182os. Titled The Laws of Harmonious Colouring adapted to House Painting, it developed a system by which colours were analogous with harmony in music. The book ran into many editions and was still selling well in the 1 84os. Cole read it before he reviewed Charles Eastlake's translation of Goethe's Theory of Colours for the London and Westminster Review in 184o. It was still in his mind when he expounded his own theories in the Athenaeum articles. He pointed out that fashions in decoration change every seven years or . 'Sometimes the rage isfor warm shade of colouring, at othe for cold.' But it might be possible to find styles of decoration that would remain valid for ever.

It appears to us, that certain principles of decoration may be laid down, which, if recognized and applied, would make our dwellings much more cheerful and comfortable . . . not only without any additional cost, but would make the keep of them more economical, by rendering them, to a great degree, independent of the caprices of fashion. It is the absence of correct principles which causes decoration and furniture to be out of fashion  tiresome — palling to the eye, and subject to constant change,  whereas, what is really beautiful, being based on everlasting principles, is subject to no change.53

When Cole here again advocates 'principles' of design, we begin to recognise his emergence as a design reformer. As we have seen from his holiday journals, he had all the bumptiousness of youth in telling Marian what was in bad taste. Now, in the Athenaeum articles, he seemed ready to tell the world. His diary for I 1 January 1842 notes that he read Pugin's True principles of pointed or Christian architecture, published the previous year. He makes no comment on it, but in view of his ready deployment of the concept of 'true principlesin later debates about taste, it is tempting to believe that he was fired by the urgent, incisive prescriptions and interdicts of Pugin.

Anyone in the London art world in the 184os must have known about the project to decorate the new Houses of Parliament with frescoes.54 Cole moved in a circle of artists who were deeply involved in this project, so he naturally paid it some attention. Prompted by the first report of the Fine Arts Commission (which, under Prince Albert's chairmanship, was in charge of commissioning the frescoes), he persuaded William Hickson on 11 May 1842 to accept an article in the Westminster Review. This reveals him in the role of politician rather than art critic. After an initial review of the state of the arts in England, he took issue with the often expressed view that public money spent on artistic works would be wasted. This gave him the opportunity, since he was writing in the Westminster, to make a statement, from a utilitarian standpoint, in support of state subsidy of the arts.

It is the fashion to ascribe motives of economy, in respect of the arts, to the Radical party especially. We do not think that the Radical party are more enlightened on the subjet than other political parties, but it is only justice to them to say that they have generally regarded a grant for the encouragement of the fine arts as an outlay to promote public education, and have been more liberally disposed than the Whigs and not less so than the Tories, who assent to such grants for the good of the arts themselves, rather than for any moral effect they may have . . .55

It is not known where (or if) his article appeared, but there was a detailed, two-part review of the exhibition in the Athenaeum on 27 April (pp.3878) and 4 May (pp.409-10). This is almost a prescription for Cole's future life.

In the early 1 84os, the rediscovery, or re-invention, of the Old English Christmas by the Victorians was just gaining momentum, and it was typical of Cole to sense this. Through the 1 84os the Illustrated London News and Punch would do much to popularise Christmas imagery and sentiments, but as yet they had done little. Washington Irving had laid the foundation in 182o when in his Sketch Book he described Christmas at Bracebridge Hall. In ${ \bf I } 8 3 6$ Charles Dickens, with the help of his illustrator 'Phiz', presented Christmas at Dingley Dell in Pickwick Papers. The year 183 5 saw the appearance of the illustrated Book of Christmas by the journalist T. K. Hervey (a sub-editor on the Athenaeum), while the children's book Peter Parley's Tales about Christmas appeared in 1838 with illustrations by the Cruikshanks. Daniel Maclise's celebrated painting Merry Christmas in the Baron's Hall was shown at the Royal Academy in 1838, and in the same year R. W. Christmas was obviously in the air, and in $1 8 4 3$ not only did Cole invent the Christmas card, but also Dickens published A Christmas Carol, the first of his 'Christmas Books', which did more than anything to put emotion into the Victorian Christmas. So far as one can see, Cole and Dickens could not have known what each was planning, unless they told each other. But they were hardly acquainted at this point, though they had both been present at a meeting on copyright on 17 May 1843. It seems likely that the Spirit of Christmas inspired them both independently.

Cole viewed this second Society of Arts exhibition (which was more successful than the first) as a turning point. The acclaim bestowed on his tea set (and Minton's jugs) 'strengthened my conviction that an alliance between fine art and manufactures would promote public taste, and conduce to the interest of all concerned in the production of art manufactures.' Consequently he set up 'an organization of artists, manufacturers, and designers', and, resurrecting Felix Summerly, called it 'Summerly's Art-Manufactures'.68

This new project, as often with Cole's ventures, was not an entirely unprecedented idea. Indeed, in the little manifesto (fig.6) with which he announced his scheme, and which was reprinted in subsequent catalogues, he made a point of emphasising that his purpose was to 'revive the good old practice of connecting the best art with familiar objects in daily use'. In recent times, the 'good old practice' had fallen into disuse and it was very rare for fine artists to design anything to be manufactured, though the sculptor, John Flaxman, had designed for Wedgwood, and for Rundell, Bridge and Rundell, the royal silversmiths.69 None of Flaxman's designs, however, could be said to be for daily use, and Cole wanted to bring art down to earth again.

A precedent might have been found in the Art-Union Society, which had been started in 1837. This operated a kind of genteel lottery (overt lotteries being illegal), whereby subscribers might win an annual prize of an oil painting by a well-known artist, or a lesser prize of a reduction of a sculpture, or at least an engraving. In 1846 the Art-Union journal could claim that art-unions 'extend and encourage a love and appreciation of Art among the people; they actually supply annually little less than 80,ooo persons with a work of Art; and they afford profitable occupation to a large number of artists' The aims of the union were very near Cole's own, for, like him, it wanted to improve public taste and 'spread to all classes of the community that great and important feeling, a love of the fine arts'.1

Whether or not Cole took inspiration from the art-unions, his little business had a considerably wider range, for 'the Art-Manufactures will be of all kinds, and executed in pottery, wood, glass, and other materials'.74 By the spring of 1847 the business was in full swing.75 Cole made agreements with various manufacturers that he would choose the designs, while they would pay the designers. The final price would be agreed between him and the manufacturers. The latter paid all the expenses of making and transport, while Cole undertook the publicity and marketing and was paid a commission on each sale.76 The terms were tough and provoked disgruntled letters in the Art-Union journal.77 Cole later denied that he made any profit out of the business, claiming that he had incurred 'a good many hundred pounds outlay'.78 He began to issue catalogues, which were as elegant as the goods themselves. They were printed by Whittingham with headpieces and initial letters designed by Richard Redgrave. On the front page of the first one he described how medieval and Renaissance artists designed decorative articles. Invoking Raphael, Leonardo, Fra Angelico, Holbein, Dürer, Poussin and others, he declared: 'Beauty of form, and colour, and poetic invention were associated with everything. So it ought still to be, and we will say, shall be again.'

Cole went on arguing vigorously. In March 1848 he published his catalogue in a new format, as the Art-Manufactures Circular, arranging for it to count as a newspaper so as to travel post-free. It was now to feature not only his own products, but also 'brief notices of events connected with the subject of Art-Manufactures'.92 For instance, he advertised the Society of Arts annual exhibition of British manufactures and promoted a new idea broached by the society for a big national exhibition of manufactures. 'Our readers we are sure will have great pleasure in learning that this great object, in which France and Belgium, and even the small German towns, have been allowed to outstrip England, seems at last in a far way to be accomplished.'93 The attempted transformation of the catalogue into a periodical shows that Cole's itch to be a journalist was not yet assuaged, and that he wanted to reform taste by argument as well as by example.

The productions of Felix Summerly, first in two dimensions and then in three, must have given Cole great enjoyment, and an outlet for artistic creativity at a very busy period. During the 184os, in his fourth decade, he was full of energy and fertile in projects, but had not yet found a true vocation in life. Perhaps 'Art-Manufactures' offered an appropriate mission. On 30 January 1848, Mulready advised Cole 'to determine whether to go thoroughly into Art Man or to stand still'. He did choose to devote himself to 'Art Man' Not, however, as a small-scale entrepreneur: despite his admiration for businessmen, he did not choose to become one. By exercising his talents as a publicist, lobbyist and organiser, he contrived to become a pacesetter in the art and design politics of his time.
