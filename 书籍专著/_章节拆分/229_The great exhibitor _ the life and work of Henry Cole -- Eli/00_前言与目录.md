This is the first full length biography of Henry Cole, extraordinary larger-than-life 'eminent Victorian', visionary civil servant, and accomplished spin-doctor, whose pioneering ideas created the magnificent museum which is now the V&A.

By the end of his life Henry Cole was recognized as a national institution, 'Old King Cole', though he was also treated with caution as a 'playful monster'. He was a lifelong Civil Servant, in a period when Civil Servants could be buccaneering reformers rather than impassive mandarins. Described as 'a clever agitator, and a public spirited fellow', he waged many campaigns, moving from the Public Records to the Penny Post, to railway expansion, to patent law revision, to art and design reform.

With Prince Albert, he created the Great Exhibition of 185 1, and kept on organising international exhibitions for the rest of his life. He was in charge of a nationwide system of art and science education, and, again with Prince Albert, developed the South Kensington area as the intellectual quarter of London. The Victoria and Albert Museum, of which he was founding Director, remains as his best-known memorial, and is a symbol of his mission to educate through exhibiting.

This book gives a rounded picture of this extraordinary man. He is portrayed not only as exhibitor, museum director and educationalist, but as a galvanic force administering salutary shocks over wide areas of Victorian life: children's books, hackney cabs, public lavatories, choral singing, cookery, sewage disposal, to name only a few. In his own time he aroused admiration and antipathy in equal measure. For today's readers, his energy and creativity will mark him out as an inimitable Victorian phenomenon.

## INTRODUCTION The Man and the Achievements

Henry Cole  extraordinary civil servant and cultural entrepreneur - struck some people, who felt the rough edge of his personality, as a monster. Others thought him a buffoon. If you had met him in the street, you would have noticed that he was quite a small man. His head, however, was on a large scale  or at least his features were, for he had a large nose, a firm chin, and a lofty brow. His head looked the larger because he wore his curly hair in an untidy shock. In later life it became a flowing white mane, and white hair continued round his face in the form of side-burns and under-the-chin (mandarin-style) whiskers, so that he seemed to look at you out of a white halo. He usually wore glasses, but these are rarely recorded in portraits. At least from the 18 hehabitually dressed in  frock coat. Wth bagy rusers, fap coat-tails, and handkerchief trailing from his back pocket, he must often have looked dishevelled, but he always stood up straight, perhaps to make the most of his height.

We do not know what he sounded like. When one reads the pugnacious phrases of his speeches, one cannot imagine that they were pronounced in a languid drawl. Surely his voice must have been emphatic, even abrupt. If he had had a high tenor range or a window-rattling basso profundo, it is likely that someone would have mentioned it, so probably he had a normal baritone timbre. One notices that big men often swallow their voices, while small men often project theirs strongly; Cole mastered the art of public speaking, so no doubt he spoke clearly.

His predominant qualities of personality were energy, force, dynamism; and it seems likely that these would have expressed themselves physically in a brisk, vigorous demeanour. There is no evidence of a disjunction between his mental and physical attributes, quite the contrary. His hand obeyed his mind. His penmanship was, at the start, his fortune, and he always enjoyed drawing and, later, etching. We can imagine him as physically efficient, practical, bustling.

If, as a downright man with an appropriately downright name, he got results primarily by determination, it was inevitable that he would run into opposition and clashes of will. In ruling his own staff he was regarded by some as an 'autocrat',1 a 'dictator', a 'despot'.² But he was not the kind of boss who oppressed his subordinates while crawling to his superiors. He gave trouble to superiors too, and was regarded as an 'insolent proconsul', doing what he liked in his own territory, to the chagrin of civil servants in Whitehall.3 His most explosive disputes were with his subordinate Charles Robinson, curator of the art museum at South Kensington, and with his superior, the government minister Robert Lowe: if it was necessary to fight somebody, Cole did not care whether he was senior or junior, but struck out just the same. Undoubtedly Cole was a rough, tough man to work with. One of his admirers,

Sir Henry Trueman Wood, said that 'he liked his own way, and he generally got it, though his methods were not such as endeared him either to the superiors whose orders he evaded or to the subordinates whose submission he compelled'.4

But his sheer determination could sometimes overcome its own adverse effects. The statesman Lord Granville, one of his staunchest allies, recalled in 1873 that when he first met Cole, he 'did not like him at all', but he confessed that 'by very force of character, the gentleman who had impressed him so unfavourably won his firm and lasting regard'. Granville characterised Cole as 'a kind of Palmerston without the varnish'.

If Cole had been an altogether impossible colleague, he could never have achieved so much. It has to be acknowledged that he sustained many long-term friendships and professional relationships. Most of the people with whom he worked at the Society of Arts and at the Great Exhibition, such as Charles Wentworth Dilke, Matthew Digby Wyatt, Lyon Playfair and Lord Granville, were still his allies at the end of his life. And in his department were staff who worked with him throughout his 2o-year tenure, such as Richard Redgrave, Norman MacLeod, Philip Cunliffe Owen, Richard Thompson and George Wallis, none of them fools or stooges.

It was said of Cole that 'he had many really great and many extremely disagreeable qualities'.6 What made working with him worthwhile was probably the sheer exhilaration of the bumpy ride. In the first place, he was unstoppable. It was said of the South Kensington Museum, the apple of his eye, that it 'has been brought to its present magnificent proportions in the teeth of such virulent opposition and persistent ridicule as would have discouraged and disgusted most men, and against which no ordinary man could have successfully worked.'7 Cole, however, shrugged off attacks; he was 'armed in triple brass  . . invulnerable to the quill or to the acids of satire'.8 Before him others quailed. 'Timid natures gave way before the impetuosity of a knight whose sword had no scabbard, and who left himself no retreat.'9

He noted also that Fowke's successor Henry Scott said: 'I have always found [Cole] the best man I have ever served under'. 18

Cole undoubtedly was a playful monster - irrepressible, outrageous, rampant. Does the fact that he recorded the favourable opinions of others about him reveal only stark conceit? These notes certainly reveal his satisfaction, even his glee, at the recognition of his powers. But he also reveals in his writings, sayings and doings that he could be shrewd and objective, and generous to others. 'In private life', it was said, he was 'genial, indulgent, cordial, faithful, and possessing a happy gift of mimicry and an abounding sense of fun.'19 We aim to present in this book a larger-than-life character: strenuous, incorrigible, sometimes preposterous, often funny, occasionally harsh, always invigorating.

## \*   \* \*

Cole's biography is not worth writing, however, just because he was a colourful character. His achievements were formidable. Here they are in summary. For 2o years he was head of a department of the civil service, the Department of Science and Art, which administered artistic and technical education, at secondary level and beyond, in Britain. Cole started the department from almost nothing, and presided over the establishment, through the length and breadth of the country, of a chain of art colleges (science caught up later, under his successors), many of which survive today as part of our university and further education systems. The department also introduced art classes into elementary education. The part of the department's work with the highest public profile was its museum in London, which eventually grew and split to become the Victoria and Albert Museum and the Science Museum. It had satellite museums in London, Edinburgh and Dublin, fostered exhibitions and museums throughout the country, and had potent influence abroad. The central museum was on a site in South Kensington, which Albert, the Prince Consort, set up as a cultural quarter in London. After Prince Albert's death, Cole was the principal force behind the development of this site, and was directly influential in the creation of the Royal Horticultural Society's gardens, which have now disappeared, and the splendidly surviving Royal Albert Hall, Albert Memorial, Royal College of Science (now Imperial College) and Royal College of Music.

Cole came to his post as 'Plenipotentiary' of South Kensington as a result of his succss in the Great Exhibition of 185 1. Thereafter, Cole repeatedly threw his weight behind furher international exhibitions, in England in 1862 and 18714, and in Paris in 1855 and 1867. The business of exhibiting things, whether in museums or in trade fairs, could be said to be his preferred means of communication and most favoured activity.

Cole emerged as the dynamo of the Great Exhibition because he was a leading activist in the body which first suggested it, the Society for the Encouragement of Arts, Manufactures and Commerce. This private pressure group remained a power base for Cole throughout his life. He first became involved with it in the 184os when, as a small-scale entrepreneur under the characteristically jaunty trade name 'Felix Summerly', he embarked upon the improvement of design in the consumer goods market, producing cheap guides to art galleries and cultural sites, elegant and unbossy children's books (the 'Home Treasury'), and a series of domestic products ('Art Manufactures') such as a tea service, a christening mug, a bread board. He had always been interested in art and antiquities, but this was the point at which he applied himself professionally in this field.

His earlier life had been a journey of discovery in which his extraordinary talents as agitator and propagandist gradually emerged. He left school with few skills except penmanship. This got him a job copying records in the Public Record Office. By a happy chance he secured what must have been a rather better intellectual training than a conventional university education would have supplied, when he became part of the group of self-educating young men, the 'philosophic radicals', around John Stuart Mill. Here Cole imbibed utilitarian attitudes and made useful contacts, which, when he lost his job in 183 5, enabled him to mount a forcible campaign against the Public Record Office. This not only got him his job back, but also resulted in the thorough reform of the administration of the public records. After this Cole was known as an effective radical operator, and his talents were soon enlisted in the creation of the penny post, in the development of docks and railways, and in the reform of the patent laws. From here he moved on to his experiments in industrial design, and then to his career as an art administrator.

Since Cole's rise to a position of national significance was chiefly due to his personal energy and talent, he might seem to belong to that Victorian category, the selfmade man. This persona does not quite fit Cole. For his family belonged to the middle class, with members in the army and the professions, though they were going down rather than up. And Cole was educated at a reputable school, though by charity. Furthermore, like many socially rising men, he benefited from noble patronage. The favour of the Prince Consort was of fundamental importance in his life. Perhaps even more important (partly because it smoothed his path to the prince) was the more accessible friendliness of Lord Granville. A little younger than Cole, he was for long the Liberal leader in the Lords (and only just missed being prime minister): his urbanity, charm, shrewdness, diplomacy and absolutely secure social status offset some of Cole's more rebarbative qualities. While Cole never ceased to be an irritant to his society, he contrived to move from the fringes to the very centre of it.

## \* \* \*

On the strength of his achievements, both official and mutinous, Cole earned a position of eminence in Victorian Britain; indeed, he straddled an international stage. We are well aware now that eminent Victorians can be seen in various lights by their biographers. Many who led lives that attracted admiration at the time enhanced their reputations by writing self-applauding memoirs, and many were accorded the tribute of an 'official' biography that highlighted their best qualities and suppressed anything undignifed or discreditable. Henry Cole had a biography of this kind, depressingly entitled Fifty Years of Public Work and published in two volumes in 1884. Cole planned this and wrote half of it, but after his death in 1882 it was completed by his children Alan and Henrietta. It is thus part memoir and part official biography.

Victorian biography was never the same after Lytton Strachey's Eminent Victorians (19 18), in which the undignified and discreditable sides of several revered Victorian public figures were gleefully exposed. Henry Cole might have stood up to this treatment quite well. He could be as dignified, authoritarian and severe as any of his eminent contemporaries: it was said of him that 'he possessed . . . that greatest of all powers - the power of subduing other wills to his own; and this, in the opinion of many who knew him best, was the great secret of his success.'20 On the other hand, an aura of mischief and humour emanated from most of his activities, not unintentionally, but because he was, as well as a worthy, also a bit of a bounder, and enjoyed it. When he retired, it was prophesied that the comic papers 'will probably go into mourning'2' at losing one of their favourite butts. He could laugh at himself, and if Lytton Strachey had laughed at him he would probably have joined in. He was a man of both force and fun.

The tradition of the massively detailed biography still persists where eminent Victorians are concerned, for such figures usually left behind a great deal of material. Since Strachey, however, writers try to portray both the refulgent and the darker sides of their subjects. In recent years, there has been a tendency to view eminent Victorians not just as heroic, or clay-footed, individualists, but also as agents within larger political, social or cultural institutions or movements. Just as history is written nowadays not as a narrative of great men, battles and speeches, but as an analysis of forces, or an examination of institutions, or a study of representations, so biography has altered its emphasis. Henry Cole would be an apt subject for this new kind of appraisal, for almost all that he did was done in and through great Victorian institutins.2

He created and manipulated institutions, so that they exercised social control, and embodied, endorsed and promoted certain values. He might be described as an agent in the 'feld of cultural production', and a study of his 2o-year period of official life might well be entitled Henry Cole and the Exploitation of Victorian Cultural Capital'. But his work can be seen as more than an exercise of power. Since he was a 'great exhibitor', he might also be regarded as a practitioner and exponent of the art of representation.

However, to see Cole only as a surrogate for the institutions in which he was involved would be to lose something. In the last quarter of his life he concerned himself, alongside his official avocations, with rural crafts, London cabs, hymn singing, army reorm,drill in schools, music teaching, cookery and sewage disposal. It isre that these activities could be interpreted as attempts on his part to reconfigure various aspects of the established order, but they were primarily expressions of enthusiasm. It is reductive to see Cole only in ideological and institutional terms, for personality is always breaking through.Similarly, Cole' part in the Great Exhibition has several dimensions. There have been many studies of the Great Exhibition as an imperial or comercial metaphor, and Cole was as assiduous as anyone at the time in constructing such significances. But the exhibition was also for him the scene of a frantic personal scramble for success.

If we had tried to locate him precisely in each of the fields in which he acted as a cultural entrepreneur, we would have overrun the limits of a single volume. Cole's achievements in each such field could probably support a book-length analysis, and it may be worthwhile to contemplate for a moment the extent of an imaginable library of Henry Cole studies.

First, Cole's contribution to art education is a well-documented aspect of his work. Any survey of art education has to include him,23 and many accounts of English education in general signal the role played by his department in the tortuous formation of the English educational system.24 A number of dissertations have investigated his department's work more thoroughly.25 In setting up the department's educational system, Cole left curricular matters mainly to his associate Richard Redgrave, while himself concentrating on administration ('his will be the educational, mine the business point of view').26 So he may to some extent be able to sidestep the criticism that has been directed upon the department (even in its early days), that the education it offered - the highly disciplined learning of skills - was rigid and boring. Whether Cole's fault or not, however, the department's methods are quite out of tune with art education now, in which creative intuition and personal expressiveness are pre-eminently valued. So Cole does not earn much praise today for his propagation of art education, to which recent commentators have applied the adjectives 'militaristic' and 'maniacal'.27

The results of the work of the schools of art have been disparaged no less than the methods. The schools were intended to boost trade by improving the artistic design of the objects that were traded. It is by no means obvious that the latter will lead to the former (it might hinder as much as help), and it is almost impossible to make any measurable assessment of whether trade was boosted or design improved by the schools. The efforts of Cole and his allies have been dismissed as 'fully-fledged capitalist utopianism', an attempt 'to imagine and to produce a kind of field of force that will hold the anarchistic processes of production and consumption in an ideal order'.28 This may have seemed a mistaken enterprise in the 198os, when that comment was written, and when Marxist solutions to economic problems carried conviction; today, the Marxist solutions have receded, but the problems remain, so perhaps Cole's capitalist utopianism might seem relevant again. All the same, his achievement lies more in getting the schools set up than in their results or in their philosophy and methods of education.

Cole's memory is more cherished in the museum world. He created the V&A, which must be regarded, simply by virtue of its size and splendour, as one of the greatest museums in the world. Accordingly he is hailed in the professional literature as a 'museum master'.29 In this field, he was indubitably progressive, prescient of modern attitudes in his customer-oriented policies; and his amiable views stand up well in the face of much modern thinking on museums, which sees them as sites for social regulation and their curators as engrossed in the modalities of power. One recent critic proposes that 'when Henry Cole praises the museum for its educative potential, it is worth noting what he regards as its chief lesson. "It would teach the young child", he writes, "to respect property and behave gently." Cole did say this, but it was advanced as one among a string of good things that a museum might achieve, in a letter in which Cole was trying to persuade a provincial mayor to set up a museum. The words 'chief lesson' are supplied by the critic, 30 during a discussion that draws a good deal on Foucault. When propagandising, Cole cheerfully used sound bites' such as this, but, overall, his record as a museum professional is not easily discredited.

Cole's work might be seen in various contexts in English history in the Victorian period. He was, as we have noticed, among John Stuart Mill's group of philosophic radicals in the late 182os and early 18 30s, and was therefore more or less present at the birth of utilitarianism, the political system based on the ideas of Jeremy Bentham. Bentham was a philosopher and theorist who deliberately kept at a distance from practical politics, but his ideas were transformed into legislation through the efforts of a number of reformers who can be regarded as his disciples. That Bentham exercised a powerful influence was always accepted by twentieth-century historians, this view being put decisively by A. V. Dicey in Lectures on the relation between law and public opinion in England during the nineteenth century (1905). But exactly how Bentham's ideas were implemented was explored only in the 1950s and '6os, when historians began to develop models (involving stages such as 'irradiation', 'suscitation' and 'permeation')31 to explain the transition from theory to practice. There followed a lively debate about Bentham's influence on administrative reform in the Victorian period, which went under the banner: 'the nineteenth-century revolution in government'. Eventually it was conceded that, although the explanatory models helped to clarify the thoughts of those who used them, what actually went on could not wholly be explained by the models, but was inescapably affected by the exigencies of political bargaining, the ebb and flow of public opinion,32 the personalities of the reformers themselves,33 and the social and familial allegiances and combinations of those involved.34 All the same, much light was thrown on the contribution of utilitarianism to reform, in respect of the poor laws, education, the health of towns, the railways, emigration and conditions in factories and mines, in prisons and in lunatic asylums.35 Bentham disparaged the arts, and there has been little attempt to consider whether utilitarianism might have influenced cultural policy. Henry Cole and his work seem to present themselves as a test case to be investigated.

A significant impetus was given to Victorian politics by ever-increasing democratic pressure. At the beginning of the nineteenth century politics was still controlled by a small oligarchy of the propertied and titled, who settled matters among themselves, respecting each others' interests. The Reform Acts of 1832, 1867 and 1884 opened governments to pressure from a wider electorate. Even in the 182os, however, the oligarchy was already feeling the force of 'public opinion', as Jonathan Parry points out in The rise and fall of Liberal government in Victorian Britain (I993). Public opinion became a ruling concept for reformers of every kind. When the Chartists published their Charter in 1839, its aim was said to be 'to create and extend, by every constitutional means, an enlightened public opinion . . . and justly and righteously impress that opinion upon the legislature'. When the Anti-Corn Law League wound itself up in 1846, John Bright said that it had demonstrated 'that there is in public opinion a power much greater than that which resides in any particular form of government'.43 It was in Cole's formative years that public opinion became a force to be reckoned with. As an administrative reformer, he believed that all management of organisations should be subjected 'systematically to the test of public opinion'.44 Not only did Cole thus defer to public opinion; he evolved into a most ingenious and mischievous manipulator of it. He discovered how to do this when he was fighting the Public Record Office for his job, and later he put his gift for opinion-forming at the disposal of the campaigns for the penny post and the unified railway gauge. He might well be regarded as a pioneer in the art of public relations, which he regarded not cynically but with fervent idealism. 'Publicity', he said, 'with its attendant free discussion and knowledge, is the mainspring of all the progressive tendencies of the present age.'45

It is possible to view him in a much wider perspective, as a combatant  a general, indeedin what Peter Gay has called the 'pleasure wars'of the nineteenth-century.

By this Gay means the often confused and contradictory attempts to satisfy the cultural appetites of the rising bourgeoisie, in the period which saw, at its start, the displacement of a rule of taste imposed by a secure élite, and ended with the apotheosis of the artist as individualist, outsider and rebel. To accommodate art to the lives of the bourgeoisie was exactly Cole's mission, and when we see his work against the shifting trends charted by Gay, it is easier to accept some of the apparent paradoxes in his attitudes  his doctrinaire authoritarianism in matters of taste as compared with his social liberalism in welcoming the working classes into his museum; his determination to make his projects self-supporting in a free market as compared with his tenacious occupancy of posts in the state service and his importunate pursuit of public money. Gay points out that increasing prosperity caused the bourgeoisie to feel the need for 'guidance in the realms of beauty; hence guardians of taste, whether well trained or just fluent, could steer public responses with unexpected authority'.46 The dimensions of Cole's authority in the taste wars is a subject worth enquiry.

Within nineteenth-century cultural life, international exhibitions (or expositions) were a novel and conspicuous phenomenon. They have been much studied recently, not only in their economic, political and social dimensions (which were exhaustively documented at the time), but because the exposition process can be now seen as a motor of modernity and a glorification of commodities. The symbolic and metaphorical significance of expositions has also been construed, not least by Walter Benjamin.47 The more such discourses are elaborated, the more salutary it becomes to relate them to what individual agents did historically, and Cole, whose interventions in five such exhibitions are recorded in great detail, must be an important casestudy.
