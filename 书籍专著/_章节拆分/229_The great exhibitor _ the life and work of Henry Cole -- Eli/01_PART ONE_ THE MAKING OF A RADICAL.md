# PART ONE: THE MAKING OF A RADICAL

CHAPTER I

1808-1830

Henry Cole was born on 17 July 1808 in Bath. His parents, who were not well off, were living in odgings over ahop in Milsom Stree, the wide, fashionable, busting street near the centre of the city.l His father, an army officer, had been seriously wounded in 1803 by rioters in Dublin and was taking warm baths at the spa to help his recovery. His mother, Laetitia, who was very beautiful, had married his father in 1802. Henry was not born until six years later and he called himself their 'first living child', so perhaps other babies had died.

The unavoidable question about Cole's character in later life is: what was the origin of his exceptional drive and energy? He himself pondered on this, in a fragment of autobiography, begun in 1875 but not included in his 'official' autobiography, Fifty Years of Public Work. Here are his words.

I was prized and greatly indulged by both parents. . . Nothing was refused to me... When about two years old I cried al one night for the moon and both parents promised I should have it: but the moon went down and I was not pacified. . .  To my own perception my will has always seemed to be my most marked possession. I have cherished it: for years I have fixedon some decision until said will had its own way. There it was, before I could speak, and it was surey fostered by having its way from the beginning.

Henry said of his father that he was 'always kind and indulgent . . . hasty, capricious and over sanguine'.3 The tumbling series of adjectives suggests a man of goodnatured confusion. Henry Robert Cole, who was 27 in 1808, had wanted to train as an architect, and several members of his family could have helped him in that career. His father, Martin Cole, had come up to London from Bedford, and, with the help of his wife's money, had become a property developer. Beginning life as a carpenter, he was later a brewer, a timber merchant and a property valuer. At the age of 67 he handed over his businesses to his brothers and brother-in-law, and retired to start a bank in Peterborough. Since the Cole family had been established for several generations in Peterborough as bankers and lawyers, this final stage of Martin's career was a reasonable development. A practising architect in the family was the husband of Henry Robert's sister, Elizabeth: David Laing, who had been a pupil of Sir John Soane and worked with him on the Bank of England, and who became surveyor to the Board of Customs. He and Martin Cole were partners in a timber yard near Westminster Bridge. A further architectural connection was Charles Cole, another brother of Martin's, and thus Henry's great-uncle, an architect also from Peterborough, who had a successful practice and was the assistant surveyor to the Office of Woods and Forests until, in 179o, he was dismissed for overcharging.

Despite this he was master of the Tylers' Company in 18or. He lived in some grandeur in Park Lane, where he died in 1803.4

The Napoleonic Wars caused a slump in building work, so Henry Robert did not try for an architectural career after all. According to his son, he was caught up by military fever, and joined the army as a cornet in the I2th (Prince of Wales) Dragoons. After training at Maidstone, where he met his wife and married her, he was posted to Dublin. The city was in turmoil, threatened with a nationalist rebellion led by Robert Emmet. This hotheaded attempt to oust the English was not nearly as well organised as the rising of $1 7 9 8$ , and Emmet only had an unruly band of pro-French United Irishmen at his command. Nonetheless, a less sanguine man than Cole might have hesitated to take his wife into the streets of Dublin when riots were likely. On the fateful night of Saturday 23 July 1803, when the rising took place, Cole, with Laetitia and her little dog, was travelling in a post chaise when it was stopped in Thomas Street by the mob. These men at first good-naturedly handed him a pike and asked him to join them. When he refused, they shot a pistol in his face. Luckily it misfired, but the sparks from the pan scarred his upper lip for life. The men then attacked him with pitchforks and pikes, wounding him in 17 places. His wife and the dog were unharmed. The same mob, a few minutes later, murdered Lord Kilwarden, the Lord Chief Justice of Ireland. Cole was left for dead but he recovered. His wounds, particularly those in his back, were painful for the rest of his life, and he never saw active service again. He transferred into the Ioth Dragoons as an ensign early in 18o5, and was given the rank of lieutenant in March. After Henry's birth, the Coles stayed in Bath until after the birth of Henry's next brother, Frederick Lindsay, in April 181o. He was named after the 17th Lord Lindsay of the Byres, a regimental friend of Henry Robert, and was always called Lindsay rather than Frederick.

Henry Robert Cole was wellenough to go back to non-combatant military duty by 1811, when he was made a captain in the 82nd (Prince of Wales Volunteers) Regiment of Foot. He was appointed a recruiting officer, touring the North of England to persuade men to join the army to fight Napoleon. Little Henry and Lindsay were dressed up in miniature military uniforms and would ride with their father on his tours, sitting on the pummel of his saddle. At first Henry Robert was based in Chester, and young Henry remembered the ancient picturesque houses of the city. In 1813 Henry Robert was moved to Lincoln, where a third child, William, was born on New Year's Eve. Unlike the other children, who were baptised when they were four or six months old, he was christened within 48 hours of his birth, which could mean that he was not strong or had had a difficult birth. With the war over, Captain Cole was retired on half pay, and the family moved to Lewisham, a village to the south-east of London where Laetitia had lived with her two sisters before moving to Maidstone. Her family, the Downers, were merchants in the city, where her father had been a chip hatter  a dealer in straw hats and bonnets. Her mother, who died in 18oo, was Mary Clark, from a Quaker family. At Lewisham Henry and Lindsay went to their first school. Cole remembered nearly  years later:

Once in reading I stumbled at the word 'verily' and was ordered to spell it  which was quite impossible for me. So I was imprisoned in a dark closet there to await a whipping. Captain Cole attempted to eke out his half-pay by selling coal to his friends, and had an arrangement with the well-known coal merchants Cory & Cory. He was always short of money, plaguing Lord Lindsay with begging letters; and only presents from Laetitia's brother Richard kept them afloat. Richard had been a naval officer, but had gone to Berbice, now Guyana, facing the Atlantic in north-east South America, where he made a fortune as a merchant, dealing mainly in straw hats that he sent to his relations in London. Henry Cole called Richard Downer 'a stern hard uncle', and remembered how he brought a black slave boy with him when he visited England (probably in spring 18 19). The slave used to accompany Downer to Cole's father's house, where his master would 'take him into concealed spots in the garden and flog him. My spirit rebelled against this cruelty,' recalled Henry, 'and defying my uncle's threats I used to run and shout for my mother to protect the boy which sometimes stopped the punishment.'8 By this date the family had moved to Lambeth, where Charles Augustus, Henry's fourth brother, was born on 1 February 1819.

After attending the dame school at Lewisham, Henry might well have proceeded to a small private school, the sort of school attended by his near-contemporary Charles Dickens, whose family, like Cole's, clung to the impoverished lower edge of the middle class, and who several times described such schools in his novels. But Captain Cole, with poor health, five sons and an income of £22o a year, could not even afford this, and had to find a way of educating his boys at no cost. Paradoxically, this gave Henry Cole the sort of education that was normally reserved for the sons of England's elite, for he was sent to Christ's Hospital, where his family had a right to a free place.

Christ's Hospital's charter had been granted in June I 5 5 3 by King Edward VI. The school was housed in the monastic buildings of the Grey Friars to the east of the City of London, which were left empty after the dissolution of the monasteries. Like a number of other schools, it had been founded and endowed for the education of 'poor boys'. Most such schools sooner or later forgot their obligation to the poor, and by the early nineteenth century they served the rich. England's top eight boys' schools were all of this type: the boarding schools at Eton, Harrow, Winchester and Rugby; and the London schools of Westminster, Charterhouse, St Paul's and Merchant Taylors'. Christ's Hospital was regarded as the ninth in order of priority after these.9 Unlike the others it still supplied scholarships for poor boys, and in comparison with the others, fewer of its pupils went to university and more into trade. 10 In 18 17 Captain Cole succeeded in proving that through his mother's family, the Bunns, he was a direct descendant of a Thomas Lockington, treasurer at Christ's Hospital in the early eighteenth century, who had left property to provide two places in the school for his relations. So Captain Cole claimed scholarships for his sons.

On 9 January 18 17, the eight-year-old Henry was delivered by his father to the school. He was put in the charge of the matron of the 1oth Ward, whom Captain Cole tipped with a guinea. Henry was provided with the uniform of long blue coat, yellow stockings and red belt. This was a survival of Tudor boys' dress (to which breeches had been added in 18 1o). The long coat had been perpetuated through the eighteenth century as the dress of charity school boys. As it fell into disuse except at Christ's Hospital, it came to have a certain chic, but in Cole's time it probably still conveyed a mixed message: of privilege combined with poverty (plates 1,2,3). Henry wrote, I remained at the school between five and six years and had a rather unhappy time of it'. 11

Conditions at Christ's Hospital were not very different from those at the other great schools. Deprivation was often exacerbated by physical abuse. At this time, bullying and violence reached extreme proportions in the great old schools — not only among pupils, as depicted searingly in Thomas Hughes's Tom Brown's Schooldays, but also between boys and masters. This was the great age of the flogging schoolmaster - the supremely notorious example being the squat, frenzied Dr Keate of Eton. Christ's Hospital had a particularly bad reputation for corporal punishment, and the chief executant there was the Rev. Dr Arthur Trollope, the headmaster. This distinguished classical scholar and churchman was a fat man of about 5o, with protruding eyes. Cole described him as having:

Francis Cohen was in his mid-3os when Henry became his clerk. He was the son of a city stockbroker, who had him carefully educated by private tutors, and he grew up with a passion for medieval history. He was of medium height, dark and handsome, dashing, intelligent and enthusiastic. It was observed, in the idiom of the time, that 'he had the air of high breeding and the show of natural dignity that are sometimes observable in Jews of the highest type and finest quality'.21 In 182 1 he was baptised a Christian, so as to be eligible to become a member of the Bar. He was elected a fellow of the Royal Society at the very early age of 3 3, and he was a welcome caller at Holland House where he met many of the leading politicians and intellectuals of the day. There he met John Allen, Lord Holland's librarian and close friend, who was a member of the board of the Record Commission. He suggested that Cohen might edit some early parliamentary rolls, which the commission wished to see published. After some protracted negotiations Cohen was appointed a sub-commissioner in March 1822.

He employed clerks to make transcripts of the documents with which he was concerned, the 'Parliamentary writs of the time of Edward I', 22 which he then, at his leisure, edited and prepared for publication. At first he employed young clerks from the Record Office at the Tower of London. But after about a year, two of them, Thomas and William Hardy, rebelled against the low pay he gave them, and so he decided to make other arrangements. He consulted the steward at Christ's Hospital, who recommended Henry and the other two other 'Blues', George Aungier and William Peacock. Henry wrote that Cohen 'set me to fish out my own way in learning the abbreviations in which the Records both in Latin and French were written, and sent me to learn French in the evenings of the Abbe de Roussigny in Great Russell Street - an emigré'.23 These lessons went on for three years, twice a week, and evidently something stuck, for Cole was able in later life to deliver a short public speech in French in Paris in 1867.24

Cole found himself in the rather odd position of working within a public institution but employed privately to serve one of its staff. The Record Commission was a slack organisation. Although a State Paper Office, with its keeper, had existed since the reign of Elizabeth I, England's public records had been allowed to pile up, without central co-ordination, in the government departments and legal institutions which had at first produced them, and continued to own them. In an attempt to bring some order, several reports and inquiries were made during the eighteenth century, without positive outcome, but in 18oo an investigation by a parliamentary select committee led to the appointment of the first of a series of six commissions, known collectively as 'The Record Commission'. The commission was eventually replaced, after the death of William IV in 1837. By then it had lost public confidence, because it concentrated on publishing editions of certain of the records, but failed to provide adequately for the storage and listing of the mass of them. Quantities of public money were spent on employing the people who edited the records. The commissioners - over 2o of them, including bishops, peers and politicians, with only the occasional scholar who knew or cared anything about history or the sources of historical research - regarded the appointment of editors as an opportunity to extend patronage to their friends. The employment of Cohen, through the influence of Allen, was typical.

The nature of the records themselves can be conveyed in Henry Cole's words, from an article he later wrote. 'In a popular sense the term is applied to all public documents preserved in a recognised repository,' he said. Britain was especially rich 'in the possession of antient written memorials of all branches of its government, constitutional, judicial, parliamentary, and fiscal, memorials authenticated by all the solemn sanctions of authority, telling truly, though incidentally, the history of our progress as a people, and handed down in unbroken series through the period of nearly seven centuries'. The records mostly took the form of 'rolls written on skins of parchment and vellum', which were 'kept in pouches or bags of leather, canvas, cordovan, and buckram' or else in 'chests, coffers, coffins, and "forcers" bound with iron and painted of different colours, cases or "scrinia", "skippets", or small turned boxes, and hanapers, or "hampers of twyggys" (fig.1).25 They were kept in various decrepit stores in central London.

In early June 1822, Henry went to work for the first time at one of these stores in the Tower of London, where he found that he and his fellows were treated as outsiders and cold-shouldered by all the established staff, including William and Thomas Hardy. This repository was headed by Henry Petrie, who was the keeper in charge of the Chancery Records, and who had beaten Cohen to this post in 1819. It cannot have helped Cole's popularity in these early days that he spilt ink on a petition to Edward I. 'It was a terrible offence and might have caused my dismissal and altered my whole career.'26 Fortunately he was not dismissed. The rule that only pencils were to be used by those working on records was not introduced until 1840. The records were kept on the upper floors of the White Tower, and the exquisite Norman chapel was filled to its vaulted roof with records belonging to the Admiralty. Their security was threatened by the fact that the cellars were used by the army for storing gunpowder, 'enough to destroy all Tower-hill, and change even the course of the Thames if an explosion took place'. 27

The keeper in charge of this office was John Caley, the secretary to the Record Commission, who was also keeper of the Augmentation Office. The office hours were from ten to one o'clock and the clerks were allowed six weeks holiday a year. Though Caley drew a salary of nearly £ 1,ooo a year, he never came into the office but directed the work from his house in Clerkenwell, using his footman, Charles Gay, as a messenger. Cole described how a search for a record was made. An applicant would first call at the office, but if 'Charles Gay was not there, he would then have to come a second time.' Once had had found Gay, he would be sent to Caley's house, where he might or might not find Caley. Having reached Caley, he would be told to return after two days, to give Caley time to search his indexes for the required document. On the applicant's return, Caley would, if he had traced the document, tell the applicant to call again later to see it. 'The records were brought from the office, to Mr. Caley's house, and it very often happened that the precise document wanted was not produced. Parties have informed me', said Cole, that 'they have lost a fortnight in running backwards and forwards to his house.' This system, which had been operating for 15 years, was bad for the records, as they were toted around in a bag by Gay. 'More injury has been done to the Rolls within these few years, than in the two preceding centuries.' Caley never allowed 'any applicant, without exception to look into his Indexes, public or private'. For this service of searching and transcribing Caley charged what he liked, and he made over £1,2oo a year from these fees alone.

While Cole was able to observe these dismal practices, he was to some extent distanced from them, because he and his two fellows were outside the system, working directly on transcription for Francis Cohen. Cohen's task eventually resulted in four volumes, each weighing 7 1 lbs. 'I passed nine years of my life assisting . . . in the manufacture of enormous folio tomes, so heavy that no one would lift them if he could help it,' wrote Cole. 'Their sale was very small.'30

Cole tried to improve his defective education in other ways. He took German lessons, in addition to his French lessons, and developed his slight talent, but great love, for music. He had been given a flute by his father many years before, and took lessons from a Corporal Blizzard of the Life Guards' band, who also taught him fencing. He had some singing lessons from a friend, William Kingston. Once a week he visited a Monsieur Gueraint, whom he punningly described as a 'hop merchant', for dancing lessons. When he went to church on Sunday, it was not infrequently for the music. He oten went with Lindsay to a Catholic chapel to hear the wel-known singer, Madame Malibran.

In person, Cole grew to be only about 5 feet 8 inches tall; he was slight, strong and active. He brushed his reddish hair forward and had it fashionably curled, and he wore a monocle on a ribbon, then considered very smart, though he was short-sighted and should have used spectacles. He had his clothes made to measure, and his numerous female cousins hemmed silk cravats for him. Though his diaries in early years record little except his expenses and where he got his dinner, they gradually begin to note outings to plays and the opera. Cole's social life in his early 2os was passed chiefly among his various young relations. Among these were two families of cousins, the Bonds and the Webbs, who were the children of his mother's sisters and lived near each other at Epping in Essex. Henry saw most of the Bonds, where there were four girls, Charlotte, Frances, Laura and Marian ('gentle, attractive and cultivated'), 34 besides two boys, Richard and John. There was only one child in the other family, Mary Anne, whose father Thomas Webb was a veterinary surgeon. She eventually inherited Quendon Hall, farther out in Essex, from a relation, and married Captain Henry Byng of the Royal Navy. Cole visited one or other of the families about once a month, and usually walked the 2o-odd miles from central London, as there was no question of his being able to afford the fare on the coach. His younger brothers sometimes went too, and Henry was expected to keep them in order. His Uncle Webb once wrote to him when an elderly relative was expected: 'Now my dear fellow . . . I must beg you to have great steadiness of behaviour, no leap frog or Wheelbarrow driving. . . . Joking apart, do enforce upon your brother the indispensable necessity of appearing in a decent set of breeches'. As a postscript he added: 'Be sparing of the gin and water on the road'.35

Cole had some other Bond first cousins, the children of William Bond, D.D. He was rector of Hanwell in Middlesex to the west of London, where he ran a school, and was also a chaplain to the dukes of Cambridge and Kent. Among his children, those whom Henry knew best were Edward and John. Edward made his career at the British Museum, becoming a distinguished reforming chief librarian and a knight, while John followed Henry into the record service and worked there all his life, ending up a senior assistant keeper.

Cole's Laing cousins, who lived in London, were also friends and he spent evenings with them playing music. In 1825 his Uncle David Laing was disgraced because the Custom House that he had designed and built about 1o years earlier began to fall down. Among the faults, it was discovered that he had not used sturdy enough wooden piles in the foundations, and had used defective wood elsewhere. There was even a suggestion that he and the contractors, John Miles and Henry Peto, had colluded in a fraud. 36 He was dismissed and the family moved to Clapham. Henry saw quite a lot of his cousins Elizabeth and Charlotte Laing, but he came to dislike the family, noting in 1831: 'They are a mischievous set, shallow minded and rude'.37

Cole amused himself as young men will. The theatre (of which he was 'passionately fond'),38 the opera and art exhibitions became regular treats. On 12 March 18 27 he recorded that he had been 'to what is called a Ball', from which, after spending six or eight hours devotedly attending a Julia Westby, he returned home at half past five in the morning. 'A mighty exertion for one accustomed to regularity.' He had an eye for a pretty girl, when he could see accurately. On 5 July 1829 he met two sisters. 'Without my glasses I had formed a pleasant opinion of them: which was quickly dispelled in putting on my spectacles.' He evidently had a taste for pranks. At New Year's Eve 1830, he dressed up as a female friend's female chaperon, hiring a dress, wig and prince-of-Wales feathers (fig.2). He went to a party at the house of the Hughes family in Gower Street, and survived the whole evening undetected, though, as he said, 'suspicion was resting heavily upon me'. He regarded his female cousins with interest, as emerges from his correspondence with his fellow clerk, William Peacock. Peacock was the son of the bursar at Christ's Hospital, and a close friend of Cole's already at school. William could not bear the tedium of copying and transcribing old texts, and so in 1828 he went to India under the patronage of Lord William Bentinck, the new governorgeneral o Bengal. Over he next  years he wrote Cole $7 5$ letters, which Cole kept. In one of them he says: 'You speak of the beauty of your Cousins at Epping & immediately subsequent ask me if the climate has had any physical effect upon me. Verily I suspect the beauty of Marian has had a physical effect upon you. How say you?'39 But Henry was at this time attracted in another direction. He was in love with Emma Hughes, of the Gower Street family. He proposed marriage, but was turned down by her mother. He suffered a great deal and confided in William Peacock, who was sympathetic.

The Cole family, with their housekeeper, a widow named Mrs Cook, changed lodgings several times during these years, which Henry disliked very much. When they moved, yet again, on 15 April 1826, he wrote sadly in his diary that they had 'moved to 18 Stamford St. Q? How long shall we remain here?' As it turned out, the family lived there for many years, and Cole made a watercolour drawing of his room. It is likely that he executed it during the spring of 1827, while he was having his second course of lessons with Charles Wild. He seems to have set himself an exercise in perspective: the drawers of the chest-of-drawers are open and a cupboard door is ajar. The room is small and contains pieces of furniture that probably belonged to his landlord. It has a fireplace that can never have been lit, as the close-carpet runs up to the grate, with no hearth showing. The pot-plants on the table by the window show Cole's interest in botany (plate ).

Cole had many quarrels with his father about the position in the household of his housekeeper. 'Mrs Cook must & will go,' he confided to his diary on 3o March 1828. But, although he expected 'some decisive measures' on I I May, he discovered on the 3 1st: 'Mrs Cook is not to go. God knows how the business will terminate.'
