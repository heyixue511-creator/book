# CHAPTER 2 The Philosophic Radicals 1826-c.1833

The house in Stamford Street, to which the Coles moved in 1826, belonged to the writer, Thomas Love Peacock, then aged 4 1. He worked at the East India Company, one of several 'Assistants Under the Examiner' who administered the company's Indian business at long range through correspondence. He kept two rooms in his house for his own use when in London, but had sent his family to live in the country, and allowed the Coles to occupy the rest of the house. In early life Peacock won recognition as a poet and the friendship of Shelley. Later he became celebrated for brilliant, satirical novels of ideas, including Headlong Hall and Nightmare Abbey. Genial, generous and a witty conversationalist, he took an immediate fancy to Henry, who was nearly 18. Frequently Cole would walk into the city with Peacock, and 'generally about once a week I passed two hours in the evening at Stamford Street with him listening to a perpetual flow of his wit and stories, oftentimes profane'. By 'profane' Cole signifies Peacock's cheerful atheism, which unsettled Cole's faith.1

Peacock frequently gave Cole an 'order' — a free ticket — for a theatre or a concert, and sometimes he got Cole to make notes on a performance to assist him in writing a review when he was unable to go himself. This launched Cole into journalism, his first independent review being a short notice of Malibran's performance in La Cenerentola, published in The Globe in April 1830. Cole wrote: 'What emotions of pride were aroused in me by the publication of this contribution'.² Through Peacock, Cole met a number of owners and editors of newspapers and magazines, such as John Bowring, the first editor of the Westninster Review; Walter Coulson of The Globe, who had once been Jeremy Bentham's secretary; and Albany Fonblanque, the editor and later owner of the Examiner. Cole was soon writing weekly musical reviews for him.' By 1834 Cole had moved on from reviewing, and after that his journalism was mostly devoted to fighting the various causes he took up. It has been rightly said that 'Cole was not in journalism for journalism's sake'.4 All the same, the opportunity that Peacock gave him to become a writer for periodicals was an important opening for him.

An even more important favour that Peacock performed for Cole was to introduce him to John Stuart Mill and his circle. This contact directed Cole's attention to the philosophy of utilitarianism, by which all his future activities were influenced; it brought his political interests into focus; and, above al, supplied him with the sort of enlightenment that he had missed by not going to university.

When Peacock joined the East Inda Company in 1819, another new entrant was S   il son John Stuart into the company in 1823 as a clerk, John then being 17. On 7 November 1826 Peacock took Cole to the house of George Grote, the middle-aged radical politician, banker and historian, and there introduced him to John Stuart Mill and to the 'disquisitive set of young men' (as Peacock called them), who clustered around Mill, meeting twice a week early in the morning for reading and discussion.

John Mill (plate 6) was described by Thomas Carlyle, who first met him at about this time, as 'a slender rather tall and elegant youth, with small clear roman-nosed face, two small earnestly-smiling eyes; modest, remarkably gifted with precision of utterance . .  a gifted and amiable youth. We had almost four hours of the best talk I have mingled in for long'.6 Cole also fell under John Mills spell. He wrote that he 'was 2o years of age when we first met. I was two years younger. He had already become a writer in periodicals. No two courses of early training and experience could have differed more than ours did. It might seem that we had little in common but I appreciated in him what I imperfectly understood and we soon grew intimate'.7

Mill had an extraordinary upbringing. His father James, a dour and driven man, had raised himself from an obscure Scottish background to literary success in London. Through writing a History of British India, a long-drawn-out project that loomed over John's childhood, he succeeded in obtaining his senior post with the East India Company. The overwhelmingly powerful influence in James's life was the political philosopher Jeremy Bentham. They met in 1808, when Bentham was 6o and James Mill 35. Bentham was already renowned for his writings, which sought to devise a political system in which the greatest good should lie in the greatest happiness of the greatest number, a system in which deference and precedent should have no value, but in which rational efficiency should prevail. Although Bentham believed that his ideas were capable of transforming the way his country was run, he kept himself remote from practical life, working on ever more complex refinements of his system. James Mill became his disciple and interpreter, synthesising his views and trying to bring them into practical effect by promoting them among radical politicians. He also found the opportunity to apply them practically in the upbringing of his son, in which Bentham took a keen interest. John Mill was to be educated to be a demonstration of Bentham's idea of what a perfect human being should be. He was to grow up totally rational, free of the waywardness of emotion, and free of the imposed principles of religion.

This education had its quaint, even comic aspects, especially during the summers that the Bentham and Mill families spent together at Forde Abbey in Dorset. There, ignoring the large number of rooms that the house contained, they all worked together in the saloon, an enormous room hung with tapestries after designs by Raphael. The Mills sat at one table, James the father writing his History of India, and the child John working at problems of logic. At the far end of the room, Bentham, surrounded by cushions to which he pinned his notes, wrestled with his great scheme for universal education, the Chrestomathia. From an early age John, the eldest of the Mill children, was expected to superintend the lessons of his six younger siblings, three girls and three boys. He was allowcd no friends outside the family, no toys, and no games except battledore and shuttlecock in the great hall, which Bentham enjoyed and took very seriously. The children's mother was ignored. Under this regime, Mill became extraordinarily precocious. He started learning Greek at three, was doing algebra at eight, logic at twelve, and economics at thirteen.

Mill insisted that 'mine . . . was not an education of cram', because it involved continuous, searching explanation of everything from his father, rather than learning by rote. Nonetheless it was very demanding. It prevented him from 'having any great amount of intercourse with other boys',8 and from going to university, which Bentham and James Mill dismissed as dominated by the Church. John Mill's sequestered education led him, in about 1826-7, into severe depression - one of the most celebrated nervous breakdowns in English intellectual history. On recovering from depression, Mill remained faithful to the philosophy he had learned from Bentham and his father, but he softened its rigours, and he and the young friends he had by this time made became a second generation of more tolerant, liberal and realistic utilitarians.

Cole did not study Bentham's long and difficult writings - he preferred listening to lectures to hard reading - but he must have absorbed Bentham's views at second hand from John Mill, and have absorbed also Mill's admiration for Bentham. When reading Bentham, Mill recalled, 'I felt taken up to an eminence from which I could survey a vast mental domain'. Every page of Bentham 'seemed to open a clearer and broader conception of what human opinions and institutions ought to be'. Bentham's principle of utility 'fell exactly into its place as the keystone which held together the detached and fragmentary components of my knowledge', and offered a prospect of 'practicable improvement' which 'was sufficiently large and brilliant to light up my life, as well as to give a definite shape to my aspirations'. For Cole, Mill's enthusiasm must have been exhilarating, and the doctrine of utility  that all things should be valued in so far as they contributed to the good of the majority, and that practical efficiency was the prime virtue - must, for a young man of Cole's direct, impatient temperament, have offered a sharp weapon for cutting through the world's muddle.

Inclusion in Mill's circle gave Cole the equivalent of a university education. His work with the records was giving him an academic training of sorts, but hardly the kind of intellectual challenge that Mill's group could provide. Mill, barred by his father from a university education, had instituted for himself a programme of selfeducation, through a study group, started in the winter of 18223. He called this the 'Utilitarian Society', since its main aim was to foster Jeremy Bentham's ideas. It broke up in 1826, but was soon revived. It now \`assembled to the number of a dozen or more. Mr Grote lent a room of his house in Threadneedle Street for the purpose. .. We met two mornings in every week, from half past eight till ten, at which hour most of us were called off to our daily occupations.'10 This group Cole now joined. They started with political economy, proceeding to syllogistic logic. Cole confided to his diary that he was often out of his depth, and remembered that Whately's Logic was 'diffcult to master'. 11

In addition to discussions in the group, Cole and Mill talked together. Cole's work often took him to the records deposited in the Tower of London, and on the way there he would frequently call on Mill at India House. When the Mill household later moved to Kensington in 1 83 1, Cole used to entertain Mill to tea in his new chambers in the Adelphi. They talked of reason and poetry (5 October 183 1), Shakespeare's characters (19 October), the 'difficulty & even impossibility of translations' (2 November), the principles of government (1 2 November), Mill's own character, as he veered between a utilitarian and a mystic, a logician and a sentimentalist (23 November), despotism and revolution (5 March 1832), the scientific and poetical views of life (16 May), and so on.15

Cole's relationship with Mill was not merely a matter of intellectual stimulation. Cole very much liked Mill: he recorded that his 'admiration & esteem for his talents encreases each time that I have the pleasure of seeing him'. 16 Mill must have valued Cole, not as an intellectual sparring partner, of which there were plenty in his group, but as a cheering companion. Mill met Cole, it seems, just as he was emerging from his mental crisis. Cole later wrote that 'our acquaintance began about three years after that period in his life', but the exact date of Mill's crisis is uncertain, and since Mill covered it up as best he could, it seems possible that Cole was on hand at its height. At any rate, as Cole recalled, 'John Mill confessed to me that when his feeling of hopelessness of life was most acute he had seriously contemplated suicide'.17

Cole recorded that Mill told him that the reason for the crisis was 'that he thought his education was a failure'. Mill's interpretation of his crisis, expressed later in his Autobiography, was, in essence, that his education had made him both intellectually advanced and emotionally retarded. Feelings, he concluded, 'were the greatest and surest sources of happiness'. But 'my education . . . had failed to create these feelings in sufficient strength to resist the dissolving influence of analysis, while the whole course of my intellectual cultivation had made precocious and premature analysis the inveterate habit of my mind'. As he recovered from his depression, 'the cultivation of the feelings became one of the cardinal points in my ethical and philosophical creed'.18 Whether Cole fully understood Mill's crisis at the time we cannot be sure;

but later (having had the opportunity to read Mill's Autobiography) he assessed his own upbringing in Mill's terms. 'I myself certainly had no consciousness of having been taught to cultivate "the dissolving power of analysis", although I realised that my parents had subjected me (perhaps without deliberate intention) to a cultivation of the feelings.'19

What Cole was able to offer to Mill is not difficult to understand: the friendship of an uncomplicated contemporary. In Cole, Mill found an ordinary young man, who had played and fought with other boys at school, who had explored adolescence in a large family of male and female siblings and cousins, and had been in love. All this Mill had missed during his cloistered and taxing upbringing; he had not had 'an apprenticeship in human relations'.20 Despite acknowledging in his Autobiography that this is what he needed, Mill did not, in that work, include much about personal relationships, for it was meant as a record of his intellectual development. Many of his friends, it has been noted, "get minimal attention'; and Cole, 'with whom he was intimate early and late . . is ignored in the Autobiography'.21 Nonetheless, Mill spent much spare time with Cole over seven or eight years in the late 18 2os and early 1830s.

Cole often joined in Mill's regular Sunday rambles, long walks into the country around London and back. Usually the company consisted of Mill, and his two closest friends, John Arthur Roebuck (plate 9) and George Graham; with Cole, Horace Grant, Edwin Chadwick (plate I 1), who was Jeremy Bentham's secretary, and J. H. Elliott, who became a deputy in the city. Cole remembered the atmosphere of these sessions as 'a virtuous state of disputation & pleasantry', a mixture of "walking, talking and glee'.22 One Sunday the group started out at seven in the morning and walked to the Greyhound at Croydon, where they had 'a substantial breakfast on rump steaks. After breakfast John Mill read aloud the Banquet of the Ancients from Peregrine Pickle, one of the most humourous scenes in the English Language, and a capital aid to digestion at any meal'. After striding along the edge of the Downs 'discussing and laughing the whole time', they reached Keston Common in the evening and had some bread and cheese and beer. They returned to London by way of Chislehurst, and at Dulwich John Mill remarked, 'I feel as if I had walked between forty and fifty miles'.23

Mill, who was busier and far more methodical, kept his collection in neat little parcels that he opened only once a year to add new specimens. In so far as Cole adopted the philosophy of utilitarianism, it was Mill's 'soft' version to which he leaned. When he refers to utilitarians, it is as a group to which he does not belong. For instance, on reading a review of Tennyson's poems, he comments that 'the Reviewer is a utilitarian & like the rest of that species knows but little of Poetical criticism or Poetry itself'. And, when recording a meeting of the London Debating Society, where utilitarianism was discussed, Cole notes that Charles Buller was 'the only disciple of that system present'.3' Probably he used the term utilitarian primarily to characterise the older generation: Bentham, James Mill, Francis Place, the radical tailor, David Ricardo, the economist, Joseph Hume, MP, Henry Brougham, Albany Fonblanque, Perronet Thompson, John Austin. It was a younger group which surrounded John Stuart Mill.32 We have already met some of them: Edwin Chadwick, who was to become a great reforming civil servant; Horace Grant, the educationist, and J. H. Elliot. Among them were friends whom Mill esteemed for their intellectual prowess: George Graham, 'a thinker of originality and power on almost all abstract subjects', William Ellis, 'an original thinker in the field of political economy', and William Eyton Tooke, "a young man of singular worth both moral and intellectual' 33 There were also young men out to make a mark in politics, for the utilitarians cherished hopes, in the 183os, of forming a political party. Before the Reform Bill, two from the older generation were Members of Parliament: Henry Warburton, a timber dealer turned politician, who entered Parliament in 1826, and Edward Strutt, from 1830. In December 1832, George Grote (plate 8) was returned to the reformed Parliament. Already 38, he was soon acknowledged, owing to his gravity and ability, as chief of the utilitarians in the Commons, but he lacked a flair for leadership and eventually subsided into a life of scholarship. Others who entered Parliament in 1832 were: John Arthur Roebuck, small but handsome, a firebrand orator and always regarded as something of a demagogue; Sir William Molesworth (plate 10), a slightly cranky Cornish landowner, who had become the eighth baronet at the age of 12 and whose social assurance led him to adopt various extreme views; and his fellow Cornishman, the amiable and amusing Charles Buller. Charles Villiers, a scion of an aristocratic political family, followed in 183 5. These men hoped to apply Bentham's views in the world at large. Bentham was over 7, writing little, seeing very few people, and quite outside politics. The Mills, while promoting his ideas, were also outside politics, as they held official positions with the East India Company. But the utilitarian MPs might make Benthamism a political reality.

It was inevitable that this group would enlarge Cole's political consciousness. This had already been aroused by the debates at the London Debating Society. The subjects discussed included literature, as we have seen, and philosophy ('Whether Logic is more curious than useful', 'Whether Metaphysics are practically useful'); and some lighter topics ('Whether chivalry has been beneficial or pernicious'). Very often, however, they were political issues: 'That the Wellington Administration is entitled to the confidence of the Country', 'The Benefits of Church Establishment', 'The influence of Aristocracy upon Morals and Manners is pernicious', 'That the country is on the eve of some political change and that unless concession be made by the Aristocracy a struggle is to be expected', 'The Ballot'.34 Cole would have been well briefed, through the debates and other contacts in the Mill circle, on the issues which particularly pre-occupied the radicals in the period before the Reform Act of 1832: the need for regular parliamentary elections, the enlargement of the franchise, and the secrt ballot. There is little sign from his diaries that he was a political activist. His spare time was divided between self-improvement (through reading, and attending lectures and debates) and the enjoyment of culture (plays, opera, art galleries), with preference given to the latter. When, however, the House of Lords defeated Lord Grey's Reform Bil in October 183 1, he rushed out indignantly, like everyone else, and worked for a National Political Union.

After the Reform Act of 1832, the utilitarians in Parliament had high hopes of increasing their influence. Though they often supported the Whig ministry they ostentatiously sat with the opposition in the House of Commons. They proved, however, to be too individualistic to make a cohesive group, and they were disappointed in their preferred leader, Lord Durham, known as 'Radical Jack' They did adopt a distinguishing name: 'philosophic radicals' The epithet 'philosophic' was chosen by John Stuart Mill to indicate that the group carefully thought out their policies from fundamental principles, in the manner of philosophers, 'who, when they are discussing means, begin by considering the end, and when they desire to produce effects, al easily be ridiculed. The political aims of the philosophic radicals were based on their equal distaste for both the Whigs and the Tories. They regarded both groups of politicians as 'aristocratic factions', as 'two sets of scramble-mongers, one to grasp and the other to keep the victuals of office'.37 They saw themselves, on the other hand, as champions of that 'greatest number' whose 'greatest happiness' was to be sought, and whom they characterised as 'The People'. The conflict between Aristocracy and People would emerge, the philosophic radicals thought, as the dominating issue in politics. 'The problem will then be reduced to its simplest terms', said J. S. Mill. 'Who is for the aristocracy and who for the people, will be the plain question.'38 There was a moment in 1836 when the philosophic radicals thought they held the balance of power and might achieve a political breakthrough,39 though this prospect soon vanished. Another tempting prospect was that the radicals would absorb the Whigs and defeat the Tories.40 Neither did this come to pass.

Though the political manoeuvrings of the philosophic radicals were probably not of intense interest to Cole, it is not difficult to see how their political attitudes may have stood him in good stead later. When he eventually became a reforming civil servant, his success must have been greatly assisted by an ability to hold to a radical, personal agenda, while distrusting, and keeping at a distance, both the main political parties. Cole always regarded himself as an advocate for the people, though this does not mean that he, any more than his friends, saw himself as supporting those on the very lowest levels of society. Roebuck remarked that the lowest classes were no better than the highest: 'they are both . . . idle, profligate, rapacious . . . . There is . . . very little difference, except in outward manner, garb, and language, between a gambling, horse-racing, profligate rich man, and a thieving, drinking, gambling beggar'.41 It was in the virtuous mechanic that Roebuck placed his trust, and likewise Cole, in his cultural politics, always tried to advance the interests of the artisan. In various ways, then, the politics of the philosophic radicals helped Cole to get a sense of the political landscape through which, in the longer term, he was to cut his path.

The Mills worked in the world of ideas rather than in the political struggle, and their preferred means was the writing of (unsigned) articles in periodicals. 'Journalism is to modern Europe what political oratory was to Athens and Rome', said John Mill.42 This led to their involvement with a monthly journal, the Westminster Revicw, founded in 1824 to serve as the house magazine of the philosophic radicals. 'The need of a Radical organ to make head against the Edinburgh and Quarterly (then in the period of their greatest reputation and influence)'43 had impressed itself so forcibly on Bentham that he provided the finance necessary to set up the review. It was edited by John Bowring, a disciple of Bentham, who was to have an interesting, if erratic, career. Although it caused a sensation at its first appearance, as John Mill conceded,44 it lost momentum, and Mill severed his connection with it in spring 1828. Cole contributed to it  what might appear to be a dry piece of constitutional history, 'The Parliaments of our ancestors' in 1834, turns out to have a mocking radical sting as Cole shows how the current House of Commons was as constricted as the Black Hole of Calcutta. And, very briefly, he edited it in 1 840.

The influence of John Mill and the philosophic radicals upon Cole was critical in forming his views and sharpening his criticism of the world. One area of human activity on which they had advanced views was birth control.49 The rapid expansion of the population and the possibility that there would be insufficient food to feed all the hungry mouths exercised many, not least Thomas Malthus. The utilitarians looked for practical expedients, and favoured the limitation of conception. This subject, however, was virtually unmentionable. One writer on the subject, Henry Bickersteth, issued his thoughts under the prim title, Medical Hints for the use of Clergymen (1829). Among the utilitarians, Francis Place was principal spokesman, in his book, Illustrations and Proofs of the Principle of Population (1822), and, more accessibly, through popular broadsheets (addressed to 'The Married', both 'in Genteel Life' and 'of the Working People'), which were distributed free in huge numbers. John Mill became concerned with the problem, and at the age of 18 (when his practical experience of contraception was probably non-existent) was arrested, with a friend, for dropping leaflets into the areas of houses for servant girls to read  an incident which was hushed up.50

Cole's debt to Mill and his circle seems to have encompassed almost all of life, from the most basic human urges to the highest flights of mental activity. Cole must, then, have brought a utilitarian perspective to bear on his own life. The Record Commission was just the sort of organisation that the philosophic radicals most abhorred, for it was run by members of the Tory aristocracy and wasted huge sums of the people's money. Cole was soon to bring utilitarian reformism to bear on it.
