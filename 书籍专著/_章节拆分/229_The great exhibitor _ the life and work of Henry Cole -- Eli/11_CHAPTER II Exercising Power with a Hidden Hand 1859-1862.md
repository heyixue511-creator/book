# CHAPTER II Exercising Power with a Hidden Hand 1859-1862

Returning from his foreign break on Saturday 5 March, Cole went round the museum with Redgrave. 'All expressed their pleasure at my return.' A week later, at his first meeting with his ministers, he reported 'the highly effective state in which, after six months absence' he found the museum.' One of the first things he did, in the warm glow attending his return, was to invite the Robinsons to take tea on Monday 7 March, and to present to Mrs Robinson an enamelled chain which he had brought back as a present for her. Robinson, however, was chafing at his lack of advancement, and on Thursday 17 March Cole found himself having a 'long discussion with Robinson about his future prospects', which ended when 'I left the room', presumably in annoyance or impatience. The next day, Robinson 'asked if he might be called "Director" instead of Keeper' of the museum. These were portents of disagreements between Cole and Robinson that would grow over the next few years. But for the time being amicability was restored, and on 25 November Cole's daughter Tishy stood as godmother to the Robinsons' son Francis James.

During 1859 and 186o, Cole was concerned with three longish-term projects, all to some extent architectural. One was the continued enlargement of the museum. Cole had started to build out northwards from the iron building with the gallery for the Sheepshanks pictures, and, while he was away in Italy, Redgrave and Fowke had pressed ahead with galleries for the Turner and Vernon pictures, followed by a range running west-east along the northern edge of the site. It was now planned to build a range along the eastern edge of the site, thus creating a three-sided open courtyard. At some stage it occurred to Fowke, or Cole, or both, that this could be cheaply covered with a glass roof to form further galleries. Cole and Fowke were discussing this idea on 26 April 1859, and it was approved by the board on 7 May. It eventually resulted in the North and South Courts. These were to bring the museum much more space  space on a grander scale than the cramped earlier galleries had provided, and space that invited an elaborate scheme of interior decoration, which John Physick has described in detail.2

The second project that engrossed Cole was the development of the main space of the 1851 commissioners' site, generally known as the 'quadrangle'.3 As we have already seen, the Horticultural Society ('Royal' from December 186o) agreed in 1858 to create a formal garden here, and Cole, Fowke and Redgrave, with the architect Sydney Smirke, had been commissioned by the prince to advance the project. While Cole was away, the society and the commissioners had been negotiating a lease, but little else happened, and Cole now had to supply renewed impetus. In the first week of April 1859 there were repeated meetings of the team, along with Dilke and the prince, and by July 7 the grounds were 'nearly staked out'. A. Nesfield was commissioned to design the garden itself. The project went ahead more or less experimentally, with many meetings to mull over progress. Cole obviously found it frustrating that he was not really in charge. The prince's close interest in the smallest details slowed things up, and provoked Cole to private laments in his diary, such as 'all in confusion & no decisions'. On the other hand, when the prince did make up his mind, he could be exigent: 'wanted everything done at once as usual', grumbled Cole.

Cole's third project was the forthcoming International Exhibition, scheduled for 1861. Cole had energised the Society of Arts into proposing this in 1858, and the society had tried to arouse the interest of the 185 1 commissioners. Little progress had been made, however, in Cole's absence. He managed to secure a meeting with the prince less than a week after his return, on  March. The prince was cautiously supportive, requiring the society to gather expressions of interest from exhibitors, and to set up a guarantee fund, before the commission became further involved. In the face of Cole's enthusiasm, he conceded that he 'was disposed to take Moderate Evidence of the Chances of Success'; but he needed more reassurance. Cole started to bustle around raising funds, but in the spring war broke out between France and Austria, and, although it did not last long, it created sufficient alarm to convince everyone that the exhibition must be postponed until 1862. So this project went off the boil again.

When Cole resumed his seat at his desk in the Department of Science and Art, one problem that awaited him was what to do about science. At a board on 3 1 March, his senior Minister, Lord Salisbury, 'proved very desirous of doing something, & said he wd get rid of the name if nothing were done'. When discussion was renewed on 4 April, Cole recorded that there was no agreement. However, the young John Donnelly produced a scheme for expanding the provision of scientific education, which was adopted on 2 June, and which marked, in Donnelly's view 'a new era . . . in the working of this branch'.6 Lord Salisbury had no chance to see the results of the new policy, for Derby's short-lived Conservative government fell after a parliamentary defeat on 3 1 March. Palmerston formed a second administration on 18 June — but only after the premiership had been offered to Lord Granville who, though a favourite with the Queen, did not yet have the political weight to unite the various interests in the Liberal party. Palmerston gave back to Granville his previous post of president of the council, so once again he became Cole's minister.

His vice-president was Robert Lowe. Cole had briefly served under him when the Department of Science and Art was still part of the Board of Trade in 18 5 5, and when Lowe was beginning his ministerial career as vice-president of the board. When Palmerston returned to power in 1859, Lowe hoped for promotion to the cabinet, but had to be content with the vice-presidency of the Board of Education at the Privy Council. Though this was 'a subordinate office in a hard working and non-glamorous branch of the administration', it suited him perfectly, in the opinion of his biographer, since Lord Granville was busy as leader of the Liberals in the Lords and left the running of the department to Lowe; and Lowe 'discovered that the permanent officials he would be associated with in the Privy Council were the same kind of "true votaries" he had worked with so harmoniously at the Board of Trade'.7 Cole was a man of the same mould as Lowe. After his first board meeting with Lowe on 23 June he judged him to be 'very intelligent indeed'. Redgrave, too, was impressed: 'Mr. Lowe differs greatly from our late sub-chief, Mr. Adderley; Mr. Lowe is very shrewd, sees the real point at once, and decides on principle and not on detail.

As Granville noted, however, 'Bob Lowe . . . is disliked by many in the House'.9 Indeed, he was very widely disliked, on account of his uncompromising opinions, his combative assertion of them, and his refusal to suffer fools gladly. In this, he was not unlike Cole, though even more abrasive. Cole clearly admired him. He thought him 'very genial and witty', and noted down his droll phrases, such as a 'brogue strong enough to hang a hat on' or 'nonsense enough to fill a cart'. He relished Lowe's snubs, as when Lowe returned a paper on scientific education by Donnelly with the remark that he did so 'as the reprieved Convict did the Prayer book to the Chaplain with thanks having no further use for it'. No doubt, when Lowe told Granville that 'Cole is in an economical mood' and had cut his budget by £ 5oo, he smirked colludingly as Lowe continued: 'but I put it on again & the Treasury passed it. I gave Cole a blowing up and told [him] I wd not reduce the Estimates because the Treasury reduced Taxation'. 10 He must surely have relished that harsh quality in Lowe, which probably both Lowe and Cole thought of as clear-sighted intellectual honesty, but which was always apt to veer towards self-righteousness. As, for instance, when Lowe said: 'The Problem of Education was simply how to give the children of the Labouring Poor under I1 years of age such Education [as] shall enable them to walk in that path of life into wh: it shall please God to call them. Let us clear our Minds of all Cant, as Dr Johnson said.'11 Lowe and Cole probably excited the more high-handed tendencies in each other - which was stimulating for them while they had the same aims in view, but eventually proved explosive when they disagreed.

Cole's private life in 18 59 seems to have included more time for relaxation than he had allowed himself before his holiday. The year 186o was even more relaxed than 18 59. The family spent July and August at Shere. From 25 August until 6 September, Cole made a tour in Normandy with Harry and Alan: they kept diaries and made sketches, and, binding these up afterwards, gave the resulting volume to Marian on her 49th birthday on 17 October 186o.20 The trip aroused memories for Cole of a previous trip 'when I was about 18', and he found much changed. There were some things to complain about. The 'stink of stables and drains' caused him to get up in the night when staying in Dieppe, and apply 'a remedy with the Cologne bottle'. At Le Havre he disapproved of the 'absurd and indelicate' shorts that French men wore for bathing; Alan commented, 'It disgusted Papa - who you know prides himself on his plunging'. The party conscientiously visited historic monuments, recording such judgements as 'a fine specimen of massive Romanesque work' (of the Abbaye aux Hommes at Caen). They looked at the Bayeux Tapestry, and Harry noted that 'the whole is a good type of the uneducated state of the minds of females about I,ooo years ago'; no doubt he was here endorsing, without quite getting the tone right, his father's progressive perspective on the woman question.

Not long after the Normandy excursion, Marian and Hennie accompanied Cole on a trip to Scotland, staying for a while with Cole's friend Robert Napier, the marine engineer, at West Shandon. Here, on 18 and 2 1 September, Cole made some etchings. He had learned this craft earlier in the year at Shere from Seymour Haden, an accomplished practitioner. On 28 July he tried to etch a view of the cottage, but made a mess of the perspective. On 8 August Haden revised the etching and printed proofs (plate 39). Cole and Haden had another common interest. On Sunday 29 July they rambled round the Surrey countryside looking for sites on which they could build houses, a project that was to absorb them in 186o. For Cole there was again much singing in the family and drilling with the Volunteers. Cole seems to have had enough mental leisure to reflect on his achievements. Stimulated, perhaps, by what he called a 'Sortation of Papers &c.' on I5 January, he soon 'Commenced Catalogue of my writings', which occupied him during several evenings in March.

On the official side of life, the Horticultural Society's garden and the 1862 exhibition continued to loom, but neither project had urgency at this stage. If, then, action was in abeyance, the year 186o gave Cole several opportunities for utterances of policy. He testified before three parliamentary select committees: that on public institutions in March, and those on the British Museum and on the South Kensington Museum in July.

The purpose of the committee on public institutions was (to quote its full title) to "inquire whether it is in the Power of Parliament to provide, or of this House to recommend, further Facilities for promoting the Healthful Recreation and Improvement of the People, by placing Institutions supported by General Taxation within the reach of the largest Section of the Tax-payers, at Hours on Week Days when, by the ordinary customs of Trade, such persons are free from toil'. In other words, how to make museums more accessible to the working classes. Cole was the first witness to take the stand, on 1 March 186o, and had an easy ride, for it was acknowledged that the South Kensington Museum was in the lead in the accessibility stakes. All he had to do was to assert, with characteristic ebullience, that I think that at South Kensington we have got nearly to the extreme of public convenience', and describe the crowds of working people who visited the museum when it was open (as other museums were not) in the evenings.

He was able to reassure the committee that providing alcoholic refreshment for visitors was quite safe. The committee on the British Museum was moved by William Gregory, MP, who later recalled that 'the main point of the enquiry was whether the natural history collections were to be removed elsewhere'. This was under consideration because the museum was so pressed for space. Gregory's committee was 'well received by the House, and with acclaim by the heads of departments in the Museum, with the exception of Mr. Panizzi, who could not bear any interference with this great establishment over which he ruled with absolute authority'. It eventually reported that the museum should expand on its present site, 'much to the annoyance of the Government, who were anxious to have Prince Albert's Kensington scheme carried out'  and thus supported the proposal to transfer the natural history collections to South Kensington — and to that of Panizzi, who was bent on getting rid of the scientific men, with whom he had no sympathy  of whom, indeed, he had expressed himself with sovereign contempt'.23 The committee's recommendations, vigorous in first draft, were watered down so as to become, in the words of Henry Layard, 'a positive insult to the common sense of the House of Commons and the public',24 and did not make much difference in the long run.

For Cole, testifying to the committee presented an opportunity for empire-building. He wanted to take over the British Museum's medieval holdings (assembled quite recently by Augustus Wollaston Franks), and had an agreement that he could do so with Panizzi, the director, who had no more sympathy with medieval antiquities than with natural history.25 When asked whether he wanted to establish a general collection of medieval art' at South Kensington, Cole bumptiously answered: 'If Parliament thought that Kensington would be a good situation for that purpose, having already a very large collection, it would not be difficult to add the little collection in the British Museum'.26 But although this was much talked about, it never happened.

For Cole the most important of the three committees was that on his own museum, which the department had agreed to as a means of countering parliamentary criticism. For Parliament was not sympathetic to artistic institutions. A philippic in the Art-Journal, urging the government to act with 'bold and generous hand' in order to 'raise the Arts to that high position which the tastes of the people and the exigencies of the nation now imperatively demand', attacked the obstructive attitude of Members of the Commons.

Of late . . . we regret to say, it has been made specially difficult for any paid official, however skilled, zealous and honest, to serve his country in the public promotion of the Arts. Whoever may have received parliamentary money is at once subjected to unmeasured and unscrupulous attack. Idle gossip and stale jokes are carefully compiled and elaborated for the amusement of the House.

No doubt Cole recognised himself in this portrayal of the persecuted arts administrator who 'is made the butt for party shots, and oft-times arraigned before the country as a culprit'. The writer thundered on:

There is scarcely an Art institution in the country which has not suffered repeated and inveterate attack from the pretended patriotism of so-called independent members . . . The modest estimates of the Government are thus often carried with difficulty, and its circumscribed plans and scanty generosity curtailed, and sometimes wholly defeated.

It was in such an atmosphere that Robert Lowe (who had aggressively remarked to Cole that 'if he was to have the trouble of a Committee he wd not "Spare the Philistines"') moved for the select committee on 19 June, to the accompaniment of the usual parliamentary noises. One MP grumbled that 'there was a tendency to unlimited expense at Brompton', while another urged that 'the question for the Committee to determine' should be 'whether the South Kensington Museum ought not to be done away with altogether'.27 The committee met for eight sessions in July.

The museum was under attack, so Cole retaliated. Redgrave recalled him putting up a characteristic performance under cross-examination. ´'He certainly loves strife, he courts a "spar," and always makes a good stand-up fight. Some of his hits have been most clever.'28 Cole was on the stand for two and a half days. In fact, much of what he said was simple description of the museum. After dealing with its administration, he reviewed each of its sections: the architectural museum, the educational collection, the animal products collection. In respect of the latter, Cole enlivened his testimony by showing specimens and discoursing learnedly on wool production and isinglass. He went on to speak of the food museum ('I should say that after the pictures, which is the most popular collection, the food collection is the next most popular') and the models and patents collection. It is important to remember that, only three years after its opening, the museum was (notwithstanding the rapid growth of its art galleries) still visibly a general educational museum covering a wide range of artistic and scientific subjects. Consequently, it had to be defended as such. But when the question was raised whether it had achieved enough success to give value for money (the committee carefully added up exactly what it had cost the nation), the ground shifted somewhat. The question then focussed on the ornamental art collections. Had they improved the quality of British manufactures?

A rather small group of witnesses from the manufacturing sector were rounded up to testify: three from Sheffield, one from Birmingham, and Colin Minton Campbell from Stoke-on-Trent. So far as they could, they spoke in South Kensington's favour. It was easier for them to praise the effect of their local art schools (which were supported, of course, by South Kensington) than to vindicate the usefulness of the distant South Kensington Museum. They tended to take the line that the museum directly affected public taste, and this subsequently affected manufacturers. As Colin Minton Campbell said, 'the public taste is improved, owing to the opportunities afforded of frequenting the South Kensington Museum, and also to the facilities for going abroad and visiting various collections, which obliges us to do different works which otherwise we would not have been induced to do'. Cole's life speeded up in 1861. In the domestic sphere, he and Haden were constantly involved in the building of their new country houses on adjoining sites that they had found at Witley, in Surrey. On 29 January, Cole noted that 'Harry began the plans of Witley House'. It was entirely characteristic of Cole not to commission designs from a professional architect, but to try to do it himself, or at least to keep it in the family. His son Harry would in the future train in the Royal Engineers and be involved in the design and construction of several buildings, including the National Training School for Music in South Kensington. But at this stage in his life Harry, however talented, could hardly have been capable of designing a house professionally, and much of the design work must have been done in the office of the contractor, John Kelk. Already Kelk had worked at South Kensington on the conversion of Brompton Park House, and on constructing the Sheepshanks and other galleries, and he was to be the contractor for the 1862 exhibition building. He and Cole were therefore on friendly terms. Cole was constantly visiting the site through the year as the house went up, and there was a topping-out ceremony on 19 October, with a feast in the local schoolroom. Haden was, already on I 2 June, regretting that the cost of the houses was 'rather beyond him', and he seems not to have gone ahead with his, Kelk building it as a speculation. Cole's house still exists: partly tile-hung in deference to the local vernacular style of architecture, it is basically of red brick with a picturesque irregular, gabled outline and tall chimneys.32

The year 1 86 1 was a significant one in the history of English education, since it was then that 'payment by results' was established in elementary education.33 The chief movers of this change were Robert Lowe, and R. R. W. Lingen, secretary of the Education Department since 1849 (when, on the retirement of James Kay-Shuttleworth, he vaulted into the position over the assistant secretary, Harry Chester, an old crony of Cole's at the Society of Arts). Cole, however, played an important role in the background. Payment by results (i.e. making grants to schools proportionate to the success their pupils achieved in tests in reading, writing and arithmetic) has usually been deplored by educational historians and theorists. Matthew Arnold called it 'the heaviest blow dealt at civilization and social improvement in my time'.34 It is said to have reduced learning to a soul-destroying and mechanical process, to have degraded teachers from professionals into piece-workers, and to have turned the inspectorate into inquisitors. Nowadays, payment by results is enjoying a revival in esteem, and these judgements may seem harsh. Certainly, there is little reason to suppose that elementary education before the introduction of payment by results was enjoying a golden age.35

A second reason for introducing payment by results was to raise standards. The third reason for adopting a new system was not openly admitted, but is generally accepted as a potent factor in the thinking of all concerned. Most of the elementary schools in England, while supported by government grants, were actually run by 'voluntary bodies' associated with the churches - by the National Society representing the Anglican Church, and by the British and Foreign Society representing the dissenting sects. The two societies were at loggerheads with each other, but would not countenance education falling into the hands of anyone else, since they believed religious indoctrination to be the most important part of their work. State control of education, which naturally commended itself to politicians and civil servants, was regarded by the voluntary societies as opening the floodgates to a raging torrent of secularism. Earlier attempts to increase state control had been unsuccessful, though one advance in the war of attrition with the religious bodies was the introduction of scientific secondary education through Cole's department. The support of the Prince Consort for this greatly helped. Granville lamented that 'the present state of religious feeling' inhibited educational change, and praised Prince Albert's advocacy of scientific education as 'a godsend to the Government'.39 Cole knew very well that his department constituted the advance troops in the battle for secular education (though personally he was not irreligious, attending church regularly on Sundays, and conducting household prayers).40 It is interesting to overhear him and T. H. Huxley chatting in summer 186 1 in the midst of the preparations for payment by results: Huxley (who taught science in Cole's department) remarked that their efforts to improve scientific education 'were the most important ever taken against Parsonic influence'.41 Lowe, and behind him Granville, had to be careful in their public statements in the payment-by-results controversy to defer to the churches,42 for it would have been 'political suicide' to do otherwise,43 but there is no doubt that they too wished to reduce 'Parsonic influence'.

Religious opposition was still too strong to allow the government to take the risk of legislation. The cabinet were relying on Lowe to do something energetic, but agreed with him that an educational reform bill was impractical. It was decided that changes must be made by altering the internal rules of the Education Department, which could be done without reference to Parliament. Thus what emerged was not a bill but a 'Revised Code'. To many this stratagem seemed evasive, especially as the code was announced in a departmental minute on the day that Parliament was prorogued for the summer recess, after which the responsible ministers quickly went on holiday.44

The new system thus took the world by surprise, and was thought to be a wild move. A colleague of Granville's commented that 'people in general seem to think that you and Lowe rushed out, like Irishmen at a fair, to have a blow at something or somebody'.45 Historians, on the other hand, have found precedents: the idea had been aired before the Newcastle Commission,46 and might even be traced back to the first secretary of the Education Department, Kay-Shuttleworth.47 What Lowe needed, however, was not theoretical precedents but practical advice on how to proceed, and this Cole was able to provide, for his department had been operating a system of payment by results for a decade in its art schools. The system was mitigated by annual bonuses, which teachers could obtain as a result of possessing the various certificates of competence issued by the department, but their main earnings were controlled by results. When Donnelly set up the department's science schools (which expanded from 5 in 1859 t0 38 schools or classes with 1,330 pupils in 1861, reaching 799 schools with 34,283 pupils in 1870), he adopted a similar system for paying science teachers.48 So Lowe consulted Cole on Thursday 9 May: "asked me to prepare a Scheme for paying for results in writing, reading & Arithmetic'. The next day Cole talked it through with Donnelly, and on Sunday he wrote a brief on the subject for Granville, countering criticism from Kay-Shuttleworth.49 On Friday 17 May, Lowe discussed Cole's ideas with him 'and seemed disposed to try the experiment'. The matter that chiefly pre-occupied Cole in 1861 was the forthcoming International Exhibition. By the end of 1859 it had been rescheduled for 1862, and in 186o a guarantee fund was set up with five trustees. Cole urged the 185 1 commissioners to support his plan for decennial exhibitions (which he had pushed through the Society of Arts) by dedicating a permanent exhibition site at South Kensington. While willing to support the 1862 exhibition, the commissioners did not want to make a longer-term commitment, and there seemed every prospect of a row between the commissioners and the Society of Arts. Cole was so angry that, in a letter of 2 1 May 186o to the prince's secretary, General Grey, he denounced the 185 1 commissioners as 'traitors'.55 In the end, the trustees were turned into a new royal commission for the exhibition of 1862.

One of the matters in which Cole was most active was the exhibition building.58 This was intended to fill the remaining empty portion of the commissioners' 'quadrangle', south of the Royal Horticultural Society's gardens. The commissioners seem not to have been altogether happy with what might have been regarded as a piece of cronyism, and were conscious that Fowke was not a professional architect but a military engineer. In the years since his sick leave, then, Cole had, while apparently keeping in the background, presided over the expansion of the museum, pushed hard for the development of the rest of the South Kensington site, influenced the momentous introduction of payment by results into the English education system, and swept everybody before him into the 1862 exhibition.

## Consolidating the Stronghold 1863-1867

If Cole's impact on the world around him in 1861-2 had been forceful, even painful for others, he was no less hard in his dealings in the years immediately following. As he turned back to the museum after the 1862 exhibition, he found himself facing the fiercest and most damaging revolt that had yet afflicted his kingdom, in the disaffection of J. C. Robinson.1 Robinson was 1 5 years younger than Cole, and, when Cole plucked him from obscurity to become curator of the museum, had virtually no qualifications for this post except an intense enthusiasm. He learned quickly, but the opportunities to do so were provided by Cole. The development of the museum as a historical collection of decorative art was Cole's policy, and the implementation of it by buying in bulk from existing collections - Bandinel, Bernal, Gigli-Campana, Soulages  was entirely a consequence of Cole's buccaneering approach. Robinson, for his part, took ful advantage of what proved to be for him a unique crash course in the connoisseurship of medieval and Renaissance art. His position in the museum brought him advantages, but soon his experience and knowledge proved a great asset to the museum. Cole backed him when in 1857 he started a 'Collectors' Conversazione', to create a network of private collectors, and was at his side at the early meetings. These meetings grew into the Collectors Club, later the Fine Arts Club, which remained a power-base for Robinson.2 As he gained experience and knowledge, the museum sent him on purchasing trips abroad: to Lyon in summer 1854, Paris in summer 1856, Dresden and Vienna in summer 1857, Rome and Florence in summer 1859, southern Italy in winter 186o and Aix-la-Chapelle and Florence in 1862. He was indefatigable in his search for works of art. Redgrave, when travelling with him, found antique shops 'unendurably tedious',3 but Robinson loved the chase. In describing his purchasing expeditions to Cole in 1858 he spoke of 'ransacking' towns, and he also used the word, almost 2o years later, to his occasional travelling companion, Sir William Gregory, MP.4 In 1859, in a letter from Italy to Cole that remarked in passing that 'the Austrians are ravaging the country without mercy', he wrote of his own purchasing feats: 'Florence has not had such a raking out as this within the memory of man' His collecting urge had a predatory edge.

As we have seen, he came to feel that his contribution to South Kensington deserved greater recognition than he was receiving. Cole was paid more than twice as much as Robinson. Cole, however, had a much wider range and heavier weight of responsibility than Robinson. The rational side of Robinson's mind could always recognise Cole's outstanding qualities as an administrator, but in Robinson's scale of values collecting and connoisseurship were far more important than administration, and he knew that in these fields he was far ahead of Cole. When Cole took a hand in purchasing, as he often did, Robinson could be roused to fury. A bitter moment came in 186 1. Robinson was authorised to go to Italy to study Renaissance sculpture and 'to inspect Mediaeval Exhibition at Florence'.6 But the funds for this were withheld from him, and instead Cole went to the International Exhibition in Florence, authorised to buy exhibits for the food, animal products, structural and educational divisions of the museum, as well as examples of contemporary design. This offended Robinson's sense of priorities, and he thought that Cole had bought 'a great number of objects of inferior nature'.7

Still, while Cole was busy with the 1862 exhibition, Robinson was given free rein to set up what proved to be almost a rival attraction. Fowke's new 'glazed court' in the museum was nearly finished. It comprised two courts, in fact. The North Court opened in April 1862, and Cole had worked with Robinson on laying out the exhibits over Christmas 1861. The South Court took longer to finish, with its elaborate decorative scheme. It was decided that it would open in 1862 with a loan exhibition of works of medieval and Renaissance decorative art from British private collections, organised by Robinson with help from his contacts in the Fine Arts Club. The exhibition, which showed over 9,o0o objects from 553 lenders,8 was a stunning demonstration of 'The Art Wealth of England' (this phrase was used as the title of a portfolio of photographs issued to accompany the exhibition). But the organisation of it left something to be desired. Exhibits were still being unpacked and arranged a month after the opening; the publication of most of the catalogue was considerably delayed; and the arrangement of exhibits on the ground was confusing, there being "an utter absence of anything like chronological order'.9

She calmed down eventually, and became capable of registering again what was happening in the world. In Cole's official life, the question of payment by results cropped up again.15 The revised code, on which Cole had advised Lowe and Lingen, applied only to elementary schools supported by grants from the Education Department. It might have seemed that it would have no effect on the Department of Science and Art, which prided itself on already having a system of payment by results. In his annual report for 186o, indeed, Cole gave statistics showing how his department had applied payment by results over the previous five years, and, with a hint of complacency, noted: This statement may be useful and interesting to those who advocate the applicability of the system of paying on results to general education.'16 Lowe and Lingen, however, having removed direct government subsidy from the elementary sector, were now on the lookout for further anomalies. Their eye fell on various practices in the Science and Art Department that needed reform.

The 'revolt of the masters' was headed by John Sparkes, headmaster at the Lambeth School of Art, who founded the Association of Art Masters to co-ordinate opposition to the department. Great resentment was felt on the part of provincial masters and management committees towards the bureaucracy in South Kensington. A statement from the Paisley School of Art asserted that, 'while the masters' salaries have been reduced' and would be further reduced by the new "uncertain system, styled payments on results', the number of officials in the central department had been increased, 'and their salaries enormously augmented'.23 A letter in the Manchester Guardian analysed the finances of the department and pointed out that from a budget of £68,401 16s. 2d. for art education the total expenditure on provincial schools was only £11,859 7s. $7 d .$ , leaving all the rest to be spent on South Kensington. 'Is it not monstrous that more than five-sixths of the entire grant should be consumed in London?'24 It was not only to the London art schools that this money was directed, but also to the South Kensington Museum, for in his accounts Cole did not distinguish the museum from the schools. 'Mr. Cole's policy has hitherto been so to mix it up with the general system of education  to make it, in fact, part and parcel of the schools - as to render it difficult or impossible to separate one from the other when considering the money spent on both.'25 The money spent on buying exhibits for the museum was disguised under the head 'Examples, diagrams of science and art, &c.'. This aroused such furious comments as this, from Walter Smith, headmaster of the Leeds school:

Gorgeous courts arise at South Kensington, Venetian glass and majolica plates are purchased at fancy prices, whilst provincial Schools of Art, in important centres of manufactures, are crippled and curtailed; the Schools of Art and Schools of Design, throughout England, are to be made mere elementary drawing classes, in order that South Kensington may have a public curiosity shop.26

The fact that the museum had just completed a set of official residences, described as 'rooms for officers' in the accounts but 'construed to mean mansions for officers'27 by some vigilant critics, rubbed salt in the wound. And for many the problem was not just that South Kensington was an 'Augaean stable of jobbery and corruption, against whose doings the heads of nearly all the provincial and other schools are now loudly protesting'.28 The problem was Henry Cole himself, who had made South Kensington 'a snug place for one man to fill with his personal friends and nominees, however unfit they may be for the work given them to do'.29

The provincial schools eventually, on 24 February 1864, organised a deputation to see Lord Granville, with many MPs in tow and Beresford-Hope as chief spokesman. Granville stonewalled against their criticisms, and hinted that the matter should be turned over to a select committee. On 8 March Sir Stafford Northcote successfully moved for one to be set up, and it took evidence during eleven sessions in April and May. As well he might, Cole seems to have been nervous of appearing before it, but Lowe (who was a member of the committee) said encouragingly, 'you gave excellent evidence before & will do so again'. Cole had the advantage that he and Northcote were old friends, and they conversed and corresponded about how the evidence should be presented.30

The committee saw 21 witnesses, mainly art school masters and industrialists. Cole and Redgrave appeared on the stand together to answer for the department. In 246 pages of evidence and a further 214 pages of documents in an appendix (including a sort of census of the art schools), the committee's report provided a detailed record of the working of the art education system. Buried in the evidence, at paragraph 4,3 13, is one of Cole's neatest mission statements. When asked to state the 'leading principles of your Department', he replied: 'To improve the taste and artknowledge of all classes of the community, having especial reference to the influence of that taste and knowledge upon the manufactures of this country.' That he chose this phrasing  stressing that the department aimed directly to influence taste, and thereby indirectly to improve manufactures - reveals, perhaps, that he knew that he was vulnerable in respect of two matters. One was the question of whether his art education system had actually had any effect on British manufactures. He always stoutly claimed that it had, but the committee remained agnostic, merely commentin that it had been 'struck with the conflict of opinion which prevails respectig the proper functions, and the actual value'of the art schools. The other problematic matter was the question whether the South Kensington Museum (which influenced taste)

had been privileged at the expense of the schools (which might be thought to influence manufactures). The committe concluded that 'the Museum is undoubtedly to be regarded as an instrument of Art Education, and its cost must be reckoned among the items  the Natinal Grants or the promotion that eucation' but it id not allow Cole to get away with his shifty accounting procedures. It recommended that 'a strict line is drawn between the sums voted for the purchase of works of Art, which are for the most part to be deposited at South Kensington, and those voted for the direct encouragement of the provincial schools', and the department in due course complied.

Cole's often repeated insistence that the schools would pay for themselves was dismissed by the committee: it was their 'opinion that it is improbable that the schools will ever become self-supporting upon the present system; nor do they think that the substitution of payment by results . . . will tend to make them so'. Furthermore, the committee concluded 'that the system of payment by results, introduced by the recent Minutes, is not well adapted to Schools of Art', and recommended a capitation system. Cole was, therefore, bruised in this enquiry. On the crucial question, however — whether there had been a 'breach of faith' with the art masters — the committee found in his favour.31 It concluded that there had not been a contract with the masters, and that the government was entitled to change its methods of administration in response to changing circumstances. Once again, then, Cole came out of a fight as winner, even if somewhat bloodied.

The department issued a reply to the committee, in which it more or less announced that it would go on as before,32 and Cole coasted along relatively smoothly in the years 1864-6. He had now achieved a position of enough eminence to pass from one meeting to another, taking decisions and launching projects, in the comfortable knowledge that well-trained subordinates and allies would take on the hard work of implementation.

Cole's other memorial project was for what became the Royal Albert Hall. The idea of having a great concert hall on the South Kensington site was there from the beginning, in the prince's first notions. As preparations were made for the 1862 exhibition, it seemed likely that the concert hall would be realised as part of the exhibition building, but rising costs caused the abandonment of the great hall, as an economy measure. The story of its development has been well told elsewhere.73 Two strands of narrative have to be interwoven, the architectural and the financial. In both Cole was a leading actor. When the hall was more or less finished (on 20 January 1872), Cole was shown rough designs he had made for it in November 1863,74 and was pleased to find that they were 'curiously like what is now built'. Between 1863 and 1872, however, the history of European architecture was sifted for ideas and precedents. As a building type, the concert hall was comparatively undeveloped (though, as Marcus

Binney points out, London was 'much better served with large halls than any city on the Continent'), so there was plenty of scope for bright ideas. An amphitheatre had been Cole's first conception,75 but in 1862 several architects provided designs for a rectangular hall, while Matthew Digby Wyatt offered a circular one, in both Gothic and classical styles. Sir Gilbert Scott took Santa Sophia in Constantinople as a model for a domed hall, which he offered in Byzantine or Gothic styles (or both: 'early Gothic with a tinge of Byzantine'). Travelling abroad in 1863, Cole was impressed by the Spanish Hall in the Hradschin Palace in Prague. In 1864 the amphitheatre at Arles was suggested as a precedent, in 1865 that at Nimes. A Venetian treatment was discussed in 1865, and an exterior elevation combining the Colosseum and Bramante. In 1866 Greek influence jostled with the influence of the greater churches of East Anglia, while Cole was advancing the claims of the amphitheatre at Pula in Istria. Several tours were made by those involved in the design to study foreign precedents. The genesis of the Royal Albert Hall is a prime case of style-mongering, which could only have taken place in the mid-Victorian period, when rampant historicism and the rapid spread of railways supported such an approach to architecture.

What made the difference, however, was the involvement, from first to last, of Cole and his South Kensington design office, which managed to keep control of the project, and hold at bay other architects. Fowke made the first plans. Despite his sudden death in December 1865, his spirit lived on in his successor, Colonel Scott; and the Albert Hall, as realised, stands as the finest and largest monument to the South Kensington Renaissance Revival style, in brick and terracotta.

Le Play had applied Gallic logic to the layout of the exhibition building. As one commentator said, 'the great Paris Exhibition building is like an oval pie-dish, which the nations of the world have been invited to fill, each occupying a slice without prejudice to its neighbours'.89 The building was, in fact, a flattened oval, with two flat sides, and semicircular ends. Logically it should have been a circle, for the 'slice' system to work properly. It consisted of seven concentric rings of iron and glass halls. Each ring was devoted to a different kind of exhibit. The very central part was a garden. Then, in the smallest ring, came the history of labour, an innovation. Fine art came in the next ring, then the decorative arts. In the fourth ring from the middle came household equipment and furniture; then in the next ring textiles, jewellery, weapons and other manufactures. By far the biggest gallery was devoted to Machinery; and finally the outermost ring was devoted to food, with restaurants that spilled out into the surrounding park. If you followed any ring right round, you could have a comparative view of the entire world's production of, say, furniture; while if you crossed from circumference to centre, you could study the whole culture of a single nation. This was the theory, but of course, as Cole said, it was a 'fallacious theory, that every nation would exhibit an equal proportion of objects in each group'. 90 Often there was not room within a country's 'slice' for all its exhibits, and overflow objects were widely scattered, with 'no clue whatever by which to find them'.91 Not only did Cole consider the French attempt to impose an intellectual structure on the exhibition unsuccessful but also, as a seasoned exhibition designer, he disliked the shape of the building, being perturbed by the fact that 'the whole of the space assigned to Great Britain consisted of zones on the curve'. The effect of this 'was to prevent any lengthened views, to interfere with the arrangement of pictures, to prevent symmetrical laying out without great sacrifice of space, and to render the arrangements for working the machinery difficult and costly'.92

Another professional matter that elicited criticism from Cole was the jury system. He had argued after the Paris 1855 exhibition that juries were pointless, and now returned to the theme again, arguing that 'the jury judgment merely follows and confirms public opinion' — indeed, 'is often absolutely in arrear [sic] of public opinion, and even unjust'. Furthermore, it was impossible to assemble jurors with enough combined expertise to provide authoritative judgement on such a vast range of products as was always gathered at an international exhibition, from 'the make of a lady's corsets . . . to . . . the merits of a discovery in electro-magnetism'.93

Another was triumphantly clinched at the French International Exhibition, taking advantage of the presence in Paris of many of the crowned heads of Europe. This was a scheme for the exchange of reproductions of works of art.

Cole believed in the usefulness, for museums and art education, of reproductions, alongside genuine works of art. Reproductions in three dimensions, such as plaster casts and electrotypes, and in two dimensions, such as photographs, lithographs and etchings, were acquired by the museum and circulated to the art schools. To be able to study reproductions was especially helpful when the originals were far away from London. Getting hold of reproductions usually meant diplomatic negotiations, followed up with expeditions by craftsmen. One of the museum's biggest campaigns had been in 1864-5, when a cast had been made of the great Portico de la Gloria at Santiago de Compostella. 'The casting of this immense structure was an operation involving a sea voyage beset by numerous hazards - storms and fumigation against cholera included  as well as protracted and delicate negotiations with the ecclesiastical authorities.'98 It occurred to Cole that such projects would be easier if some kind of international co-operation could be established, and in 1864 he proposed an 'International Exchange of Copies of Works of Fine Art', a proposal that, with Lord Granville's help, was circulated worldwide by the British Foreign Office.

At the Paris exhibition, Cole had made sure that the South Kensington Museum was well publicised. He exhibited not only examples of the decorations used on the buildings, but also reproductions of works of art commissioned by the museum.

These were exhibited in the British section, 'where may be seen specimens of French, Italian, Spanish, Portuguese, German, Swiss, Russian, Hindoo, Celtic, and English art'. It occurred to Cole that he could give his scheme for the exchange of reproductions a boost by getting it ratified by the international celebrities who were in Paris at the time. Having drafted a 'Convention', he rushed round to the British Embassy on 3o May and got it signed, for Britain, by Prince Alfred. The next day, Princess 'Vicky', crown princess of Prussia, promised to obtain the crown prince's signature, which was added on 1 June. Rather embarrassingly the crown prince's signature took up the space intended for that of Prince Napoleon, but he too signed on 24 June. Some 'members of the reigning families throughout Europe' signed, representing Britain, France, Prussia, Hesse, Belgium, Russia, Sweden and Norway, Italy, Austria and Denmark. Each country was supposed to set up a commission to expedite exchanges. It does not appear that the grand plan proceeded much farther than this, but the convention does, at one, amusing, level, illustrate Cole's tirelessly roving eye for the main chance, while, at a more serious level, it surely represents the first formal attempt at international co-operation 'for the benefit of museums of all countries.99

The breadth of Cole's artistic sympathies can be measured by the fact that he was at this time trying to boost the museum's coverage not only of Byzantine art but also of Indian art, especially Indian architecture. Again one asks why he turned to that subject at this moment. Indian decorative art had been highly esteemed at the museum from its earliest days, when it purchased much contemporary Indian material from the Great Exhibition, and at South Kensington this material was exhibited in a gallery specially decorated by Owen Jones in Indian style. But the subject does not seem to have concerned Cole much until the mid-186os. His eyes might, perhaps, have been opened to Indian sculpture and architecture by the published work on Indian architecture of James Fergusson (who had lectured at South Kensington in 1857 and had been manager for a couple of years of the rival Crystal Palace in Sydenham) and particularly by a lecture given by Fergusson at the Society of Arts on 19 December 1866. The hall, splendid as it was, had a struggle to achieve success as a venue for performances. Its situation was a disadvantage, as it still is. Although transport links between South Kensington and elsewhere are not bad, they are not well adapted to the swift assembly and dispersal of large concert audiences. The main problem for the hall sprang, however, from the financial arrangements that Cole made in order to raise enough cash to build it. To attract subscriptions, he sold many seats on 999- year leases. This meant that a concert promoter could never hope to attract a full house. Some I,3oo seats out of the total of 6,ooo were permanently reserved; and these included many of the best seats. The problem of the 'seat-holders', though mitigated by various measures in later years, haunted the hall. Its management was regularised soon after the opening. Hitherto run by a provisional committee, it was given a new constitution on 25 May 1872. The seat-holders now formed a corporation, while management was in the hands of an 18-man council. The honorary secretary of the council was John Donnelly, but paid help was brought in, in the shape of Henry Cole's nephew Wentworth Cole, who ran the hall until his death in 190o.

The year 187 I, as we have seen, was a year of triumph, and Cole was able to note down in his diary many tributes from the great and good. Of course, there were always some who, like the 185 1 commissioner Edward Thring, disparaged 'Cole's Great Touting Schemes'. After delivering a hymn of praise to South Kensington, his anger against Lowe seems to have run away with him. The various accounts of his speech in the press143 suggest that he was spluttering almost incoherently as (disclaiming any political animus  'I am speaking only of science and art. I have nothing to do with politics') he excoriated Lowe as 'a wilful man, with a very bad, extremely bad judgment  no statesman whatever, but a sort of milk-and-water Rabelais' (meaning that he told feeble dirty jokes).14 Lowe, claimed Cole, had 'dealt with science and art in a miserably parsimonious way', when Chancellor of the Exchequer, and 'had no competency whatever to pass any judgement' on these subjects.

Then, reaching the nub of the matter, Cole denounced Lowe for trying 'to put his foot upon South Kensington Museum by proposing that it should be under the management of a firm of fifty persons. Could any of those merchants present fancy their business conducted by fifty persons?' The 5o persons were the trustees of the British Museum, and Cole now proceeded to work his audience by naming them individually: the Archbishop of Canterbury, the Lord Chancellor, and so on down to the 1st lord of the Admiralty, who was actually beside him on the platform. One newspaper reported that 'as Mr. Cole mentioned these names they were nearly all cheered, more especially the first and the last named'. 'Once or twice the rev. chairman was obliged to call Mr. Cole to order. Altogether the scene was hardly a dignified one.'

The speech was widely reported, and Cole was generally regarded as having gone too far in his remarks on Lowe. He made a public apology in a speech at the Kidderminster School of Art on 23 January 1874. It was a cunningly limited apology. His remarks, he said, 'have caused regret to some of my best friends; and I must have been wrong in making them, and regret having done so'. He did not, however, apologise directly to Lowe. He admitted that while Lowe was his minister, 'he had shown me uniform kindness, had taken a cordial interest in the working of the institution, and had on all occasions been its champion', but he did not recant his view that now Lowe had become the 'opponent' of South Kensington, 'and seriously endangered the . . . museum for which I would lay down my life'. 145

A more sober speech, and one of Cole's best, was that 'On the national importance of local museums of science and art', delivered on 21 January 1874 at Birmingham Town Hal.146 By now Cole probably knew that the threat was receding from South Kensington, so he made only very brief reference to it. The reflective speech (while digressing on to the standardisation of army buttons, the pollution of rivers, and the punctuality of trains) was principally an argument for state support for education and museums. Cole now urged that state intervention was no longer to be regarded as a bugbear. 'As popular government extends, so do the demands for the assisance of centralised action increase.' The speech expressed quite visionary ambitions for a national museum service.
