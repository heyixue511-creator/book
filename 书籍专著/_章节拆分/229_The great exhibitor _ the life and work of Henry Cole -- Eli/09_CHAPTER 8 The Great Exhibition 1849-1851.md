## CHAPTER 8 The Great Exhibition 1849-1851

The Great Exhibition opened and closed with the singing of Handel's 'Hallelujah Chorus' Sometimes it has been interpreted as if it were itself a sort of Hallelujah Chorus  a grand, harmonious outburst of praise, celebrating the stable values of Victorian Britain. Recent accounts, however, have stressed that the exhibition, like Victorian Britain, was 'a site of contention, a source of conflict and debate', ' and have criticised previous accounts as having 'little appreciation of the complexity of the question'.2 Complexity and contention would have been recognised all too easily by those who were actually involved in setting up the exhibition. Cole, for one, was not loath to explain all the difficulties he had gone through. He left two accounts. One was in his introduction to the general Catalogue of the exhibition. In supplying this official account he was instructed by Prince Albert that 'accuracy is . . . the first essential' and that 'there should be total absence of anything like "puffing".3 So, with due sobriety, he explained the problems that had to be surmounted. First, from small beginnings in the Society of Arts, the project had to be built up into an international event, under the patronage of Prince Albert. Then the government had to be won round, money had to be raised, and support mobilised throughout the country. Third, a site had to be secured and a building erected at high speed. Finally, the exhibits had to be assembled and put on show. In the 1oo pages that Cole devoted to the exhibition in Fifty Years, his second account of it, he took pains to emphasise that at each of these important stages he played a decisive part. It would be wise to bear in mind a reviewer's judgement that Cole's account 'is not in all respects accurate, and he, unconsciously no doubt, has somewhat overlooked the services of his coadjutors' (the review was in the Athenaeum, so was probably well-informed), but on the whole the evidence bears him out.4

This chapter does not give another conventional account of the exhibition, but a Cole's-eye-view. Lest this should give the impression that preparing for the exhibition was nothing more than one long squabble, it is necessary to remember that the enterprise was an inspiring one, especially for people of the time, who believed so strongly in the value of making and selling things, and even more especially for Cole, for whom the exhibition of art-manufactures had now become a life's mission. Cole manoeuvred tirelessly to bring the scheme to fruition, and to win for himself a dominating position in an enterprise in which it seemed that many more important people outweighed him.

The Society of Arts' exhibitions of British manufactures in 1847, 1848 and 1849 seemed to Cole to have opened a prospect of something more ambitious, so he persuaded the society to send a petition to Parliament. In this he tried to show  for it was he who wrote it  'the progressive success of the annual exhibitions of the Society of Arts, and that they had now become a source of income to the Society, after paying their expenses'. He proposed a similarly self-supporting 'National Exhibition' to the government.

After flying this kite, the society's group of exhibition enthusiasts went to test the wind in Paris, where the latest of the French exhibitions of manufactures was held in the summer of 1849. Leaving for Paris on 29 May, Cole joined Matthew Digby Wyatt, from whom the society had commissioned a report. They stayed in a cheap hotel and Cole remembered that 'we were awakened at midnight by the stifling odour of emptying the cesspools, altogether a novel sensation, which years have not obliterated from my mind'. 6 On 1 June Cole went with Herbert Minton (who was also in Paris) to see Louis Buffet, the French minister of agriculture and commerce. For long Buffet had thought that the French should hold an international exhibition, but had failed to find supporters for such a scheme. In Fifty Years, Cole quotes Buffet's proposals at length, which suggests that he regarded them at the time as a significant contribution to the climate of opinion he was trying to create. Buffet gave the British party tickets to visit the exhibition before it opened.

It was held in a specially built 'Vast parallelogram' of a palace (675ft × 328ft; 206m x 1oom), painted outside to look like stone, while inside 'all the pilasters are papered & painted in a species of graining to imitate chestnut wood, and even the ceiling is covered with the same work. . . . The architecture of the whole is mesquin, although the gigantic scale of the building necessarily elevates the general effect into something of impressiveness'.7 In the course of the next week, the English visitors, meeting at the exhibition or dining together, discussed the possibility of a British international exhibition, and Cole asked everyone he met what they thought of the idea. He came back to London on 7 June, leaving Wyatt to complete his report.

The first public disclosure of the Society of Arts's hopes for a major exhibition of British manufactures in London in 185 1 came on 14 June 1949, when Prince Albert, as president, gave away the prizes at the society. With his permission, Scott Russell, as secretary, mentioned the project in his report. A few days later the prince's equerry, Col. Phipps (plate 3 I), wrote to Cole on the prince's behalf to arrange for further discussions on 3o June. At the meeting on 3o June, next day, attended by Cole, Francis Fuller, Thomas Cubitt and John Scott Russell, these ideas were formally adopted, as recorded in the minutes taken by Russell.9 For the Royal Commission Cole offered to prepare a draft, 'grounded on precedents of other Royal Commissions'. The membership of the commission would include cabinet ministers, and people representing 'the various interests of the community'. Also, significantly, 'for the execution of its details, some of the parties present and who are also Members or are officers of the Society of Arts, and who have been most active in originating and preparing for the execution of this plan should be suggested as members'. As to finance, the prince brought up the difficulties of raising loans or subscriptions, and suggested that the Society of Arts could collect and hold a fund. He thought that £1oo,ooo might be raised. He wanted 'to carry forward this business without delay'.

The following morning, a Sunday, Cole called early on Edwin Chadwick, who had wide experience of Royal Commissions, and by Tuesday he had sent his draft to the palace. As would often happen, others did not meet his promptitude with equal speed. The commission was not set up until January 185o, and at that point a later draft by Cole, based on the Record Commission of 183 1, was used. Cole was already, even at this early stage in 1849, seeing the exhibition as one of a series, as is revealed in the phraseology of his offer to go to Osborne and see the prince, to 'answer the end of promoting the large periodic exhibition'.10 It was about now, in the summer of 1849, that the term 'Great Exhibition' began to be used.

In early July, Cole was busy with the select committee on the Schools of Design, but the exhibition soon displaced other concerns. On Wednesday I 1 July he was asked to call on Henry Labouchere, president of the Board of Trade, to brief him about the Paris exhibition. The following Friday evening he set off with Fuller and Scott Russell for a meeting next day with the prince at the royal retreat, Osborne House, on the Isle of Wight. The prince revealed his provisional list of commission members, with Russell and Fuller included as commissioners and Cole as secretary. Since it was essential in pushing the project forward to secure the support of the government, the prince had also summoned Labouchere, who now joined the meeting. It was unlikely that the government would take action before the imminent end of the parliamentary session, so Labouchere asked the exhibition lobby to set to work to strengthen their case. Cole suggested that during the parliamentary recess the Society of Arts might collect 'more evidence as to the readiness of the great manufacturing and commercial interests to subscribe to and support the undertaking'.11 They could then brief the government when it met again in the autumn. They had lunch with the prince and the equerries, and talked for so long that they missed the boat, but the prince summoned the Fairy to take them to Southampton to catch the train back to London.

Although Cole must have felt that he was on the brink of a great adventure, he was not without worries. On 26 July he was fetched from his office because Marian was ill. She was pregnant and - although she had been well enough a couple of weeks before to launch the Manchester, John Scott Russell's new ship, at Millwall  she was now haemorrhaging. Seymour Haden called in Dr Robert Lee, the well-known obstetrician of St George's Hospital. 'She was delivered in the night, & did not suffer very much'. The next day she was 'quiet & not in pain' and continued to recover from her miscarriage. Nonetheless, it took its toll, and on holiday at Herne Bay in the midle of September she was still easily overtired.12 In midsummer, as she lay at home convalescing, Cole was out and about, campaigning.

In working up support, money was of the essence. The Society of Arts decided that it must find a builder who would put up a building at his own risk, so as to have something settled to encourage businessmen to support the exhibition. Fuller took on the task of finding a contractor, and within a few days (with the help of his fatherin-law George Drew) he persuaded James Munday to come forward. Munday did so 'mainly with a view of assisting his nephew Mr George Munday, as he conceived that this would be a first-rate opportunity of introducing him to the world as a Contractor on a large scale'.13 The building was likely to cost $\scriptstyle \pm _ { 7 5 } , \supset \infty ,$ and in addition the Mundays promised a prize fund of £2o,ooo. They thought that the building would revert to their possession at the close of the exhibition. The contract was made on 23 August, with a clause, which Cole suggested, that if a Royal Commission wanted to do things differently, then the contract could be cancelled, with compensation, before i February 185o. This gamble on the part of the Mundays made a great difference to the way the exhibition could be presented to the public. As Col. Phipps told George Drew in December, the prince had 'no hesitation in acknowledging that it was owing to [their] liberality and public spirit . . . that it became possible for him to bring the scheme of the Exhibition of the Industry of All Nations before the Government and the public, in a shape ensuring the practicability of its execution'.14

To negotiate the contract and plan the exhibition, the society now set up a special executive committee, consisting of Cole, Fuller and Drew, with Digby Wyatt as secretary. Russell could not be a member, as he was the society's paid secretary. At the end of July, the prince, as president of the Society of Arts, officially approached the home secretary, Sir George Grey, for government approval for a Royal Commission, and was assured that the request would receive 'careful and deliberate attention'.15

Various official strings had thus been pulled. Such an ambitious project as a 'Great Exhibition', however, needed wider support than that of officials in London. This is where Cole was able to make a difference, for it was decided that he and Fuller would embark on a nationwide lecture tour, arousing the interest of manufacturers and communicating this to the government. On 1 August, Cole and Fuller saw the prince at Osborne for a third meeting. Here they were equipped with a 'diploma' signed by Col. Phipps, 'by Command', which stated that the holder was authorised by the prince, as president of the Society of Arts, 'to collect opinions and evidence with reference to . . . a great Exhibition . . . to be held in London in the year 1851, in order that His Royal Highness might bring the results of such inquiry before Her Majesty's Government' 16 Prince Albert then departed on holiday. Cole and Fuller, with Scott Russell who arrived too late for the meeting, watched the royal yacht Victoria and Albert steam away down the Solent carrying the Queen and her family on a visit to Ireland, after which they resorted to their holiday home at Balmoral, arriving on 1 5 August. When their tour reached Glasgow, the trio made a diversion to report to the prince at Balmoral, and 'went through all points'20 Cole wrote to tell John Scott Russell, then in Germany, that the prince 'was much pleased with the Success . . . . We intend to get 2ooo names of the best promoters in the next six weeks and the Prince is going to appoint additional missionaries'.21 Balmoral at that time was not the grey forbidding palace that is now so familiar, but its predecessor, a modest whitewashed granite country house that the Queen had begun to rent the year before. The royal family 'live there without any state whatever', recorded Charles Greville, 'like very small gentlefolks, small house, small rooms, small establishment. There are no Soldiers, and the whole guard of the Sovereign and the whole Royal Family is a single Policeman'.22 Cole, no less than Greville, was surprised at the informality of Balmoral, especially when he heard the prince tell a valet to escort my wife' out.23

From Balmoral the 'missionaries' went on to Perth, Dundee, Stirling and back to Glasgow, where a public meeting was 'very unanimous'.24 Then they proceeded to Greenock, where they saw the Provost, and from there crossed to Belfast, travelling south to Dublin. There was a lot of goodwill in Ireland following the royal visit, and the lord mayor of Dublin organised a large meeting at the town hall. Cole made a speech in which he said that 'the various and conflicting interests of parties had been debated by the Prince with great ability. . . . The Prince was not moved to these inquiries by any man or body of men. It was his own spontaneous act, irrespective of external influences'. Fuller followed this, saying 'that among all the men Mr. Cole and himself had met in their inquiries at the great manufacturing towns of England and Scotland, in which they had heard the opinions of the most eminent men in trade and manufacture, "the best informed man they came into contact with on all the points of their inquiry was the Prince himself".25 Cole thought the meeting was 'most successful'. The two men then went happily towards home, spending an afternoon at Bangor in North Wales to visit Robert Stephenson's wrought-iron Britannia Bridge across the Menai Straits, where they 'went into the Tubes', and toured the workshops. Cole rejoined his family at Herne Bay, where he found Marian 'much improved'.26

Unfortunately, unknown to Cole, reporters were in the audience in Dublin, and what was said about Prince Albert was published in the Dublin papers and copied by the English ones. Col. Phipps reprimanded Cole for over-exposing the prince, and Cole apologised, saying that he had not known that reporters were present, and hoping that 'his Royal Highness will look leniently on this somewhat premature declaration and ascribe it to the very exuberant desire which all seemed to manifest'.27 The prince was 'somewhat annoyed' to find himself represented as the sole inventor of the scheme, and told Phipps

to remind Mr. Cole that the strictest privacy was originally observed, and to caution him not to be drawn away by degrees from the original position. Praising me at meetings looks as if I were to be advertised and used as a means of drawing a full house, &c. &c. Mr. Cole excused himself about Dublin . . . . In London additional caution will be required.28

It was, however, inevitable that once the press had shown an interest 'our visitations necessarily have assumed a somewhat less private character', as Cole put it when he reported to the prince in October.29

Early in October, Phipps had to reproach Cole again. 'I see by the accounts in the newspapers, that at some of the country meetings, Mr Fuller has again entered into details as to the prizes, and has also held out the expectation of the medals being presented by the Queen. I was in hopes that I had already been sufficiently explicit upon this head.'30 Cole replied that he had done all he could to warn Fuller to be circumspect. He added:

There seems a fatality that you must be mis-reported. I have felt this ever since the Dublin meeting, and so strongly at Canterbury and Dover, that I spoke at both meetings as if tongue-tied and I felt quite beaten on seeing the reports. Notwithstanding all my pains, the reporters put things in a way I never did. . . . I assure you that I am very nervous and uncomfortable.31

Privately, however, he must have reflected that he could hardly be expected to drum up support for a great public enterprise by maintaining tight-lipped secrecy.

Touring the country making speeches was an expensive business. Cole had to borrow £ roo from Fuller, which he repaid the following April.32 Although he was on the stump, Cole was still employed by the Record Commission, and when in London worked in his office at the Carlton Ride every day. In mid-September he discussed his extra-mural work with Lord Langdale, who, fortunately, took a lenient view of 'moonlighting'. On 17 October the Egyptian Hall was packed. As the prince was still avoiding the limlight, it fell to Cole to make the mai pech. It was, Cole reorde, 'my first ulic spech', and he posted old Dilke at the back of the audience ready to signal to him in case he could not be heard. His speech was repeatedly interrupted with cries of 'Hear Hear!' and cheering. He began by following the prince's instructions to make it quite clear that such preparations as had already been made were not final. Then he tried to paint for his audience a picture of what the exhibition might be like.

We shall have spices from the East; the hops of Kent and Sussex, the raisins of Malaga, and the olives of the Pyrenees. . . . Of Mineral productions, we shall have gold from California and from the East Indies; silver from Mexico, Russia, and Cornwall; iron ore from Wales, Wolverhampton, and Tunbridge Wells. . . . Then we shall have clays from Bideford, Truro, and perhaps from Putney . .. . In machinery we shall have the steam-engine in all its endless applications, both to land and water purposes. We shall have marine engines in all their varieties; and we shall probably have such machines as Messrs Whitworth of Manchester have recently constructed, measuring to the fifty-thousandth part of an inch. We may have the looms of the Dacca muslin weaver, and the last new power-looms made by Messrs. Roberts. ... I should not wonder if Messrs. Applegarth were to exhibit a printing-machine like that now used by 'The Times' which pours forth 1o,ooo copies of that newspaper per hour.

Some of his remarks revealed his concerns as a design reformer. He suggested that Britons would learn from the section on plastic arts and sculpture 'how much the French are in advance of us in the manufacture of bronzes', though he hoped that Britain could catch up. And he trailed before his audience the prospect of seeing Indian textiles 'which are now almost unknown to us', and which did in the event make a great impression on him.

After stating that the exhibition would be held in Hyde Park, and would be funded by private subscription rather than from taxes, he reached his peroration. 'In short, London will act the part of host to all the world at an intellectual festival of peaceful industry, suggested by the Consort of our beloved Queen and seconded by yourselves — a festival, such as the world has never seen before.' The Times recorded that 'Mr Cole resumed his seat amidst much cheering from all parts of the Hall'. 36

Formal resolutions were passed in favour of the exhibition, for the setting up of a city committee, and for agreeing that the funding would be by voluntary subscriptions only. In the evening Cole made a tour of newspaper offices to make sure that he had been reported correctly, reaching home well after midnight. In the morning he received a note from Col. Phipps which read, 'Your meeting in the City appears to have been most successful.'37 Cole reprinted his speech in its entirety in Fifty Years, as well he might, since it was obviously one of his most immediately effective interventions in public life.

It was clear by now that a Royal Commission would be appointed, as the prince had requested, and Cole recorded that the 'preparation of it was confided me'.38 During the next week he went twice to Windsor Castle to see the prince and discuss how the Society of Arts would work with the Royal Commission. The prince was worried that the House of Commons might yet reject the whole plan. He was in a despondent mood, partly because of the sudden death of George Anson, his private secretary. 'He was my only intimate friend. We went through everything together since I came here. He was almost like a brother to me.'39 Anson's health had been poor for several years and he suffered from bad headaches. All the letters that Cole received from the prince had been written by Col. Phipps, who was an equerry, rather than the secretary proper. After Anson's death, Phipps continued to write letters for the prince, but was promoted to be privy purse and treasurer to the prince and cofferer to the Prince of Wales. As secretary, Col. Charles Grey was appointed. He belonged to one of the great Whig families who were close friends of the Queen, and had been sent to Saxe-Coburg to fetch the prince to England for his marriage. He became for Cole a most friendly, tactful and useful ally at court, first as the prince's secretary, and later as the Queen's. He told Cole to call on him at any time.

Early in November Wentworth Dilke joined the executive committee. Robert Stephenson was elected on to the council of the Society of Arts, and at Russell's suggestion also came on to the executive committee. By now, the committee was set up in an office at No. 1 Old Palace Yard in the Palace of Westminster, with smart headed writing paper. The government agreed to a Royal Commission, and it became urgent to decide who would be its members. On I 1 December Labouchere was surprised at the delay and Lord Granville said he would write to Col. Phipps. There were difficulties in finding statesmen willing to lend their support to such a risky project. Free traders were in favour, but there was no enthusiasm from high Tories, such as the duke of Richmond. Prompted by a 'confidential letter'40 from Phipps, Cole and Fuller invited Richmond to serve, but, an arch-protectionist, he declined.

There were other, more personal, reasons that held some politicians back: 'The Ministers could not of course commit themselves by publicly avowing their disapprobation of an undertaking commenced under such high auspices. It might, however, readily have been foreseen that they would be averse to such a scheme, because whilst it was sure to give them a great deal of trouble, it would afford them no compensation in the shape of patronage.' This was written by the disgruntled mathematician and inventor, Charles Babbage, who for many years had been struggling to get offcial help for his calculating machine. He went on: Those, however, who usually reflect and retail the opinions of the Government, were by no means silent; at i  U heh  eal i "pooh-poohed".41

While there was opposition to the exhibition, antagonism was also mounting against Cole himself. Russell told him \*that someone had said I was a great jobber & wondered why he had connected me with the Scheme!. Cole was angry and noted that Russell 'rather stumbled out an explanation on perceiving my indignation'. Edwin Chadwick, who was a true friend of Cole's, called on him expressly to tell him that "znd rate people were jealous of me, & wondered that the Prince shd set me to o the thin r Frans alrvecalle  ol' e n wasao 'vy j However, Cole had his supporters. Richard Cobden told him that 'he had full confidence in any arrangements to which I was a party'. And, as Thomas Cubitt reassured him, 'The Prince was fully sensible of the share I had in the Business & appreciated it.42

Cole and his fellow lobbyists from the Society of Arts were worried about what positions they might ultimately hold in the organisation of the exhibition. By now they accepted that they would not be on the Royal Commission itself, but hoped that the society's executive committee might in some way be set apart from other committees serving under the commission. Russell further suggested at dinner one evening with Cole and Fuller that 'we three shd be distinct & superior to other members of Ex: Comm'.43 They were also worried about whether they would be paid. Cole's salary at the Record Office was £450, to which he could add something for his journalism and literary work. Among the enthusiasts for the exhibition there was an optimistic feeling that the Mundays would make huge profits, and that some of that money would find its way to them. Cole had no such hopes, and with his ever-increasing family he needed to be paid while he worked and could not afford to wait for the jackpot that might or might not be emptied into their laps at the close of the exhibition. He hoped for £ 1,200 a year and told the prince so, while Fuller expected £ 1,000. Drew, Russell and Cole had dinner at Fuller's house one evening in November and Drew agreed to ask the Mundays to pay £ 1,20o a year to Dilke and Cole, and £1,000 to Fuller. Russell said he 'would make no obstacle to this arrangement'.4 But later he changed his mind and 'said the public would regard £ I 2oo a year as a job, that he shd be content with 7oof for his services'. Cole agreed with him, but wrote, 'Fuller upbraided me with Upsetting the nicest pail of milk worth to me £ 1o,ooo at least. . . . Altogether very quarelling [sic] & personal'.45 Fuller later said that Cole had got 'a letter from Messrs. Munday agreeing to give him £ 1,2oo a year for his services; and left the remainder of the Executive Committee to make such arrangements with the Contractor as they might be able. . . . I made application to Messrs. Munday for a similar letter, which they declined to give'.46 Such bickering over terms, however unbecoming, is inevitable when a large enterprise is launched. It was particularly vexatious for Cole, since his financial position was less secure than that of the others.

Cole's position was further complicated when he was officially seconded from the Record Office to work full-time for the executive committee. When he called on Lord Langdale at his house in Roehampton on 28 December  the day that he officially transferred to the executive committee - Langdale 's[ai]d he grieved that I sh[oul]d leave the Record Office: thought I ought to be amply indemnified: to have fixed payment & no uncertainty: w[oul]d help me as much as he c[oul]d & w[oul]d see Trevelyan'. When Cole himself consulted Sir Charles Trevelyan at the Treasury, he thought my proposal reasonable & honourable'. Cobden and Milner Gibson both understood Cole's need to be properly paid. The prince, however, insisted that 'there must be perfect harmony and no jobbery', and that any remuneration should be left to the commission. Cole was in the position of poor relation, among distinguished people who could afford to work for nothing. To be paid might well mean that he lost face. Later, at the first meeting of the commission's finance committee held at the Board of Trade on 29 January, he had to stand up for himself and tell the committee 'I would not take an office or Salary which implied an inferior position to anyone else'.47

Behind this statement, and some of the disagreements just described, was not only Cole's discomfort at being a player among gentlemen, but also a rivalry that had grown up between him and John Scott Russell.48 It had already surfaced publicly. On 13 December, when Cole stayed at home 'physicking', Dilke came to tell him that there was evidently a feeling in Ex Comm: that He and I were working against Fuller & Russell'.49 Russell was a successful and still-rising marine engineer, while Cole was a minor government official, even if he was well known as a reformer and troublemaker. Russell felt that Cole was his protégé and, as Cole recorded a day or two before, 'He said that my office was essentially that of Secy, said we wd be friends on this understanding. Wanted me to agree to £8oo a year for the whole time, he having £7 5o for a part only'. 50 The commission was eventually to give Cole £8oo a year with an extra £25o to cover the work that he had done at the early planning stage. While Cole thus struggled to protect his own position, there was little official progress on the commission. A preliminary public announcement that a Royal Commission would be appointed was put out on 2o December, but discussions as to who should be on it still dragged on.

Christmas intervened. Cole had a children's party on Christmas Eve with charades and a Christmas tree. (The Christmas tree was an innovation in the British Christmas; Prince Albert introduced it from Germany, and after the royal family's tree had been featured in the Illustrated London News in 1848, the public hastened to copy it.) The guests at Cole's party included Thackeray's two little girls, the Dilkes, and Fuller's daughters. On Boxing Day Cole called on Labouchere at the Board of Trade, but there was no news about the commission. Cole spent 27 December walking with his cousin John Bond. They visited Cole's father at Kew, and then walked to Richmond and Roehampton, returning to Kensington over Hammersmith Bridge. On 28 December Cole noted in his diary, with irritation, 'Names in Commission not at all settled'. On New Year's Day Cole again saw Labouchere, who thought that the secretaries ought not to be named alongside the commissioners. Cole pleaded for Russell, who was away in Berlin, to be a secretary. However, later in the day at a meeting at Windsor, the prince 'thought that Russell's absence was significant [and] that he wd not make Commission his primary business. Proposed not to name him as Secy, but as a Commissioner'. Then they discussed whether Digby Wyatt or Samuel Redgrave (Richard's brother) might be a secretary. Cole suggested himself, saying that 'I wd see that matters went right if Secy'. To which the prince emolliently replied: 'I'm sure you would', 1 but gave Cole no further encouragement to aim at this post.

The Royal Commission was gazetted two days later. None of the people who had so far done the work of promoting the exhibition were on it. Prince Albert was the president, and Lord Granville the vice-president. From the political world came Lord John Russell, the prime minister, and Lord Stanley, the leader of the opposition, with MPs Robert Peel, Thomas Baring the banker, William Gladstone, Henry Labouchere, the radical Richard Cobden, Philip Pusey and William Thompson. The duke of Buccleuch, the earl of Ellesmere and the earl of Rosse represented the aristocracy. The latter was president of the Royal Society (Britain's premier scientific association) and was therefore balanced by Charles Lyell, president of the Geological Society and Charles Eastlake, president of the Royal Academy. The artistic world was also represented by Charles Barry, architect, and Sir Richard Westmacott, sculptor. Since the exhibition was to be concerned with manufactures, it was obviously necessary to include industrialists and businessmen: Thomas Bazley, chairman of the Manchester Chamber of Commerce; Archibald Galloway, chairman of the East India Company; Thomas Field Gibson, a Spitalfields silk merchant; John Gott, a Leeds wool manufacturer. Finance was represented by the MPs Baring and Thompson already mentioned, together with banker Samuel Jones Loyd. William Cubitt, builder and president of the Institute of Civil Engineers, might be presumed to have a special capacity to help with an exhibition building. This group had in common a sympathy for the liberal side of politics.

Cole was disappointed in his ambition to be secretary, but at least Russell was not put over his head as a commissioner but was appointed a joint secretary. The other joint secretary was Stafford Northcote, Cole's adversary from the Schools of Design committee. An Etonian with a first from Balliol, he may well have been chosen as some kind of counterpoise to the uncouth men from the Society of Arts. To restore good relations, Cole wrote him a placating letter, which he read over to the prince beforehand. When he had his first discussion with Northcote, he found him 'very cooperative & pleasant'.2 From then on the two men worked harmoniously together until Northcote retired six months later because of his father's illness, his place being taken by Edgar Bowring. The members of the Society of Arts's executive committee were appointed as the executive committee of the Royal Commission, with Robert Stephenson as chairman and Digby Wyatt as secretary. Cole emerged, therefore with no office. He was only one member among others of a subordinate committee. He undoubtedly felt that he had been pushed down. Later, in Fifty Years, he recalled that from this moment 'my relations with the Prince were of necessity altogether changed'. There was now an official distance between them, whereas 'up to that time I had had the privilege of being consulted by him on all occasions, for several months'.53

Now that the commission was in place, it might be expected that work on the exhibition would speed ahead. So far as Cole was concerned, however, the next few months brought only frustration and jockeying for position. The prince called the first meeting of the commission for 1 1 January 185o. Cole and Fuller attended the meeting and heard the decision taken to ask the Society of Arts to cancel the contract with the Mundays. It was clear that, without the £75,ooo that the Mundays were prepared to put up, the commission would have to find the money for its own building. So the task was no longer a matter of collecting assurances of goodwill towards the exhibition, but of finding substantial cash to set up the organisation and run the exhibition.

The commission met again on the following Friday. Stephenson, Cole and the rest of the executive committee hung about outside the meeting and were never called in. They heard afterwards that their reports had not been adopted, even though the Prince had approved them. No instructions reached them. It was becoming apparent that the Royal Commissioners were ready to attend meetings, but averse to decisive action. They were peers, politicians and notabilities, who had been appointed to lend weight rather than dynamism to the commission. It did not help the committee that the sympathetic vice-president of the commission, Lord Granville, was away in Paris for the first three weeks of January. He later told Cole that the executive committee members 'were objects of suspicion' to the commissioners.

The prince must have known that the grandees on the commission would not want to be told what to do by the middle-class members of the Society of Arts's executive committee. As a journalist pointed out with hindsight,

The Royal Commission consists of noblemen and gentlemen of the nicest honour, entitled to the respect and confidence alike of the high and the humble; but it is no reproach to them to say they were entirely ignorant concerning the work they undertook .... This evil might have been remedied if a few practical men had been added to the Commission; but whether it was considered infra dig to mix up such men with the aristocracy, we cannot say: at all events, if we sift the Commission, we shall scarcely find one to compensate for the absence of experience, and other advantages in the Commission generally, and in their Secretaries.55

Cole and the other Society of Arts lobbyists wanted to be present at the meetings of the commission. But the prince urged them to be patient, anticipating that the commission 'shd find the want of us. He must beg us to give submissiveness in this point'.56 It was not in Cole's character to be submissive. He felt that he was prevented from doing the work at every turn. He could see that an organisation needed to be set up and funds collected. Government departments could give him no help. When he needed to write to towns and cities throughout the country to ask them to set up local committees he found that the Board of Trade had no list of chambers of commerce, nor had the Home Office a list of corporations. This inefficiency amused Cobden. Cole discussed his position with Mulready, who like most of his friends told him to keep calm. 'Advised patience & then instant action when resolved upon.' Rumours and gossip were flying around: Charles Cole told Henry on 19 January that he had 'heard that Wyatt had a contingent interest of £2ooo, & that the Ex:[ecutive] Com:[mittee] was at a "fix" with Comm[issio]n'. The week before, Milner Gibson had shown Cole an article in the Manchester Examiner 'imputing disingenuousness to me'.57

On 22 January John Scott Russell came back from Germany, where he had been building gunboats for the Prussian government, and the difficulties between the executive committee and the commission were compounded by the prickly rivalry between Cole and Russell.8 At Windsor on 28 January the Prince was quite blunt about the position of the executive committee. He would not admit it to the commission's discussions, telling Cole, Northcote and Russell that he 'objected to attendance of the Ex: Com: on the ground that it would make unnecessary the attendance of the Members of Commn. He thought the Ex Com might remain as it was'.59

Cole and Dilke told him the news and whipped him up into such a rage at the idea of being displaced that he too resigned. The man who was expected to save the day, Lieut.-Col. William Reid, Royal Engineers, was nearly 6o years old, a Scot and a distinguished soldier (plate 3o). He had served under Wellington, and, during the various campaigns in which he fought, he had been wounded four times and had three horses killed under him. He spent many years in the West Indies, where he became governor of the Windward Islands and where he did all he could to get some education for the black population, who were very poor since the abolition of slavery. Back in England in 1848, he was elected a vice-president of the Royal Society for the research he had done into the law of storms. He had recently taken command of the Royal Engineers at Woolwich. After his death in 1 8 58, the Royal Society obituarist wrote of him: 'It has been said that his singular simplicity of manner and total absence of pretension caused the distinguished men, with whom he was associated . . . to wonder at first what had led to his selection for the office.'67 Over 2o years later, Cole called the appointment 'prudent and successful',68 but at the time, in the privacy of his diary, he gave vent to hisfrustration. His laments at enforced idleness continued. 'Nothing to do but look at proof of Col: Reids prospectus'; 'waited doing nothing till 5'; 'Nothing whatever to do'; Nothing to be done'; 'meeting of Commission. On the building site, the columns were up by mid-November, and 8oo carpenters were at work. The ribs of the great transept that covered the tall elm trees were of wood, as were the beams (which in the illustrations of the building can be taken for iron) and the floors. All the glazing bars that supported the glass in the walls and roof were of wood, cut on a steam-driven machine that Paxton had developed, and for which he had received a medal from the Society of Arts in 1841. The magical quiet of the site was now gone, and The Times reported that 'listened from without it seems as if some merry and incongruous village bells were in agitation for the celebration of a marriage'.114 Some 2,o00 workmen were taken on each day, and 20 horses.

As the building arose, stretching from near what is now the top of Exhibition Road to a point east of the Knightsbridge Barracks, it became known as 'Paxton's Palace' and then 'The Crystal Palace'. This name is often said to have been used for the first time in Punch on 2 November 1850.115 Punch, however, was alluding to Paxton's speech at Derby, reported in the Illustrated London News of 19 October 1850, where the term 'Crystal Palace' had previously appeared in print.116

In spite of a strike, the glazing of the nave was done by the third week in November. In the first week of December the wooden transept ribs were hoisted into position. Then scaffolding was attached to special hooks and 5oo painters painted the roof, moving in teams from one end to the other. The roof did not prove to be watertight. In January 185 1 there was a bad storm when 72 feet on the south side of the transept was lifted up. In March there was another bad storm and the roof was damaged in the same place. Cole threatened Fox, two weeks before the opening, 'that if the roof was not proved to be watertight before the end of the week: we shd publish it to be failure'.117 All turned out well.

With so much wood in the building, and with such an enormous space to be filled with inflammable materials, Cole was very concerned about the danger from fire. He collaborated with Mr Braidwood, the superintendent of the London Fire Brigade, and submitted a paper that amazed the commissioners and caused them to order 9- inch fire hydrants all round the building, and to have the Sappers trained to fight a fire. As already mentioned, Col. Reid brought in the Royal Engineers, or 'Sappers', to help with the organisation. The officers worked on the details of planning and the ordinary ranks acted as porters when the exhibits began to arrive, as well as serving as firemen. In command was Capt. Henry Cunliffe Owen, who worked with Cole on the allocation of space to the exhibitors.

The Royal Commission was able to hold its first meeting inside the Crystal Palace on 5 December 1850. Afterwards, the prince gave 250 gallons of beer to the men. This piece of public relations was reported and illustrated in the weekly newspapers. One reported that, as the brewer's dray arrived, 'Hurrahs were vigourously [sic] given, to the manifest pleasure of the Prince, who repeatedly acknowledged them'1 Notwithstanding the benefits such publicity could bring, the royal commissioners were wary of the press. Lord Granville was the only one who wanted them to be allowed into the building. It was not until February 185 1 that an artist from the Illustrated London News was allowed in officially. In The Times account of the building work given above, it is pointedly mentioned that the report is written from the outside of the building. When it was proposed that the press should not be admitted free, but only allowed in if they paid like everyone else, Cole 'entreated Commissioners not to make so fatal and suicidal a rule'. He had to explain that it was in the interests of the exhibition 'to attract the Press to come and report on everything that was going on as much as possible'. He carried his point and Dilke wonderingly commented: 'You get your way when you are in a minority of one'.119 Cole, with so many campaigns behind him, fully appreciated the usefulness of the press and the damage that it would do if thwarted.

It fell to Cole to decide how much space each exhibitor should have, and where the exhibits should be placed in the building. The prince's broad classification scheme had already been considerably revised by Playfair. Now Cole's dispositions were a further departure from the prince's ideas. Lord Granville agreed to Cole's plan on 3 December, but "said the Prince would suspect him of conspiring against him'. The prince made a last stand for his ideas at a meeting on 16 December, but ruefully accepted Cole's plan. Half the building was reserved for Britain and its colonies, and here exhibits were grouped according to Playfair's 3o classes. The other half was allotted to foreign goods. It was impossible to intermingle all these contributions so as to observe the 3o-part classification, so each country was allowed to organise its own display. Space was allotted to countries in proportion to their trade with Britain. So France was given most space, though it was very late in applying for it. It must have been Cole's sheer joy in practical problem solving that caused him to angle for the very testing task of allocating space. Despite his inexhaustible ingenuity, it must have been frustrating sometimes. In his introduction to the official catalogue he ventured to let off a little steam about the impracticality of others, lamenting that the terms 'superficial' and 'square' feet were often misunderstood, and 'the expressions "horizontal" and "vertical" space disregarded or confounded.' One exhibitor mistakenly requested '27,ooo superficial feet  to be arranged in such a way as would occupy half the length of the whole Exhibition!' However, Cole sorted out all these problems, and gracefully acknowledged that 'whilst almost every exhibitor desired some kind of special arrangement, convenient to himself, but inconvenient to everybody else, almost every one submitted to a curtailment of space, and a constraint on his wishes, with a patience that greatly lightened the labours of the Executive Committee.'120

No doubt the commissioners were glad to leave such brain-teasing problems to Cole. We can imagine him, when the exhibits began to arrive, bouncing around in his spectacles and top hat (plate 32), and literally putting the exhibitors in their places. The difficult ones he would pass on to Col. Reid, who would turn his long, earnest countenance upon them and soothe them with his slow calm. It was said of Reid: 'It was curious to see the enraged and frantic exhibitor (the foreigner particularly) swearing at the injustice and favouritism which had consigned his article to some obscure corner or some bad light . . . pass into the presence of the Chairman of the Executive Commiee, and presently merge all cheerfulness and contentent'

Owen Jones designed a colour scheme, 'parti-colouring', which would make clear the articulation of the building, and provide a receptive background for exhibits. He used the primary colours of 'blue, red, and yellow in such relative proportions as to neutralize or destroy each other. Thus no one colour will be dominant or fatigue the eye.' To avoid colours clashing, or tainting each other, he 'interpose[d] a line of white between them, which will soften them & give them their true value' 124 At the end of February, and with eight weeks to go to the opening and goods pouring in by the cartload, it seemed unlikely that this time-consuming scheme would be completed by the 1 May. It was taken down in the nick of time. As the exhibition was due to open on i May, it was high time to make arrangements for an official ceremony. On 1 5 April, Col. Phipps told Cole that the Queen would grace the exhibition with a state opening, but that this must take place in private. She had been attacked twice about a year before, and her fear of crowds persisted. The next morning, Cole took his introduction to the catalogue to show the prince (who thought it too long), and tried to persuade him to let the public in at the opening. The next day, he was at the building before seven, and found Richard Cobden, William Cubitt and Thomas Bazley all voicing disapproval of the proposed private ceremony. Under pressure from Cole and his allies, Lord Granville went straight from the meeting to Windsor, and with difficulty persuaded the prince to agree to a public ceremony. Reid and Cole. Practical arrangements fell to Cole. It was decided to admit season-ticket holders to the opening, with the result, naturally, that the sale of season-tickets soared.

On 3o April, Cole arrived at the building by 6.oo a.m., and discussed details with Col. Reid and Lord Granville. At two o'clock the building was cleared of workmen and exhibitors, so that the final arrangements could be made for the great occasion on the following day.

The opening ceremony did not go as smoothly as Cole had planned. The police proved to have been badly instructed, and did not know the nave from the gallery.

Special places had not been reserved for the commissioners. Crowds pressed towards the throne where the centre of the ceremony would be, and had to be held back by Captain Owen and his Sappers. 'Excepting this everything went off remarkably well,' wrote Cole, and next day he received 'universal Congratulations on the success of the opening'. He was constantly in the building, early and late, during the next few weeks, perhaps irresistibly drawn to a blaze of glory that in many ways was a personal triumph. The royal family paid repeated visits during May and June (Cole took pains to record them in Fifty Years), which no doubt enabled him to put himself forward in a good light.

If Cole now paused to contemplate his achievement, he would have reflected that first and foremost, the Great Exhibition was a great spectacle. The building  vast but strangely insubstantial, sparkling among the greenery of Hyde Park - was a spectacle in itself. 'A blazing arch of lucid glass / Leaps like a fountain from the grass / to meet the sun!' versified his friend, Thackeray. 126 Inside, against Owen Jones's subtly variegated colour scheme, crimson drapes provided a glowing background for unimaginably diverse exhibits. Many were household goods: in the days before department stores no-one was accustomed to see so much of so much, all together. Other exhibits were huge. Apart from the machinery section, where cogs and cranks clattered away under the impulse of steam power, the main aisles of the building were crammed with giant objects ranging from a colossal bronze lion from Bavaria, through prodigious porphyry vases, fountains, lighthouses and telescopes, to trophies of Canadian timber and American rubber, intermingled with statuary. Thackeray again, writing as a comic Irishman: 'There's statues bright / Of marble white / Of silver and of copper; / And some in zinc, / And some, I think, / That isn't over proper.' The bishops wanted the improper statues to be covered up, but thy remained on view, unashamed.128 Visitors could see manufactured articles from advanced western countries, which, though astoundingly profuse, were fundamentally familiar; they could also, perhaps for the first time, wonder at the exotic, even bizarre, artefacts from eastern and 'primitive' cultures. The exhibition was a window on the world, as well as a temple of consumerism. The credit for designing the architectural setting of the exhibition must go to Paxton and Owen Jones, with Digby Wyatt in the background. It was Cole who was responsible for the disposition of exhibits, so he too must take some credit for the brilliance of the spectacle.

The exhibition was also a community festival on a hitherto unparalleled scale. It was the stated intention of the organisers that all classes should mingle in the Crystal Palace. Although the Royal Commission's working classes committe, on which Cole served alongside Dickens, Thackeray, the bishop of Oxford and three Char h to the working classes to attend, especially in the shape of graduated admission charges. Top people were encouraged to take season-tickets, which secured them privileged admission at all times. From the opening day for about a month the admission charges were high enough to ensure that most of the visitors were from the comfortable classes. But from 26 May onwards there was cheap entry at one shilling, on Mondays to Thursdays, and working people came in crowds. Many 'common people' came on works' outings sponsored by their employers, or on specially subsidised trains, but some at their own cost and under their own propulsion. A third important feature of the Great Exhibition was its political significance. It was the first truly international festival. 'All Nations' seems to have become a catchword. When two foreign visitors came to blows, urchins gathered round shouting 'Go it, All Nations!'134 Apart from such little incidents, the nations met in amity, and world peace was fostered. The nations did not meet, however, in equality. What made a nation estimable in the exhibition's terms was its success as an advanced capitalist, industrial society. So nations such as Britain and France, with their profusion of consumer goods and industrial products, were the leaders. Nations such as Spain or Canada, which could yield up abundant raw materials but were not yet far advanced in making things out of them, cut a lesser figure, as did places like Switzerland or the Scandinavian countries, which exhibited peasant handicrafts as prominently as industrial products. Modern commentators have often felt uncomfortable over the position of 'indigenous populations of the southern hemisphere'. 135 Their displays were at any rate prominent, since they were placed around the edge of the central transept, presumably because of their exotic appeal. But it is argued that they were presented as ripe for exploitation by greedy colonialists. This is to some extent true. The general catalogue's introductory comment on the section devoted to 'British Possessions in Australasia', for instance, points out that 'many of the objects are of great importance to the merchant seeking a new source of known materials'. But it also calls attention to 'interesting collections of native produce'. The comment on British Guiana becomes quite eloquent about such indigenous objects, praising 'that neat but rude and simple industry which, with little or no elaboration of the raw material, produces implements and ornaments from the most convenient substances yielded by nature'. The argument over whether the Great Exhibition was an imperialist racket will continue. Cole's view on the matter was that the coverage of the colonies was 'imperfect'; consequently, 'the idea suggested itself to me that we wd have a Colonial Exhibition in a few years. Dilke agreed . . . & Reid said "the idea was worthy of you" 36

Cole had an opportunity to expound a political interpretation of the Great Exhibition in a lecture 'On the International Results of the Exhibition of 18 5 I', given at the Society of Arts on 1 December 1852, as the last of a series on the exhibition, suggested by Prince Albert. This lecture was written at speed in the closing days of November 18 5 2. It has been pointed out that it draws heavily on Bentham's 'Plan for Uniealneual eplIal but this indebtedness does not make the lecture any the less interesting as Cole's most sustained effort at a political credo, along radical, Cobdenite lines. He urges that culture can be a more successful internationally unifying force than conventional governmental diplomacy. 'The men of Art, Science, and Commerce, have hitherto had but a very subordinate voice in the regulation of their own interests, which had been too much left to the professional superintendence of their brethren of Politics, Law, and War.' He believes that such events as the exhibition can 'keep nations from going to blows as hastily and foolishly as they have been accustomed to do' 138 And he goes on to advocate a raft of beneficent measures that might be furthered through international co-operation. Already, he points out, first steps have been taken towards standard international postage rates and patent laws, harmonisation of quarantine regulations, and subsidised cultural exchanges. The time is now ripe for harmonising international commercial law; weights, measures and coinage; customs administration and passports; and copyright law. And at the conclusion he veers off into a vision of international bibliographical control for libraries, urging the British Museum Library to lead the way in compiling a 'Universal Catalogue' to which each nation would contribute bibliographical records of its own publications, thus avoiding much duplication of work. This idea was to re-surface in his project for a universal catalogue of art books in the 1860s.

This lecture shows that Cole was able to see the exhibition in the widest of contexts. But his most intense reaction to it came in the narrower context of design reform. It came home to him and others that the exhibition showed that, while British goods were best so far as quality and price were concerned, they were certainly not the most beautiful in the world. His already existing conviction that British design must be improved was confirmed by the exhibition, and the repercussions of the exhibition were to shape his future life.

Although, in the summer of 185 1, his way ahead was not yet clear, he must have felt that he had successfully climbed the ladder of success. The strained relations between the grand commissioners and the middle-class activists were now moothed over, and Cole had arrived as a person of repute.
