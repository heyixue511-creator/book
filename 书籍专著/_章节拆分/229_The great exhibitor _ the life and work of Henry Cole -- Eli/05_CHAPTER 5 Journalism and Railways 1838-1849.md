## CHAPTER 5 Journalism and Railways 1838-1849

By the beginning of the $\tau 8 4 0 \mathrm { s } .$ Cole had gained a reputation as a campaigning reformer and polemicist. If public relations consultants or lobbyists had then existed, he would have been able to earn his livelihood that way. During the $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 0 s ,$ however, while he was busy with a wide variety of issues, his interests increasingly focused on artistic matters, and, as things fell out, it was in the arts that he ultimately made his mark. His turn to art during the $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 \bigcirc \mathrm { s }$ is dealt with in the next section, while this chapter follows out his activities in the $1 8 4 0 s$ as journalist, publicist and campaigner.

Although he had thus effectively relinquished the possibility of becoming a professional journalist, he did continue to hanker after the role of editor. As we have already seen, he had a first, brief stab at this in spring 1837 with The Guide. Unabashed by failure here, Cole was soon projecting 'a weekly journal to promote National Education in this country ... to be called the Portfolio of Public Instruction'. This was to be concerned with education in its practical rather then philosophical aspects, and its propectus4included an interesting diagram f the rent educational system, which would be keenly scrutinised in its columns. It was to be published by Henry Hooper, who had published The Guide and The Post Circular. Nothing came of this venture, but in the following year, when Cole heard that the printer Charles Reynell (who had printed The Guide) had bought the British Ladies' Newspaper, he contrived to secure the editorship of this. A 16-page, foliosize weekly, it seems to have lasted only for nine issues, from 6 January to 3 March 1838.6 Cole preserved some advertising posters for it, including a large one on yellow paper which reads like a parody by Dickens: 'TO THE FAIR sEX!! / THE BRITISH LADIES' NEWSPAPER / Addresses you Weekly / on all subjects in which the / Most Lovely of Creation / are deeply interested.'

When Cole compiled a sort of curriculum vitae in 186o, he described himself as both 'Civil Administrator & Author' and made brief reference to his earlier journalistic work: 'He contributed to the Westminster, & British & Foreign Reviews.'7 These were the most prestigious periodicals for which he wrote, and he no doubt recorded them in preference to less esteemed journals. He failed to mention, however, that he briefly served as editor of the Westminster.

Founded in 1824, the Westminster amalgamated with John Mill's London Review in 183 5. But the resulting London and Westminster Review, financially backed by Sir William Molesworth, did not prosper. Molesworth, who was losing money, withdrew in 1837, and Mill, after trying to manage as well as edit the journal, wanted to step down by 1840. After the political demise of Lord Durham and the weakening of the radical party in parliament, he felt that he had done all he could for the cause of reform. It is also possible that he was tired of dealing with the (nominal) editor of the Review, John Robertson, whom Carlyle had once described as 'a burly Aberdeen Scotchman of seven and twenty, full of laughter, vanity, pepticity and hope',8 but later dismissed as a 'a flathoofed jackass'.' Furthermore Mill was 'drained so dry' financially by the journal and other calls on his purse 'that I cannot incur the smallest extra expense on account of the next number of the review, and, all things considered, I would not recommend your doing so'. 10 Mill's recommendation to Cole to steer clear of the journal indicates that Cole was already angling to take over. He and William Hickson pushed hard and achieved their aim, since there were no other obvious or eager candidates to replace Mill. In these rather unpropitious circumstances, Henry Cole thus got a chance to fulfil a great ambition: to edit a prestigious journal. He was to be unpaid editor, while Hickson was to provide the money. Mill hoped that they would keep Robertson on as editor, as he was poor and needed his salary, but he had to go home to Scotland. Mill insisted that the name of the journal be changed back to Westminster Review, so that the readers would know that he was no longer involved with it.

Cole bustled round, calling on contributors at home or catching them at the Reform Club. Romilly, Buller and Thackeray all promised him articles. But Carlyle, recognising that Hickson and Cole lacked the prestige of John Mill, declined to allow them to reprint his series of lectures on heroes and hero-worship. He wrote disparagingly to John Sterling: 'The Westminster Review has come into the hands of Cole and Hickson; to be conducted on new principles! What words are adequate? No words'.11 In writing to his mother he called Cole a 'Dud' as editor.12 He seems to have been right, for Cole reported that the sales of the first number were 'very languid'.

Cole soon lost heart. His 'enthusiasm waned with surprising speed', comments one r   o While Cole was clearly a talented controversialist, and a skilled manipulator of information, it may well be that his forcefulness and partisan intensity deprived him of the breadth of sympathy (and the range of contacts) that a successful editor needed. By the end of July 184o, after only a few months, he left the Review to Hickson, who remained owner and editor for over 1o years. Hickson's wife, whom Cole liked, was cool to him for many months afterwards for having abandoned the loss-making Review to her husband. It was to be nearly nine years before the Hicksons again came to dinner with Cole and his family in 1849.

Though Cole had forsaken the Westminster, there were still openings for him elsewhere in journalism. One of them was the weekly paper, the Athenaeum, which was primarily a literary review, but covered all cultural matters to some extent. Cole first wrote for it in 1837, but a lifelong connection developed when he became friendly with Charles Wentworth Dilke, the son of its proprietor (plate 29). He was two years Cole's junior, and a sort of pig-in-the-middle in his family, caught between a famous father and a notorious son, who both shared all three of his names. 'Old' Charles Wentworth Dilke is best remembered today as a friend of the poets Keats and Shelley, but later in life, alongside a post in the navy pay office, he became proprietor and editor of the Athenaeum. Founded in 1829, this was faltering when Dilke bought it in 1830. He transformed its fortunes by reducing its price from a shilling to fourpence and increasing its size, and it so far succeeded that in 1845 it was said that it 'is now to literary journals what the "Times" is to newspapers'. I4 Cole already knew Dilke senior, for the Athenaeum supported the penny post and Cole had met him during the campaign. When Cole was asked to dinner on 1 October 1842 to meet the whole family, he wrote that 'there is a homeliness ab[ou]t them which puts you at your ease and in old Dilke especially good common sense with decision of character'. The middle Charles Wentworth Dilke, usually known as Wentworth, married Mary Chatfield in 1843, and they had the third Charles Wentworth Dilke, later to become an MP, whose promising political career was blighted by his involvement in a divorce scandal. The three generations of Dilkes lived together in a house in Sloane Street. Old Dilke was able to retire from the editorship of the Athenaeum in 1847, and, as propor, lin   r  C'v ur prominent member of the Horticultural Society, and founded a periodical, The Gardeners Chronicle. Cole began to write articles for this, as well as for the Athenaeum, to which he often contributed 'gossip', 'pars' (i.e. paragraphs) and 'miscellaneous notices'.16

In the early 1840s Cole was very busy with literary work, producing guidebooks, children's books and some artistic publications, which will be discussed in the next chapter. In the midst of this, he found time for yet another attempt at being an editor: of a newspaper dealing with births, deaths and marriages. Cole started proposing this to publishers on 6 June 1844. It sounds like the sort of journal that might appeal primarily, perhaps only, to people with a highly developed interest in public records, like Cole himself. Indeed, he described it in his promotional literature as 'a kind of personal Doomsday book - highly interesting and useful to the present time, and indispensable to the future' 17 The principal point that Cole advanced in its favour was that the compulsory legal registration of births, marriages and deaths had been in force only since 1836, and that these hitherto unpublished facts should be made available in published form.18 One of the publishers whom he lobbied conceded that births, deaths and marriages 'were the most interesting of human events & most read in the N[ews] Paper',19 and Cole made this point with characteristic emphasis in his publicity. In England and Wales, he claimed, 'a birth happens every minute, a death every minute and a half, and were marriages spread over the whole day, every fourth minute would witness the celebration of one. The hopes, fears, and sympathies of twenty millions are stirred by these events'.20

Longmans, with whom Cole was already working, agreed to take on the project on 27 August, even though the senior partner, Thomas Longman, 'could never get over the idea' (as he confessed on 2 1 October) that births, deaths and marriages 'were not sufficiently interesting' to the general public. Cole urged his projected paper, which was to be titled the Historical Register, upon all his contacts. Macaulay, on 9 November, had doubts; the scientist Richard Owen, on 2o November, thought it 'one of the Happiest Ideas' of the time; on 27 November 'Old Dilke thought the preparations for Hist: Register as good as possible & if we failed it wd be for want of a public'. Cole was so busy rallying contributors that, he noted on 2o December, 'diary neglected: but every minute at Register'. The Register started publication in the first week of the New Year, and there is nothing in Cole's diary for the first I 1 days of January, so hard was he working.

The Historical Register described its subject matter in its masthead as 'Family Events, National and Private Biography, Life and Health', and announced that it was produced 'with the Sanction and Assistance of various Government Departments'. Presumably the latter supplied the details of 'B.D. & M.', as Cole referred to the prime subject-matter of the 16-page, folio-size weekly journal. All Victorian newspapers looked grey and grim, with tightly packed columns of small type, and the Historical Register was as grey and grim as any. The core of each number consisted of several pages in minuscule type, simply listing births, marriages and deaths, together with a statistical analysis of mortality in London. After this were a few articles, usually biographies or obituaries. The lightest of these was a piece on 'The Christmas Pudding' in number 1; the darkest, a piece on 'Prussic Acid' (as a poison) in number 5. In the pages preceding the B.D.& M. came news, mostly copied out of other newspapers. On the front page was a section headed 'News of the Week. Summary'. In number 4, this began: 'The news of the week is in general unimportant'. Number 5 started with: 'Her Majesty, the Prince, and Royal Family continue in the enjoyment of excellent health'. The level of excitement rose a little in number 6:

'Her Majesty opened Parliament in person on Tuesday'. But number 7 was back to 'Her Majesty, Prince Albert, and the Royal Family continue to enjoy good health', and number 8 recorded that 'Her Majesty, Prince Albert, and the Royal children, returned to town from Brighton on the 2oth, in the enjoyment of excellent health'. Although this is hardly a fair sample of the paper's contents, it does indicate that the Historical Register did not have a very keen edge.

Alarm bells began to ring as soon as I4 January, when Longman said that the Register 'did not look very promising'. He brought in a Mr Maunder on 18 January, in the hope that he could 'work . . . the paper into Success'. On 4 February, Longman agreed to enlarge the paper to 24 pages, and 'to placard and try for 4 weeks more'. By the end of February, a shutdown seemed inevitable. Cole tried to get Bradbury and Evans to take over the paper but they declined on 27 February. The final number appeared on 8 March.

Cole's diary suggests that at this point he may have experienced a degree of agonising re-appraisal. He rearranged his room on 1o March ('taking down shelves &c.'). On Sunday 16 March and through the following week he did his accounts for the last three years, hitherto neglected. And he was slightly ill, with 'unhealthy urine'. His doctor and friend Seymour Haden warned that this portended gout, but his prescription was that 'I need only take some Soda in the Ev[enin]g'.21

Cole continued to be very busy with other projects, including journalism. He now found an outlet in collaborating with Wentworth Dilke in writing about railways, a highly topical issue. The 184os were the days of 'Railway Mania', as the network of new railways spread out across the country. In 1844 Dilke started the Railway Chronicle, to cater to the rising public excitement over this transport revolution. There were already many such journals in the market,22 the principal one being the Railway Times, which had been started in the 183os and which claimed for itself a weekly circulation of 27,000 by 1845. In 1845 there were over 700 railway companies, already working or being floated, many of them fraudulent. Cole wrote a spoof of a prospectus for the Railway Chronicle: 'The Great National Direct Independent Lands' End and John O'Groats Atmospheric Railway, with Steam Ferries to the Scilly and Orkney Isles, and Coasting Docks at both Termini. Provisionally registered Capital, £9,500,00o; in 95,000 shares of £100 each'. Such was the hysteria at the time that some people took it seriously.23

Cole wrote not only about the politics and economics of rail travel, but about the interesting new experience that it offered to sightseers. Guides for railway outings had been published since the late 183os, and 'Railway Rambles' to places near London had already appeared in the Penny Magazine in 1841. Cole came to this genre through writing a series of 'Day's Excursions out of London' in the Athenaeum, in autumn 1842 and 1843. At first, he envisaged that his readers might ramble by foot, carriage o steamer as much as by rail, but gradually rail became the preferred method, and Cole offered thoroughly practical advice: 'The seats at the end receive the most pitiless currents of wind and rain  air at alltimes  seasoned with the cinders from the engine.'24 The purpose of the expeditions was antiquarian, to se old churches and historic buildings in such places as Esher, St Albans, Rochester or Winchester. Some of Cole's articles were re-issued in book form in 1843 as Felix Summerly's day's excursions out of London.

Cole proceeded to a further series of articles in the Railway Chronicle,25 which later appeared in book form as Felix Summerly's pleasure excursions.26 His personal railway mania then led him to produce 'travelling charts'. His innovation here was to make the railway journey itself the central feature. The Railway Travelling Charts: or iron road books, for perusal on the journey included details not only of 'towns, villages, churches, mansions, parks', but also of 'stations, bridges, viaducts, tunnels, cuttings, gradients'. These features were all marked on a sort of map, which adopted a traditional format for road maps, in which the road meandered down the middle of a page, with details of places and landmarks on each side. In Cole's charts the railway line went vertically down the centre, flanked by text, and by illustrations in the form of charming little wood engravings after such artists as Richard Cox, jun., Charles C. Pyne, and Fraser Redgrave, the half-brother of the painter Richard Redgrave. The charts were individually printed on long strips of paper, which folded up into handy pamphlets, and were published from the Railway Chronicle office. They described the lines from London to Brighton (fig.3), Cambridge, Oxford, Southampton, Tunbridge Wells, Guildford and Birmingham, among others.

Cole also wrote general articles, and paragraphs of news called 'gossip, accident and progress of works' for the Railway Chronicle, and on at least one occasion, in March 1846, acted as sole editor. His work brought him into contact with Wentworth Dilke's associate, John Scott Russell, a Scot whom Cole first met over dinner at Rowland Hill's. Russell had arrived in London with a huge reputation as a naval architect, an engineer and an experimental scientist. He had made discoveries about the behaviour of waves and the displacement of ships' hulls. In 1837 he was given the Gold Medal of the Royal Society of Edinburgh for his paper $\mathfrak { s } _ { \mathrm { O n } }$ the Laws of Resistance of Floating Bodies' (reprinted in the Athenaeum), and he was elected a fellow and made a member of the council. He also made a name at the British Association. He was at the cutting edge of experiments with steam propulsion, both on land and at sea. He would have liked to be a university academic, but had to earn his living as manager of a shipbuilding firm on the Clyde, where he designed and supervised the building of some of the first ocean-going steam ships for the Royal Mail Line. He realized that if he stayed there he would never be anything more than an employee, so he moved to London with his wife, two children and hardly any money. They lived in a two-roomed flat in Westminster, which was so full of books that they could hardly move. He and Cole became close friends and they were in many ways very similar: within a few weeks of the same age, clever and energetic, and in their different ways ambitious. Russell was determined to reach the top in his chosen career, while Cole's ambition was tempered by a public-spirited streak that he had learned as a youth from John Stuart Mill. Russell was un-pompous (though pomposity was an occasional vice of Cole's), but not unaggressive. Cole, never physically violent, was amazed to see Russell knock a man down for pulling at his arm at Croydon Station one day (5 October 1845) when he and Cole had been out together for a long Sunday walk. Russell was in charge of the money at the Chronicle and at first Cole was paid £2 2s. od. a week and then $\mathcal { L } _ { 3 }$ , with extra for 'compilation' or editing.

London to Exeter by way of Bristol, was the most effcient and successful company at that time. Its track was brilliantly engineered by Brunel, and ran on a gauge of seven feet and half an inch. Brunel had invented this width of rail for his first railway to Bristol, and he believed passionately that this was the best and only width to use, clinging to his invention through thick and thin. However, the first railway in Liverpool in 1829 had been built to a gauge of 5 ft 8% ins  the 'narrow gauge' which became the almost universal width for the rest of the country. By 1845 there were 2,00o miles of narrow gauge to only 300 miles of Brunel's 'broad gauge'. Early in that year the Great Western applied to the government for permission - a private act was needed  to extend its network to Oxford, Worcester and Wolverhampton and to join it with the South Wales Railway, of which Brunel was also the engineer. This brought opposition to the broad gauge into the House of Commons, and on 25 June Cobden asked for a royal commission to consider bringing in an act to make all railways, existing and to be built, conform to the narrow gauge. The report of the commission was published early in 1846 and favoured the narrow gauge. He wrote a long pamphlet, Observations on the Report of the Gauge Commissioners and distributed it to all Members of Parliament. After the bill became law in August 1846, Cole was inspired to intervene with a pamphlet of his own, addressed to George Carr Glyn, the chairman of the London and Birmingham Railway. This trumpeted his frustration at the way that the supporters of the narrow gauge had stood by doing nothing while Brunel was allowed to have his way. 'Keeping aloof' from controversy, said Cole, was 'an egregious mistake'. 'Public questions are not carried in this country upon their intrinsic merits. It is by the energies of those whose private interests are touched by them.' In the battle of the gauges, the London and Birmingham directors had shown 'feeble supineness', and now ´uniformity of gauge is temporarily sacrificed, and worse still for us, a ruinous competitive battle will have eventually to be fought between us and the Great Western'.28 The London and North Western Railway was the company most threatened by the incursions of the broad gauge, and, roused by Cole's pamphlet, 'they engaged my services to create a public opinion to support Uniformity of Gauge as best for national interests'. Once more Cole was in business as a lobbyist.

By this time the L.N.W.R.'s lines stretched from London to Edinburgh, but anyone wanting to travel from the South or West of England to Scotland had to change gauges at Gloucester. Those in favour of the narrow gauge foresaw that if the broad gauge continued its expansion there would be more junctions where passengers would need to change trains, as Cole had pointed out in his pamphlet. Cole got permission from Dilke and Russell to spend £ 1 5o on leaflets and maps to be put out by the Railway Chronicle. His principal allies in the agitation were the two Dilkes, John Scott Russell and John Chorley, who wrote for the Railway Chronicle and reviewed for the Athenaeum. Cole himself wrote at least five pamphlets, besides his week-toweek work for the Chronicle. His most successful pamphlet, which ran into three editions, was entitled A railway traveller's reasons for adopting uniformity of gauge addressed to I. K. Brunel, Esq. Cole suggested that Brunel should put himself in the place of a commercial traveller who had to change trains at Gloucester at night to go to Cheltenham,

with all your proper equipments, of samples, and packages, and great coats, and feet wrapper, and neck wrapper, and night cap and umbrella, and carpet bag, and dressing case. Make an honest actual experiment of it, and though you yourself are the inventor of the Broad Gauge, I will wager any stakes, that you pronounce the break of Gauge an unsufferable nuisance, and that you return to London a wiser man, bent on annihilating the monster you have created, and were contemplating to spread throughout the length and breadth of England.30

Although most of Cole's railway pamphlets were printed in ordinary, everyday type, this one was printed, perhaps as a courteous gesture to a great man, in Cole's favourite Old Caslon typeface, its anachronistic look being enhanced by the use of long S's. The old-fashioned appearance of the pamphlet was in comic contrast to its subject — the latest technology of the day.

As in his earlier lobbying, Cole was not content with words alone. He arranged for Henry Townsend to make drawings of travellers and porters struggling with goods, luggage, children, babies, horses and dogs to change platforms, which were reproduced in the Illustrated London News in 1846 (plate 15). Cole and Thackeray visited Gloucester together, which resulted in Thackeray writing two sketches published in Punch in May and June 1 846. In the previous year, Thackeray had written a series, 'Jeames Diary', about Jeames Plush, a footman, who makes £3o,ooo out of railway speculation, leaves his place and sets himself up as James de le Pluche (of Norman descent). Then he loses all his money, and has to revert to being Jeames Plush the footman again. In $\mathtt { r 8 4 6 }$ , Thackeray had a new series in Punch, 'The Snobs of England', but he interrupted this to write two more Jeames skits, to help Cole in his campaign.

These efforts were of no avail, and the broad gauge continued its advance until Brunel's death in 1859 caused his campaign to lose its impetus. The broad gauge was supplanted, though it lingered on the Great Western line to Penzance until the 1890s. Somehow Cole remained on friendly terms with Brunel, though his opposition was so outspoken. They knew each other personally, meeting sometimes at the house of Cole's friend, the artist John Horsley, whose elder sister was Brunel's wife. Brunel's sister was married to Benjamin Hawes, whom Cole knew from the select committee on the records. A further link existed in Scott Russell, with whom Brunel collaborated, towards the end of his life, in the building of the Great Eastern steamship.

The campaign for the single gauge may not have been a success, but as a result of his involvement in railways Cole obtained another commission as a lobbyist, when he was made the public relations man for a newly projected dock at Great Grimsby. Railways and docks were often constructed at the same time, as Cole realised when he was writing his spoof prospectus. In the past, docks had been built as farinland as possible on tidal rivers, in order to cut down the cost of transporting goods over land. With the advent  railways and larger ships, it became more practicable tobuild large docks on the coast and move goods to and from them by rail. Grimsy, in

Lincolnshire, was well placed at the mouth of the Humber, and had been a prosperous port in the middle ages. But by the late 183os it had declined so much that little ports along the East coast like Boston, Wisbech, Gainsborough and Spalding had more trade than it had. Lord Yarborough was the principal landowner in the town and in the country around, and he and other worthies took over some small railway companies to form the Manchester, Sheffeld and Lincolnshire Railways Company, so as to bring the line to Grimsby. There was also a new gas company, and a company to build a pier. The earl of Yarborough was chairman of all three companies. This brought to an end Cole's work for the railways. He had not influenced the battle of the gauges very much, and interest in it had tailed off. The Railway Chronicle ceased publication in 1849, when Dilke, Scott Russell and Cole took on the publicity for the Great Exhibition of 185 1.
