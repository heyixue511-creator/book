## CHAPTER 7 Design Reform 1846-1852

Cole's transformation from a small-time arts entrepreneur and occasional critic into an influential design pundit began with his entry in 1846 into the Society of Arts. This institution, founded by some go-ahead men nearly 100 years earlier, in 1754, was properly titled the Society for the Encouragement of Arts, Manufactures and Commerce. It became the Royal Society of Arts in 1908. As its original name suggests, it had a wide interest in human ingenuity in all its forms. In its early days the society involved itself in, among other things, agriculture, forestry and land drainage; in medicinal drugs and soap; and in the manufacturing techniques, especially in the field of textiles, of the industrial revolution in Britain. It was also interested in drawing, painting and sculpture, and in 'Fancy, Design and Taste'.1 Through its support for art exhibitions, it paved the way for the foundation of the Royal Academy in 1768, and, while it kept clear of art exhibitions thereafter, it continued to make awards to artists and art students. Its chief method of proceeding in other fields was by offering prizes for new inventions, and it contributed tirelessly to the gadgetry of nations. Founded in a coffee house, and meeting at first in a circulating library, in 1774 the society established its headquarters in the purpose-built premises it still occupies, in a building that formed part of the Adam brothers' redevelopment of the Adelphi area, south of the Strand (plate 28).

In the early decades of the nineteenth century, after many successful years, the society was running down, and by the early 1840s it was almost bankrupt. It had only 320 members² and was about to sell its investments to pay off its debts. A rescue committee under a young patent lawyer, Thomas Webster, was appointed in 1841, and was mastering the problems by 1843. At this point, the president, the duke of Sussex, died, and was succeeded by Prince Albert. The assumption of this office marked an important stage in the prince's creation of an independent sphere of influence for himself. In 1845, a permanent management committee, called the council, was created, and the society began to recover.

Its new secretary, during 1843-5, was Francis Whishaw, an acquaintance of Cole through Rowland Hill. 'He was very energetic and successful in personally canvassing for members, inducing one hundred and forty new members to join within a few months.' Furthermore he changed the format of meetings, which were now devoted to papers and discussions and 'soon became the acknowledged reunions of all the leading professors of science and art'.3 Whishaw was succeeded in $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 _ { 4 } \mathrm { ~ 5 ~ }$ by a closer crony of Cole, John Scott Russell, who, as a vice-president of the sister society in Scotland, had been welcomed with open arms by the society in London, when he joined in $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 4$ Through Russell, Cole began to get involved. From the moment Cole arrived in the society, 'his enterprise, energy, and perseverance stimulated its proceedings and supplied much of the driving force that gave the Society a new importance in the land'.4 The society, with its wide interests - embracing art, science, technology and trade  provided an ideal arena in which someone like Cole, with his all-purpose ingenuity, could ride any number of hobbyhorses.

The revived society, at the instigation of Whishaw, also embarked on exhibitions of contemporary manufactures. As we have seen, Cole won a prize with his tea set in the 1846 exhibition. He then seems to have seized control of the exhibitions as a means of promoting his efforts in design reform. It was announced early in 1847 that Prince Albert, as president of the society, had agreed 'that the department of the Society which would be most "likely to prove immediately beneficial to the public was that which encouraged most efficiently the application of the Fine Arts to the various manufactures of the country". 5 Thus Cole's personal mission had now been adopted, with royal support, as the mission of the society. A new exhibition followed soon after, devoted to 'British Manufactures and Decorative Art', which eclipsed previous exhibitions and was presented as the first of a new annual series. Appropriately, perhaps, for an introductory exhibition, it not only showed modern manufactures, but also had an opening historical section showing works of decorative art from the Elizabethan period, through the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, to Wedgwood and Flaxman.6 Running from I I March until 3o April, this exhibition attracted some 20,000 visitors.7

Another exhibition followed in 1848, running from 15 March to 29 April, and attracting some 7o,ooo visitors. This exhibition, hailed as in every respect, superior' to the previous year's show,8 was honoured with an exhaustive 12-column review in the Art-Union. 'Already', commented the reviewer, 'the spread of one species of artistic propriety is real and palpable; we mean that propriety which regulates the due proportion between any object and the material of its composition.' 'Lightness and freedom' in metalwork were commended, and in glassware 'simple and pure outline', with no cutting. The only dubious feature of this exhibition was (as we already know) the preponderance of Felix Summerly material. The display of the exhibits, in which presumably 'Summerly' had a big hand, was praised as 'in excellent taste'. 10

In the summer of 1848, the Society of Arts moved even further in the direction of pure art, with a retrospective exhibition of the paintings and drawings of Mulready.11 This was the first of a series of such retrospectives of contemporary British artists intended to build up towards the foundation of a National Gallery of British Art, which had been proposed to the society by Cole in a paper given on 27 January 1847.12 Then, he had suggested Landseer as the first artist who should be so honoured, but somehow his old friend Mulready emerged as preferred candidate. Cole spared no pains to promote his friend's reputation, himself writing a notice that this was really a piece of cheerleading, designed to convince readers that this was an exhibition they should not miss. Cole described the animation of the private view, as 'artists and intelligent cognoscenti asked each other 'where was the old Venetian whose colour was finer? where the Dutchman that was more closely or more truly imitative of nature? and, above all, where was the old master of any school or any period, whose power of drawing' could compare with Mulready's. All these art exhibitions were not to the taste of some members of the Society of Arts, who were more used to inspecting such things as Hick's elliptograph, Cameron's improved method of bleaching palm-oil, Captain Dickinson's diving-bell, Osborn's window-sash suspender, Augustus Bosanquet's improved champagne cork or Dr Warnsborough's leaden nipple-shield (all these being on the programme in 1842-3). Signs of discontent emerged at a meeting on 22 November 1848 when the council faced complaints that, for a couple of years, 'Fine Arts' had been unduly privileged over 'the Mechanical Arts and Manufactures'. It acknowledged that 'this may perhaps be in part true'. The excuse was that 'in reforming the operations of the Society it was necessary to do one thing at a time. The Council have postponed the mechanics — not lost sight of them', and promised to pay more attention to them. 16

In 1849, however, the exhibition of British manufactures (7 March to 28 April) reached new splendours. The society's rooms, it was said, 'present an appearance of excessive brilliancy, if not of magnificence'.17 Bright lights were reflected from 'metal work, of a very superb and costly description' (including the still celebrated  or perhaps notorious — silver centrepiece, girt with dogs, designed by Prince Albert), while 'the specimens of glass likewise contributed greatly to the brilliancy of the scene'. Silks, carpets, lace and shawls appeared for the first time, adding further richness.18 Again, some 7o,ooo visitors attended and, unlike in previous years, the exhibition made a small profit. If such success continued, commented the Art-Journal, the society "will have to congratulate themselves on having effected one of the most startling triumphs upon record relative to the advancement of Decorative Art'. It discoursed at some length on the fact that the society was now looking ahead to a much more ambitious exhibition in 1851.19

Cole's irresistible progress was, however, briefly interrupted in 18 50, when there was a power struggle at the society. Already on 9 January Cole's diary faces the possibility that it might be necessary to 'fight for the exhibitions in the Socy of Arts'. Thomas Webster 'attempted to carry a motion in the Council to the effect, that all future exhibitions of manufactures and pictures should be stopped', but fost. He then tried to change the bylaws of the society, so as to diminish the influence of Cole and his allies, and, on 4 March, won. Cole regarded Webster as an opponent not only over the issue of exhibitions, but also in relation to patent law. Reform of the patent law was another of Cole's causes at this time, but Webster, as a patent lawyer, had a vested interest in preserving the system Cole was attacking. So Cole saw two parties contesting for supremacy, Webster's representing 'Patents or Retrogression', and his own, representing 'Exhibitions or Progress'. In the face of Webster's manoeuvrings, 'the Exhibition party took no trouble at all, being too content to leave their successful efforts to revive the Society to speak for themselves - and were defeated'. When Webster achieved his change in the bylaws, the progressive party - Cole, Russell, Fuller, the two Redgraves and Dilke  resigned from the council in disgust.21 Disgust turned to anger when they saw the slate of candidates assembled by Webster to replace them: just a gang of patent lawyers, in Cole's view. They decided to fight back, with support from the very top ("both Col Grey and Col Phipps' - Prince Albert's right-hand men  'thought there ought to be a fight at the Socy of Arts')22 and put up their own slate of progressive candidates. Cole's diary shows that over Easter weekend 8 5o he was constantly busy with the 'Society of Arts election', presumably canvassing vigorously. The vote was taken on 3 April, and the progressive party re-took control of the council.

This had not been originated by Cole, but arose from a suggestion from the Archaeological Institute. Towards the end of 185o, Cole became chairman of the council of the Society of Arts: the new bylaws required the council to elect its own chairman annually.27 One of the new bylaws compelled the chairman to deliver a policy statement at the beginning of his term. This Cole did on 15 January 185 1. Most of his measured speech would not be inappropriate on the lips of a modern managing director propounding a mission statement. But it also included this resonant Victorian sentiment.

It is with societies as with individuals, they do not, cannot prosper by following mere formulas. An individual who has no purpose in the world but to vegetate may as well not exist; and whether he be rich or poor, the world soon finds out that he is little less than an incumbrance, and treats him with indifference. To prosper, indeed, in these times, the man must be at work. We find the same analogies existing with societies. Unless they prove their ability to work, and work to some useful purpose, they become virtually extinct. A society cannot exist merely upon its name. Not only must it be alive to perform the functions it affects to do, but it must perform them in accordance with the advancing knowledge and increasing demands of the time.28

The Society of Arts continued to work with and for Cole through the rest of his life. In the immediate future, it and he would be chiefly preoccupied with the Great Exhibition.

Cole's activities as Felix Summerly and his work with the Society of Arts led to his involvement with the government schools of design.29 He had been well aware for years of the London School of Design and its provincial satellites. William Dyce, headmaster of the schools from 1838 to 1843, and the most powerful influence on their philosophy, was an acquaintance, perhaps a friend; and Cole was certainly on terms of close friendship with Richard Redgrave, John Horsley, H. J. Townsend and J. R. Herbert, who were all employed at the School in the 1840s.

The London School of Design had been founded in $\overline { { 1 8 } } 3 6$ as the government's swift, indeed pre-emptive, response to a select committee of Parliament on arts and manufactures, which sat in $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 3 5$ and $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 \boldsymbol { 3 } \in$ This was prompted by lobbying from various directions. The painter Benjamin Robert Haydon was pursuing a personal vendetta against the Royal Academy, which called in question the work of the Academy schools. Many members of Parliament were concerned, in the post-Napoleonic-war period, with the economic damage that the old enemy, France, might be doing to Britain. Such people as Dr John Bowring, whom Cole had known as editor of the Westminster Review, were arguing that French consumer goods were selling better than British because they were better designed, and that the best way for Britain to catch up was by improving design education. The radical MP William Ewart accordingly moved for a select committee and, as its chairman, steered it towards its strong recommendations in favour of a 'Normal School' of design, and 'the formation of . . . Public Galleries or Museums of Art . . . open gratuitously to the people'.30

The new school opened on 1 June $\mathbf { I } 8 3 7$ in rooms in Somerset House previously occupied by the Royal Academy, rooms which were now conveniently vacant on the Academy's move to share the new National Gallery building in Trafalgar Square. The first headmaster was the architect J. B. Papworth, probably only a makeshift appointment until someone better qualified could be found. William Dyce soon appeared to fill this role. He had been running a school of design in Edinburgh, along with Charles Heath Wilson. Dyce laboured to establish the government school, writing a textbook on design and founding branch schools in the provinces. His was not an easy task, however, because he was answerable to many masters. The school was ostensibly run by a council of artists and businessmen, but this was too big, and its members either failed to come to meetings or interfered too much. Real power over the school was exercised by the Board of Trade, which provided the money, but in matters of policy and educational philosophy the board was not much help. In $\tau 8 4 3$ Dyce, tired, disillusioned and under pressure from the council, stepped down to become inspector of provincial schools, and Charles Heath Wilson took over. Wilson fell out with both staff and students, and from $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 5$ the London school was embroiled in acrimony, in what was called the 'rebellion of forty-five'. Wilson's chief adversary on the staff, J. R. Herbert, was got rid of, but conditions did not improve and in $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 6$ his three senior colleagues publicly voiced their complaints. These colleagues were the architect C. J. Richardson, and the painters H. J. Townsend and Richard Redgrave (the latter standing in for J. C. Horsley who was on long leave abroad). Their letters of complaint (Redgrave's addressed venturesomely to the prime minister) had an effect. The council of the School of Design set up an internal inquiry over Christmas $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 6 { - 7 }$ , followed by another in summer 1 $8 4 7$ As a result, Wilson was dismissed but not replaced, a new smaller committee of management was formed, and in the school itself authority was left in the hands of a triumvirate of masters, which by $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 8$ (after some shifting around) comprised Redgrave, Townsend and Herbert. It was obvious that a triumvirate was an unstable arrangement, and the Board of Trade knew it had a problem on its hands. Cole was called in as a troubleshooter.

Though Cole's diary entry implies that he had not previously conversed with Shaw-Lefevre, they must have known each other by sight and reputation for many years. Shaw-Lefevre was a founder member of both the Athenaeum and the Reform Clubs, and he was working with the Board of Trade when Cole was helping Rowland Hill with the penny post. After talking to Cole he asked him to call at his office, which Cole did a few days later.

Through Shaw-Lefevre, Cole was introduced to the two Board of Trade ministers in Lord John Russell's Liberal government. The president of the Board of Trade was Henry Labouchere, a conscientious politician just turning 5o, with a solid reputation as an administrator. He had good Whig connections, though they were city rather than aristocratic ones, as both his mother and his wife belonged to the banking family of Baring. He was later to become secretary for Ireland and colonial secretary, and eventually Viscount Taunton, taking the name of the constituency that he represented throughout his life. His vice-president, a figure to be of central importance in Cole's life, was Lord Granville, a rising star in Liberal politics, in his early thirties $\scriptstyle ( p l a t e 2 4 )$ Known to his friends as Pussy and to his immediate family as Gink, Granville was born with a golden spoon in his mouth, though his rather squashed features hardly had the stamp of nobility. His family name was Leveson-Gower, the same as his cousins Lord Gower and the duke of Sutherland. Through his mother he was closely related to the dukes of Devonshire. Another cousin was the political diarist Charles Greville (a grandson of the duke of Portland), who shared his house in Bruton Street from 1849. His father was ambassador in Paris, and after education at Eton and Christ Church, Oxford, Granville spent a short time as an attaché in his father's embassy. As Lord Leveson, and through the influence of Lord Grey and Lord Carlisle (a cousin), he was given a safe seat in Parliament in $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 \boldsymbol { 3 6 }$ With genuine ability he soon made his mark as a Whig politician, so that at the age of 2 5 he was already an under-secretary in the Foreign Office. Despite his commitment to free trade, his appointment provoked howls of protest from the radicals, notably from John Bright of Manchester, as an example of Whig nepotism, for it was discovered that through his grandmother he was related to Lord John Russell, the prime minister.32 However, he weathered this storm without trouble, being a smooth, conciliatory and reliable operator. Much later he was to recall that, when he first met Cole, I did not like him at all'.33 Indeed, he was so wary that, when Charles Buller, at Cole's request, interceded for him with Granville, the latter (as Cole recorded) 'asked "If I shd get him into a scrape". To which Buller loyally replied 'that I had never done so with him'. 34 Granville and Cole became political allies for the whole of Cole's career and into his retirement, and, as far as the social gulf between them would allow, the two men were friends.

Cole got his official commission from the Board of Trade on 14 September. His task was to follow up a suggestion he had made to the Board for re-invigorating the schools by employing them 'in the production of designs for the Government departments'. Cole had urged that the schools should be exploited in this way partly because it would do them good, and partly also because of 'his belief that the schools might be made to a certain extent, if not altogether, self-supporting'.35 Here is an early statement of what was to be a leitmotiv in Cole's campaigning, later to be constantly repeated. From now on through the autumn, Cole was occupied with the school, spending a lot of time there, and no doubt getting under the feet of W. R. Deverell, the secretary to the new committee of management, and of Stafford Northcote, the Board of Trade official who dealt with most of the school's business. Northcote had started his career, after a first in classics at Oxford, by becoming secretary to W. E. Gladstone, who (still a Tory) served as vice-president and president of the Board of Trade from 1841 to 1845. When Gladstone resigned, Northcote stayed on at the Board of Trade and became legal adviser. Cole 'thought it my duty . .. to consult very freely with Mr. Northcote' in compiling his reports,36 and Northcote greeted the first as just the thing wanted'3 But it is perhaps hardly surprising that, under the continued impact of Cole, Northcote became guarded, if not hostile. Cole and his sympathisers eventually saw Northcote as 'Jesuitical', as he tried to keep his balance in the disputes.38

Cole produced three brief reports.3 In the first, dated 26 October 1848, he argued that designs for decorative art objects required by government departments should be 'systematically originated in the school by some of the more advanced pupils, and made to aid its present course of instruction as the culminating point of it'. Throughout its short history the school had been bedevilled by the question as to whether it should be a drawing school (a 'mere drawing school', as critics would say scornfully) or a school that gave some sort of practical insight into crafts and technical processes. Most people were agreed that it ought not to try to train technicians, but to produce designers who knew enough about techniques to make designs that could be successfully executed. 'Mr. Redgrave appears to me to draw the line exactly' wrote Cole, when he said, "We do not want to teach the art or process of weaving, but the art of designing for weavers".' Cole was all for practicality  at this point, anyway, though he was to change his views later  and he was able to quote from many earlier policy statements of the school and from recent testimony from teachers such as Dyce, Richardson and Townsend to back his view that this was the true function of the school. He felt that if students'designs were carried through into production, this would furnish an acid test of whether the school was succeeding in its educational purposes. 'If it be made a condition, as my proposal supposes, that the design must be useful, and its use is guaranteed, the course of instruction will become self-acting as it were, for it cannot halt at any stage before realising successful design. There is no escape from doing the right thing. Here speaks a Benthamite, a believer in self-regulating systems.

Cole's first report, making the theoretical case for using the schools productively, was politely received by the Board of Trade, so he proceeded to his second, dated 16 November, in which he considered how to put into practice his idea. He revealed that he had investigated how decorative art objects were currently commissioned by the Board of Works, the Admiralty, the Ordnance department and the royal palaces. So far as the last named were concerned, he had engaged the interest and support of Prince Albert. He now suggested that the Board of Trade should collect information about what departments wanted, should commission designs from the schools, and should get them prototyped by manufacturers. These designs, if successful, could then be put into production, and made available, not only for government use but also on the commercial market, in order to raise money for the government.

After presenting this report, Cole 'could not conceal from myself that . .. my suggestions were not very acceptable . . .; in fact that was what a member of the committee, Mr. Poynter, said expressly to me'. Faced with opposition, Cole 'saw that any such plan as I proposed would not touch what, in fact, I never concealed from Mr. Lefevre or Lord Granville appeared to me to be the inherent defect of the school, which was, as I thought, its constitution and management'.40 So Cole set to and wrote a third report, dated 29 December, which denounced the administration of the schools. 'An ungrateful task', he rather sanctimoniously remarked, but it would be 'a dereliction of duty' not to do it. He advocated a 'complete change of system'. He reviewed at length the lamentable history of the administration of the schools, citing as the principal defect the fact that  and here the Benthamite speaks  too many people without direct and accountable responsibility were supposed to run the school, and could not. The corollary of this arrangement, 'as in all cases where the responsibility is divided among numbers', was 'that almost the whole machinery is guided by the judgment of some one individual who is not responsible'. It is obvious whom he had in his sights. 'It is the secretary, rather than the committee, who originates proceedings, and gives the key-note to decisions . . . the secretary becomes the actual manager, irresponsible and unrecognised, far less cautious and prudent than he would be if he were fully recognised as manager.' Cole stopped at denunciation, not supplying any suggestions as to how things should be improved, except for pointing to the reform of the public records as an illustration of how to do it. Concluding that 'the defect being in the system, it is the system which must be altered', Cole resigned his official commission from the Board of Trade, refusing to accept the promised fee of £ 1o0.41

When challenged over the 'fallibility of artists in matters of taste', he insisted that 'I do not admit the fallibility in such matters of taste. I think taste has certain broad rules, like all other mundane subjects. People who attend to the matter can understand those rules, and arrive at conclusions which are generally the same.'44 He here assumes an oracular tone, which he was to adopt ever more readily.

The committee's draft report, ostensibly written by Gibson, was in fact composed by Cole, who produced a severely critical first version, expressing his own views. However, he misjudged the mood of the committee, who would not accept his draft, even when he toned it down. Finally, after long disputes, the committee backed a much tamer draft provided by another member.45 The final report was, in Cole's words, 'a thing of compromises'.46 This was widely acknowledged in the press, where the report was described as 'a severe disappointment',47 as 'wishy-washy' and 'a very "yea-nay" sort of document', which 'might as well have remained unwritten',4 and as 'milk-and-water', an example of how 'Parliamentary etiquette and the unwillingness to give personal offence to "powers that be" have thrown the veil of polite ambiguity over the ugly yet honest faces of Truth and Candour'.49

As we have seen, while battling for power at the Society of Arts, Cole identified two parties: 'Patents or Retrogression' vs. 'Exhibitions or Progress'. He belonged to the latter party, but his enthusiasm for exhibitions did not exclude a powerful desire to do something about patents also. When initiating his 'Felix Summerly' art-manufactures, he had taken care to register the designs he commissioned, and this had made him well aware of the current state of the British patent laws. The result was that he was eager to apply 'Progress' to 'Patents'.53

One of his first moves to forward patent law reform was linked to his developing relationship with the Board of Trade in late 1848. On 6 September 1848, he wrote to the joint secretary of the board, Sir Denis Le Marchant, offering his services as a consultant to report on both the Schools of Design and the patent laws. Interestingly, his letter was mostly about patents, with only a brief note at the end about the Schools of Design.54 Cole was at that time aiming at 'effecting a concentrated system', embracing education, promotion and protection in design and invention. He drew attention to many problems over intellectual property law: uncertainties over the extent of various kinds of rights, obstacles and delays in obtaining patents, the complexity of finding out what patents had already been granted, the difficulty of getting redress for infringement in 'what has been called a lottery of justice', and the imperfections of the system of specification.

In respect of decorative art objects, he pointed out that protection was currently afforded under the Copyright Act of 18 1 5, and the Registration Act of 1843. Under the latter act, protection was for three years, a fee was paid, and redress was relatively effective. Under the former, no fee was paid and the protection lasted 18 years, but redress was uncertain. Literary copyright lasted for 28 years. Cole believed that the Registration Act could be regarded as a first step in the reform of the patent laws, but it had its snags. For instance, 'it does not make clear whether an article registered in one class is protected from piracy in another; say, if registered in earthenware, is it protected also in iron or plastic materials generally?'

Above all, patents were expensive, 'seldom less than 25ol., and oftentimes more', and this discouraged inventors from seeking them. It thus also reduced the possible income to the state from patents. Cole did not think patents should make money, but he did claim, using one of his favourite words, that 'the whole system of promoting useful design might be made self-supporting to a very considerable extent'.

When, in response to this letter, the Board of Trade gave Cole his commission, it did not encourage him to pursue the question of patents, so he dropped it and concentrated on reporting on the Schools of Design. He then took up patents at the Society of Arts. He had already flown a kite at the society when on 9 March he 'carried resolution against "Tax on Invention". His resolution alluded to the cost of obtaining patents, the 'difficulties and anomalies' encountered in this bureaucratic process, and the inadequate protection afforded.5 Later in the year, on 4 September, the society set up a committee on the rights of inventors, which included, among its 29 members, Cole himself, Milner Gibson, Owen Jones, Herbert Minton and Charles Dickens — who, as an often pirated best-selling author, was already known as a campaigner for improvements in copyright. It was Cole who instigated the committee, and he was the driving force behind it.6 He offered to compile a report, which was in proof by the end of September, and printed on 2 December.57

Cole's treatise has been described as 'a remarkable document for its time, containing powerful rhetoric against the Chancery system'.58 Cole focussed first on the fact that 'a British subject has no rights of property whatever in that intellectual labour which produces invention or scientific discovery, excepting such as he can obtain by petition from the Crown'. In a strange survival of the feudal system, a Briton could only obtain legal protection over an invention by begging that it be bestowed as a privilege by the monarch, in the form of 'letters patent'. Though Britain, as Cole thundered, was 'the greatest manufacturing nation in the world, possessing boundless capital and most active industrial energy, combined with a vast amount of inventive ability, to which the genius of the people gives the most practical development', it was far behind other countries in its intellectual property laws.

By the end of the 1840s Cole had made himself an influential figure in design reform, advising the Board of Trade, and running powerful campaigns through the Society of Arts. It was only to be expected that his old love of journalism would reassert itself. In $\mathbf { 1 8 4 6 - 7 }$ , he had toyed with the idea of an 'Arts Newspaper', perhaps to be titled The Manufacturer.61 In March 1849 he started up a monthly magazine to campaign for design reform: the Journal of design and manufactures (plate 23, fig.7). This ran for 36 numbers, until February 1852, and might, perhaps, have kept going indefinitely. But by then, Cole had accepted an official post under the government, which meant that he had to cease campaigning.

Cole's previous attempts in journalism  The Guide, for instance, or the Historical Register  had been directed at a popular market. He had aimed to succeed in masscirculation journalism (though the 'mass' then was much smaller than now), and had failed  obviously because, despite his talents, he did not have the popular touch. By contrast with these earlier efforts, the Journal of design was a 'niche' publication, aimed at a particular interest group. It turned out that the group was large enough, and prepared to pay enough, to sustain the publication. Cole was negotiating about the new journal with Edward Chapman, of the publishers Chapman and Hall, on 6 December 1848, and found him well disposed towards the project. They came to an agreement, on 5 January following, that Chapman and Hall would pay the expenses of publication and divide the profits. The select committee on the Schools of Design, patent agitation at the Society of Arts and in Parliament, the Paris exhibition of 1849, and preparations for the London 185 1 exhibition were all covered. Indeed, the journal proceeded steadily on throughout the tumult of the Great Exhibition, providing a 'Monthly Report of Progress' that tells us much about what Cole was up to there.

Each number of the Journal of design had the same structure. As well as the specimens and illustrations of new manufactures, there were sections devoted to 'Original Papers', book reviews, 'Institutions' (under which Cole could campaign about the Society of Arts and the Schools of Design, among many other things), 'Table Talk' (soon to become 'Miscellaneous'), and correspondence. Although the journal includes a mass of interesting information, Cole did not intend that it should be merely a newspaper. It was also intended to teach, by including a 'doctrinal part', which would feature 'treatises developing sound principles of ornamental art'.63 The first three numbers, for instance, reprinted lectures on ornament by William Dyce, which set out many of the principles that came to constitute the Cole creed. (For example: 'Designs geometrically constructed and capable of repetition are absolutely necessary for the decoration of floors and all spaces where the notion of flatness and solidity must be preserved.')64 Later, in June-August 1849, came lectures by Redgrave on botany as a source of ornament, and in January and February 185 2 a lecture by Lyon Playfair on industrial education. There were further contributions from Dyce, and from John Bell, J. C. Horsley, Owen Jones and Matthew Digby Wyatt.

The most pointed piece on 'doctrinal' lines came in the number for August 1851, and was entitled 'Universal Infidelity in Principles of Design'. It quoted scathing articles about the Great Exhibition from The Times and the Morning Chronicle, and made play with the slogan "'art has its dogmas and its orthodoxy'. At this time, as the impact of the Great Exhibition sank in, many in the art world came to the conclusion that it had exposed an enormous amount of bad taste in British goods. This was the burden of the quoted newspaper articles. The Times derided the over-elaborate ornament that characterised the manufactured goods, and found itself able to admire only the machinery (where beauty arose from 'the stringent application of mechanical science to the material world', carried through with 'truthfulness, perseverance, and severity') and the building, of which it declared: 'All is plain, simple, and mathematically severe; yet who can enter that vast interior and not feel his heart swell within him at the solemn and majestic impression which it creates.' As for the Morning Chronicle, it was appalled by the variety of styles that had been imitated by ornamentists. 'The Exhibition shows that we are most skilful mimics . . . . But what do we create?'. In its editorial comment introducing these excerpts, the Journal declared: 'The very best things in the Exhibition are the least ornamented.'65 This theme was taken up again in the penultimate number in January 1852, where a piece reflecting on design reform over the last 15 years, asked 'Which direction is ornamental art likely to take in this country, toward elaboration or simplicity?'. The answer was the latter.

It is this kind of writing that has earned for the Journal the modest reputation it enjoys as a publication of significance in design history. In the 193os, as the Modern Movement in architecture and design began to be appreciated by advanced Englishspeaking critics, attempts were made to find precedents for its aesthetic in English art criticism of the nineteenth century. Opinions such as those just quoted were music to the Modernists' ears. Richard Redgrave, Matthew Digby Wyatt and Owen Jones were first presented as 'Prophets of the Modern Movement' in 1937.66 Cole and the Journal of design were brought forward to join them in 1948,67 and thereafter were regularly acclaimed, especially in the works of Nikolaus Pevsner.68 If such critics were inclined to attribute rather too distinct an outline to the doctrines of Cole and his allies, in their zeal to see them as proto-Modernists, other critics have looked more searchingly at the journal.69 One of them stresses that at first 'much confusion reigned in the editorial office' over what might be true principles of design; and among the new manufactures illustrated were 'all kinds of designs, later to be the targets of scorn and anathematisation'. Through the journal can be traced the development of the ideas of Cole and his group (from naturalism to conventionalism), and by the end a 'clear and consistent set of principles' was formulated.70

We can assume that Cole wrote most of the unsigned articles and paragraphs in the Journal of design. Often enough, his own voice seems to come through, as when he dismisses the retrogressive party at the Society of Arts as 'twaddling with incipient patents, nursing jealousies as to the origination of this lever or that crank'.71 And, although he had to hand over the editorship to his brother Charles for a time when the demands of the Great Exhibition became all-consuming, we can be sure that he was deeply involved as an editor, and that the journal stands as his intellectual construct. It is here, if anywhere, that we meet Cole as a thinker, rather than an administrative force.
