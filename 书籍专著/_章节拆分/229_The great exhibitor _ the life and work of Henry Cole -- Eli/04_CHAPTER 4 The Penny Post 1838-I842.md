## CHAPTER 4 The Penny Post 1838-I842

'Of all the events with which my career has been connected, no one I feel surpasses . . . the adoption of the Uniform Penny Postage. All the progress of mankind is helped on by freedom of thought expressed in writing. . .. It will be the glory of England for all time that she was the first country to adopt this ray of light, and the fame of Rowland Hill will be imperishable, as having discovered Uniform Penny Postage.'1 These are exalted words with which Cole describes the next project in which he was involved. But the penny post was a momentous development, the Victorian equivalent of today's internet.

Rowland Hill's famous pamphlet, Post Office reform; its importance and practicability, came out at New Year 1837. A small first edition, privately printed, and circulated to friends and politicians, was quickly followed by a larger edition, published by Charles Knight. Cole had a copy of the first edition (which remained with his books until they were sold by Sotheby's in 1893) so he may well have been a favoured recipient. His diaries are missing for 1835 and 1836, but it is likely that he and Hill (plate I 3) got to know each other during these years. Hill must have known Cole by repute, if not personally, and he would have read the article on the public records that Cole had written for the Penny Magazine. Their first known meeting was at the house of Francis Place in the spring of 1837. Place was also a favoured recipient of the first edition of Hill's pamphlet, but, as he told a friend, 'he had not thought it worth perusal, having supposed that it was only some nonsensical scheme for carrying letters all over England for a penny, and being wearied out with wild-goose proposals for all sorts of impractical measures'.2

Hill's scheme was indeed for carrying letters, weighing half an ounce or less, all over England for a flat prepaid rate of one penny. The existing postal system, the Royal Mail, had been set up as a government department in the late seventeenth century. Letters were carried by post boys on horseback until about 178o, when improved roads made possible the Post Office's dark red and black mail coaches, with coachmen and guards in red and gold uniforms. Postage was charged by distance and paid for on arrival by the recipient. While some towns, including London, had a local penny post, the average cost of postage was 8/d. in the 1830s, a huge amount for poor working people, who often refused to accept letters. A glance at the address would transmit the news and the letter could be declined. Hill himself had once, when on holiday in Scotland, conveyed his whereabouts to his family by messages on the wrappers of old newspapers, sent through the post. There were various ways of evading the high charges. Businessmen, wanting to write to several addresses in the same town, would write the letters on one very large sheet, which was cut up on arrival and the pieces distributed by hand. Letters were often entrusted to travellers going to the appropriate destination: once, when Cole and Marian went to visit their cousins in Essex, they found that every passenger in the coach was carrying a letter for somebody else.3

The government, and by extension members of the Lords and Commons, were exempt from paying postage. 'They could', as Cole mischievously remarked, 'receive an unlimited number of letters free of postage, of any weight, even a pianoforte, a saddle, a haunch of venison.' Their signature at the bottom left-hand corner of the outside of the letter meant that the post office would stamp it 'Free'. Politicians would spend hours signing their names on blank sheets of paper in order to have a supply of 'franks' to give away, or even sell, to friends and supporters. They were supposed to send out 10 a day and receive 15, but in the 1830s over 7,00o,ooo franks a year were sent or received. Cole would often get franks from Charles Buller or Edward Protheroe. The government regarded the postal service as a revenue-raising tax, so attempts to reform it had to ensure that changes did not reduce revenue. Although this requirement inhibited change, it is obvious that the obstructive system was ripe for reform.

Rowland Hill came from a radical, reforming background. The Hill family's school, Hazelwood, near Birmingham, became famous throughout the world for its liberal methods of teaching, and was visited by such figures as Jeremy Bentham, Henry Brougham, Thomas Malthus, Joseph Hume and Robert Owen. The school was so successful that Hill and his brother Matthew started a branch school in 1827, at Bruce Castle, in Tottenham, north of London. At the school there was no beating discipline being maintained by committees of the boys  and no fighting or bullying. Science was taught as a core subject, which was unheard of at the time. By 1833 Rowland and Matthew had handed this school over to their other brothers. Matthew then read for the Bar and entered Parliament, and Rowland devoted himself to inventing and to radical reforms. With his brother Edwin he developed and patented a rotary machine for printing newspapers, which would have been successful but for the fact that the excise tax had to be impressed on each sheet of a newspaper, and the Treasury would not allow it to be done on a continuous roll. Hill was also concerned with the development of South Australia.

After publishing his pamphlet, Hill had an opportunity to explain his ideas on the penny post to a government Commission of Inquiry into the Twopenny (local) Post on 13 and 23 February 1837. Here he dilated on his idea that the fact that payment had been made should be signified by 'a bit of paper just large enough to bear the stamp', a sticky-backed 'label'.4 Hill's scheme was so novel that in early discussions envelopes had to be described as 'little bags called envelopes' and stamps as 'small stamped detached labels - say about an inch square  which, if prepared with a glutinous wash on the back, may be attached without a wafer'.

After the publication of the second edition of Post Office reform at the end of February, Hill's ideas gained the support of the press. He sent Cole papers for him to use in The Guide. Petitions were presented to both Houses of Parliament. In November 1837 Robert Wallace succeeded in obtaining a select committee of the House of Commons to look into the matter. Wallace, MP for Greenock and a founding member of the Reform Club, had been agitating in Parliament for the improvement of the postal service since 1833, even before Hill took up the cause. He had given Hill a 'half hundredweight of raw material' to help him write his pamphlet.6 It was as the select committee got under way that Cole became involved.

At this juncture, Cole was on the point of taking a decision that would have changed the whole path of his career. For in February 18 3 8 he was asked by Charles Buller to accompany him, as his secretary, to Canada, where Buller was bound on official business with Lord Durham. Durham was the white hope of many radicals, as the man who would become their prime minister if they ever achieved power. A strange mixture, he was a fabulously rich coal-mine owner, possessor of a Gothic castle and a peerage bestowed by the Tories, and had a romantic Byronic air, intensified by his poor health. He was, nonetheless, genuinely accessible to the humbler classes. His radical credentials were established by the part that he played as lord privy seal in Lord Grey's cabinet, in the preparation of the Reform Bill. The fact that he got quite out of step with his colleagues and eventually resigned, only enhanced his reputation as radical leader-in-waiting. After some time on the fringe of political life, he was sent out as governor-general of Canada, to settle ominously rising tensions between French and English colonists, with Buller as his right-hand man. Cole would have liked to go with them. He said that 'the temptations to do so were very great, but the tears of my dearest and best adviser, and my recent return to public service with the Records, prevented my acceptance'.7 Cole did well not to join this mission, for Durham tried to take a strong line, miscalculated, to the embarrassment of the government at home, and soon resigned in an atmosphere of muddle and recrimination, from which his reputation never recovered. At the time when Marian shed tears before Henry, she was expecting another child, and there was relief when, on the night of 3 1 May/1 June, a girl was born, 'fat and healthy', after Marian had been in labour for only two hours. They named her Laetitia after Henry's mother.

When Buller's offer arose, Cole had just started to work for Hill. Hill's postal reforms were being promoted by a pressure group of city merchants  the 'Mercantile Committee' - led by George Moffatt, MP for Herefordshire, a wholesale tea importer. Hill, meeting Cole by chance in Piccadilly, on 9 February 1838, asked him to 'assist him with Post Office Agitation', and a few days later Cole became secretary of the Mercantile Committe. As soon as Cole had received Lord Langdale's permission to take on the extra work - 'on the understanding that my work on Public Records was not prejudiced'  he went to the city to confer with Moffat and William Ashurst, a solicitor and parliamentary agent who acted as legal adviser to the committee. Moffatt and Ashurst, like Cole and several other supporters of Hill, were members of the Reform Club. Drawing on his experience in running The Guide, he planned a broadsheet that would contain just enough news to pass as a newspaper, though it wouldprarily be an organ of propaganda. Charles Whiting 'agreed to take the risk of a paper if the Mercantile Committee would take 500 copies at £6. 6'. The paper was titled The Post Circular. Or Advocate for a Cheap Swift and Sure Postage. Since (as we have seen) newspapers were carried free by the Post Office, this paper duly went free, and Cole thus forced the Post Office to carry propaganda against itself free of charge all over the country. The Post Office, Cole crowed, thus became 'the chief instrument for reforming itself'. The first number of the Circular appeared on 14 March, published by Henry Hooper, the well-known bookseller of Pall Mall East, who supported the campaign. The Circular was written by Cole, and it ran during the parliamentary session. About 1,7 5o copies were published of each number, and they were widely distributed to all who seemed to Cole to be influential: 'three thousand clergymen . .. every provincial newspaper ... most of the permanent and subscription libraries throughout the United Kingdom . . . Town Clerks . . . Chambers of Commerce . . . the commercial rooms of most respectable inns', and many individuals. A few were sold for 2/d. Sometimes a whole issue, apart from a few snippety news items, consisted of a specimen of a petition, with instructions for how to collect signatures and send them to a member of the House of Commons or the House of Lords, post free. The 1oth number contained a poster ready to be put up. Cole used his experience and colour sense to have the poster printed in a striking light red and green.

The select committee began sitting in March and did not finish taking evidence until July. On 17 March, it agreed on the establishment of a uniform rate of postage throughout the country. Thus Hill's vital principle was established. But the committee would not accept the penny rate, and only after much argument, and forceful chairmanship from Wallace, did it settle for a two-penny rate. Henry Warburton, Cole's radical friend, was recruited to prepare the report, and after some weeks had gone by, Hill asked Cole to help Warburton by preparing abstracts of the evidence. Nonetheless, there were more delays and Hill became anxious, as did Cole. In October, Hill wrote to tell Cole that Warburton had promised the report would be ready for the printer in the following week, but added: 'Warburton is not easily led'. He suggested that the Mercantile Committee might be able to put pressure on him, and remarked: 'I am much amused by your perseverance. If we could bring together 20 such men as yourself & Ashurst we should quickly carry the question'.° The report finally appeared in March 1839, when it was welcomed by the press. The Spectator devoted a special issue to it and printed it in full.

The reformers kept up the pressure, urging the government to implement the select committee's suggestions through legislation, and, indeed, to go farther and introduce the penny rate, as at first envisaged. The Mercantile Committee revived the Circular to this end, and Cole clearly relished the opportunity again to exercise all the arts of persuasion. In reply to the charge that the penny post would greatly increase the amount of mail carried in the mail coaches, to the extent that the transport costs would increase twelvefold, Cole resorted to graphic communication. He drew a caricature in the number for 30 April 1839, showing a mail coach, laden with parcels of different sizes, demonstrating how a small bag of letters paid the cost of transporting a large bag of newspapers and another of franked letters that were taken free. He took the statistics on which the caricature was based from those published by the Post Office itself. Doubtless pleased with his work, he had an extra 3,ooo copies of this issue printed and sent them to all members of the Lords and Commons.

He even tried drama as a means of persuasion. The previous autumn he had written a scene about postal reform to go into a Christmas pantomime, Fair Rosamund. It was accepted by the eminent actor-manager William Macready, and handed on for performance at the Drury Lane theatre, but there was not enough rehearsal time to put it on. Now Cole followed up this dramatic experiment by writing a skit on postal reform, which put the arguments for and against the penny post into the mouths of the Queen, Lord Melbourne, Lord Lichfield (the postmaster general) and Rowland Hill. The Queen, speaking on behalf of reform, has much the best of the argument, and concludes by suggesting that Lord Lichfield and the secretary of the Post Office should step down. 10 Some 2,ooo copies of this were printed and sent to the Lords and Commons, and another 2,ooo were handed out. A cheap edition of 10o,ooo copies was published and was either sold or given away. Then in March 1839 Cole arranged with the publishers, Chapman and Hall, for a further 40,ooo to be bound into one of the monthly parts of Dickens's Nicholas Nickleby, then being serialised.

Despite such mettlesome campaigning, the prospects of legislation still seemed remote. Fears were not allayed when, on 25 May, Lord John Russell, then Home Secretary, assured Warburton privately that the government hoped to introduce the penny post. Moffatt, with Cole's help, immediately arranged a public meeting at the Mansion House to collect more petitions. After a few days, when nothing more had been said in Parliament, he ostentatiously cancelled the meeting and announced that it was postponed until the government did more than 'hope' to bring in the measure. Cole thought that it was Moffatt's 'sagacity and courage' in doing this that forced Lord John to announce that there would be a resolution to introduce the penny post. This encouraged but did not satisfy the reformers, for they felt that a resolution could easily be rescinded and what was needed was an act of Parliament.

Petitions in favour of the penny post continued to come to both Houses of Parliament. Cole exploited this device of persuasion to the full, estimating that by 20 July 1839, 2,007 petitions with 262,809 signatures, had been gathered. On 5 July Spring Rice, the Chancellor of the Exchequer, presented his budget and promised a bill, provided that the House would pledge itself to make good any resulting reduction in the revenue. Possible loss of revenue was still the great impediment to change, and enabled the Chancellor of the Exchequer to keep the whip hand in matters of postal reform.

A few days later the postponed meeting at the Mansion House was held and 'passed off brilliantly'. A petition with 12,500 signatures, that had been collected that day, was presented to the House of Commons, and despite further obstruction the Postage Duties Bill was brought to the House on 18 July. On 24 July it went through the committee stage unopposed in three quarters of an hour. There was still the possibility that the Lords would throw it out, and Cole occupied himself, during much of this 'critical period' before the bill came on, in calling on peers to get them to attend the debate and vote for it. He found that considerable support could be expected in the Upper House, provided that the bill was well explained. Accordingly he prepared some notes for Hill to take to Lord Melbourne.

When the bill reached the Lords on 5 August, Cole and Hill sat on the steps of the throne. Richard Cobden, with whom Cole had stayed in Manchester when campaigning for postal reform, exclaimed, 'There go the Corn Laws!' He felt 'that new flood-gates of knowledge were . . . opened' by the new postal legislation, and that prospects were thereby improved for reform in the field with which he himself was most concerned, the Corn Laws. Cole was very nearly caught up in Cobden's movement. The Corn Laws had been introduced after the Napoleonic Wars, to protect Tory landowners, who grew most of the wheat in the country. Their effect was to keep the price of bread artificially high, which was a great hardship to the poor, particularly in the new industrial towns like Manchester, where Anti Corn-Law associations had been established. Manchester's association was much more active than that in London, and in 1839 Cobden sent John Smith, chairman of the Manchester Chamber of Commerce, and some others to London to try to revive the London association. The Manchester party called on Henry Warburton, who sent them to see Francis Place, and he gave them a letter of introduction to Cole. Consequently they tried to persuade Cole to become secretary of the Anti-Corn Law League. He was too busy with postal reform, and declined this interesting offer.

He did what he could to help Cobden, however, and persuaded his new friend William Makepeace Thackeray to do some drawings that pointed out the price difference between bread in England and on the Continent, where it cost less by a third.15 In a letter to Cobden, Cole recommended Thackeray as 'a genius, both with his pencil and his pen. His vocation is literary. He is full of humour and feeling'. 16 He and Cole had first met two years before when Thackeray was editing The Constitutional. They had much in common, as they were both struggling to support their families, and they were both grieving over the death of a child (Thackeray's second baby daughter having died that spring). Thackeray's sketches were published in The Anti-Corn Law Circular, which had been founded to campaign in the same way as Cole's Post Circular had done for the penny post. Cole also helped Henry Warburton collect material for a speech on the Corn Laws in the House of Commons. It was another seven years before the potato famine in Ireland precipitated the repeal of the Corn Laws in June 1846. Cobden would later pay tribute to Cole as 'a clever agitator, and a public spirited fellow'.17

One of the matters in which Cole was to become most involved was methods of pre-payment. Even before Cole joined Hill at the Treasury, the lords of the Treasury had, on 23 August, invited 'artists, men of science, and the public in general' to send in ideas of the best way to arrange for this. He could not return to work for two weeks, and, when he did get back to the Treasury on 23 November, he was faced with a difficult meeting with officials from the Post Office.

At the Post Office, both the staff and the politicians were against any change at all. Lord Lichfield, the Postmaster General, had said of the penny post that 'of all the wild and visionary schemes which he had ever heard or read of, it was the most extraordinary'.22 But he eventually came round to acquiesce in the measure, because he chose to believe that the revenue to the Post Office would go up. Colonel Maberly, the joint secretary of the Post Office, was a more serious adversary, as he worked from within the institution itself. He was socially smart, having served in a fashionable regiment and married the sister of an Irish peer. He looked down on Hill, calling him 'that man from Birmingham', and resisted every change until he was finally moved to the Board of Audit in 1855. His entry in the Dictionary of National Biography says that 'for more than seven years Maberly continued in authority, and improvement of every kind was delayed and some millions of public money were wasted'. This is less bland than is usual for an entry in the DNB. It was written by George Boase, the Cornish scholar and bibliophile, who came from Penzance and fully appreciated the need for an efficient post to outlying parts of the country. Hill had already encountered Maberly in the course of his earlier agitation, and when he met him formally for the first time in September he wrote of the meeting: 'I was received with great apparent civility, but little things were said & done, I firmly believe with the intention of annoying me. To promote the new system, Cole compiled a Post Office Almanac. But this was stopped when Kelly's, the big publisher of directories and almanacs generally titled 'Post Office Directories', took out an injunction against it. In his opinion, no copyright had been established by Kelly's. Within a few days he issued a new edition of his Almanac, with a defiant statement inside the cover pointing out that 'the Injunction obtained by the Proprietor of "The Post Office London Directory" applies chiefly to the old Post Office Regulations', which had now lapsed. Another by-product of Cole's work was a letter balance. He foresaw the need for accurate small weighing machines to be used at home to ensure that letters were under half an ounce in weight, and accordingly designed one. It was published by Henry Hooper and it quickly sold out.31 Cole made £3o out of it, paid him by Henry Hooper, which may have been a commission or a payment for the copyright. Hill, with his brother Edwin's help, had meanwhile arranged for scales with weights to be designed and made for use in Post Offices. After stamps went on sale, other weighing machines and spring balances were quickly available to save the public visits to the Post Office. Edwin Hill joined the team to supervise the machinery to print the stamps.

The penny rate for London was brought in on 5 December 1839, with a uniform rate of fourpence for the rest of the country. On 10 January 1840 the penny rate for the whole country began, even though no stamps were ready and the public had to take their letters to a post office to pay their Id. Hill and Cole tactfully stayed away from the main Post Office at St Martin's le Grand on that first day, but they heard that when the windows closed 'the mob, delighted at the energy displayed by the officers, gave one cheer for the Post Office and another for Rowland Hill'. Two days later, Cole visited the main Post Office 'to see operations', and found it 'very noisy & crowded at the Pay Windows'. 32

The great hall was nearly filled with spectators, marshalled in a line by the police, to watch the crowds pressing, scuffling, and fighting to get first to the window. ... Formerly one window sufficed to receive letters. On this evening six windows, with two receivers at each, were bombarded by applicants. As the last quarter of an hour approached, and the crowd still thickened, a seventh window was opened, and that none might be turned away, Mr Bokenham made some other opening, and took in letters and money himself.33

The famous 'penny black' stamp was introduced on 6 May. There had been dire warnings in the press that stamps would be forged or used repeatedly. To combat this many complicated designs had been submitted in the prize proposals, but in the end an engraved portrait of the Queen was chosen as the motif on the stamp, because she was unmistakeable.34 A medal designed by William Wyon, engraver to the Royal Mint was used as the source of the Queen's image. Copied by Henry Corbould, and engraved by the Heaths, it had an engine-turned background, and was printed in sheets of 240.

The Treasury decided to publish a decorated cover to celebrate the new cheap postal system. While Cole was still laid up with his bad leg, he made a drawing and discussed it with his friend, the engraver John Thompson. Several well-known painters were asked to submit ideas, but Francis Baring did not like any of them and on his advice, Cole approached William Mulready, one of the most successful painters of the time, 63 years old, and a Royal Academician of over 2o years' standing. When Cole called on him on 13 December, he found him 'very intelligent and affable'. 'He appeared readily to enter into the idea & agreed to make a trial.' Within two days Mulready made 'a highly poetic design', which Cole showed to Hill, who liked it. Over the next week there were several meetings at Cole's house with Mulready, Hill and Thompson to discuss the design and its engraving. Cole admired the extremely careful finish of Mulready's work: what Mulready himself once called "an ostentatious mastery of execution'.35

The design was approved by the Royal Academy and the cabinet, and all thought well of it. William Wyon took the trouble to write to Cole about it: 'Although I expected much from him [Mulready] it exceeded my expectations & I should really regret it if so admirable a design should be lost to the public'.36 John Thompson engraved it on brass, William Clowes printed it, and it went on sale on 1 May. On 2 May the first of the rude comments about it came out in The Times. 'We must say that we never beheld anything more ludicrous than the figures or allegorical device by which it is marked with its official character . . .. Cruikshank could scarcely produce anything so laughable'37 Comments like this were published over the next few weeks, and there were some cruel caricatures. Many poked fun at Mulready's Irish origins. All observed what even the Queen, who was sent a proof - and also Mulready, Thompson, Hill, Cole, Baring, Wyon and the Royal Academicians - had failed to notice: that one of the angels lacked a leg. Hill wrote in his diary on 12 May: 'I fear we shall be obliged to substitute some other stamps for that designed by Mulready, which is abused on all sides. In departing widely from the established "lion and unicorn" nonsense, I fear we have run counter to settled opinions somewhat rashly. I now think it would have been wiser to have followed established custom in all the details of the measure [the introduction of the penny post] where practicable. '38 In his autobiography, Cole wrote: 'The postage cover was for dry commercial use, in which sentiment has no part . . . Had an allegorical fresco for any public building been required to symbolize the introduction of the universal penny postage, nothing could have been better than Mulready's design.' The adverse publicity was a disaster for Mulready, but it did bring him to the notice of Francis Baring's younger brother who became one of his patrons. And for Cole the episode brought lifelong friendships with both Mulready and the whole of the Thompson family.

Cole went on working at the Treasury until 1842, trying to bring in a parcel post and to improve the sending of letters abroad. Characteristically, he preserved all his work relating to the penny post in albums, which are now in the Victoria and Albert Museum. Then the paths of Cole and Hill parted, Hill becoming a director, and later chairman, of the Brighton Railway. He returned to the Post Office in 1846, where Maberly was still in charge and still opposed to every change. It was not until 1854 that Hill was appointed sole secretary, with his brother Edwin as his assistant. Honours and honorary degrees were heaped on him, and in 1864 the Society of Arts gave him its Albert Gold Medal. He was the first recipient of this honour, instituted in memory of the Prince Consort.

Cole, in his work for the penny post, learned much about printing and illustration, which influenced his future interests. But what stands out in his postal work is his zest and exuberance as an agitator. When campaigning for the reform of the Record Commission, he had been fighting for his livelihood, a serious business. During the postal reforms, however, personal survival was no longer a problem, and he could throw himself into campaigning with enjoyment, and exult in his talents for mischief and irreverence, earning Rowland Hill's praise for 'those amusing devices with which my friend Mr. Henry Cole knew so well how to strike the public eye'.39
