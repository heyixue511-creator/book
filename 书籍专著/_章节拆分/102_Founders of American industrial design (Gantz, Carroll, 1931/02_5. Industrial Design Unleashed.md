## 5. Industrial Design Unleashed

The Fortune article was written anonymously, but is now known to have been authored by architect George Nelson (19081986), 26 at the time, and the winner of the 1932 Rome Prize in architecture. Nelson was then the assistant editor of Architectural Forum magazine, and would within a decade become a nationally known industrial designer. Nelson was born in Hartford, Connecticut, and graduated from Hartford Public High School in 1924, and from Yale in 1928 with a degree in architecture. In 1929 he was hired as a teaching assistant while obtaining his second bachelor's degree in fine arts at Yale in 1931. In Rome, he traveled throughout Europe and met many modernist pioneers, whom he interviewed for articles in Pencil Points magazine. Through these articles, he described the work of Walter Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, Le Corbusier, and Gio Ponti. In 1933 He returned to America to devote himself to writing. In other words, manufacturers had failed to attract new consumers through the design of new and different products, precisely the service that industrial design offered. Nelson stated the essential case for industrial design:

Whenever two products are equal in point of utility and price, the one that looks more attractive to the purchaser will be bought first. When the demand is sufficient to take only one of two products, the maker of the attractive product stays in business, the maker of the other goes bankrupt.²

After this do-or-die challenge to his business audience, Nelson briefly provided historical context by describing how the art industries traditionally and effectively used design, but that the engineer-run artless industries of the 20th century were unprepared and unstaffed to do so when style became important to their largely functional products. He then described in detail how industrial designers worked, and what benefits they could bring to business. The main thrust of the article focused on "a few leading industrial designers" and what they had done in the last few years by citing their previous experience, their clients, their fees, their typical achievements, and photo-portraits of ten of them. Those included were many who have already been described in this book. Quotes that follow are from the Fortune article.

Norman Bel Geddes, now a tousled, curly-haired 41-year-old, was described by Nelson as a "bomb-thrower" and the "P.T. Barnum of industrial design," but three pages are devoted to his background in stage design, his business practice, and his book, Horizons, including an entire sidebar page, "Story of a Stove," that was devoted to his stunningly modern and simple, but practical, stove concepts for the Standard Gas Equipment Corporation (SGE). The president of SGE, Frank Roberts, had walked into Bel Geddes's office and requested a new stove design within two weeks and offered to pay \$1,500. Bel Geddes explained that he did not just draw pretty pictures, that study to produce a better stove would take a year, and required \$50,000 as an advance of royalties. Shocked, Roberts conferred with his directors, but he decided to take the risk.

Bel Geddes put an engineer in the SGE plant, studied competitors' methods, and interviewed hundreds of housewives and store dealers. His report and recommendations numbered 300 pages. Photos in the article showed how Bel Geddes reduced the number of stove components currently stocked by SGE from hundreds to just 16 through standardization, which he demonstrated like a showman with colored block models. When the stove appeared on the market in January 1933, the first one all white, Robert's decision was vindicated: "Housewives wanted the Geddes stove. Utilities that had never heard about the line before welcomed it. Sales rose, doubled, held their gains."

## Nelson concluded the article,

Most manufacturers agree that the industrial designer has stimulated all those he has met in the company's organization, given them new ideas, new energy, a new atitude. Designers will not cure our industrial ills, for our ills lie deeper than design. But the success of industrial designers is symptomatic of a changing attitude on the part of the manufacturers, a growing recognition of the old American ideal of progress: cheaper and better goods for all.4

Nelson would establish a New York architectural design office in 1936 with William Hamby, which would continue until after the war, and Nelson would become a prolific and dynamic leader in postwar furniture design and establish his own office in New York in 1947.

The article had an immediate impact, not only on the small community of industrial designers, who benefited enormously from the national publicity and recognition, but on the large segment of the business community that had little or no previous knowledge of industrial design and how it could increase sales. Suddenly they made the connection between their primary problem of diminishing sales and the obvious solution of new designs to generate new demand. The traditional manufacturing image of industrial art as simply the addition of decorative surface embellishment, had been transformed into industrial design, the reshaping and modernizing of the entire product.

The February 1934 Fortune article spurred industrial designers to toot their own horn. They organized an Art and Industry Exhibition at Rockefeller Center, sponsored by the National Alliance for Art and Industry, that featured 1,000 products by 100 designers, to illustrate "what designers are doing .. to help the engineer sell his mechanical devices." The exhibit included Raymond Loewy's iconic chrome-plated, teardrop, highly streamlined pencil sharpener (which was never produced); Henry Dreyfuss's check printer, a bathroom scale by Harold Van Doren; furniture by Gilbert Rohde; and a Wurlitzer radio by Russel Wright. By this time, the popularity of Art Deco and French Art Moderne was fading, and streamlining was in the ascendancy. Egmont Arens reported "an amazing response and welling up of national enthusiasm for 'streamlining,' which had captured the American imagination to mean modern, efficient, well-organized, sweet, clean, and beautiful."

MoMA's purpose, of course, was to establish machine-made products as a legitimate form of modern art, in the same way it had promoted that status for architecture in its 1932 exhibition, and to obsolete the Arts and Crafts movement, as Philip Johnson said, "to demonstrate . that the decorative arts were no longer in existence, but that art could still be made without the handcraft approach—so it was an anti-handcraft show." In fact, the Arts and Crafts movement had already expired. The famous Wiener Werkstätte (Viennese Workshops), founded in 1903, had just been closed, not by the Nazis, but because the high prices for its products had caused it to fail in its social cause of ensuring that the life of everyone was enhanced by everyday objects designed by artists. Now industrial designers were actually implementing that idealistic objective in America.

MoMA had another less obvious agenda, and that was to co-opt industrial design, this new art, under the leadership wing of architects, as had been the case in Europe. MoMA hated streamlining, which was harshly criticized by MoMA director Alfred Barr: "Their designs are aimed at sales appeal of the most superficial kind."1 MoMA resented that advertising artists, set designers, writers, automotive and furniture designers were dominating industrial design and being heralded in the press and business world. MoMA wanted to take over the new profession for architects. This goal remained for the rest of the decade, when MoMA would launch a full-scale attack to dominate industrial design, which would succeed only in modifying the general style from streamlining to "organic" design. But the term "streamlining" still lives in our language, to describe the simplification and speeding up of things, as in phrases such as we streamlined our office procedures."

The MoMA Machine Art Exhibition also established MoMA's collection protocol for the future, as MoMA acquired 100 of the manufactured objects in the exhibition into its permanent collection, a practice it would continue for decades, just as traditional museums acquired collections of paintings or artifacts. The exhibit boosted the sale of American-made goods, strengthened the growing alliance between artists and manufacturers, and broke down the wall between art and commercial products, just as Duchamp had done in 1917 with his urinal as art. In 1928, following the lead of GM, Chrysler had established an internal Art and Color Department of five, headed by Herbert V. Henderson, but the department was subordinated to Chrysler's powerful engineering staff, and designers were relegated to cosmetic details such as grilles and hood ornaments. Chrysler's genuinely new innovative engineering design, under way since 1927, was by leading engineers Carl Breer, Fred Zeder, and Owen Skelton. Breer had designed the successful first Chrysler in 1924 and claimed it was his idea in 1927 to "follow nature" by designing a scientifically engineered, streamlined car. It was radically innovative in engineering and esthetics.

Wind tunnel experiments led to an ideal streamlined form. To achieve the tapered aerodynamic tail of a teardrop, the engineers moved the engine up over the front axle and placed the rear seat within the wheelbase rather than over the rear axle. This more evenly distributed the weight between the front and rear wheels, making a more comfortable ride and better handling. Rather than a single flat plane, the windshield was comprised of two panes meeting in a V and sloping to the rear. The body was the first unitary construction, with the frame and body shell integrated into a lightweight but rigid structure.

After the basic body form had been determined aerodynamically, stylists under Henderson were allowed to add cosmetic Art Deco details, such as parallel, decorative chrome elements on the grille, bumpers, and hood vents. The grille looked like a chrome waterfall splashing over the huge hood. Norman Bel Geddes, the country's foremost industrial designer, was engaged as a consultant in September 1933, and he reduced the number and widened the vertical chrome bars on the waterfall grille, raked the windshield back, and added side vents for cooling. The modernistic interior had rubber mats instead of carpets, and the seats were mounted in a frame of steel tubing. Introduced to the public in 1934, the car was hailed as the first functionally streamlined production car, the Airflow, used for both the Chrysler and DeSoto new models. Bel Geddes happily promoted the design to the public.

The Airstream would outsell the Airflow by five to one the first year, and nine to one in 136. The market failure of the Airflow would go down in automotive history as the result of styling too advanced for popular acceptance, and the failure of engineers to grasp the subtleties ofappearance design. The entire industry concluded that even meticulous and innovative engineering could not sell an ugly car. The Airflow failure would become a powerful argument for stylists, who possessed the elusive skill of designing for public emotions and tastes, rather than just for practical and functional purposes like engineers.

Despite the disaster of the Airflow, and despite the scientific fact that streamlining of cars had little effect on operation except at high speeds, streamlining would remain a popular style for the remainder of the decade, because the style was imprinted on consumers' minds as an icon of modernity and progress, which they hoped would take them out of the continuing Depression.

Streamlining was front and center on May 26, 1934, as the Century of Progress Exposition reopened in Chicago for its second year. Headlining the opening was the national demonstration of several brand new, streamlined trains. On May 26, the diesel-powered Burlington Zephyr, known as the "Silver Streak," set a speed record for travel between Denver, Colorado, and Chicago, Illinois, by making a "dawn to dusk" dash over the 1,015-mile distance in 13 hours and 5 minutes (normal trains took 25 hours) at an average speed of 77 mph, and arrived at the Century of Progress Exposition on opening day to appear on the Wings of the Century stage for display. The Zephyr was built by the E.G. Budd Manufacturing Company, Philadelphia, for the Chicago, Burlington and Quincy Railroad (CB&Q), commonly known as the Burlington, to promote and advertise passenger rail service. Christening was on April 18.

The Zephyr consisted of three cars: the power car, baggage-coach combine, and the coachobservation car. It was made of bright stainless steel, requiring no painting or maintenance, and was fastened together using a new technique of electric spot-welding developed by Budd. The entire train weighed the same as a single conventional train car and reached 112 mph during its record run. A 600 hp, eight-cylinder, Winton engine powered a generator, and the electricity generated was fed to electric traction motors connected to the axles in the front wheel truck. There were four trucks, one between each car, and one at each end. The nose design was a slanted half cylinder, often referred to as a "bathtub shrouding" or "shovel nose," with forward-facing windows to provide full visibility for the operator.

The exterior streamline design was by aeronautical engineer Albert Gardner Dean, in collaboration with Philadelphia architects John Harbeson (1888-1986) and Paul Philippe Cret (1876-1945). After display at the exposition, the train was taken on a 31-state, 222-city publicity tour. More than two million people saw the train before it entered regular service in November between Kansas City, Missouri, and Lincoln, Nebraska. The very name, Zephyr, would soon be applied to products to capitalize on its popularity as a term of speed and modernity, such as Kem Weber's streamlined Zephyr digital clock design of 1934, and the Lincoln Zephyr of 1936.

The Burlington Zephyr was a hard act to follow, but there was another streamliner that tried to do so. The gasoline-powered M-10000 (its first design patent number was D100,000, followed by U.S. patents D100,001 and D100,002), produced by Union Pacific Railroad, visited the Chicago exposition after a tour of 68 cities that totaled 13,000 miles, everywhere attracting press attention and crowds that approximated one million. It was the first internal combustion, lightweight, streamlined, express passenger train in the United States, delivered to the railroad on February 12, 1934. Named on the design patent were Eugene Adams, Martin P. Bloomberg, William H. Mussey, and William B. Stout, all engineers at Pullman-Standard, the body developer.
