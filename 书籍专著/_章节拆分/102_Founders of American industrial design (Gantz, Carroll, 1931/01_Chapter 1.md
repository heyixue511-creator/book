## The "Artless" and "Art" Industries

The Machine Age (19181941), as it was known even at the time, not only included the Great Depression, but also the preceding decade after World War I when America led the worlnindustrialization, scice, tehnology, engineering, and consuerism. Excet for brief postwar depression from 1920 to 1921, as President Warren Harding took office, the 1920s in America were a period of economic boom and prosperity, as American industry returned to peacetime production to meet consumer demand for the many new mechanical b     - tities and at affordable prices.

Among these were automobiles, led by Ford's Model T, which by 1918 comprised 50 percent of the auto market and which epitomized American mass production, also known then as Fordism. Many home appliances used electricity, which, although only half the country was electrified, mostly in urban areas, was becoming more widespread. Machines, large and small, were the leading edge of technology. Refrigerators were replacing iceboxes, ranges were replacing woodstoves, and vacuum cleaners were replacing carpet sweepers. There were electrical appliances such as mixers, fry pans, coffee pots, waffle irons, juicers, irons, roasters, and toatrs. To many consumers, many of whom were women, these industrial products for the kitchen were perceived to be as magical a technology as personal home robots would seem to us today.

Prices sound very reasonable today (\$260 for the Model T and \$52 for a vacuum cleaner in 1918), but in 2013 dollars, the Ford would cost \$4,020 and the vacuum cleaner \$804. Still, many households had good wages (\$2,000 per year in 1918, or \$30,700 in 2013 dollars), and since almost 50 percent of working-class wives worked for wages, many were two-income families. Credit buying was also available. Can you imagine paying 40 percent of your annual salary for a vacuum cleaner? Still, to many families, and with installment plans becoming quite common, it seemed like the American Dream coming true. After 50 years of invention and development of a massive industrial infrastructure, including gigantic steam engines, hydroelectric plants, electrified cities, factories, railroads, bridges, and paved roads, average citizens were finally seeing the products of mass production and industrialization in their own homes. Technology had finally reached their personal, daily lives.

The production system had been refined, improved, and made more efficient by Philadelphia engineer Frederick Winslow Taylor (1856-1915), who in 1911 published The Principles of Scientific Management, a system of efficiency experts using stopwatches to measure every step of production in order to reduce time and labor. This process of industrial organization became known as Taylorism, and was known everywhere as the companion to Fordism, the twin jewels in the crown of mass production.

All mass-produced, new, electrical, and mechanical consumer products were invented, designed, and built by engineers, the backbone and champions of 20th-century industrialization. Many were famous and known worldwide. Herbert Hoover, who would become U.S. president in 1928, was himself a wealthy mining engineer. Thomas Edison (1847-1931), Henry Ford (18631947), and Orville Wright (18711948) were still alive and being celebrated as American heroes as late as 1929.

They had brought electric light, the phonograph, the automobile, and the airplane to the world. All engineers were highly respected and admired as heroes of the age. The number of engineers had increased exponentially since 1870, when there were only a hundred. By 1900 there were 45,000, and by 1930, half a million. They were building modern bridges, dams, roads, skyscrapers, airplanes, ships and trains, as well as consumer products such as automobiles, cash registers, telephones, appliances, industrial machinery, clocks, tractors, shovels and pitchforks.

Engineers were trained in hundreds of technical schools and were familiar with a range of materials, stresses, structures, manufacturing processes, costs, and the many technologies, such as electricity, steam, and combustion engines, that were required to power the manufacturing equipment or the mechanical devices. The overriding objective of all engineers was to create a physical structure which, whether a suspension bridge, an airplane, a vacuum cleaner, or a toaster, was strictly functional, economical, and efficient. These were, after all, machines intended to provide humans with transportation, power, and labor saving, and these were the functions expected, highly admired and acclaimed by the public.

Most consumers were delighted with any of the new mass-produced electromechanical goods being made available, despite the fact that they were generally rather mechanical looking and ungainly in appearance, and although for many they were still rather expensive. But their mechanistic look was initially a positive image of technology. The main thing was that they performed their functional objective, were available at reasonable prices, and offered a higher standard of everyday living. For those many new consumers who had never had a vacuum cleaner before, its function, not its appearance, was of primary interest and the greatest source of user satisfaction. Functional performance, power, and labor saving were the coins of the realm, and all the new appliance manufacturers competed successfully on that basis.

The term "artless industries" had evolved to distinguish certain mechanical products from the traditional "art industries," as defined by the Twelfth Exhibition of Contemporary American Industrial Art at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York, October 12 to November 22, 131.' The "art industries" were the only industrial "art" supported, promoted, and exhibited by museums. These were industries producing decorative arts, which included ceramics, glassware, enamelwork, furniture, leatherwork, printing, jewelry, art metalwork, lighting fixtures, carpets, drapes, silverware, textiles, and wallpaper, or any product intended as a decorative object for the home. A number of these were in fact functional as well as decorative, so their designers were necessarily involved with form and function as well as surface decoration.

By and large, these "decorative arts" industries grew out of the Arts and Crafts movement of the 19th century, where skilled craftspeople originally selected appropriate materials and made both decorative and functional objects individually by hand, shaping and/or decorating the surfaces manually or by hand-operated machines of the era. The craftsman was in control ofall the physical and functional aspects of a product, as well as its final appearance. Even after machines were developed to produce such products in quantity, many were still designed before manufacture by craftspeople, who therefore still controlled both the functional form and its external decorative treatments.

This personal involvement of a single individual craftsman in the total design of a particular product was common historically, and resulted in famous craftsmen names such as Paul Revere (silverware), Thomas Chippendale (furniture), Eli Terry (clocks), and Duncan Phyfe (furniture). Ever since Leonardo DaVinci (1452-1519), art and engineering had gone hand in hand. From the beginning of industrialization, art and industry were closely related. In the early 19th century, the "mechanic arts" or "useful arts," as they were called, included a wide range of technical skills, such as mechanical design, decorative design, mechanical drawing, materials analysis, crafts, machine design, production planning, art, factory design, physics, mathematics, etc. Over the century, as industrialization advanced and as educational institutions tried to teach such comprehensive technical skills, these all became individually named specialties and were separated from one other. Thus did art and engineering become separate disciplines, with specialized skills and differing motives.

As 20th-century manufacturing produced more and more consumer products in greater and greater quantities, and as factories become more complex technologically, more impersonal, and more industrial in character, fewer and fewer American craftspeople or artists were interested in the artless industries dominated by engineers, dirty machinery, time clocks, and crass commercialism. Even the art industries held limited appeal to artists because such applied art generally went unrecognized and required working nine to five in a factory environment that was less than clean, quiet, or dignified.

At the time, the terms "industrial art" and "applied art" were the most commonly used terms for art careers in industry, and as noted above, they were not popular choices for aspiring artists. But there was another, more obscure term used occasionally in the fine arts field since 1: "industrial design," also referring to the use of artists for industrial purposes. This term had more recently been specifically defined for industrial purposes in 1913 by the U.S. commissioner of patents, Edward B. Moore, who urged modifications of existing regulations to protect the property of "industrial design, the distinguishing form of products that have marketable value" (italics in original).

The existing regulations he referred to were design patents, which protected the exterior appearance of an object against copying since 1842, when they were first initiated by the patent office for "any new and original shape or configuration of any article of manufacture not known or used by others before."4 Many museums encouraged the copying of European products by American manufacturers because they were not protected outside of the country of origin. Commissioner Moore was concerned that even U.S. design patents were not being enforced or upheld aggressively in America, thus discouraging innovative designs by Americans.

America found itself in a conflicted situation in the 1920s. It commanded the most productive manufacturing industry in the world, as well as the most lucrative market for consumer products. Factories were making as many products as possible, and a stable economy enabled a mass market of middle-class consumers to buy as many products as industry could produce. Nevertheless, a few art museums and organizations, who were familiar with the European potential to dominate world markets, were warning that America was far behind Europe in applied arts and was in desperate need of more future designers in industry to compete globally. The works of many of these European artists and a few Americans were first shown in America at the International Exhibition of Modern Art in New York in 1913, the so-called Armory Show, since it was held at the city's 69th Infantry Regiment Armory, at Lexington and 26th Street. Most American viewers, including President Theodore Roosevelt, rejected the new styles as obscene or primitive, certainly not art, and most American artists continued to paint or sculpt in highly traditional styles. America was not yet ready for modern art.

In 1914, as World War I began, modern artist Duchamp (1887-1968) took a step that challenged the very definition of art. He took a common manufactured wire bottle rack, signed his name to it, and declared that it was art because he said it was art. In 1915, he and Francis Picabia (1879-1953) both visited New York and were publicized as prominent French artists who for the first time were, as they said, "seeking America in matters of art." Picabia praised what he saw:

I adore New York. There is much about it, which is like the Paris of the old days.. I have been profoundly impressed by the vast mechanical development in America. The unconventional work of European artists had no effect whatsoever upon the 1915 Panama-Pacific International Exposition in San Francisco, which had been planned since 1911, primarily to celebrate the completion of the Panama Canal, but also to herald the recovery of San Francisco from the disastrous effects of its 1906 earthquake.

The architecture, sculpture, and murals featured were clearly in the traditions of the 19th century, with highly realistic, classical, but allegorical statues and murals, mostly nudes, suggesting abstract ideas of abundance, progress, conquest, creation, power, etc., and historical depictions of conquistadors, pirates, and miners. Yet despite criticism from traditionalists, modern art marched forward. In 1917 Duchamp submitted a mass-produced ceramic urinal, titled Fountain, to the Independent Artists Exhibition in New York as "art," which aroused outraged protests but nevertheless established a fundamental principle of modern art: art is whatever the artist chooses to define as art. In this case, it also suggested that common utilitarian objects could be viewed as art, which may have been a surprise to the manufacturer of the urinal, or any company making similar utilitarian products.

Other European countries evolved independent styles of modern art. In 1917, Piet Mondrian (1872-1944) and Theo van Doesburg (1893-1931) initiated the Dutch movement of de Stijl (the Style), which featured primary colors arranged asymmetrically in rectangular paintings, or abstract furniture. Another former employee and protégé of Peter Behrens, Swiss-born architect Charles Edouard Jenneret Gris (1887-1965), was also intrigued by American Fordism and Taylorism, only he used it in reverse. Designing a slaughterhouse in Paris in 1916, he included a conveyor system to "disassemble" the animals. This practice, used in the United States since 1867, was often claimed by Ford to be the inspiration of his assembly line for automobiles. In 1917 Gris established a small brick-making factory, and by 1920 he considered himself an industrialist as well as an architect. It was then that he adopted his pseudonym, "Le Corbusier" (the Raven), under which he began publishing L'Esprit Nouveau (The New Spirit), a periodical with themes of order, rationality, engineering, and technology. Twenty-eight issues were published over the next five years.

As an architect, Corbusier dreamed of producing houses the way Ford produced automobiles. In 1921 he published sketches of a house called Maison Citrohan (Citrohan House), using the analogy of France's mass-produced car based on Ford's assembly line, the Citroën. His concept, he declared, could complete houses in three days made of artificial materials such as concrete. Over the next few years, Corbusier designed a number of private homes in Paris based on his Citrohan House concept. They featured stark interior white walls, furniture made of tubular steel, and single bare bulbs in light fixtures. The effect was pure, simple, modernist design that followed the German Bauhaus style and which he called "a machine for living in." In 1922, Corbusier developed plans for a "Contemporary City" for three million inhabitants that he hoped would reorganize society along lines of American industrial models. In 1923, he published Vers une Architecture (Toward an Architecture), a book summarizing his philosophy of a future world functioning like a machine.

But Europe still retained its historical reputation as the center of art and design, and in fact Europeans have a number of legitimate historical claims for initiating what we now call industrial design because of the many Arts and Crafts groups that advanced the quality of design esthetically in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. However, what we call industrial design today is more broadly defined than as simply designing consumer products in a modern style, but as the process of designing products for mass production by machines in collaboration with engineering, marketing, and manufacturing, for the purpose of consumer acceptance and affordability, as well as for corporate profit, as it is practiced today. It is the Machine Age in America, in the 1920s and 1930s, that defined, pioneered, and implemented this modern process in reality, rather than in European theoretical prototypes that were designed without any manufacturing, cost, or sales considerations, and were therefore only of academic, or esthetic value.

The exposition was unique in one key respect: former similar expositions traditionally celebrated past national accomplishments. In this one the intention of the organizers was to establish new standards. Exhibitors were required to display only "those exhibits that were not dependent of the art of the past."1 Invitations went out to all countries except Germany, which was still regarded as the villain of the Great War. Twenty-six countries accepted, except for China and the United States.

China did not accept because of disruptions in political matters, and the United States ostensibly because the State Department did not recommend appropriations to do so, due to lack of support from commercial interests. As Herbert Hoover, then secretary of commerce and in charge of the Coolidge administration's industrial policy, explained to the Fourth Annual Exposition of Women's Arts and Industries:

The advice I received from manufacturers was that while we produced a vast volume of goods of much artistic value, they did not consider that we could contribute sufficiently varied design of unique character or of special expression in American artistry to warrant such a participation.2

Obviously, U.S. manufacturers saw no significant consumer market potential in Europe, but more to the point, they had nothing to display that met the exposition's intent of new standards or its specific criteria of products that were "not dependent of the art of the past." Most U.S. products of the time were, in fact, copies of European designs. Hoover's decision did not set well with the cultural establishment, where many were already quite aware of how advanced European design and fashion had become. When Charles Richards, the same art educator who conducted the 1922 disappointing study of industrial arts education, asked

Hoover to delegate a commission of 100 representatives of trade associations, along with some artists and designers, to visit and report on the exposition, Hoover gracefully complied.

After a year of freelancing, Teague left Calkins & Holden in 1912 to work from his own typographic studio, creating graphic designs for magazine ads. In 1920, when he designed frames for the famous Arrow Collar ads, he was already recognized widely for what were known as " Teague borders."

By the mid—1920s, the demand for border designs weakened, and Teague became involved with commercial packaging. He was intrigued by the Paris exposition of 1925, and in June 1926 he traveled to Europe to view Bauhaus work in Italy and was inspired by the work and writings of Le Corbusier. Upon his return, he reestablished his office devoted to package design.

In 1927, Richard F. Investors had been buying stocks on margin, that is, on credit, for years. Brokerage firms would lend \$9 for every \$1 an investor deposited, which vastly overpriced the stock market. Even before the stock market crashed, many businesses were making more products than they could sell and were overstocked. # Streamlining and the Chicago Fair

The German Bauhaus, much admired by MoMA, was in serious trouble in Dessau, Germany. Faced with political and financial pressure, Gropius and Moholy-Nagy had resigned from the Bauhaus in 1928 and went to England, while architect Hannes Meyer, a committed Marxist who attempted to link the school to a vision of social and political transformation, assumed leadership. Some students were communists, others Nazis, both escalating political tensions. Architect Ludwig Mies van der Rohe (1886-1969) had become director of the Bauhaus in 1930, replacing Meyer, who had been forced by Nazis to resign at the same time that communist students were expelled.

In the month before the MoMA exhibition, the Dessau city council, pressured by the powerful National Socialist (Nazi) German Workers Party under Adolph Hitler, who was not yet in full power, dissolved the Bauhaus in that city, due to a "Jewish-Marxist art manifestation," a travesty that may have inspired MoMA to elevate the legacy of the Bauhaus to near martyrdom.

As a last-ditch effort to save the school, Mies van der Rohe, the last director of the Bauhaus, would move the school to Berlin, but within a year, Hitler would close the school in April 1933, and its faculty and students would scatter around the world, many coming to the United States later in the decade. There were other similar schools in Germany that were much larger in scope and number of students than the Bauhaus, such as the Industrial Art School in Berlin, with distinguished graduates such as Bruno Paul, Adolph Meyer, Kem Weber, and Mies van der Rohe himself, but such schools were not shut down by Hitler and thus were essentially forgotten in the United States.

What attracted these early designers to enter a strange world of machinery and mass production? Of course, as mentioned earlier, industrial design had suddenly become a financially lucrative field, as manufacturers scrambled to find talent to meet intense public demand. But aside from that, there is something very satisfying to a creative person to know that his or her design would not just be printed in a magazine, exhibited in an art gallery, win an award, or sold in small quantities as a decorative craft, but would be mass produced, sold by the thousands, and distributed throughout the country to a large audience of the general public who would use and interact with his or her design on a daily basis. It was a feeling, not only of public recognition and acceptance, but one of a personal contribution to society, making the world a better place to live. This was a powerful incentive.

New York, of course, was the American center of culture, business, and style, and many of the early leading designers were working there. John D. Rockefeller, Jr. (19741960), had developed Rockefeller Center, a 12-acre complex in Midtown Manhattan, beginning in 1929. In 1930, Donald Deskey had designed home interiors for Rockefeller, a client connection that helped him win the competition for the interior design of Radio City Music Hall in Rockefeller Center, an extravagant Art Deco landmark that opened in December 1932 with a lavish stage show featuring Ray Bolger and Martha Graham. Deskey would work on the interior until 1934. The Music Hall would be restored to its original condition in 1999.

But the biggest attractions in New York were its famous skyscrapers. New York skyscrapers were then capturing the imagination of the world. The Chrysler Building in New York City had been completed in May 1930, becoming the tallest building in the world (71 stories, 1,046 feet). Started in 1928, it was a classic Art Deco design by architect William Van Allen (1883- 1954) for Walter P. Chrysler (1875-1940), president of the Chrysler Corporation he had formed in 1924 to manufacture Chrysler automobiles. Chrysler had been Time magazine's Man of the Year in 1928. Eleven months after the Chrysler building was completed, in April 1931, the 102-story Empire State Building was completed to capture the title of the world's tallest, at 1,454 feet.

These skyscrapers had been the inspiration of Paul Frankl for his stylish Skyscraper furniture in 1925, and he had written several books to publicize his business and to promote the new modern styles. In 1928 he had published New Dimensions: The Decorative Arts of Today in Words and Pictures, and in 1930, Form and Re-Form: Practical Handbook ofModern Interiors. Now, in 1932, he published Machine Made Leisure, capitalizing on the popular term "machine," which to the public was shorthand for the wonder of industrialization and mass production, just as "digital" or "computer" would be shorthand for the "Information Age" of the 1980s and 1990s.

But the skyscraper styles popularized by Frankl would soon be replaced in popularity by "streamlining," a style based on the public fascination with high speed, as celebrated in the new age of aircraft and racing cars, such as the Supermarine S.6B British seaplane which set the world speed record of 340 mph at the Schneider trophy air race of 1931 (and which would be developed into the famous British World War II fighter plane, the Spitfire), and Malcolm Cmbe Blu Bircr .h. he eek metal embodied in these vehicles symbolized speed and were used by Norman Bel Geddes to envision the future in his 1932 book, Horizons. Bel Geddes based his designs on the concept ofspeed, as he said in his book, "speed is the cry of our era, and greater speed one of the goals of tomorrow."1

The term was originally used by English biologist/mathematician D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson (1860-1948) in his influential 1917 book, On Growth and Form. # Industrial Design Unleashed

By 1933, eight years after the Paris Art Deco Exposition of 1925, which had sounded a wake-up call, warning that America was far behind Europe in modern design and therefore in international economic competition, there were perhaps three dozen industrial designers who were working in the so-called artless industries, a drop in the bucket compared to the 50,000 needed in the post—World War I consumer markets, as recommended in 19i8 by Walter Sargent (1868-1927), then director of the Department of Fine and Industrial Art at the University of Chicago. Of course, World War I had dramatically suppressed the European advance in industrialization, and the Depression had stopped America's economy in its tracks.

At the bottom of the Depression in 1933, industry was stalled. Many manufacturers still resisted investment in new tooling for new designs because sales had declined dramatically, and they were in the red, struggling to stay in business. Many were bewildered by a situation foreign to their experience—a situation in which the functional but homely merits of their products were not enough to sell them. Those who sought qualified designers to design new products found that the few industrial designers that were available were too busy to take on new clients. But the vast majority of manufacturers, businessmen, and engineers were simply unaware of modern design or industrial design. They were not among the elite sophisticates who frequented art museums and international art expositions, or who read art magazines. They were practical people, concerned about the nuts and bolts of manufacturing, not art. Many of them were about to learn about industrial design and its leading practitioners for the first time, through an influential article that reached the entire business world.

The success of the Chicago Fair in 1933 was the first glimmer ofhope for the future experienced by the public in three years.
