In 1933, PRR had decided to replace its current P5a electric locomotives with ones that were more powerful and more streamlined. PRR asked General Electric and Westinghouse to design prototypes that were capable of 100 mph, a streamlined body shell, and a central control cab. Features from both prototypes were refined and combined to produce the final design.

The Westinghouse prototypes were based on six plaster models made by Donald Dohner (mentioned earlier), Westinghouse's director of art in its engineering division, in 1933 when the project began. One of these models, shown in photographs, closely resembles the final design of what became the GG-1 prototype, which essentially was completed by August 1934, three months before Loewy was called in. By all traditions of attribution, Dohner should have been credited with the styling of the GG-1.

Loewy was called in for consultation on October 25, 1934, by PRR vice president and heir apparent Martin W. Clement to "enhance the GG-I's esthetics." A fee of \$5,000 was agreed upon, but he had only about a week before the production order was to be placed, so his contributions on the existing prototype were limited to the color scheme, which became Brunswick Green, with five gold pinstripes running along the sides and curving down on the front and rear toward the ground. Loewy also claimed he had recommended a smooth welded body instead of the riveted one used in the prototype, but there is some evidence that the rivets were the result of rushing the completion of the prototype and that they were never intended for use on the production models. One hundred thirty-nine production models would be built between 1934 and 1943, and many remained in service until the 1970s.

In any event, Loewy was retained for \$20,000 annually as the primary design consultant of PRR for the rest of the decade. Until his death, he publicly claimed the design of the GG-1 as one of his most important achievements and was exclusively credited with its design in all literature pertaining to the GG-l. Loewy was its ultimate promoter, not Dohner, who was essentially a modest man, who would, within a month after he designed the models, leave Westinghouse to begin his career in industrial design education, for which he is better remembered.

At Carnegie in 1933, optional classes in industrial design, based on Dohner's 1930 syllabus, had been offered to students and were in progress as Dohner rejoined the faculty in 1934 and initiated a new degreed program in industrial design at CIT, the first school to do so. Assisting Dohner was Art and Design Department head, Wilfred Radio, and faculty members Kostellow, Russell Hyde, Fred Clayter, and Robert Lepper (1906- 1991). Lepper had been hired to fill in for Dohner's position when he left for Westinghouse in 1930. Lepper was born in Aspinwall, a suburb of Pittsburgh, and graduated with a degree in painting from CIT in 1927. He spent a year or so in Europe soaking up modern art and joined the faculty at CIT in 1930, where he painted "machine art," such as his 1932 Crankshaft, a realistic image ofa Lincoln 12-cylinder crankshaft with pistons floating in the air. Because of his interest in mechanical things, he was more or less drafted into the new industrial design degree program in 1934.

So in the fall semester of 1934, third-year students were offered an option for a degree in industrial design, along with other third-year options of illustration, interior decoration, or art education. The first class included three women and two males, all to become the first degreed graduates of industrial design in the world. Dohner took them to manufacturing plants, such as Westinghouse, Fostoria Glass, and ALCOA (Aluminum Company of America), to show them how things were made, and then instructed them to design new products based on what they had seen.

Elsewhere, modern design continued to be publicized. From November 5, 1934, to January 6, 1935, the Metropolitan Museum of Art mounted an exhibition, Contemporary American Industrial Art, by 20 architectural and industrial designers that featured entire-room arrangements by architects except for a full-scale replica of an industrial design office and studio designed by Raymond Loewy. Architects and museums related all industrial design to architecture because they believed that architects should dominate the new field, as was the case in Europe. Accordingly, many of the rooms were traditionally credited to name architects, who selected all the decorative furnishings to keep industrial design "in its proper place" under the supervision of architects.

However, this time, a number of industrial designers were invited to participate in the event, including Viktor Schreckengost (chinaware), Donald Deskey (an entire dining room), Gilbert Rohde (Herman Miller clock and furniture), Gustav Jensen (rugs, wall treatments), Peter Müller-Munk (silverware), Walter von Nessen (display case and metalware), Lurelle Guild (silverware), Russel Wright (metalware, floor lamps), Alfons Bach (furniture) and Walter Dorwin Teague (on the cooperating committee). Still, the products shown were all from traditional ers, such as the elegant gas range Teague had recently designed for the Floyd-Wells Company in Royersford, Pennsylvania, that rivaled Bel Geddes's SGE range in simplicity, whiteness, and modernity. But attempts by architects to supervise and control industrial design were too late. The horse of industrial design was out of the architectural barn. Industrial designers already owned the field ofboth mechanical and decorative, modern mass-produced consumer products.

Streamlined trains were still in the national news at the end of the year. On December 27, 1934, the New York Central Railroad (NYC) exhibited its new steam locomotive at Grand Central Station, with a streamlined shell painted in gunmetal gray and called the Commodore Vanderbilt. Named to honor the original Commodore Vanderbilt locomotive of 1870, it was the first to utilize streamlining by adding a streamlined shell over a steam locomotive, NYC's J-1E 46-4 "Hudson" No. 5344.

An undergraduate student at Cleveland's Case School of Applied Science, Norman F. Zapf (19111974), designed the shell for his 1934 senior thesis, "The Streamlining of a Locomotive." Zapf tested scale models in a wind tunnel and concluded that at typical passenger speeds of 75 mph, streamlining reduced drag by 30 percent and required 350 less horsepower than a nonstreamlined locomotive. Nearly all his suggestions were incorporated into the final Commodore Vanderbilt. The nose form was similar to the "bathtub" design of the Burlington Zephyr. Carl Kantola, an in-house New York Central employee, worked on adapting the design to the required mechanical modifications. T in the world of design and in the public arena, were looking to legally protect their innovative, modern, American designs from the wholesale copying that had previously been common practice for European designs. That had been the primary concern of the American Union of Decorative Artists (AUDAC) when it was formed in 1927. Now, in 1933, to address AUDAC's accusations of design piracy, representatives and designers in the furniture industry joined together to found the National Furniture Designers' Council (NFDC) in order to draw up a code for enjoining piracy for the National Recovery Administration (NRA).

The purpose of the Roosevelt administration's establishment of the NRA in 1933 was to eliminate "cutthroat competition" by bringing industry, labor, and government together to create "fair practices" and to set prices. Industries were invited to submit codes of fair competition, and the furniture industry did so. But in 1935, the U.S. Supreme Court found that the NRA law was unconstitutional, ruling that it infringed on the separation of powers under the constitution. The NFDC disintegrated, but the seed for a furniture industry design organization had been planted, and in July 1935, Lawrence H. Whiting, director of the American Furniture Mart, called a special meeting of furniture designers, including those from the now defunct NFDC, and engaged Richard Bach, of the Metropolitan Museum, and Alfred Auerbach (18811944), design and merchandising consultant as well as editor of Retailing magazine, to serve as speakers for the meeting. The proceedings of the meeting are unknown, but the result, within a year, would be the forming of a furniture design organization, which will be described later.

That year, Al Lambach, mentioned earlier, returned to Chicago to work for Merton Gershun at the Chicago Furniture Mart, and also for the Tell City Furniture Company in Tell City, Indiana, where he designed radio and phonograph cabinets for Zenith, Clinton, Traveler, and Emerson. Because home radios of the day were traditionally enclosed in furniture for placement in living rooms, a number of furniture designers in the 1920s and early 1930s were actively designing in this field.

Furniture, as one of the traditional art industries, was stillin the good graces of museums, such as Bach's Metropolitan Museum, and of course the architects as wel, since many famous European architects, and equally famous American architects such as Frank Lloyd Wright, had been designing furniture for generations. A number of pioneer industrial designers had designed furniture too, including Norman Bel Geddes (Simmons Company, 1928); Paul Frankl (Skyscraper furniture, 1925); Gilbert Rohde (for Herman Miller Furniture Company, 1932-1935); John Vassos (RCA radio cabinets and his portable phonograph of 1935); Nathan Horwitt (Beta Chair for the Howell Company, Geneva, Illinois, 1930); Kem Weber (armchair by Lloyd Manufacturing Company of Menominee, Michigan, 1934); Donald Deskey (since 1926); and Russel Wright (Haywood-Wakefield, 1934). But most of those above had designed in the modern styles, not the traditional historic styles of many designers in the furniture industry.

Throughout the 1930s, Wright and Walter Von Nessen had designed scores of modern metal accessories for the Chase Brass & Copper Company. In 1935, Wright had renamed his firm from Russel Wright Incorporated to Russel Wright Accessories, and designed a natural maple line of furniture called Modern Living, made by Conant Ball Company for Macy's, which was widely promoted. The popular line would later be called American Modern, produced in bleached wood "blond" maple, and would make Wright famous. He opened a showroom for his business in New York, with the help of businessman Irving Richards, under the name Russel Wright, Inc.

In 1934, one of the pioneer furniture designers, Paul Frankl, relocated to California and became an active interior designer, working in a style that would be called "California Modern" at his gallery on Rodeo Drive in Beverly Hills, where celebrities such as Fred Astaire, Katherine Hepburn, Walter Houston, and Alfred Hitchcock became his clients. Other designers were working for celebrities. Around this time, Margaret Bourke-White (1904-1971), the famous Fortune photographer, approached John Vassos to design her studio in the Chrysler Building in New York, as well as the interior of her apartment in Tudor City. He did, and they remained close personal friends until her death.

Furniture and furnishings were still the mainstream of the traditional art industries except that now modern design was replacing some of the traditional styles of historical periods. But the new mechanical, mass-produced products of the artless industries remained in artistic limbo as a category yet to be accepted by museums as legitimate art.

Museums, including the Museum of Modern Art, and architects still had serious reservations about other mass-produced, mechanical products in which industrial designers were exclusively excelling, such as stoves, refrigerators, sinks, toilets, radios, office machines, etc. Elitists balked at calling these items furniture let alone works of art. To the elite artistic world, these products were commercially embarrassing, far exceeding the profitability of furniture or the decorative arts, and far more financially rewarding to the designers than any architect could expect. Accordingly, many regarded this arena of design as pure commercialism, the enemy of fine art, and were reluctant to embrace such mechanical products on an equal footing with furniture.

Now Müller-Munk was jumping into the nascent industrial design education field. At Carnegie Tech, in Pittsburgh, upon finishing the first year of the degree program he initiated, Donald Dohner was contacted by his former employer in the Pittsburgh

public schools, James Boudreau, who had become dean of Pratt Institute's Art School in 1928. Boudreau wanted Dohner to organize and lead an industrial design program at Pratt, similar to the one he had just started at Carnegie. Having also worked with Boudreau as a lecturer and consultant at Pratt in 1932, Dohner accepted, and Müller-Munk was called in to replace him as head of the program at Carnegie.

In the fall, Müller-Munk and Alexander Kostellow would continue Dohner's curriculum and practice of taking students to tour local manufacturing facilities and having them design products based on the production methods they had seen. As a matter of fact, it is this unique accommodation of the client manufacturer's production and economic limitations that distinguished industrial designers from their predecessors, the pure artists and industrial artists. Pioneer industrial designers were developing the unique philosophy of industrial design as a multidisciplinary process that was not only art, but a balance of economics, engineering, materials selection, and human convenience considerations.

Their other distinguishing characteristic was their commitment to modern design, rather than the imitation of traditional period design styles, still clung to by many industrial artists, and the most modern style, now known as streamlining, was at the forefront of transportation design. On May 29, 1935, the first of a series of streamlined Hiawatha trains, the Twin Cities Hiawatha, began service from Chicago, through Milwaukee, to the twin cities of Minneapolis and St. Paul in morning and afternoon runs. Operated by the Milwaukee Railroad to compete with the Burlington Twin Cities Zephyr and North Western Railway's Twin Cities 400, the Class A 4-4-2 steam locomotive, built by the American Locomotive Company (ALCO), was designed by ALCO design consultant Otto Kuhler (1884-1977) with a nose form similar to the Burlington Zephyr, but without the front cab windows, the cab being in the rear as with all traditional steam locomotives. The nose was graced with horizontal stainless steel wings with a red engine number at its center. Kuhler also designed the famous Hiawatha "beaver tail" observation car at the rear, and all the interiors down to the napkins and draperies.

Another transportation marvel was introduced in 1935, the Scarab, engineered by aviation engineer William Bushnell Stout (1880-1956), with the exterior designed by John Tjaarda. On December 2, 1935, in New York, a frigid and blustery day, several hundred men and women lined up outside the 35th Street offices of the WPA for a chance to enroll in the Design Laboratory, a pioneering school sponsored by the Federal Art Project (FAP), one of the WPA's cultural projects. Its brochure caimed it had been create to supply a hithertounfulfilled and pressing need in America" for people with skills to devise, package, and promote consumer products that were styled to sell, thereby stimulating economic recovery. Clearly, this noble purpose was inspired by the incredible success of industrial designers in doing precisely that over the previous eight years.

By emphasizing "coordination in the study of esthetics, industrial products, machine fabrication, and merchandizing," the school would "train designers, not specialized craftsmen, by correlating thorough instruction in the general principles of design and the fine arts with shop practice." Two hundred twenty people enrolled that day, more that the school's administration anticipated, and there were still hundreds more on a waiting list.

The school was the result of political action by the Depression-era avant-garde, the unprecedented public arts bureaucracy of the New Deal, the militant industrial unionism of the Congress of Industrial Organizations (CIO), which had just been formed within the American Federation of Labor (AFL), the United Auto Workers (UAW ), and the radical socialist politics of the Popular Front (a broad coalition of different political groupings, often made up of centrists and leftists).

The collaboration was an example of how modernism was viewed by some as a means to control cultural production by political action and thus prevent its dominance by business interests and capitalism. The idea of democratizing American culture was attractive to many elites of the time, who resented the new lucrative art form of industrial design being controlled by big business and, by the way, a handful of industrial design celebrities getting rich in the process.

The school organizers recruited well-known industrial designers such as Raymond Loewy, Walter Dorwin Teague, Donald Deskey, George Sakier, and Russel Wright to lend legitimacy to an advisory board by the use of their names, and lured furniture designer Gilbert Rohde to serve as director. It is likely that these men were attracted by the idealistic intention of training young designers, as stated in the brochure, and as desperately needed in the field, rather than by the hidden leftist ideology behind it.

This ideology was obvious in major study texts required by the curriculum, including The Theory of the Leisure Class, by Thorstein Veblen, published in 1899, which claimed that reform of industrial art had to be through the technology of machines, and highly influential sociologist/historian Lewis Mumford's book, Technics and Civilization (1934). Mumford considered streamlining as mere expressive and "outmoded decoration" and insisted that functionalist esthetics (such as the geometric abstract forms at the Bauhaus) was more appropriate for society, a view shared and promulgated by the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA), which venerated Bauhaus architects, and which would enthusiastically take up the cause of functional esthetics. The recent closure of the leftist Bauhaus by the Nazis as "a Marxist manifestation" generated much political sympathy throughout the liberal and progressive art community, which actively sought to bring exiled Bauhaus refugees to the United States to lead the modern m B of patriotic defiance against Nazism and war. The long-term success of many product designs of the 1930s seems to conflict with the concept described in 1932 by Elmo Calkins of "planned obsolescence," which predicted that annual design changes spur consumer spending. But the longevity of certain product designs can be explained by several reasons. First, while many consumers are eager to obtain newer versions of a product, there are also some who are not and who will keep and use a favored product until it expires mechanically.

Second, many new, industrial-designed products, for the first time in the 1930s, acquired a shape, style, and configuration that seemed right"for its assigned task, establishing what is called a "typeform," that is, a form that seemed to many the ideal for a particular type, or category, of product. Other manufacturing competitors, noting the popular success of a particular brand, would imitate the design, therefore increasing public familiarity and acceptance of the standard typeform until some substantially different, more popular, configuration replaced it as the new typeform for that particular product. The KitchenAid mixer survives because it is regarded as the ideal typeform for this particular category of products.

The power of historically successful designs is illustrated by the automotive industry, which has had dozens of different, successive typeforms over the years, but would remain nostalgic about car designs of the 1930s, which produced the first designs that were truly modern. Detroit in the late 1990s and early 2000s would develop a number of so-called retro designs, intending to recall that period, and which were intended to replicate the original typeforms. They included the (new) Volkswagen Beetle (1994), the Plymouth Prowler (1994), Chrysler's PT (Personal Transportation) Cruiser (1999), Buick's 2001 Blackhawk, Ford's 2002 Forty-Nine, and Chevrolet's HHR (Heritage High Roof) in 2006, all in 1930s styles.

But in the late 1930s, the problem was too many cars, and not enough roads. Since half of all households now owned an automobile, the U.S. road system was being challenged by an increasing number of cars on narrow, crowded roads. It was an obvious problem addressed by Norman Bel Geddes, on assignment for the J. Walter Thompson advertising agency in 1937. Based on his experience with his scale-model racetrack of the 1920s, and his war game of the early 1930s, he designed a model City of Tomorrow with expressways featured in a national advertising campaign for the Shell Oil Company. The model would be expanded to become the Futurama exhibit for General Motors at the 1939 New York World's Fair, which in turn would inspire the future federal interstate highway system. Jumping from highway systems to cocktail parties, Bel Geddes also designed a Skyscraper cocktail shaker and a Manhattan serving tray in 1937.

The automobile industry was transformed during the Depression. Car production fell from 5.6 million in 1929 to 1.4 million in 1932. Dozens of auto manufacturing brands in 1929 were reduced to 10 in 1935, which included the Big Three of Ford, Chrysler, and General Motors. GM, with five new models since 1930, led the pack and in 1936 was unionized. In 1937, GM's Art and Colour Section would change its name to the Styling Division. Harley Earl had a staff of 200 designers, cranking out annual model changes on the fleet of GM cars. Earl supervised five studios, one for each division (Cadillac, Buick, Oldsmobile, Pontiac, and Chevrolet), as well as 12 special studios.

Yet he wanted more designers, and so GM and its Fisher Body Division initiated a talent search by sponsoring and promoting a national, 1:12 scale model (one inch equals one foot) "Dream Car" design competition that awarded college scholarships to the best original student designs for a four-door sedan (later various different body styles were added). It was called the Fisher Body Craftsman's Guild, which would continue for 30 more years, encouraging a new generation of automotive designers, many of whom would end up in Detroit, and not a few in Earl's studios.

Actually, the contest was a major addition to an earlier Fisher Body Craftsman's Guild contest that was initiated in 1930, not to innovate designs, but to accurately replicate Fisher plans for a miniature scale model (1:15 to 1:18, where one inch equaled 1.25 to 1.6 feet) of a 19th-century horse-drawn Napoleonic coach, the trademark adopted by Fisher in 1923, and which appeared on all production Fisher bodies made for GM. There were two competitive categories: the Junior Division (ages 12 through 15) and the Senior Division (ages 16 through 1). Winners won prize money for a scholarship to a college of their choice: \$5,000 for each of the top two winners in each category. That was more than double what a man typically earned in a year! This political objective stemmed from MoMA's basic assumptions (some would say biases) that guided its policies.

First, like virtually all museums in the United States for decades before, MoMA at that time believed that Europe was, and should be, the center and global leader of allartistic endeavors, particularly in modern art, architecture, and design. MoMA had touted the Bauhaus exhibition as modern design based on the "best of Europe," implying that America should imitate Europe. In fact, in the previous March, MoMA had exhibited the architecture and furniture designs of Finnish architect Alvar Aalto (1898-1976) to reinforce European superiority in these fields.

As a result of this Eurocentric template, European architects were regarded by MoMA as more significant than American architect Frank Lloyd Wright, even though Wright had inspired many of these same European architects nearly 30 years before and recently had designed a simple, modern Usonian ranch house community that would become a model for most U.S. postwar suburban developments. With similar logic, European Bauhaus designers were regarded by MoMA as more significant than American industrial designers, despite the fact that Americans were the first to actually design mass-produced products for mass consumption in a highly industrialized environment.

Second, MoMA, at that time, believed that architects had been, and should be, the leaders not only in architecture, but in all artifacts within a home, including furniture and accessories. Historically, this had been true for many famous architects, both in Europe and in the United States. This was certainly true at the Bauhaus as well, and since MoMA was under the control of architects, there was an assumption that because of this history, architects should rightfully be leading the new profession of industrial design. Many architects were particularly upset not only because industrial design in the United States over the previous ten years had been led, not by architects, but by advertising artists, stage designers, display designers, commercial artists, writers, and art critics, to name a few, but even worse, the leaders, such as Loewy,

Dreyfuss, and Teague, had become national celebrities and commanded incredible fees far higher than architects could hope for.

Lastly, at that time, MoMA, like many art museums, defined fine art as pure, aesthetic, individual expression, based on philosophy, theory, or rationality, and without any intent of commercial gain, mass production, or popular appeal. In fact, it was generally believed that art was good only if it defied popular tastes, traditions, and commercial interests. The Bauhaus fit this idealistic template combining artistic, counterculture, avant-garde, academic experimentation, historical context, and intellectual theory.

American designers did not fit this template. Their crassly commercial designs used popular "gimmicks" such as streamlining or skyscrapers, were acclaimed by the public through mass purchasing, and made lots of money for rich industrialists. What was there for a sophisticated, elite art museum to like? MoMA's curator of architecture and industrial art, John McAndrew, praised the Bauhaus industrial designs as 'timeless,' and "free of both modernistic and streamlined aberrations," but lambasted the designs of designers like Loewy, Dreyfuss or Bel Geddes as "not properly modern," for "imitating superficially the forms of modern art," and for "restyling almost everything so that what we owned might look old fashioned as soon as possible" (the accusation of "artificial obsolescence" surfacing).

These assumptions dominated MoMA policies for years, and put them at loggerheads with many practicing industrial designers and leaders of the profession, who believed that it was American industrial design practitioners of the Machine Age in America who defined the practical implementation, process definition, education, practice, and commercial success of industrial design in mass production and marketing. Industrial design in America was much more than aesthetic purity or 'fine art, as MoMA narrowly defined Bauhaus contributions. It was the complex and iterative process of design: the advance planning of a product to include a reasonable balance of functionality, engineering and manufacturing feasibility, user emotional wants and ergonomic needs, manufacturing costs, appropriate retail prices, suitable materials, and also an artistic and attractive visual presentation to persuade consumers to buy. Fine artists did not do this.

On April 30, 1939, the World's Fair in New York, optimistically titled The World of Tomorrow, held its grand opening attended by 206,000 people, with President Franklin Roosevelt giving the opening address. His speech was not only broadcast on radio networks but was also televised. NBC used the event to initiate regularly scheduled television broadcasts in New York City. An estimated 1,000 people viewed the Roosevelt telecast from about 200 television sets scattered throughout the New York area.

The trademark symbols ofthe fair were a 610-foot-tall Trylon spire, whic was conneced by what was then the world's longest escalator to a 180-foot-diameter sphere called the Perisphere, both gleaming white and dominating the site at Flushing Meadows. The Perisphere housed a diorama called Democracity, which depicted a model future utopian city and suburbs of 2039 on the banks of a river. The city radiated outward in widening concentric circles around a single towering skyscraper at its center. A great hydroelectric plant on the riverbank powered the city, and the surrounding, rolling countryside was full of parks and towns.

Designed by Henry Dreyfuss and Julian Everett, the diorama was viewed from above on a moving sidewalk, as if seen from an airplane at 7,000 feet, while a multi-image slide presentation was projected on the interior surface of the sphere, serving as a movie screen as large as an acre. In the diorama, miniature cars entered and left the model city via high-speed roads that connected to beautiful green towns clustered around factories where they worked. As night fell, the domed sky grew dark and stars came out, like a real night sky, and lights came on in the model city. Then a thousand-voice chorus was heard, and images of a dozen different groups of people (farmers, miners, engineers, etc.) were projected on the dome. The images repeatedly dissolved and were replaced by larger images, as if people were converging on Democracity. When they had arrived, shimmering light rose from the horizon to engulf the entire dome, then faded and the show was over. After the presentation, visitors exited the Perisphere on a 950-foot-long Helicline, an elevated spiral ramp that partially encircled the sphere threequarters of the way around on the outside.8

The message for the future was clear. People would no longer have to live in a city. They would live in the countryside or garden apartments, around the city. Factory workers would live in green towns just like executives and white-collar workers. Democracity was a vision of modern suburbs and planned communities. Henry Dreyfuss Associates (HDA) also designed the fair's administration building and the AT&T exhibit, which featured "Pedro the Vodar," an early voice synthesizer robot.

The fair was divided into differently themed "zones," such as the Transportation Zone, the Communication and Business Systems Zone, the Food Zone, the Government Zone, the Community Interests Zone, the Production and Distribution Zone, etc. Different wall colors and colored lighting distinguished zones from each other. The Transportation Zone included the General Motors (GM) pavilion that featured a 36,000-square-foot Futurama exhibit designed by Norman Bel Geddes in the Highways and Horizons building. It was a diorama of a section of the United States, comprised of miniature highways, towns, waterways, 500,000 individually designed homes, 50,000 miniature vehicles, and a million miniature trees. Incredible details, such as "working" waterfalls, low clouds fashioned with chemical vapors clinging to mountainsides, clotheslines, and cow patties that many visitors never noticed, created a spectacular overall effect. Bel Geddes had perfected the diorama from his 1937 City of Tomorrow exhibit for Shell Oil, and from his earlier models of horse racing tracks and war games in the 1920s and early 1930s, described earlier.

Behind the GM and Ford buildings was the Chrysler Motors Exhibit building, designed by Raymond Loewy. Chrysler's building housed the Transportation Zone's focal exhibit, which featured Loewy's Rocketport exhibit. A futuristic rocket, shot out of a giant gun, would take passengers to London. Every twenty minutes, visitors were admitted to a semicircular area, in total darkness except for the spotlighted rocket on its launchpad. There were blinking lights, white and colored, along with a deep rhythmic sound, the whirling of motors, compressors, and high-frequency sound waves, conveying a sense of activity and suspense.

Then the sound of sirens: Liftoff! A blinding flash of hundreds of strobe lights and the roar of compressed air suddenly released conveyed the optical illusion of the rocket disappearing overhead into the blackness. Then total silence while a gradually diminishing point of light faded away into the stars.4 Public statements by the fair's science director acknowledged that "rocket flight is at present impossible, but the problem is almost wholly limited to the invention of a proper fuel."is Within five years, rockets would in fact be raining on London, not from New York, but from Hitler's rocket bases in Germany. At the fair's "Railroads on Parade" extravaganza, the S-1 met face to face with Henry Dreyfuss's 20th Century Limited, pitting against each other the two most dramatic, streamlined, steam locomotives in the world, and their two most famous industrial designers.

In the Production and Distribution Zone was a focal exhibit designed by Egmont Arens that had a large, concave movie screen showing simple, everyday activities, such as ordering food in a shop, and then all around it were scenes of the far-flung businesses upon which this simple act depended. More interesting was a display showing boxes devoted to each of the main categories of consumer spending; food, clothing, housing, health, etc. The government decided on four income levels: "Subsistence," a family income of \$800 per year; "Maintenance, "\$2,000; "the Good Life," \$2,500; and "Luxury," \$5,000. The average of these categories was \$1,266, far below the "Good Life." In 2013 dollars, these arbitrary income levels would be \$13,400, \$33,500, \$41,900, \$83,900, and \$21,200, respectively."6 The claim in 1939 was that a third of the country makes less than "Subsistence," and nine-tenths, less than the "Good Life." In 2013, the median income, about \$51,413, would be considered well above the "Good Life" using 1939 standards.

The focal exhibit for the Community Interests Zone, designed by Gilbert Rohde, made the point that people depend much more on each other than in the past. In a huge room with a semicircular wall, a series of scenes were arranged before it. One by one, each scene was lit up chronologically, and a narrator intoned, Scene 1, 1789: "No leisure, no time to play. Eight hours to sleep and sixteen to work." The last scene was 1939: "Eight hours' work, eight hours' rest, eight hours' leisure." The lights fade and a spotlight picks out the words, "For What?" An electric phonograph, according to Rohde, controlled the entire timing of the presentation.17 Rohde also designed the exhibit for the Home Furnishings pavilion. That same year, the Herman Miller Company, for which Rohde headed design, pioneered in the use of showrooms for controlled furniture selling in New York and Chicago.

Russel Wright designed the focal exhibit for the Food Zone, which was by general agreement the most entertaining of the focal exhibits. The climax of the exhibit was a sixty-foot egg, inside of which was an avocado with five glowing jewels in its skin: a flock of lobsters flying inland from the sea, an aqueduct spilling roses into the desert, an eye blinking within a cave, and a can holding a clock racing backward. The jewels in the avocado symbolized carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins and minerals. The lobsters signified that inlanders now eat fresh seafood. The blinking eye reminded all of "man's victory over night blindness through vitamin A foods." The aqueduct meant that irrigation made wastelands flourish, and the backward clock illustrated that "canning had perpetuated harvest time."18
