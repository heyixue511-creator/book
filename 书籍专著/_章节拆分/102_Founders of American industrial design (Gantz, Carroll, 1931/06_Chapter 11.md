Raymond Loewy's fascination with automotive design extended into his private life. In 1946 he took two 1940 Lincoln Continentals and custom redesigned them for himself and a friend. Nathan George Horwitt designed a wristwatch in 1947 with a round, black face without numerals, symbols, or lines to mark hours or minutes, and with only a single gold dot marking the twelve-o-clock position, intended to be evocative of a sundial. Vacheron & Constantin-Le Coultre, a Switzerland firm, originally manufactured it. But Zenith Movado would proceed to produce the exact same design in 1948, without any credit or compensation to Horwitt. A prime example of design piracy, it would take 27 years in the courts until Movado finally settled with Horwitt for \$29,000 in 1975. The watch would be included in the permanent design collection of the Museum of Modern Art in 1960 and become known as "the Museum Watch." A wall clock of the classic Horwitt design was produced in 1960 by the Howard Miller Clock Company and is still being produced today.

In his 1940 book, Industrial Design, Harold Van Doren had explained how the unfortunate term of "streamlining" had been associated with industrial design since about 1934:

Streamlining has taken the modern world by storm. We live in a maelstrom of streamlined trains, refrigerators, and furnaces; streamlined bathing beauties, soda crackers, and facial massages. True streamlining is not nearly so important, nor so frequently met with in problems of industrial design, as the general public probably thinks.

The manufacturer who wants his laundry tubs, his typewriters, or his furnaces "streamlined" is in reality asking you to modernize them, to find the means for substituting curvilinear forms for rectilinear forms.. to conform to the current taste, or fad if you wil..He expects, too, that unnecessary exposure of mechanical parts will be eliminated, that buttons will be substituted for levers, and control panels and dials will be organized into simple and easily read groups, tied in wherever possible with related elements of the principle forms.

In 99 cases out of 100 this procedure is utterly unrelated to genuine streamlining. Then why does the client refer to it as such: . Simply because, in the unbelievably rapid growth of the American language, words soon lose their specific and restricted meanings and assume a general significance embracing far wider fields than originally intended.²

The original term "streamlining" correctly referred to automobiles, aircraft and trains, which moved at high speed and therefore were made more efficient with streamlining. But when used on stationary objects, it was understandably criticized as unnecessary, and postwar designs generally abandoned the practice, except for Detroit and the automotive industry in general, ando course it is still used there today. But in the late 140s, Detroit was the recipient of criticism, even though automobiles had reasonable justification to use streamlining under the original meaning. But streamlining was no longer the problem. Simply put, it was more a matter of an excessive amount of decorative chrome and extraneous details such as tail fins, elaborate hubcaps, and hood ornaments that Ravmond Loewy would call "jukeboxes on wheels" in a 1955 speech he would deliver to the Society of Automotive Engineers.

The same critics also did not approve of the Detroit term "styling," which by now had become almost synonymous with decorative surface embellishment, rather than the ergonomic consideration and convenience of passengers. Cars were so low that entry was made difficult even without a hat. There were no seat belts or air bags. You may recall that Harley Earl had changed the name of his group at General Motors from the "Art and Colour Section" to the "Styling Division" in 1937. The term "styling," for many, suggested cosmetic decoration, excessive streamlining, and insincerity, rather than the more comprehensive practice of industrial design, which would have considered safety devices and user convenience. Had Earl used the term "industrial design" instead of "styling," he could have avoided such criticism, but he did not. It seemed that he and his colleagues envisioned their work as something different than designing toasters and refrigerators.

It was the beginning of a sustained split within the industrial design community, with three more or less independent groups: the single-product designers, who worked mainly, but separately, in the home furnishing industry (American Designers Institute); the automobile industry (Detroit); and the multiproduct/multiclient designers who had founded the Society of Industrial Designers in 1944. As the number of industrial designers began to increase exponentially after about 1948, when the first wave of new graduates arrived in the field, these three groups would slowly blend into the single identity of industrial design over the next 20 years.

Ford introduced its first postwar car design in 1949 that would become an automotive classic. As mentioned earlier, Henry Ford II had in 1946 selected the design concept of industrial design consultant George W. Walker over that of Bob Gregorie, the head of Ford' styling studio, causing Gregorie to resign. Walker had actually submitted three quarter-scale clay concept models to Henry Ford II, one each by three of his staff members: Elwood Engle and Joseph Oros, Walker's key designers, and a model maker, Dick Caleal, just hired by Walker because of the clay model he had made at Walker's request. It was Caleal's model that got Walker the job with Ford.

How Caleal, a lowly model maker, created a concept that won out over two of the best automotive designers (three, if you include Gregorie) in the industry was unknown at the time. But a story of intrigue would come out, essentially because of Robert Bourke, then head of Loewy's Studebaker team in South Bend. Studebaker's chief engineer had ordered Bourke to cut Loewy's staff because of budgetary reasons. Bourke had to fire several people, including one of his model makers, Caleal. Now unemployed, Caleal had approached Walker for a job and was told by Walker that he would be hired if he built an acceptable quarter-scale clay model of a concept proposal for the 1949 Ford, for which Walker was trying to get a contract, within a week.

Caleal excitedly called Bourke with the good news and asked if he could help him plan the model. Bourke, wanting Caleal to get the job with Walker, gave him a wooden "buck" or armature for the quarter-scale clay model, and some wheel models. Bourke and three other designers agreed to help Caleal with the model on evenings in Caleal's Mishawaka, Indiana, home when he had the model up to rough size, in order to help him meet the deadline. They did so on Caleal's kitchen table, heating the modeling clay in the kitchen oven. Based on Bourke's rough sketches, they all worked on the model at the same time, Bourke handling the front grille and motif.

The use of the term "streamline" on the cover of Time magazine with Loewy's picture illustrated the persistent popularity of the term in 1949 to describe industrial designed products. By this time, the surviving holdout for 1930s "streamlining" was the automotive industry, led by General Motors. Most design professionals were abandoning the term, as well as the practice, on stationary products. Nevertheless, by coincidence, that same month, Harold Van Doren, then president of SID, who was also part of SID's promotion campaign, wrote a feature article, "Streamlining: Fad or Function?" in the October 1949 issue of Design, the monthly journal of the British Council of Industrial Design (CoID, established in London in 1944), which had just begun publication of its professional journal. In the fall of 1949, the 1950 Studebaker was on the market, priced at \$1,487 (\$14,500 in 2013) and designed by Loewy's South Bend Studebaker studio, with a staff of 30 under Robert Bourke. The body was basically the same as the successful 1947 model for cost-reduction purposes, so the design needed a dramatic front end to make it obviously different than the 1947. Bourke was competing for its design with Virgil Exner, who was in 1947 working directly for Studebaker's vice president of engineering, Roy Cole. Bourke won the competition and secured the Studebaker account for Loewy, Inc., over the next seven years with his famous aircraft nose spinner (see the 1949 Ford, described earlier) to create the now classic "bullet-nose" Studebaker. The first month, Studebaker sold more cars than any in its history. During its production life of three years, over 500,000 would be sold. Studebaker would become the largest independent carmaker, fourth under the Big Three, and the only serious challenger to Detroit styling. In 1948, Exner had left Studebaker and returned to Detroit to work for Chrysler, where he would become director of styling.

Ever since 1940, when it created its own separate department of industrial design, the Museum of Modern Art, led by architects, had been trying to establish its influence on the field of industrial design, which had been monopolized by nonarchitect Machine Age pioneers. MoMA had consistently criticized the designs on the market as "modernistic and streamlined aberrations." In 194l it had conducted its Organic Design in Home Furnishing Competition, won by architects, and promoted "organic" design (no "vain ornamentation or superfluity"; read: no streamlining) as its aesthetic standard. In 1946, the new director of MoMA's industrial design department, Edgar Kaufmann, Jr., had invited members of the American Designers Institute (ADI) and the Society of Industrial Designers (SID) to attend a Conference on Industrial Design: A New Profession. The discussion was largely philosophical and resulted in no specific actions. If MoMA's objective was to gain a voice in the new profession, it failed to do so. So MoMA decided to go it alone and jump into the consumer market, acting as an arbiter of public taste.

In January 1950, the first Good Design exhibition opened in the marketing center of the Chicago Merchandise Mart. It was conceived and launched by Kaufmann, in collaboration with MoMA's director, René D'Harnoncourt, and Wallace O. Ollman, general manager of the Mart, who issued this joint statement:

It is the first time an art museum and [a] wholesale merchandising center have cooperated to present the best examples of modern design in home furnishings... [We] believe and hope that in combining their resources they will stimulate the appreciation and creation of the best design among manufacturers, designers and retailers.10

There would be three Good Design exhibitions annually over the next five years: at the winter market in January and the summer market in July, both of these at the Merchandise Mart, and in the fall, selections were transported to New York for display at MoMA during the pre-Christmas period. Selection of home furnishings for the exhibition was by a threeperson committee consisting permanently of Kaufmann, and a designer and retailer appointed for each show. Public opinion was studiously avoided. Selections of 150 to 250 products were chosen from the showrooms of various manufacturers at the Mart and relocated to the exhibition on the 17th floor. At the winter market, the exhibition was open only to wholesale buyers, but after that it would be open to the public, with a guided tour offered for a fee. A well-known designer or architect designed each exhibition. A "Good Design" tag was attached to each selected design, all of which were available in the open market, and each manufacturer could purchase a kit of promotional materials, including "Good Design" tags, labels, suggested window displays, and literature to promote sales of their product as a MoMA-selected "Good Design."

A number of "Good Design" awards went to imported European products. Europe had largely recovered from World War II, found distribution for their products in the United States, and promoted them through trade exhibitions. Germany, Japan, and Italy had rebuilt their manufacturing capabilities, opening the gates for exported automobiles, furniture, and electronic products to the American market, and stiffening the competition for all U.S. manufacturers. Among these imports were Scandinavian products in 1948; the Volkswagen Beetle from Germany, which entered the U.S. market in 1949; and the French line of porcelainenameled cast-iron cookware, Le Creuset, in 1950.

The Museum of Modern Art and other U.S. museums promoted European design aggressively through exhibitions of Italian designs and designers such as Marcello Nizzoli (1887- 1969) of Olivetti (typewriters), and Corradino D'Ascanio (18911981) of Lambretta (motor scooters) and Giovanni Ponti (1913-1993). It seemed as if art museums were clinging to their age-old assumption that European design was always superior to American design and were urging U.S. designers and industry to imitate.

It never seemed to occur to museums that U.S. design had a unique personality of its own, or that it was U.S. design that was inspiring and leading the rest of the world to seek American markets. If other countries wanted to access the enormous U.S. market, they had to design for the U.S. consumer, and to accomplish this they often needed American industrial designers to advise them, or to hire American designers to work overseas.

But now, another war began, only 10 years after the last, this time in western Asia.

The electrical and automotive industries.. have reveled in . superfluous shiny decoration . to catch the customer's eye and attention... The effect of this has been deplored by every serious designer.. Chrome and fancy trim used freely without a design need now can be easily taken off.

At a 1954 talk to the local advertising club in Minneapolis, Brooks Stevens had described the mission of industrial design as planned obsolescence," a phrase that would become almost as controversial in industrial design as "streamlining" had been. By 1956 Stevens was using the phrase frequently and defining it as instilling in the buyer the desire to own something a little newer, a little better, a little sooner than is necessary."Actually, to us in the 21st century, this sn lxa what aps wih theatt newt vrsippone new version of a popular product. Although Stevens admitted to originating the phrase, he did not, and he may not have known it, because he was a student at Cornell at the time when Elmo Calkins, the "Dean of Advertising" partner of Calkins & Holden advertising agency, in his 1932 book, What Consumer Engineering Really Is, wrote that

obsoletism is another device for stimulating consumption. The consideration of style is a consideration in buying many things. Clothes go out of style and are replaced long before they wear out. That principle extends to other products-motor cars, bathrooms, radios, foods, refrigerators, furniture.7

Calkins went on to say the principle of obsoletism was the solution for solving the Depression because buying things increases prosperity for business, consumers, and the country. In fact, that is precisely what happened.

But in Vance Packard's 1957 book, The Hidden Persuaders, Packard (1914-1996) accused industry for "manipulating" consumers and "inducing" desire. He pointed to Stevens' planned obsolescence," and by implication, all industrial designers, as the leading culprit in this "immoral" practice. In fact, he called Stevens "the crown prince of planned obsolescence." Packard's book reached a wide audience and was hailed by many as a noble cause, because the idea of secretly and deliberately manipulating consumers was considered underhanded in some way. Packard viewed the practice as wasteful, but producing only products that are functionally "necessary" because "old ones" were no longer usable might be morally "frugal," but would be totally unacceptable to modern consumers wanting something newer and better. Consumers have not just functional "needs," but also upward mobility "wants and aspirations." Packard, a reporter and writer, obviously knew little about business, design, or consumers, but it was a time when the public had become suspicious of industry in general, partly because anti-Vietnam War activists referred derisively to the "wartime military-industrial complex."

Nevertheless, Packard's book put industrial design on the defensive, and in 1958, Dave Chapman, Walter Dorwin Teague, and Henry Dreyfuss mounted a campaign to publicly address critics. They openly spoke out against what they called "phony obsolescence" that added gimmicks and superfluous changes to products, but they agreed with "planned obsolescence" that resulted in genuine product improvements. Stevens himself argued that the item that is replaced by a new product often finds its way into a lower-priced secondary market and is purchased by someone who otherwise would not have been able to afford a new one. He saw it as essentially recycling of material goods that benefited the lower class at the same time as it produced a healthy economy.

The controversy faded in a year or so, but it was an early warning that social issues were becoming more relevant to the public than technological or business issues. The generation of Depression survivors was aging. Many of them had since 1930 been without a decent standard of living and had struggled for five years after 1945 to acquire the material wealth and comfort of the middle class.

Now their children, the so-called baby boomers, blessed since birth with all the comforts and security their parents provided, had the luxury of concerning themselves with social, political, and moral issues such as civil rights, clean air, clean water, and environmental concerns. These would soon become mainstream preoccupations with the boomer generation, along with the nuclear age. The first nuclear power plant, in Shippingport, Pennsylvania, went into service in 1957, and in 1958, the first nuclear-powered merchant vessel was launched. # Postmodern and Passages

Finally, after 30 years, designers were tiring of the black boxes and white cubes of Bauhausstyle modern design. It was Italians who broke the ice in 1981, when an avant-garde Milanese group founded an organization called Memphis. It created the design media event of the year with garish furniture designs with bold colors and laminates that emphasized surface decoration more than function. Presented to the beat of rock music in the manner of a fashion show, the designs were deliberately designed to be antifunctional, preposterous, and faddish. They were colorful, cheerful, and playful, the direct opposite of Bauhaus traditions. One of the designs, the Casablanca sideboard, would become the icon of Memphis.

By 1984, some U.S. educators were seeking new theories to free design from the formal dictates of so-called modern design. They came up with likening design to language, which they called "product semantics," defined as, "The study of the symbolic qualities of man-made forms in the context of their use and the application of this knowledge to industrial design. It takes into account not only the physical and physiological functions, but the psychological, social, and cultural context, which we call the symbolic environment." Got that? Although this was a highly academic and theoretical theory, many designers began to abandon the formal "good design" of the 1950s.

Many new designs were being described as "postmodern," which meant that they had departed from the formal, Bauhaus-style, simple, geometric style to more informal, expressive, humanistic, and playful styles. These included cameras that suggested humanoid facial features, modern furniture that imitated 18th-century styles such as Sheraton or Chippendale, teakettles with a litte bird whist  h spout, a batroom hower ue with -haped ors gesting waves, and travel irons that looked like miniature steamships. In 1990, Alessi would introduce a lemon juicer called the Juicy Salif that looked like an insect and would become a cult object.

In effect, design now offered an infinite variety of styles that could just as easily be based on traditional historical forms as those of future science fiction. The "ideal" style was that which best fit the specific individual product's function and usage and its primary audience, the consumer. By this time, computer aided design (CAD) was driving the manufacturing, engineering, and industrial design of products. Installation of CAD technology was revolutionizing inhouse and consulting design firms across the industry. The historical restrictions of machine tools that controlled design were now replaced by computer-aided tooling that enabled designers to specify complex shapes without regard to those earlier physical restrictions. CAD proficiency was a prerequisite job requirement for all industrial designers and for anyone in the product development field. The days of hand-drawn renderings of product concepts were over, replaced by realistic computer-generated illustrations.

They are all gone now, those extraordinary designers of the Machine Age, and many are forgotten. Their proud legacy is today's prospering profession of industrial design, which they invented in the 1930s, practically out of thin air, by transforming our everyday environment of 19th-century, unwieldy, unattractive, mechanical devices, from automobiles to toasters, office equipment, and kitchens, into sleek and attractive works of art that enhance our daily experiences emotionally and visually, as well as functionally. Today, we regard our man-made physical environment of mechanical, electronic, and digital devices as an unremarkable and a natural result of modern technology, as if they magically appeared out of nowhere.

But in 1930, neither technology nor engineering knew how to shape the products it produced by the millions into forms that were designed to address human convenience, utilization, or emotional needs that made them attractive and desirable to consumers. Ordinary artists, the Machine Age designers of the 1930s, bravely ventured from their comfortable lives into a new world of complex, dirty factories, business offices, and brutal sales competition in order to transform everyday products into modern design, which encouraged people in the depths of the Depression to visualize a better future and lifted a failing manufacturing industry into profitability. A recovered economy led to the highest standard of living in the world after World War II, and the machine age designers established an educational system and business models for the new profession that became integrated into the modern world of manufacturing, production, and sales.

Today's industrial designers perform the same services for manufacturers and consumers that the machine designers of the 1930s did, only better and faster. The process remains the same, except that instead of hand-drawn illustrations of proposed designs and manually prepared mechanical drawings, computers now perform these tasks with the instructions of designers. Prototype models are often not built by hand but generated into three dimensions by computers. Contrary to many pessimists in the 1960s, who concluded that all consumer products had already been "designed" to perfection, technology continues to generate new products that need to be shaped and modified for human use and understanding, and this is likely to continue indefinitely.

Many of the organizations and designers mentioned in this book ceased operations over the years due to death or business closure, but, surprisingly, quite a few of the design organizations of the founders survive to this day, a tribute to the tenacity of their founders and a source of pride to their heirs.
