During the New York World's Fair, the Laboratory School of Industrial Design had published A Design Students' Guide to the New York World's Fair, supervised by a new faculty member, Paul Rand (1914-1996), who would become a famous graphic designer. It sounded like sour grapes from the jealous MoMA crowd.

Teague recognized the interdisciplinary nature of problem solving, stating, "We see that we are not building little gadgets. We are building an environment, and we designers have to work with scientists, engineers, technologists, sociologists, and economists." He was trying to correct the view that many held about industrial designers: that they were egotistical, independent, and obsessed with personal aggrandizement.

Norman Bel Geddes wrote his second book, Magic Motorways, using materials from his design of Futurama in the General Motors pavilion of the World's Fair, which, as you recall, was based on Geddes's 1937 City of Tomorrow exhibit for Shell Oil, as well as his futuristic concepts in his 1932 book, Horizons. These high-speed road concepts had been evolving since 1908, and the Automobile-Verkehrs und Übungsstrasse (Automobile Traffic and Training Road, or AVUS) implemented in Germany in 1921 was the first controlled-access road in Europe, which was also used as a racetrack. Nazis built the more extensive Autobahn starting in 1932. As auto traffic increased in prewar America, these concepts had become influential in the development of U.S. freeways and postwar interstate systems. In fact, both the Pasadena Freeway and the Pennsylvania Turnpike, the earliest such major systems constructed in the United States, were both opened in 1940.

The third book in 1940 was by Harold Van Doren, who wrote Industrial Design: A Practical Guide. It was essentially a how-to book on how to become an industrial designer, with detailed illustrations, specifics of presentation, model making, basic principles of design, educational programs, sketching and rendering techniques, mechanical drawing, design patents, and working with clients. In short, it was a textbook for students of industrial design. Van Doren was the first to formulate a good definition of industrial design:

Industrial design is concerned with three-dimensional products or machines, made only by modern production methods. Its purpose is to enhance their desirability in the eyes of the purchaser through increased convenience and better adaptability of form to function; through a shrewd knowledge of consumer psychology; and through the aesthetic appeal of form, color, and texture.

Van Doren also recognized that designers needed to suppress their egotism and celebrity status to blend in with industry:

Once the industrial designer had made a dent on industry, it was perhaps natural for him to exaggerate his own importance to the scheme of things. Indeed, one might almost say that he would not be a good designer unless he had that sort of excited enthusiasm that makes salesmen sell and designers create. But in sober moments, he must have realized that, important as his contribution might seem to him, its relative importance might not be so great. As a rule, the artist is, and should be, only one of the gears in the train that includes management, sales promotion, advertising, engineering, research—all those departments making up the complex mechanism of modern commerce.7

These designers clearly recognized that industrial design had matured into a formal profession essential to the business world and wanted to document their personal leadership in the new field. They sensed that a new era was dawning and could see the expanding opportunities ahead.

Despite, or perhaps because of, the threat of war in 1940, the Depression of the 1930s was essentially over. People were spending again, a housing boom was in progress, 80 percent of American households were wired for electricity, 66 percent owned a car, 52 percent had electric washing machines and refrigerators, and 42 percent had vacuum cleaners. But now the problem became one of inventory shortages of household goods in the face of increasing consumer demand. As industry was preparing for increases in production to meet this new demand, the Defense Department was offering lucrative defense contracts for essential military supplies and equipment. Although still neutral, America was providing allies such as Britain, China, and France with large quantities of essential war materials, and many industries shifted to war production, leaving consumer needs unfilled. Many stores were limiting sales to their regular customers only.

That same year, Edgar J. Kaufmann, Jr. (19101989), wrote to Alfred Barr director ofMoMA, proposing an Organic Design in Home Furnishing Competition. Kaufmann had been a resident apprentice in architecture at Frank Lloyd Wright's Taliesin East School and Studio from 1933 to 1934, and his father, Edgar J. Kaufmann, a wealthy businessman and owner of Kaufmann's Department Store in Pittsburgh, had commissioned Wright to design Fallingwater, Wright's famous 1936 modern house built over Bear Run Creek in Fayette County, Pennsylvania.

Kaufmann's proposal fit MoMA's strategy perfectly on a number of levels, and in fact was highly influential in determining the postwar direction of furniture design, and eventually the entire field of industrial design. First, MoMA saw furniture as a field in general that, except for Herman Miller, had yet to be dominated by modern design, particularly modern American design by pioneer industrial designers, as had the automotive and appliance fields. Most furniture makers were still imitating historical period styles, such as Colonial, Chippendale, Queen Anne, Arts and Crafts, etc. So if MoMA could establish and promote modern design standards for furniture, it could claim leadership in a category traditionally viewed as the historical province of architects.

Secondly, the use of the term "organic" was distinctly an architectural term used almost exclusively since 1908 by Frank Lloyd Wright, America's most famous architect, for his own work. It was derived from his mentor, Louis Sullivan (18561915), whose slogan "form follows function" became the mantra of modern architecture. Wright changed the phrase to "form and function are one," using nature as the best example of this integration. In his 1939 book, An Organic Architecture: The Architecture of Democracy, Wright wrote, "So here I stand before you preaching organic architecture to be the modern ideal .. determining form by way of the nature of materials."

But since 1932, when the Museum of Modern Art promoted the new International Style based on European architects, Wright was regarded by many U.S. architects as an old-school, irascible, irrelevant has-been. So it was somewhat ironic that MoMA would use this term at all for this competition. However, the term served MoMA's purpose of relabeling 'modern design' with an obvious architectural term, rather than "streamlining," a common term that had been associated with American industrial designers during most of the 1930s, and which MoMA had repeatedly denigrated as "modernistic and streamlined aberrations."

According to Noyes, "The purpose of the contest was to discover good designers and engage them in the task of creating a better environment for today's living." Noyes defined design implicitly as teamwork, so all contestants except textile designers were to be teams of two or more designers. The competition itself was to be a business deal, a collaboration between museum, designers, and corporations. MoMA ensured that that would be the result.

Throughout 1941, the war in Europe continued to intensify. In April, the four Axis powers, Germany, Italy, Hungary and Bulgaria, invaded and dismembered Yugoslavia. Greece fell. In June, Germany invaded the Soviet Union, overran the Baltic States, and laid siege to Leningrad. In December, a Russian counteroffensive drove the Germans from Moscow suburbs into retreat. Finally, when Japan bombed Pearl Harbor on December 7, President Roosevelt declared war on Japan the next day. Within a week, Germany and its Axis partners declared war on the United States.

Now on a full war footing, Roosevelt's "Arsenal of Democracy" quickly moved into full speed. Most domestic manufacturers were overloaded with military contracts for aircraft, tanks, arms, munitions, and military equipment, essentially ceasing to produce consumer goods. The last passenger car, a Pontiac, was produced in February 1942. While General Motors was making tanks, the Styling Section under Harley Earl was designing camouflage for military vehicles and weapons during the war. Ford was making Jeeps; the Hoover Company was making bomb fuses and helmet liners; and General Electric was making gun turrets, howitzers, bazookas, and aircraft engines.

To avoid inflation, the Office of Price Administration (OPA) established price ceilings on manufactured goods still on the market, including refrigerators, irons, radios, ranges, typewriters, phonographs, and washing machines. Americans were told to "use it up, wear it out, make it do, or do without." Rationing was imposed on gasoline, shoes, butter, four, fish, canned food, coffee, cheese, sugar, and meat. New home construction was stopped to make construction materials available for the military or government purposes. People started "victory gardens" in their backyard to provide food for the table and were urged to collect metal scraps, tin cans, paper, silk, nylon, and grease for the war effort. There were no products made of steel, chromium, tin, aluminum, copper, or nickel, or even some plastics such as acrylic, nylon, or Bakelite, all of which were required for the war effort.

Many young men were enlisting and being drafted into the armed forces and being replaced in the workforce, in many cases by women. Registration for the draft had begun in the summer of 1940 for unmarried males between the ages of 18 and 45, and male enrollment in colleges decreased dramatically as many potential students entered the services, as well as many recent graduates. Thousands of men and women relocated to new "defense towns" being built near major new manufacturing plants around the country to work on production lines of war goods. Everyone was affected, including those in the industrial design profession.

In 1942, Frederick Hurten Rhead, the designer of colorful Fiesta dinnerware in 1936, passed away at age 62 while still heading design at the Homer Laughlin Company in Newell, West Virginia. Fiesta had remained popular until the war, when the government took control of all available uranium for the atomic bomb, thereby taking away the vibrant red-orange uranium oxide used by Homer Laughlin in Fiesta. Without that dramatic color, sales of Fiesta declined gradually, and it would finally be discontinued in 1973. But in 1986, on the 50th anniversary of its introduction, the line would be revived at a time when vintage design would be embraced.

Walter Von Nessen also passed away in 1943, at age 54. After his death, his wife closed their studio, but she revived it after the war to continue production of his lamp designs, as well as innovative lamps of her own. Designer Stanley Wolf, who would purchase the business in 1954, joined her and would continue the Von Nessen tradition with his own designs, as well as designs commissioned from Elizabeth Kauffer and George Nelson in the 1960s. Today, Nessen Lighting in Mamaroneck, New York, is still producing Von Nessen's famous swing lamps of 1927, as well as a complete line of modern lighting fixtures.
