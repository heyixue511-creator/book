I could feel the tears welling in my eyes. They always did at this part of the keynote where I described diversity as getting the invitation and inclusion as getting invited to dance, but that they are not enough. I tell the audience a secret—that I once got bullied out of a job after standing up for myself and the team when an executive director came in to give a “swoop and poop” on the work that the team had done.

“The next day,” I say, “my teammates would not meet me eye to eye. Within the week, there were meetings about me, to which I had not been invited. By the end of the week, I had been reassigned from plum assignments. Always having an exit strategy in place, I soon left the company and found another position. Inclusion is not enough because if you choose to dance the salsa instead of the foxtrot, you get punished. Thus, takeaway #2 is that asking diverse peoples to dance to a white European, male CIS, hetero, middle-class, abled body and mind, Christian status quo (i.e., the power structure) is genocide to our spirits.”

After an interview with David Brown for the advertising magazine The Message, he asked in a follow-up question via email if this experience was a reflection of “bad management and unhealthy for any employee.” I explained to him that when you are Black and female, the lack of acceptance of who you are, the lack of respect, compounds the toxicity of any work environment because it is a reflection of how the whole society sees you. In other words, each difference that I have from the list of normative identities means that the entire world is, more often than not, also a toxic environment.

The sad thing is that this experience was not the first or the last time that I was bullied from a work environment. Much like the proclamations made by design firms and institutions after the summer of 2020, the high-tech firms and design education institutions where I have worked all championed diversity and inclusion. In fact, my presence was often used as a tangible proof of their commitment. Yet there are clear reasons why my experiences at these design firms and institutions were failures of diversity and inclusion. The diversity was never allowed to reach a critical mass to influence the culture, strategic direction, and real power structure of the institution. Instead, my inclusion required me to culturally assimilate to the dominant norms, often working against my own values. By having you

understand my experiences of failed diversity and inclusion initiatives, my intention is that you will find success by refocusing your efforts on the real systemic change of decolonization.

## Of Black Squares and DEI

When design firms and institutions made up some of the 28 million Instagram users, including myself, who posted their Black Squares in solidarity with the #BlackLivesMatter movement in the summer of 2020, I was skeptical. Like most other Black folks, I had experienced performative allyship before. The skepticism was so widespread that immediately in June there was a burst of newspaper and magazine articles and blog posts explaining what performative allyship was and how allies, especially white allies, could avoid it. Forbes and Fortune, Fast Company, the Guardian in the UK, Elle Australia, and even Goop published articles. My favorite was “Performative Allyship Is Deadly (Here's What to Do Instead)” written by Black sociologist Holiday Philips on Forge Medium on May 9, 2020.

For most of us Black, Indigenous, Latinx, Asian-Pacific, Middle Eastern, and other nonwhite European folks, our skepticism of performative allyship is passed down through the generations. My parents’ generation, growing up during the United States Civil Rights Movement in the 1960s, had described white folks who said they supported civil rights, but still would not rent housing to Black people. My grandmother's generation told stories of stores that wanted Black dollars, but only if they agreed to be served by the backdoor. My great-grandmother's generation generally had very few stories of allyship to tell. There are stories of individual kindness, for example of the big German woman, Katrina, after whom my greatgrandmother was named, but not stories of general white allyship. Even though my family had lived in Indiana itself (that is, in the North) for at least seven generations, which was two generations more than could be traced to the Woods family homestead, we carried enough genetic memories and contemporary reports from the South, even if just Southern Indiana, to remind us of the daily horrors of white supremacy and thus to never fully trust white people.

For me, anyone's action is performative allyship if they don't give up something precious in order to advance decolonization or social justice.

And what little trust is put into white people has to do with the few examples of these sacrifices in our family stories as well. For example, my Uncle Sonny discovered that the mother of my Great-Grandmother Jennings was not Indigenous as the family stories told, but rather white and of European heritage. She had lied on her marriage certificate so that she could marry a Black man. Yet her disownment by her family was so complete that she gave each of her children the names of her lost brothers and sisters. So, we give major Prince-level side eye, but also carry with us the temptation to give white people the benefit of the doubt.

Thus, my skepticism turned to curiosity when I started receiving calls from design firms and institutions asking, “What can we do?” after hearing me talk about OCAD U's Black cluster hire at the “Where Are the Black Designers?” conference on June 27, 2020. These vetting conversations made it clearer to me that these people were sincere in at least making big plans through hiring initiatives. Hiring initiatives are very important because they are what can really change the structure and focus of the firm or institution. Hiring initiatives have always been the place I have started in decolonizing design for very practical reasons:

► Revolution happens from within. For a firm or institution to change, it needs us diverse peoples to be in the position to help the organization make different decisions to bring about the change.

► Diverse peoples bridge to external communities. If the firm or institution wants to connect with a community, it needs to have insiders within that community as insiders to the firm.

► Hiring initiatives directly confront the history of systemic exclusion from paid employment that has left diverse communities marginalized economically. There are caveats in terms of fair pay and working conditions. But one of the ways in which we diverse peoples around the world are left vulnerable is by firms and organizations not hiring us.

During the month of June 2020 in the midst of #BlackLivesMatter protests against the murder of George Floyd, OCAD University announced the results of our Black cluster hire with the appointment of five Black fulltime faculty to the Faculty of Design. It was the beginning of the institution making amends for the 144 years of zero representation of full-time Black faculty in design. Because OCAD University was ahead of the curve by a year, we were able to help inform and guide other Black hiring initiatives as they were being announced and planned both in industry and higher education throughout North America. In July 2020, Rhode Island School of Design announced its intentions for its own ten-person Black cluster hire across its art and design programs in response to student and faculty activism. In August 2020, University of California Irvine announced their investment in the Black Thriving Initiative faculty cluster hiring program by hiring ten FTE (full-time equivalent) faculty over the next three to four years. In November 2020, McMaster University announced a cluster hire of twelve Black faculty. While the design industry did not explicitly call for Black cluster hires, they did announce intentions to increase the hiring of Black people, in spite of the pandemic.

After a June 16, 2020, open letter from Call for Equity from People of Colour in Advertising and Marketing (POCAM), the advertising industry, especially in Canada, was quick to respond with hiring initiatives. POCAM co-founder Stephanie Small, a Black woman who was formerly the creative operations manager at Taxi Agency Toronto, led the establishment of Black Taxi in July 2020. This special division embarked on a rewriting of Taxi's HR policies and job descriptions (both directly influenced by OCAD U's Black cluster hire work), including shifting the agency's outreach efforts, eliminating unpaid internships, and partnering with organizations to provide mentorship. I am personally aware of its success because at least two OCAD University Black advertising students directly benefited from Black Taxi's efforts. In June 2021, POCAM established a tracking system for the 106 agencies and clients who answered The Call to Equity.

In July 2020, Publicis Groupe assigned leadership to Mia Pearson, Brent Nelson, and Stephanie McRae for its Diversity and Inclusion initiatives across all eight business units with the consolidation of funds and then an additional US\$45 million in funds. It identified four pillars of focus: education (first focused on anti-Black racism), outreach (to improve its pipeline), talent management (retention and promotion), and giving back (community partnerships and philanthropy).

I had the opportunity to speak with Stephanie McRae, the Black female head of HR for Leo Burnett and Saatchi and Saatchi, when they were finalizing their priorities. It was clear that Publicis had a sophisticated understanding of the issues. A year later, according to a report published in Adweek, Publicis's US hiring and promotion efforts showed modest increases among Black (5.4% to 5.9%), Hispanic or Latino (8.1% to 8.3%), two or more races (1.9% to 2.7%), and Asian (15.1% to 15.4%) groups; a steady rate of American Native (0.4%) groups; and decreases in white groups (69.1% to 67.3%).1  The positions of greatest growth were at the entry level, with the company recognizing that more work was needed to increase representation at the leadership levels and with Indigenous groups.

In July 2020, two hundred CEOs of Canada's largest institutions signed the Pledge to the BlackNorth Initiative against systemic racism to have “3.5 per cent of executive and board roles in Canada held by Black leaders by 2025.” 2  By June 2021, it had increased the number of signatories to 500 companies and institutions across Canada, although still not disclosing any increase in the number of Black people appointed to executive or board positions.

## DEI Is Not Decolonization

While these hiring initiatives are important, they are not the same as decolonization. Even as diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives, they can be subject to pitfalls that might doom them to failure. One of the ways in which I determine whether a North American or Australian design firm or institution is focused on diversity and inclusion or on decolonization is whether their hiring initiatives are for Indigenous Peoples first.

My first Indigenous hiring initiative was as the program leader for the Master of Design (Design Anthropology) Program at Swinburne University of Technology in Melbourne, Australia. In the specialization Indigenous Ways of Knowing, co-created by Dr. Norm Sheehan, members of the local Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities, and me, we successfully hired four Aboriginal faculty to teach the courses for the specialization as well as the fundamental design module on 2D design and Indigenous Ways of Knowing. What makes me proud is that these four went on to use the

teaching credentials gained at Swinburne to find higher positions in education, arts, and culture.

OCAD University's first cluster hire to address underrepresentation was an Indigenous cluster hire. I initiated this program after two failed single searches for an Indigenous faculty member and an Indigenous administrator. As an institution, OCAD U was not ready yet because we did not know enough about what decolonization really meant to us. I proposed to my fellow deans that we pool our searches together for a total of five positions. We would have the provost lead the search and have a majority of Indigenous faculty on the search committee. Once Provost Jill Siddall accepted the proposal, there were critical steps to make it happen:

1. Our Office for Diversity, Equity, and Sustainability Initiatives (ODESI) had to reconfirm that we could hold the Indigenous-only cluster hire under the Special Program of the Ontario Human Rights Code, which allows for positive discrimination where there is demonstrable underrepresentation. ODESI was responsible for creating the context for decolonization for ten years before I arrived at OCAD U. Because they are independent of the university, they have been able to hold the university accountable for the systemic changes needed to create a work and learning environment based on principles of human rights, equity, and accessibility. This work can be done even at places that do not have a Special Program; I based my idea of a cluster hire on the work that University of California, Riverside, had done from 2015 to 2017 to use interdisciplinary subject-matter research clusters to boost diversity and inclusion of Indigenous, Black, and Latinx scholars.

2. The OCAD U Faculty Association (i.e., the union) and HR had to work together to negotiate a different format for the hiring committee so that the provost would chair it, there would be majority Indigenous faculty and student representation, and two deans would be on the committee. This format was outside of our normal Memorandum of Agreement, and its successful negotiation highlights the importance of having union support for decolonization efforts.

3. Human Resources had to prepare the training of the committee members and adapt the hiring database system to enable Indigenous

candidates to self-identify.

4. The wider OCAD University community had to endorse the concept through the university's senate.

Both Australia and Canada have federal mandates to support hiring Indigenous first. Swinburne University had a Reconciliation Action Plan based on the Bringing Them Home report on the stolen generations of Indigenous children, and following up on the 2008 formal national apology by Prime Minister Kevin Rudd. OCAD University had a Presidential Task Force on Underrepresentation and followed the recommendations of the 2015 Canadian Truth and Reconciliation Committee, especially the call “to develop with Aboriginal groups a joint strategy to eliminate educational and employment gaps between Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal Canadians.” 3 Although some aspects of the Indigenous cluster hire were contentious, the community rallied around the idea and we successfully hired five new Indigenous faculty members in 2018.

If you are not figuring out how to eliminate the barriers to hiring Indigenous Peoples in your firm or institution, and specifically people Indigenous to wherever you are located, then you are not engaging in decolonization. As discussed in chapter one, decolonization means ceding the power of decision making to the original peoples of the land. Hiring Indigenous Peoples in critical mass and in real positions of power will help you avoid the several pitfalls of diversity, equity, and inclusion hiring initiatives.

There are many pitfalls, but in my career, two are most likely to cause a firm's or institution's DEI and decolonization initiatives to fail: (1) looking for a supertoken, and (2) forcing cultural assimilation to white supremacy cultural values.

## Beware of the Supertoken

The first pitfall is seeking a supertoken. When I am asked what it takes to become the first Black dean of a faculty of design anywhere in the world, my answer is straightforward. I am a supertoken, which I define as an individual from marginalized group(s) whose talents are so desired by institutions that they are able to overcome their innate aversion to the individual's identities in order to have access to those talents. Former

United States President Barack Obama is a supertoken. Almost any Indigenous, Latinx, Asian-Pacific, Middle Eastern, or other nonwhite European person who is “first” at anything is more likely to be a supertoken.

My talent is “everyday genius” level intelligence, specifically for pattern recognition and the creative synthesis of new patterns. When I was in my late twenties, a mentor once gave me the book The Gifted Adult: A Revolutionary Guide for Liberating Everyday Genius by Dr. Mary-Elaine Jacobsen.4  The book's premise is that to be a gifted adult means not just having a high intelligence quotient, but that you operate at a different level of sensitivity and intensity than other people. Thus, because you process information more quickly, you might move from A to D while skipping steps B and C, which means you have to cultivate the patience to walk people through the B and C steps that you skipped. Your energy and intensity are high, which means you might have three times the productivity of your peers, which “makes them look bad.” The book helped me to realize that while people may want my talents, they may not want me. Part of it is the disruptiveness I bring to systems, but part of it is also that those talents come in a young-looking, Black, cisgender female, which breeds resentment because white supremacy and sexism say that I shouldn't be that smart or that successful.

While the supertoken might originally break down barriers, they also can be used to erect new barriers. The factual answer to how one becomes the first Black dean of design is that I had to have a PhD from Stanford University, worked at least seven years in the high-tech industry as a consultant, led the US National Design Policy Initiative, and served as an Associate Dean of Learning and Teaching at a university in Australia. The list of accomplishments in my CV are so rare that not even I feel as if I can live up to them. So it is dangerous if I am used as the “standard.” It could mean that other marginalized people might be blocked from access or advancement because they are not “like Dori.”

Seeking a supertoken might bring surface diversity but not necessarily inclusion, and definitely not decolonization. The supertoken exists because they have already excelled in systems that were meant to crush them. Their presence in a firm or institution does not automatically change the system.

In some cases, the supertoken might be the one upholding the system with an “I made it through, why can't you” attitude. So if you are seeking a supertoken to hire, make sure that it is one who is willing to use their privileges to dismantle the systems of exclusion for others. It is in this way that they might aid your decolonization efforts.

## Forced Assimilation Is Harmful

The second pitfall that could cause a firm's or institution's DEI and decolonization initiatives to fail is bringing in diverse employees and forcing them to assimilate to the dominant culture, which is oppressive to their communities. Because of European colonization, in many places the dominant culture is white supremacy culture. In chapter three, I discussed in detail Kenneth Jones and Tema Okun's research on white supremacy culture and how design by its definition has been steeped in it. When it comes specifically to hiring, the design firm or organization has too few diverse employees to shift the organizational culture. Studies on gender parity for women have found that at least 30% of leadership positions needed to be filled by women in order to shift the decision making of the company.5 While in my work experiences I have never been the only Black employee in the entire organization, I have most often been the only one or one of two Black employees in the office. This real minority status often left me feeling vulnerable because I found that if I couldn't persuade others to share my ideas, my input would always get rejected. And many times to prevent this from happening, I would have to rely on the combination of a white male authority figure and a white female ally to support my ideas and then force or persuade others to accept them. Numerical minority status creates a lot of pressure on the diverse individual to assimilate, to not rock the boat, and to be self-erasing in order to fit in and be accepted. Without a critical mass of diverse employees, firms and organizations experience high turnover of these individuals. As I described takeaway #2 in the beginning of this chapter, asking diverse people to dance to a white European, cisgender male, hetero, middle-class, able-body and mind, Christian status quo (i.e., the power structure) is genocide to our spirits.

There are other pitfalls, of course, but these two are the ones that I have seen most reflected in my work experience and in the actions design firms and institutions have taken. For example, many of the organizations who signed the BlackNorth Initiative are seeking a supertoken to fill their quota of 3.5% Black representation in their executive and board roles. If an organization has a board of 30 members, meeting the 3.5% quota would mean including just one or two Black candidates. Because of the need for so few Black board members, an organization would want “the best,” which the supertoken represents in their excellence in the current system. Many of the hiring initiatives are focused on expanding their internship programs. Bringing only one or two very junior diverse young people into the firm or institution creates strong conditions for them to assimilate because of the real imbalance of power in their junior positions and because they will lack a peer group of other diverse interns.

## Countermeasures

Fortunately, there are countermeasures to avoid these pitfalls. Seeking a supertoken can be counteracted by addressing and redefining standards so that they take systemic exclusion into account. Assimilation can be disrupted by conducting a cluster hire that builds enough critical mass to create at least a diverse subculture in which diverse employees can live their cultures freely and have this space serve as a base for wider institutional transformation.

My work on OCAD University's Black cluster hiring in design in 2019– 2020 involved implementing the direct countermeasures to the pitfalls of hiring for diversity, inclusion, and decolonization that I had experienced. Knowing how it happened so successfully might be useful to you. These were preconditions that I set up for the success of the Black cluster hire:

► I had been clear from the beginning that I intended to address the 140-plus years of zero representation of full-time Black faculty. It was probably in my second or third Dean of Design Dispatch within the first three months of my tenure as dean that I expressed the need to address this gap. I needed to ensure early on that the community knew change would be coming, so that the OCAD University community had ample time to adjust to the idea.

► I built upon the previous success of the Indigenous cluster hire. Thus, I was able to take what we had learned about what worked and how we could have improved that process and implement these aspects into the Black cluster hire.

► I had built authentic relationships with the Black community not just in Toronto, but in design. These relationships were important to demonstrate that I had community support (a recruiting base) for the hire and that the community would recognize the accomplishment.

► I had to be willing to take risks so that the institution could take risks.

The importance of those preconditions was that they set the context for understanding and trust, which would allow the rest of the work to be done through partnerships within the institution as well as outside of it.

The first challenge was getting the university to agree to have a Black cluster hire in the fall of 2019. One of the simple ways to dismantle tokenization and supertokenization is to bring more than one diverse individual into the firm or institution; in fact, three is the minimum. The Provost's Office calculated that there was budget to hire three design faculty, so I understood that I could meet the threshold for minimal critical mass. But before going to the Provost with the idea of making it specifically the Black cluster hire, I vetted it first with my program leadership teams, the chairs and graduate program directors. Because they understood the intention to address Black underrepresentation and there had already been a successful model with Indigenous faculty, they agreed unanimously. I then confirmed with our Office for Diversity, Equity, and Sustainability Initiatives that the Special Program that allowed for the Indigenous cluster hire could be used to make the case for a Black cluster hire. The director of ODESI confirmed. With expectations that a Black cluster hire might following the Indigenous cluster hire, Professor Lillian Allen, a Black member of the Faculty Association, confirmed that it had approved a Black cluster hire in the summer when I reached out.

So finally, I went to the university's upper administration and asked if we could do a Black cluster hire. The dialogue went something like this:

Me: I think it is time to do a Black cluster hire.

The University: No, right now is not the best time. The Indigenous cluster hire was too much work for the Institution, and everyone is

stretched very thin.

Me: I agree that it might be too much for the entire institution. What if we just did it in Design? It would not require any changes to the MOA. You won't have to chair the committee. It will be like a normal search except that there would be one supercommittee and all the candidates would be Black.

The University: Maybe, but you have to check with the chairs.

Me: Done already, we met, and they said that it would be about time.

The University: You need to check with ODESI.

Me: Done already. They say go, and HR also says we are ready to go with the self-identification survey in the application forms.

The University: We will need to meet with OCADFA.

Me: From my understanding, they approved the Black cluster hire this past summer, but also since we do not need a special committee led by the provost, we don't need a change to the MOA. It can be a normal committee.

The University: Let me think it over and talk to the others.

Me: Okay, but since we have little time, I will start working on the call.

The process of making the Black cluster hire happen was my pre-empting and countering every reason they could possibly say no. The university's upper administration saw this process as doing their due diligence in addressing the risks of such an initiative. I saw it as testing my resilience to do the work necessary to counter their “No”s. Some of the barriers felt hurtfully obstructive. There was a moment when it was brought up that the Board of Governors might not approve three hires, when the three hires had already been approved in the previous Board meeting. I did the work addressing all of the concerns, because the moment I did not have a counter response, they would use my “failure” as a justification to not make it happen.

The part that I don't often talk about when describing the success of the Black cluster hire was the risks that I was willing to take. At one point, I felt that no matter how much I countered, I was not going to get a “Yes.” It was a Sunday afternoon. I remember getting off the phone with the university upper administration and exploding into a torrent of tears so hard that I could not catch my breath. I called Lillian Allen and it took me ten minutes to stop crying enough to tell her what was wrong. I explained the situation and she asked me the important question, “What did I want to do?” At this point, I felt I had only one choice, which was to leave OCAD University. Why? Because if I stayed and could not make the Black cluster hire happen, then I was just a supertoken. The most accurate way to describe what I was feeling at the moment was that it was my Kunta Kinte/Toby moment.

Kunta Kinte is a character from the Alex Haley book Roots, which chronicles the generations of Haley's family from Africa to his generation. The award-winning television miniseries based on the book premiered in 1977, and I watched it with my family when I was five years old. In one of the most visceral scenes, the overseer has Kunta Kinte tied up to be whipped because he won't accept his enslaved position, represented by being renamed Toby. The overseer whips Kunta saying, “Your name is Toby.” Kunta defiantly says, “[My name is] Kunta Kinte.” He is whipped until nearly the point of death. His body and spirit broken, he says finally, “My name is Toby.” Over forty years later, the scene still affects me.

The university upper administration probably still does not understand that each “no” or “do this first” or “have you done that” was a flick of the whip against my soul. That if I could not make the Black cluster hire happen, then I would be forgetting who I am (i.e., My name is Kunta Kinte) and allowing them to enslave me to be their supertoken (i.e., My name is Toby). This position of ultimate compromise is one in which you never want to be nor want to place your diverse employees.

Understanding the seriousness of the situation, Lillian switched tactics and asked me what I wanted to happen. And what did I want her to do? This question pushed me out of my feelings and back into strategic mode. I asked her to resolve the barrier they had put up regarding the Faculty Association, because as management, I could not do that. I asked her to see if she could talk to the administration. Then, I wrote my resignation letter, which was extraordinarily liberating. As a leader, you often have to make compromises, so it is important to know where your “line” is drawn. The success or failure of the Black cluster hire was my line. It was a relief to know that I am not willing to risk my principles for the sake of a job. My leverage in the situation was that the resignation of the first Black dean of a faculty of design anywhere would be a public relations disaster for the institution. I had built up a strong relationship with the Black communities so they would have rallied around the disrespect shown to me. And because I had hired my own personal PR firm for over a year, the news of my resignation would not be buried, but amplified throughout the community.

I gave myself until 5 PM on Monday to send my resignation letter. But I didn't have to send it. Lillian had the conversations necessary to push things forward, the OCAD Faculty Association came through with strong advocacy for the hire, and I had the final “Yes” by noon. I deeply appreciate the willingness of the university's upper administration to take this risk with me, even if the path to getting there was painful to me. So with the preconditions for success met, I could address the next challenge of transforming the hiring process. This required having a diverse search committee who understood the goals of decolonization, putting out a call to Black candidates who represented Black community values, and redefining the evaluation criteria to take into account systemic exclusion.

## Having a Diverse Search Committee

In their article “We Are All for Diversity, but …,” Özlem Sensoy and Robin DiAngelo describe how to put together a committee to avoid tokenization: “If your institution has the numbers, ensure that the search committee tilts to redress the racial imbalance by having members of color as the majority and, especially, in senior roles on the committee.” 6  By the time of the Black cluster hire, OCAD University had changed its policies to ensure diverse representation on hiring committees. Our Memorandum of Agreement allowed for an additional faculty member to serve on the committee based on their expertise and/or diversity. As there were no full-time tenured Black faculty in the Faculty of Design, we used this clause to invite another Black faculty member within OCAD University to participate. We also recruited a Black student representative. I served as the chair of the committee; thus, the most senior and the most junior member, the student, were Black. With the exception of one white female tenured faculty member and the HR representative, all of the committee members were racialized. Having chosen each faculty member, I ensured that those selected had participated in the university's cultural competency training and anti-racism/antioppression training as well as the HR-offered anti-bias training.

In my Hiring for Decolonization course, one of the frequently asked questions is, What do you do when you don't have the diversity in-house to create a diverse committee? I respond that you bring in external partners who bring that diversity. If you are in a small design firm, it might mean asking two diverse clients to serve on a hiring committee and offering them compensation, a discount, or in-kind services for their labor. If you are a medium to large institution, it might mean creating a diverse HR team first and pulling from across units to ensure there is diversity on the committee.

## Writing the Call

I never knew that it was possible for a job description to go viral, but that is what happened with OCAD U's Black cluster hire call. What do I mean by viral? A former student texted me to let me know that within an hour of its release on social media, five people had sent the call to him telling him to apply. The call went viral not because of where we placed the advertisements. Job Bank, LinkedIn, Academica, CAUT (Canadian Association of University Teachers), and Chronicle of Higher Education were not where people found it. In fact, the call made its way mostly through social media: Facebook and other Black design networks and closed groups, Black Twitter, and interviews with local Black newspapers, ByBlacks and the Source. These are the places where I personally sent the call. And in the Black tradition of call and response, when I sent out the call that spoke to Black folks directly, the community responded. And what they were responding to was being seen and understood. Here is the Black cluster hire call:

There are four major things remarkable about the call. First, I had convinced the institution to be explicit about addressing its 144 years of underrepresentation. This caused a bit of discussion with the Provost's Office and HR. They asked if I could emphasize that the cluster hire was in response to the U.N. Declaration of the International Decade for Peoples of African Descent. I compromised on leading the statement with the U.N. Declaration, but the inclusion of the 144 years of neglect was crucial for building trust within the Black communities by acknowledging the structural exclusion that had taken place within the Faculty of Design. Tip: If you have neglected specific communities, be explicit about what you have failed to do in the past, because this acknowledgment emphasizes that you are ready to do the right thing now.

Second, the call was only open to individuals who self-identified as Black peoples of African descent. This was possible because the Special Program in the Ontario Human Rights code allows for positive discrimination if an organization can demonstrate underrepresentation. Tip: If you are not located in a place that allows for positive discrimination, you can use specific subject-matter expertise in Black arts or specific demonstrable relationships to Indigenous communities to create space for diversity, inclusion, and decolonization.

Third, the call tied together Black lived experience, community connection, and their work. In earlier searches, we had learned how the phrase “lived experience” reframed the expectations of all our job candidates such that they gave a fuller picture of who they are. For example, in interviews, candidates would provide biographical information about the places where they grew up and their families. They would connect how their identities informed the kind of work they chose to do and for which kinds of communities. This tying together of lived experience, community, and work was beneficial in making the interviewing process more pleasurable for committee members, who felt they were getting to know very interesting people. It also set up a system that would eliminate diverse candidates who might tick off a diversity box, but who were not connected to diverse communities and/or did not express the relationship to their identities through their work. This nexus of lived experience, community, and work has become important in our Indigenous cluster hires, where controversy has arisen around individuals who have claimed Indigenous heritage but do not have the relationships with those Indigenous communities to substantiate those claims. But most importantly, it has helped make sure that we were seeking deep-level diversity, which management psychologists Harrison, Price, and Bell defined in 1998 as the characteristics that go beyond the surface-level differences in race, gender, age, ethnicity to recognize differences in what Laio, Chuang, and Joshi label as “personality, attitudes, beliefs, values, and lifestyle.” 7 Tip: The advantage of a cluster hire is that you can seek a cohort of diverse individuals whose lived experiences of intersectional Indigenous, Black, and/or other racialized identities have shaped different values, attitudes, and lifestyles that you want to bring into the institution.

Finally, what made the call truly go viral is that it described what the Faculty of Design needed in terms that resonated directly with the Black communities: Black speculative futures, storytelling and representations of Black lives, and hip-hop aesthetics. Black people in the community reported back to me that they cried when they read the job description, as it was the first time that they had felt seen and truly embraced as Black folks by an institution. Kestin Cornwall, one of the final hires, described his process of reading the call as, “I saw ‘Black speculative futures.’ Check. I saw ‘Black storytelling and representation.’ Check. I saw ‘Hip Hop.’ Check. That's all me.” Although the original call was for three positions, at the end of the entire search process, over 140 people, of whom threequarters were Black, applied. Because I had spent decades being part of the Black design community, I knew the language that would resonate with us. I also did some user testing at the Hue Design Summit the summer before writing the call. As designers, we are supposed to specialize in knowing our audiences, but we don't apply that to the writing of our job descriptions in an inclusive way. Tip: Use your design skills to understand the audiences you are seeking to embrace, and deliver a message tailored to their interests, not just yours.

The list of qualifications held an opening for more inclusive perspectives. We accepted equivalences for degrees, asked for demonstrated potential in teaching, and put a great emphasis on shared values about decolonization, diversity, equity, and sustainability. Yet, one of the learnings from the first Indigenous cluster hire was that we as an institution did not have a guide for evaluating candidates who did not have experience within the postsecondary sector. I also knew from previous searches how difficult it was for committees to understand which equivalencies might exist for traditional academic credentials and expectations. Thus, for the Black cluster hire, I wanted to prepare a guide to help the committee.

In analyzing the qualifications in the position description and specifically the language of the Memorandum of Agreement, I made explicit that we unfortunately had an institutional bias for a specific persona type: the traditional academic who was already embedded in postsecondary systems of design education. For education, the ideal academic candidate would need a master's degree as a terminal degree. For teaching, they would have to have taught a minimum of two years in the postsecondary sector. For relevant experience, they would have to show grants, conference presentations, and journal or book publications. All of these expectations do not take into account the systemic exclusion of diverse peoples, especially Indigenous and Black people, from the postsecondary sector. The CBC reported in 2017 that sixty-three out of seventy-six Canadian universities did not collect race-based data.8  One of the promises in the post-2020 landscape is that universities will collect and maintain this data. But even so, based on the software company Ceros's crunching of the numbers from the AIGA and Google's 2019 report on diversity in design, only 3% of designers were Black and only 0.2% were Indigenous.9  With so few Indigenous and Black students in the history of design education, how many candidates would have the six years of formal design education needed to meet the education threshold? So, I asked myself, if you were Black and excluded from postsecondary education in design, how would you have flourished in design outside of that system?

## The Praxis Star

One way to flourish would be to be a praxis star, a person with a commitment to the design industries who is doing great things in the design profession without having a formal design education. In a creative city such as Toronto, there were many examples of these individuals within the Black, Indigenous, and People of Color communities. At the time, I was thinking of the Black Canadian award-winning video, film, and television director Julien Christian Lutz (professionally known as Director X) who started out as a graphic designer and learned his trade through industry. Because OCAD University started as a technical college, there were examples of these individuals already within the Faculty of Design. But we needed to codify the industry achievements and qualifications of the praxis star to demonstrate how they would appear outside of the postsecondary system.

I used equivalency tables from the City of Barrie, Ontario, and the USbased HR consultancy Coordinated Care Services, Inc., to determine the equivalents of master's and doctoral degrees in work experience. If teaching is really about organizing and transferring knowledge from yourself to another group of people, a person who is giving design talks or workshops would be demonstrating those same skills. If relevant experience is about disseminating knowledge to wider audiences and preparing proposals that are accepted by peers, a person who gave professional talks, won small commissions or projects, or had local publications write about their work was achieving the same thing. The role of the praxis star within the institution would be to help connect us, and especially students, to the diversity of practices within the design industry.

## The Community Connector

Another way to flourish would be to be a community connector, a person doing great things related to design within the community because they deeply understand the systemic exclusion of diverse peoples in design. In Toronto, there were many examples of these individuals serving as youth program leaders, adult education providers, and religious leaders. The community connector's teaching ability is demonstrated through conducting community programs and workshops. Relevant experience is demonstrated through small projects, community talks, and self-published reports. The role of the community connector within the institution would be to help connect us and students to the specific values of making within diverse communities.

With these equivalencies in mind, I instructed the Black cluster hire committee not just to rank the top three candidates, but to rank the top candidate within each of the personal profiles: traditional academic, praxis star, and community connector. The committee did this, thus ensuring that the Black cluster hire was dismantling structures of systemic exclusion within OCAD University.

The Provost's Office was so impressed by the slate of candidates that it accepted the hiring committee's strong recommendation to hire five faculty, not just the original three.

The expansion of the hiring criteria of evaluation to take into account systemic exclusion is now the institutional standard for OCAD University. A group of Indigenous faculty and administrators refined it even more for the second Indigenous cluster hire in design. Furthermore, its principles have been enshrined in our 2021–2024 Memorandum of Agreement. Therefore, by policy, all academic hiring at OCAD U takes into account systemic exclusion. Through my course on Teaching for Decolonization, Diversity, and Inclusion, other design firms and institutions are adopting the framework as well.

## Key Takeaways for Making Amends through More than Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion

If you are looking to hiring as the way to begin to make amends for the colonial racism and systemic exclusion in design, I offer some real advice:

1. Make sure you are focused on decolonization by putting Indigenous demands first in your DEI hiring plans.

2. Stop seeking the supertoken and focus instead on addressing systemic exclusion, which only allows supertokens to get through your systems.

3. Accept that your firm's or institution's culture and practices will change because you are ceding power to those who have different experiences, lifestyles, and attitudes.

4. Write a call that embraces the community you are seeking to include by focusing on their interests, not yours.

5. Change your criteria of evaluation to include other ways in which individuals have flourished outside of your normal systems. This is where you will find creativity and innovation from their deep diversity.

The most favorite talk that I have given was for MoMA in 2019. Six weeks before the winter holidays, they had asked me to give a short talk in January of about twenty minutes on design reparations (that is, how design can help repair the damage that design has caused). I was flattered but also livid, because I was already surviving on energy vapors and they, as a powerful and prestigious institution, knew that I would drop everything, especially much-needed rest, to prepare for the talk. My therapist tells me that the wonderful thing about anger is how clarifying it is. As my anger built with each late night in preparation, I was very clear about what I wanted to say to MoMA and its wealthy patrons about reparations. Luckily, Rihanna had already expressed it very eloquently in the song “Bitch Better Have My Money.” So, on January 14, 2019, as part of the XXII Triennale di Milano Symposium in New York, I gave a talk entitled “‘B\*tch Better Have My Money’: Reparations and Design's Values, Design, Experiences for BIPOC Communities.” I opened the talk with a land acknowledgment to the Lenape peoples and lyrics from the song. Then, I laid out how design has done harm to IBPOC communities in what was basically an abbreviated version of chapters one, two, and three. I discussed the reparations that were happening at OCAD University, highlighting our Indigenous cluster hire, the work of faculty member Howard Munroe, and the work of two students, Graham Nhlamba and Pupul Bisht. Finally, I gave a call to action to help me raise \$600,000 CAD per year for a Black cluster hire.

The talk was successful in changing the conversation for the rest of the symposium, but it did not raise the \$600,000. After my talk, money and resources as forms of reparation were up for discussion. Where the money and resources were coming from and to whom they were going became important follow-up questions from the moderator, Paola Antonelli, to each panelist. To have these hard economic discussions about design's harm and potential to make repairs is crucial to decolonizing design. In this chapter, I am going to talk about money and resources, and how design firms, institutions, and organizations need to reprioritize our spending as part of decolonizing design.

## The Cost of Evil

Colonization is an economic system that seeks to transfer wealth from Indigenous, Black, and other POC communities to white people in Europe or colonial settlers of European descent. A phrase came into my head the other day, “If you are looking for evil, follow the money.” It is an original, so I get to claim the meme. I think it came to me because I've been watching a lot of film noir movies on YouTube, but also because I have been trying to calculate the full cost of reparations to Indigenous nations for the land stolen from them. There are benchmarks for Canada of what the reparations might be. As of February 2021, the Canadian government owed an estimated CA\$15 billion to First Nations families harmed by the intentional underfunding of child and family services on reserves, leading to Indigenous youth being placed disproportionately into the foster system. This is the total cost of \$40,000 payouts to approximately 250,000 Indigenous people.1  As of the writing of this book in December 2021, the Canadian government has set aside \$40 billion to compensate First Nations children, their parents, and their grandparents.2

Decolonization is about the land and the relationship of Indigenous Peoples to the land. If I did a back-of-the-envelope calculation based on the 2020 average costs of an acre of land (CA\$3,393) times the number of acres in Canada (2.467 billion), which equals \$8.37 trillion, then divided that by the Indigenous population in Canada according to the 2016 census (1.67 million), each Indigenous person would need to receive compensation of approximately \$5 million. And yet, decolonization is not about the Canadian government paying Indigenous people off and then fully owning the land. Decolonization is giving back the land. If we settlers and those who descended from enslaved Africans were to give back the land to Indigenous nations today, and they decided to allow us to remain on the land, what would it cost us non-Indigenous folks to pay rent on the lands from the Indigenous land custodians?

There are 33,481,728 of us non-Indigenous Canadians. We would all be responsible for paying the value of \$8.37 trillion, which is approximately \$249,987 for each of us to pay in rent to Indigenous custodians to continue to live on the land each year. This number does not represent back rent due, which would be reparations and thus need to be calculated for many across the generations. This number also does not represent the economic, ecological, and cultural values of the activities that have been carried out on the land, an amount which is now also due to Indigenous Peoples. The

\$249,987 per person in Canada reinforces the concept of “Have I paid my annual rent to Indigenous Peoples?,” which reminds us non-Indigenous folks that we are perpetually indebted to Indigenous Peoples for living and working on the land. Thus, the perceived costs of the efforts to decolonize design are minor in comparison to what is owed Indigenous Peoples.

Even when I think of the things that I have done to “pay my rent,” it does not come close to paying my \$249,987 due in annual rent. For example, I facilitated the 2018 Indigenous cluster hires at OCAD University, and have therefore provided employment opportunities to Indigenous people. If, for round numbers, I say that each Indigenous faculty hire provides \$100,000 in salary and benefits, the 2018 Indigenous cluster hire of three faculty in design provided \$300,000 in possible rent payment in the first year. But of course, I, individually, do not get full credit for the \$300,000. I must credit each of the approximately 600 faculty and staff at OCAD University who helped to make the first Indigenous cluster hire happen. Therefore, I get to claim only \$500 of that across the three faculty that we hired. So even in one of my best years, I still only paid a rent of \$500 out of the \$249,987 which I owed. And since two of the hires were not Indigenous to Canada, my payment in the Canadian context is only \$166.

The purpose of this hypothetical exercise is to show the level of indebtedness that those of us who live on the lands of Indigenous Peoples have to those peoples, not just in symbolic ways, but in hard currency that would affect their self-sufficiency and self-determination. We need to pay them what we owe them. We need to make similar calculations for reparations to Black communities. We need to do so for immigrant POC communities, whose labor was and continues to be exploited. Even if we know our debt is limitless, how do we begin to pay, especially if we do not have a lot of resources?

## Reprioritizing Your Design Budgets

I tell my faculty that whether your budget is expanding or contracting, all budgets are finite. Therefore, the paramount question is, what are your budget priorities? OCAD University's Indigenous cluster hires and Black cluster hires happened without gaining additional funding. I wish we could have been as fortunate as RISD to have a donor contribute money for a

Black cluster hire. The majority of the work of decolonizing design that I have done across design institutions has been possible because leadership decided to prioritize the funding of decolonizing activities within their existing budgets. OCAD University has been quite remarkable in how much it has committed to decolonizing our institution in the form of financially supporting our three faculty cluster hires, Indigenous cultural competency training, anti-racism and anti-oppression training, the hiring of IBPOC staff, the prioritizing of research funding, our Indigenous elder and scholars/practitioners lecture series and residencies, our Indigenous Education Committee and Elders Circle, and our outreach to IBPOC communities. All of this was accomplished when the university itself had annually contracting budgets, which makes its financial support even more remarkable.

One design institution that I admire for its use of existing resources to decolonize design is California College of the Arts (CCA) in San Francisco and its support of the Decolonial School. The Decolonial School is “an interdisciplinary cohort of CCA faculty committed to building a new curricular model for art and design education that foregrounds Indigenous practices and acknowledges decolonization as a continuous process.” 3  I am familiar with it because I gave a keynote at their Decolonial School Unconference in February 2020. Yet, as I described in a Communication Arts article, my relationship goes further back: “Their Decolonial School project came from assistant professor Juan Carlos [Rodríguez Rivera] hearing my talk and meeting me in 2018 at Cranbrook. He came back to CCA and shared my work with his colleagues, where they started the Decolonial School project. Over the past eighteen months, they had been holding meetings with faculty across the college and wrote a new decolonial curriculum that they piloted with students this year [2020].” 4

According to Juan Carlos, the original cohort of faculty included “Rachel Berger, chair and associate professor of the Graphic Design program; Shylah Pacheco Hamilton, chair and assistant professor of the Diversity Studies program; Susanne Cockrell, associate professor of the Community Arts and Graduate Fine Art programs; Rebekah L. Edwards, interim chair and associate professor of the Critical Studies program; and myself, Juan Carlos Rodríguez Rivera.” 5  Its current coordinator as of the writing of this book is Shylah Pacheco Hamilton, and its interdisciplinary faculty members include Susanne Cockrell, Community Arts, Social Practice and Community Engagement (SPACE) minor, and Graduate Fine Art faculty; Rachel Berger, chair of Graphic Design; Shalini Agrawal, associate professor, Critical Ethnic Studies program; Allison Yasukawa, associate professor, First Year Core Studio Program, and director of Multilingual Art and Design Pedagogy; and Angela Hennessy, associate professor, Textiles program, Critical Studies program, and Graduate Fine Arts program.6

The Decolonial School programming started with the Decolonial School Curriculum Development pilot in fall 2019, which consisted of monthly meetings, readings, and talks among fifteen to twenty faculty members. Its programming went on to include the Decolonial School Unconference, which I attended, in February 2020,7  and a list of resources. Finally, revised Decolonial School Curriculum Development sessions were held with monthly guest facilitators on three separate tracks: one for those who identify as IBPOC, one for those who identify as white, and community sessions that bring the two groups together.8

Faculty committed to the Decolonial School contributed their time and effort, receiving only service points and often uncompensated. Yet, guest speakers, the coordination of the program, and unconferences cost money. The 2019 pilot year of the Decolonial School was funded from a US\$5,000 curriculum grant from CCA's Academic Affairs budget and \$4,000 in monies collected from the four division deans. The grant was reestablished in August 2020, and \$6,000 in funding was designated to two faculty coleaders for their ongoing work. In terms of the prioritization of funding for diversity, equity, inclusion, and belonging initiatives, the college states on the DEI section of its website that “a discrete budget for these efforts will be created through existing operational funds and fundraising.” 9  There is recognition that the Decolonial School requires more funding to expand its activities, and thus it applied for a 2021 National Endowment for the Humanities grant of \$150,000, which is pending, but the majority of its efforts over the duration of the program have been funded internally by CCA.

The examples of OCAD University and CCA may make it seem as if it is easier for academic institutions to reallocate existing budgets. And yet, for all institutions, firms, and community organizations, the true discipline of decolonizing design is deciding what you are willing to give up in order to cede space for Indigenous first, Black, and other people of color who have been structurally excluded, exploited, and sometimes demonized in design. And when we fail to cede space and to cede power, it is IBPOC communities who continue to experience harm.

If I have convinced you in the previous four chapters that decolonizing design is important, what are you going to give up in order to make it happen? Here are some ideas to consider:

► If your organization was planning for a capital expansion, you might have to decide to put that on hold so that you have the resources to do a cluster hire.

► Some people, or maybe even you yourself, might need to step down from leadership in order for an Indigenous person to have the opportunity to step up.

► Eliminate or reduce your office's monthly budget for flowers, Starbucks, M&Ms, or soft drinks to hire an IBPOC illustrator full time instead of part time.

## The Key Takeaways for Reprioritizing Existing Resources to Decolonize

Each action that you take to decolonize design will help pay your rent for being on Indigenous lands in reparation for the harms that design has caused and continues to cause until it is decolonized:

1. We all owe a debt to Indigenous people for being on their land and that debt has hard financial as well as symbolic costs. Pay them what we owe them. If you are not on Indigenous land as a settler, consider where the wealth comes from in your country and to whom you might be indebted for that wealth.

2. The ethos of decolonization is stronger when you have to give up something precious in your existing budget in order to support it. But additional monies to support decolonizing are fine too.

3. There is no amount of money that will compensate the IBPOC communities for what they have lost through colonization, slavery, and systems of white supremacy. But you still need to pay up so that these communities can gain economic self-sufficiency.

# List of All the Key Takeaways

## Key Takeaways for Putting Indigenous First

1. Figure out your positionality vis-à-vis Indigenous sovereignty over the land. You must understand your own positioning in relation to the specifics of your local settler colonial state. What is the specific configuration of native-settler-slave of your country? Which identities are enmeshed in those structural relationships? If your positionality is native, the work you need to do is different than if it is chattel slave or settler. This work requires a tremendous amount of humility.

For those readers outside of North America and Australia, ask yourself questions such as who are the Indigenous Peoples of your lands? (There are Indigenous people all around the world.) Who have been the powerful and who have been the structurally excluded populations of the land? Who has been brought in to provide excess labor? What does it mean for you, as an individual and collectively as a community, to build your “good life” in the place where you are living and/or working?

2. Learn the colonial histories of the places where you are. Practices such as land acknowledgments may at first seem superficial, but they won't be if they are the first steps of learning the history of the land and Indigenous relationships to the land before colonization and within continued colonization. Decolonization is about the land. If you do not understand the land, you will not be engaged in decolonization.

For those outside of North America and Australia, what have been the histories of migration within your lands? Where did the wealth of your nation come from? Was it built from extracting the lands and resources of other peoples?

3. Make mistakes, but also make real amends. You will make mistakes. Having authentic relationships with Indigenous Peoples is the only way of being able to hold yourself accountable for your colonial ways. But understand that each mistake you make with them will cause them harm. If they trust you, they will tell you what you did and what can be done to make amends. You should follow those directives to repair the relationships and open an opportunity for healing. This will be easier for us racialized folks than for white folks, because of the histories of broken treaties between Indigenous and white people.

For those outside of North America and Australia, what harms have been done to populations who have been structurally excluded from wealth, health, education, and other resources in your country? In what ways are you or are you not contributing to their sovereignty?

4. Design objects that can transmit liberatory joy to the body and community.

There are so many people who have contributed to this process through their conversations with me, their everyday and extraordinary actions of support and nurturance, or just the exemplar of their being in the Universe.

First, I acknowledge my ancestors, whose intergenerational sacrifices, resilience, and determination for me to have a better future drive my commitment to be an ancestor to others. I recognize my great-grandmother, Katrina Middlebrook (aka Grandmugs); my grandmothers, Delores Elizabeth Tunstall (aka Mugs) after whom I am named and Bette Helms;

my grandfathers, John E. Tunstall III and George Helms II; and my mother, Leatha Laverne Tunstall.

I acknowledge my close biological family: mothers, Theresa Tunstall and Aunt Jillane Tunstall; fathers, Raymond Tunstall and Uncle Sonny (George Helms III); sisters, LaTina Tunstall and Natasha Tunstall-Smith; and brother, DuShawn Tunstall.

I acknowledge those directly involved in the development of the book. Thank you, Victoria Hindley, MIT Press acquisitions editor, for asking if I wanted to write a book and guiding me through the acquisitions process. Thank you, Mary Ann Lee, Goddess of My Schedule, for creating and protecting my time to write the book. I also acknowledge the four anonymous peer reviewers whose comments improved the manuscript immeasurably. I have undying gratitude to Salah Bachir, Jacob Yerex, and Max for lending me the 199 house as my writer's retreat, our in-depth conversations about the book and everything else, and with Nida, being chosen family, complete with evening dinners, 7:30 pm Jeopardy, and nightly movies. People say that writing is an isolating activity, but it was a communal experience due to you all. Thank you, beta-readers and those who gave me permission to share our overlaps in experiences with the world: Gabe Gonda, who reminded me to keep the human-interest element when I was doubting myself; Lillian Allen, Jason Baerg, Deirdre Barron, David Brown, Kestin Cornwall, Camille Georgeson-Usher, Caroline Langill, Stephanie McRae, Peter Morin, and Howard Munroe. Infinite gratitude to you, Reanna Sartoretto, as the professional fact-checker, whose diligence and care helped me feel confident about everything that is written in the book. I have deep appreciation and admiration for the design dream team of Ene Agi for the illustrations, Sadie Red Wing for the cover design, and Silas Munro and Brian Johnson of Polymode for the brilliant book design. I wanted to use this book to assemble the most talented Indigenous and Black designers. Thank you for making this book even more meaningful through your contributions.

Finally, I want to acknowledge my former and current students and colleagues across the United States, Australia, and Canada who have taught me why decolonizing design is important to their liberation as makers who care deeply about the world. Again, I am because we are.
