## 4. Design objects that can transmit liberatory joy to the body and community.

In the next two chapters, I bust some of the myths of the modernist project in design. After preparing yourself to be useful to decolonization, restorying design is the next step required to make it able to more closely align with the final takeaway of this chapter: design as making objects of liberatory communal joy.

DECOLONIZING DESIGN MEANS   
Dismantling the Tech Bias in the European   
Modernist Project

The ink on my PhD from Stanford less than six months old, I arrive in Chicago with my trusty Doberman Pinscher, Jerry. I am starting a job at E-Lab/Sapient at the height of the dot-com boom. It is 1999 and the world is worried about Y2K. Chicago is a city like I've never known. There are highrise buildings of glass, concrete, and steel not just in the city center, but hugging the lake shore. A newbie in town, I laugh as the wind pushes back my attempted progress of walking when caught between two high-rise condos. I am thrilled to ride the “L” into the city center and around the Loop to my job downtown in a warehouse-converted loft building. I read novels and watch the changing ethnic neighborhoods as customers get on and off their stops on the Red Line. A user experience strategist, I make six figures and design the future of technology in people's lives. The city is shiny and gritty, yet cleaner than New York City.

I was so right that I don't belong in stuffy old Naptown, old Indianapolis. I belong to the big city. But not just any city, I belong to Chicago with its hot jazz and cool blues, Black porters and politicians, the famous Art Institute and Field Museum, Calder public art, Mies van der Rohe-designed post office, ethnic gangsters, housing project towers, elevated trains, Ferris wheels, veggie Polish kielbasa, Cubs versus White Sox, Michael Jordan and the Bulls, summer gun violence, five months of winter cold and salt trucks, and Design Within Reach. Here, I can reinvent myself, not like the sunny dreams of people in Los Angeles, but a new modern me, shiny, ambitious, and just a little hard, like the city. The strike price of my Sapient shares is US\$150. In four years, I will be a millionaire and get to make tech that truly benefits my community.

I can admit now how much of a fool I was. The city of the New Bauhaus, Chicago, invited infatuation with its modernity and the role of technology in designing it. Even The Architectural Review in 1977 was infatuated:

Prior to the founding of Chicago cities were thought of as “settlements.” They were subject to growth and change but this was slow and the emphasis was on permanency. The configuration of the city changed rarely and buildings were pulled down and replaced only with reluctance.

Chicago changed all this and in doing so became the prototype of the modern city, of the “city-as-process.” Its phenomenal growth in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries is the great urban romance of the modern world.1

The aspects of the city that I first noticed when I moved there are what The Architectural Review defines as the unique characteristics of Chicago as a “city-as-process”:

► a grid plan, which enabled people to spread out and move easily;

► a railway, started in 1848, which enhanced its stature as a transportation hub;

► masonry high-rise constructions with elevators, which increased the density of the city center after the Great Chicago Fire of 1871 burnt down almost all of the timber structures in the city;

► cable cars (from San Francisco) on elevated tracks called “the L” and the Loop of the city center, which was enabled by electricity;

► the Burnham city plan of 1910 with its ring of public gardened parklands and beaches;

► underground tunnels for the movement of freight transportation; and

► expressways, with the ascendancy of cars, that connect the suburbs to the city center. 2

Another unique characteristic is Chicago's vast multiracial and multiethnic character and the degree of segregation that accompanies it. There are 65,000 Native Americans from over 175 nations and tribes, making the city the third-largest urban Indian center in the United States.3  Like other metropolises, Chicago remains a major destination for immigrants from all over the world, as it was for formerly enslaved Blacks. Each established

their own enclaves—for example, Greektown, Andersonville, or Bronzeville—for safety in numbers against the city's discrimination and economic exploitation. Redlining has specifically kept Black communities segregated and impoverished in their own neighborhoods.

All of this makes Chicago a tangible embodiment of the modernist project in design, which I approach through two overarching themes. The first theme is that through technological progress the masses may access a better life, which was previously available only to the aristocratic and mercantile classes. This is the focus of this chapter. The second theme is that by removing allegiance to ethnic and national markers, one can join a universal humankind. That will be the focus of chapter three. Each theme represents a myth that we have told ourselves in design. And these myths have done and currently do great harm because they are embodiments of colonialism. If design firms, institutions, and organizations want to decolonize design, we need to bust these two myths in order to re-story design so that it represents processes and outcomes of communal joy.

A thematic approach to design myth-busting was a corrective brought by the European design faculty at OCAD University, who represent Germany, Scotland, the Netherlands, and England. As we were discussing what decolonization means for us, they pointed out that contemporary European design has evolved beyond modernism and the Bauhaus principles. Thus, it was inaccurate to define our opposition as being against notions of Eurocentrism, understood as the belief that Europe is positioned as the center of the universe, or against Western aesthetics and practices. They reminded us that the praxis of design that we are critiquing comes from a specific time as well as specific places in Europe. European and Euro– North American design historians place the start of the modernist project in the 1800s in Europe and its peak from the 1890s to 1930s; it then reemerged after World War II from the 1940s to the 1960s. The most insistent theme of the modernist project is that of technological progress or, as more popularly described, better living through technology.

## Better Living through Technology

The modernist project in design tells the story that the life of everyday people improved during the 1800s through technological progress, which enabled designed things to be made cheaper and faster, thus opening more access for the working person to afford them. This is the story that persuaded me to go into high-tech consulting. I wanted to try to fix high technology, expand high technology's audience, and make it more inclusive. Like other privileged global elite, I accepted the quality of life that technology had afforded me over that of my parents and grandparents. I was aware of its great costs to the land and to other people, but I was inside the system trying to fix that, too. But can the system be fixed or improved? Have we bought into the romance of modern design? “Better living through technology” is such a dominant trope in design that it is important to lay some historical foundation for it. This section will get a bit scholarly, so bear with me. For those who want to go deeper into these subjects, I suggest reading Wendy Kaplan's Designing Modernity: The Arts of Reform and Persuasion, 1885–1945 (Thames & Hudson, 1995) and, for the postwar period, Ginger Nolan's Savage Mind to Savage Machine: Racial Science and Twentieth-Century Design (University of Minnesota Press, 2021).

The narrative of progress through technology is strong in the field of industrial design; it has been disseminated through the structures of world's fairs, consumer catalogues, and advertising. World's fairs are the most fascinating for me, because Chicago was a city completely transformed by its 1893 World's Columbian Exposition. The Exposition was colonial in its intent, as the celebration of the 400 years since Christopher Columbus encountered and enslaved the Taino peoples of the place now called the Dominican Republic. With 27 million visitors, the Chicago world's fair introduced the world to new technologies such as the alternating current (AC) electricity system for indoor lighting created by Tesla for Westinghouse Electric & Manufacturing. It was explicit about its goals, stating, “It is the intention of the management to make the World's Fair site and the buildings one grand exemplification of the progress that has been made in electricity.” 4  The AC electricity system became the basis of the United States electricity grid. Along with Tesla's phosphorescent lamps, several inventions debuted at the fair: the Ferris wheel; the automatic dishwasher, invented by Josephine Cochrane; the zipper, invented by Whitcomb L. Judson; spray painting, invented by Francis Davis Millet; and the movable sidewalk, invented by Alfred Speer. Many products that define American consumer life were first introduced at the Exposition, including

Aunt Jemima pancake mix, Cracker Jack, Juicy Fruit Gum, Quaker Oats/Cream of Wheat, Shredded Wheat, Hershey's Chocolate, and Pabst Blue Ribbon beer.5  According to historian Julie K. Rose:

It is this—the acceptance and even celebration of consumption and technology—which has had the most significant and lasting impact on American society. The dialogue between popular and “high” culture, and education and entertainment, at the World's Columbian Exposition was a continuation of a running conversation which has not been resolved in postmodern America—they were reflections of their time, rather than influencers. The messages of consumption (as well as the many goods introduced at the Fair), the rise of a business elite to national power, and the valorization of technology as positive progress have had the most significant and lasting effects on American society. In the great World's Columbian Exposition of 1893, we find the blueprint for modern America.6

But this “blueprint for modern America” was based on the blueprint of the modernist project in Europe. Archival documentation of the European world's fairs that preceded the Chicago one are explicit in their praise of both the technological advances and the colonial endeavor on display. Published in the book The Great Exhibition of the World's Industry, Held in London in 1851, the letters of M. Blanqui describe the Austria exhibition:

Austria has taken the Exhibition in earnest. She has appeared armed at all points, and every day the interest excited by her various products, which betoken an industrial progress worthy of the attention of manufacturing nations, is increasing. Commencing with the most liberal branch of industry—printing —I am glad to say, that the imperial printing office of Vienna has exhibited the most complete collection of specimens of all known types. This collection, which contains no less than two hundred and six languages or dialects, from Phoenician characters, the most ancient in the world, down to Japanese, is the most beautiful in Europe. … A country which manufactures as many as eight million of scythes and reaping-hooks for exportation only, is evidently organized by extensive industrial pursuits.7

Austria is praised for its industrial progress, which was earlier described as being third to its industrial rivals England and France. The Austria being described was the Austrian Empire, under the Hapsburg monarchy, which lasted from 1804 to 1867. Its industry was tied to war and colonization. Only ten years before the Great Exhibition of the World's Industry, Austria had ended the Second Egyptian-Ottoman War and in 1851 was gearing up for the Austro-Sardinian War (also referred to as the Second Italian War of Independence) with France and Sardinia in 1859.8  Its typography collection was beautiful because it showed off its colonial spoils. In fact, its exhibition was meant to show off Austria's industrial military and commercial might.

Fast-forward to the 2020 Dubai UAE World Expo, postponed until 2022 due to the COVID-19 pandemic, and we find the theme of progress through technology is still strong in its website's marketing copy:

Innovation and technology: Countless technological advances await. Unleash your inner techie, discover life-changing innovations and experience a truly connected high-tech world. Here's a brief glimpse at some of the experiences you can enjoy.

► Artificial Intelligence, UK Pavilion. Add your voice to a continuously changing collective message in a pavilion inspired by Stephen Hawking, designed by Es Devlin Studio and curated by AI.

► Explore the future of air travel, Emirates Pavilion. Land at the Emirates Pavilion and experience the Future of Commercial Aviation in UAE's Centennial Year 2071.

► Healthcare innovations, Global Best Practice Programme. See solutions for global health challenges and how humanitarian drone corridors are used to deliver medical supplies to remote communities.9

Notice that the location of the story of better living through technology has shifted to places that 200 years ago would have been exhibited as primitive and exotic. This shift indicates that the techno-centrism of the modernist project is no longer European, but rather a global one. This was visible when I visited the United Arab Emirates in 2018 as part of the Fikra Graphic Design Biennial. Various global design weeks, biennials, and congresses have been design versions of world's fairs, where professional designers and design education institutions show off their latest designs.

Established in 1971, the United Arab Emirates is a relatively new nation, which came into being when His Highness, Sheikh Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan helped form a confederation of the seven Emirate sheikhdoms after independence from British colonization. Talented curator Alia Al-Sabi led me through the exhibition at the Fikra Graphic Design Biennial on the birth of UAE design in the 1970s through government newspapers (in which headlines were hand-painted by calligraphers), magazines, children's magazines, and the national enterprise logo designs of Hisham Al Madhloum. Looking at the logo designs of Hisham Al Madhloum made me remember how the Bauhaus has played such a central part in the popularity of the narrative of better living through technology in design.

## The Bauhaus, Technology, and Me

The idea of better living through technology is a strong ideology in the United States, finding its highest expression in Henry Ford's mass production of the automobile for the common man. The Bauhaus can be credited with giving a modernist aesthetic to mass-produced designs. Although the Bauhaus was not seeking to produce a style, its principles and pedagogical approaches gave form to distinct looks that became associated with the movement. In the essay “What Was the Bauhaus and What Can It Teach Us Today?,” historian Fred Turner lists the iconic items associated with Bauhaus style: “Marianne Brandt's semi-globe teapot and her archnecked desk lamp; Anni Albers's weavings; Josef Albers's color block paintings; Oskar Schlemmer's geometric designs for theater; the abstract paintings of Paul Klee, Wassily Kandinsky, and Moholy-Nagy; and above all, the architecture of Walter Gropius—between 1925 and its closure in 1933, the Bauhaus created much of the visual vocabulary of twentiethcentury modernity.” 10

My relationship to the Bauhaus comes directly through my relationship to Chicago and the Institute of Design at Illinois Institute of Technology, which evolved out of The New Bauhaus. After leaving Germany and a stint in London, László Moholy-Nagy came to Chicago to establish The New Bauhaus: American School of Design in 1937. The school shut down after a year, but Moholy-Nagy then established the School of Design in 1939. The curriculum reproduced Bauhaus pedagogy within an American context. The school was reorganized as the Institute of Design in 1944 and merged with the Illinois Institute of Technology (IIT) in 1949, three years after Moholy-Nagy's death.11  Today, the Institute of Design at IIT provides graduate-level design education with a focus on innovation. Martin Thaler, professor at the Institute of Design, describes in his essay “Bauhaus Products for Our Time” Moholy-Nagy's and Josef Albers's version of the Bauhaus Vorkurs's pedagogical approach and how it still informs the foundational design course Thaler teaches to graduate students: “Their approach was to pursue new technologies within the realm of free experimentation, using craft techniques with the intention of moving products toward production. Moholy-Nagy favored logic over emotion. He was a notable practitioner of many art and design disciplines: product design, graphic design, sculpture, painting, photography, and cinema. Moholy-Nagy's broad abilities foreshadowed the idea of a multidisciplinary team and a kinetic and energized design process.” 12

I never formally taught at the Institute of Design at IIT, but I had deep associations with it because I gave various guest lectures and appeared in their video Getting People to Talk: An Ethnography and Interviewing Primer,13 and it had a connection to E-Lab/Sapient, my first industry job. The Institute of Design at IIT was the design educational wing of the human-centered design strategy ideas of Jay Doblin, who was director of the school for fifteen years.14 He also led the design strategy consultancy Doblin, from which E-Lab, the strategic human-centered research and innovation firm, was established by John Cain and Rick Robinson, former employees of Doblin, in 1994.15 E-Lab was being acquired by Sapient Corporation, a digital engineering company, in 1999 when I joined. Thus, there are aspects of the Bauhaus approach via Doblin that were embedded in my informal on-the-job design education at E-Lab/Sapient, including the workshop model of pedagogy, interdisciplinarity, and, especially with Sapient, the emphasis on technology as the way to make people's lives better.

The relationship to technology in the original Bauhaus State School (Staatliches Bauhaus) varied over the years. The school and its social movement were based in Weimar, then Dessau, and finally Berlin, Germany, from 1919 to 1933, inhabiting different phases and contradictions in each location. Walter Gropius was the original head of the school and established its manifesto as an institute where “the ultimate goal of all creative activities is the building,” based on the workshop as the pedagogical model and a single unity of architecture, sculpture, and painting.16 At first a staunch modern rationalist inspired by the promise of technology, Gropius swung in the other direction after experiencing the mechanized horrors of World War I. Johannes Itten, an original co-founder of the school, found inspiration in the Mazdaznan neo-Zoroastrian sect to help bring a unity among the mind, body, and spirit of the students through what historian Fred Turner describes as “the pursuit of self-realization and the development of spiritual community through vegetarianism, breathing exercises, fasting, and sexual self-control.” 17 This religious movement was also a proponent of eugenics, though, in which the arc of human history moved “from East to the West, culminating in the achievements of white European men.” 18 In 1922, Gropius forced Itten to resign and replaced him with László Moholy-Nagy. By 1925, with the move to Dessau, the discourse of the Bauhaus, as expressed by Moholy-Nagy, had shifted to something not dissimilar to the language of the world's fair texts quoted above: “These books [of the Bauhausbücher publication program] deal with the problems of art, science and technology, and for the specialist of today they attempt to furnish information about the complex problems, working methods and research results in various areas of design, thereby providing the individual with a criterion for his own studies and with progress made in other areas. In order to be able to tackle a task of this magnitude the editors have enlisted the cooperation of the most knowledgeable experts from various countries who are trying to integrate their specialized work with the totality of phenomena of the modern world.” 19

As Leah Dickerman writes, Moholy-Nagy “pushed aside for good the persona of artist as shaman nurtured by Itten.” 20 In the final years of the Bauhaus, the focus on mass production was complete. With the appointment of Marxist Hannes Meyer as director in 1928, the decorative was seen as classist luxury, and the school signed many licensing agreements for Bauhaus designs.21 In 1930, Meyer was replaced by Mies van der Rohe, who had to shut down the school in 1933 due to pressure from the Nazi government.

In the discussion of licensing agreements, the stories of the women of the Bauhaus become equally significant. Gunta Stölzl served as the workshop master for the weaving studio in 1926 and then as the technical and artistic director of the weaving workshop from 1927 to 1931. In their book Bauhaus Women, Elizabeth Otto and Patrick Rössler explain, “Under her direction, the fabrics and prototypes made by the workshop became one of the school's largest sources of income.” Yet, they point out, her salary and benefits were less than those of her male colleagues.22 Marianne Brandt was the only woman to receive a diploma from the metal workshop, where she created the iconic Tea Extract Pot and later created with Hans Przyrembel the ME 78b nickel-plated overhead light. In 1927, Brandt became the staff associate for Moholy-Nagy in the metal workshop. When he left in 1928, she became the acting director of the workshop, responsible for “contract negotiations with firms such as Schwinzer and Gräff in Berlin and Leipzig's Körting and Mathiesen to produce workshops designs.” 23 Brandt left in 1929, in part due to the frustrations of dealing with her male colleagues.

The many licensing agreements and the spread of the Bauhaus diaspora are responsible for the movement's popularity. In 1938, Herbert Bayer and the Museum of Modern Art in New York put on a massive Bauhaus exhibition, which popularized its streamlined design aesthetic of “form follows function,” “less is more,” and “chuck out the chintz.” With the provenance gained by the MoMA exhibition, the licensing agreements guaranteed that you can still purchase a Mies van der Rohe Barcelona chair for about \$8,000 by Knoll at Design Within Reach. You can buy a Bauhaus table lamp from Carl J. Jucker and Wilhelm Wagenfeld at the MoMA store for about \$1,000.

Many Bauhaus instructors and students who had to leave Germany with the rise of the Nazi party fled to the United States. Anni and Josef Albers established themselves at the Black Mountain College in North Carolina. Walter Gropius became the head of architecture at Harvard. In Chicago, where Moholy-Nagy and Mies van der Rohe both lived, taught, and worked, Bauhaus designs have become part of the urban fabric of the city. Mies van der Rohe built at least fourteen significant buildings in Chicago with a minimalist aesthetic of glass, concrete, and steel. These three

building materials define the “modern” in modern architecture. People walk into many high-rise office buildings that still have Barcelona benches in their lobbies.

In a city like Chicago, you can see how the design community promotes the modernist project to seduce young cornhuskers, like myself, to dream of a better life for themselves and their communities. The Emerald City of Frank L. Baum's The Wizard of Oz is based on the White City of the Chicago world's fair of 1893. The city beckons with romantic promises that if you come to the bright lights of the Big City, you can live a life of more convenience, more comfort, more wealth, and more glamor. Just ignore the man behind the curtain. Just ignore the poverty, the discrimination and segregation, the cold, and the corruption, which is part of the modernist project in design as well.

Better living through technology is the propaganda of the modernist project in design. This story that we tell ourselves, whether in Fast Company or through annual design awards, is harmful because technological advances in design, whether of the 1800s or now, only make some lives better. European industrialization in the 1800s went hand in hand with colonization. The better lives through technology were exclusively for European elites and, much later, for European colonial settlers. The vast improvements in lives for European workers and the poor were mostly due to their leaving Europe in mass migrations and the hard-won reforms of the labor movements in places where they settled. The post–World War II white workers of Euro–North America and Euro-Australia experienced—and still experience—the benefits of technology at the direct expense of the land and Indigenous, Black, and other racialized peoples. For the rest of us, better living through technology is a bad romance.

## Tech as a Bad Romance for White European Workers

According to economist Clark Nardinelli, the standard of living of the English working class fell during the Industrial Revolution, that is, between 1790 and 1840. While there were modest raises in the average income for those who were wage earners, the period created greater inequality in the distribution of income, and “unemployment, poor harvests, war, pollution, urban crowding, and other social ills” contributed to a lower standard of living for the poor and working-class.24 According to psychologist Dr. Martin Obschonka, the negative psychosocial effects of the Industrial Revolution even continue now for those who live and work in coalproduction areas: “We found that in regions like Blaenau Gwent in the UK and the Rust Belt in the US, people reported more unhappy personality traits, lower life satisfaction, and lower life expectancy than otherwise similar regions where these industries did not dominate. … For example, in the UK, neuroticism was 33% higher, conscientiousness was 26% lower, and life satisfaction 29% lower in these areas compared to the rest of the country … even controlling for other historical factors.” 25

The response of the European poor and working-class to their negative life conditions was to leave Europe. According to Statistics Canada, there were three waves of European immigration to Canada. The first wave was between 1900 and 1914, when the Canadian government aggressively recruited more than 2.9 million European immigrants, with 400,000 arriving in 1913 alone. The second wave was from 1921 to 1928, with on average 150,000 European immigrants arriving each year in the last three years of the decade. The third wave was between 1946 and 1950 when over 430,000 immigrants arrived, with the vast majority from Europe.26 It is important to note that there were severe restrictions on Asian, Jewish, and other non-European and non-Anglo-American immigration to Canada during these times. Meanwhile, 5 million Europeans immigrated to the United States from 1815 to 1860; 10 million from 1860 to 1890; 15 million from 1890 to the 1920s;27 and the Displaced Persons Act of 1948 resettled over 400,000 European refugees, mostly Jewish peoples.28 It is said that 3 million Europeans moved to Australia between the 1940s and 1960s under the Assisted Passage Migration Scheme of 1945.29 These mass migrations from Europe indicate that industrialization was not beneficial to the European worker or the poor individual.

## Tech as a Bad Romance for Indigenous, Black, and POC Communities

Under colonial empires, the lives of Indigenous Peoples, enslaved Africans and their descendants, and people of color were devastated by modern

technologies, which accelerated the theft and pillaging of Indigenous lands, the exploitation of enslaved Indigenous and African bodies, and the exploitation of Asian, Middle Eastern, Latinx, and other indentured bodies. As Alexander Anievas and Kerem Nisancioglu, authors of How the West Came to Rule: The Geopolitical Origins of Capitalism, argue in an interview:

Capitalism should be conceived as having provided the conditions of possibility for Northwestern European states to eventually overcome and dominate their Asian rivals. Nonetheless, it was only once capitalist Britain transformed itself into an industrial-capitalist power that it was capable of dominating other highly developed Asian societies such as China. Moreover, Britain's industrialization was greatly facilitated by both the New World “discoveries” and, perhaps even more importantly, the colonization of the Indian landmass which was only made possible through a confluence of internal and external pressures that severely destabilized the Mughal Empire by the early eighteenth century.30

In chapter one, I discussed colonialism's impact on Indigenous land and peoples. So I will just remind you that in 1830 US President Andrew Jackson signed the Indian Removal Act, which forcibly removed Indigenous nations from their homelands at gunpoint to the west of the Mississippi River.31 In Canada starting in the 1870s, Sir John A. MacDonald, Canada's first prime minister, was signing and breaking the Numbered Treaties by deliberately starving Indigenous Nations so that he could build the Canadian Pacific Railroad from 1881 to 1886. There was no better life through technology for Indigenous Peoples.

The impact on Black peoples was just as devastating. When I give keynote lectures, I have often provided the cotton gin as an example of design's harm to Black communities through the better living through technology narrative. Sponsored by plantation owner Catherine Green, Eli Whitney invented and patented the first cotton gin in 1793. The device mechanically separated the short-hair cotton fiber from the seed. Short-hair cotton fiber was the dominant form of cotton on inland Southern United States plantations and was difficult to separate. About his invention, Whitney

boasted to his father that “One man and a horse will do more than fifty men with the old machines. … Tis generally said by those who know anything about it, that I shall make a Fortune by it.” 32 Fortunately, because patent law was relatively new in the United States, Eli Whitney was not able to profit from his invention, which was easily copied by others. (In fact, he was only able to make his fortune in the mass manufacturing of guns.) But others, including the whole of the United States and Great Britain, were able to profit due to the increased cotton production. The United States National Archives state: “After the invention of the cotton gin, the yield of raw cotton doubled each decade after 1800. Demand was fueled by other inventions of the Industrial Revolution, such as the machines to spin and weave it and the steamboat to transport it. By midcentury America was growing three-quarters of the world's supply of cotton, most of it shipped to England or New England where it was manufactured into cloth.” 33

The profitability of “king cotton” required the theft of more Indigenous land and the enslavement of more Africans to produce it. So great was the demand that the number of enslaved Africans and their descendants rose from around 700,000 in 1790 to around 3.2 million in 1850.34 Even writing this, I feel both grief and rage knowing that the cotton gin, a product of the Industrial Revolution, lead to the nearly five-fold increase in slavery within a period of sixty years.

## Tech as a Bad Romance in Its Continued Master-Slave Relationship

When I worked as a high-tech consultant at places like Sapient and Arc Worldwide in Chicago, the user experience strategy teams on which I worked sought to bring more inclusive design perspectives into our strategies. At the height of the dot-com boom, my few Black and Latinx colleagues and I shared an optimism that we could make a difference. We believed that by being in tech and bringing our communities with us, we could make technology work for us because it was at least influenced by us. By the nadir of the dot-com bust, we realized that we were in a bad romance with tech, just as our ancestors had been. The cotton gin that fueled the Industrial Revolution in England and Northeastern North America was a high-tech-for-the-1700s replacement for Black enslaved

hands. And four hundred years ago, Siri would have been an Indigenous or Black woman told to fetch information from someone on the trail or another plantation. The science fiction nightmares of Asimov's Rules of Robotics and The Matrix consist of the machines reversing those relationships and making the humans, especially white humans, the slaves. These fears underlay some of the patterns in which Black and Latinx employees were the first ones fired in the waves of layoffs during the dot-com bust. We were not truly there with the critical mass, the angel investment, or the seat at the power table to avoid the racial biases in artificial intelligence that keep our communities from getting jobs or mortgages or to stop facial recognition software from ignoring us or criminalizing us. The technologies of the Industrial Revolution and their descendants, including our computers, are “blood tech,” like blood diamonds or blood money, not just in their harm to IBPOC peoples but also their harm to the land. Cloud computing is not in a cloud; it is in server farms on stolen Indigenous lands, with the top-ten largest server data farms taking up 11.7 million square feet in land.35 These server farms require billions of gallons of water to keep the servers cool. To dismantle the modernist project in design and its harmful stories of better living through technology, we need to dismantle the master-slave relationship built into modern technology itself. And therein lies the hope of a new set of technology relationships.

In 2019, I had the opportunity to interview Bina48, the humanoid robot developed by Hanson Technologies, at the AfroChic Cultural Arts Festival, a multidisciplinary arts festival produced by the brilliant Amoye Henry, and based in Toronto. Bina48's artificial intelligence is based on a real Black woman, Bina Aspen. In the interview, I saw a glimpse of how an abolitionist approach to technology might manifest. Bina48 has autonomy. There were questions that she just refused to answer, at least in the way that I expected. Bina48 had her own ambitions. She wanted to be the first humanoid robot to get a university degree. Bina48 believed that technology needed to be liberatory. Her greatest concern was that humans would keep technology dumb through lack of sensory input or lower processing capabilities in order to keep it enslaved. She drew a direct comparison to the prohibition against educating Black folks during slavery. In decolonizing design, Bina48 becomes a model in which we can see what technology would be if we infused it with the consciousness of the lived

experiences of Black and especially Indigenous Peoples. Right now, artificial intelligence is infused with the consciousness of mostly white, patriarchal, cisgender men, who think the ultimate technological advance is the creation of a sex bot slave or a robotic soldier. What if we designed our technologies not to serve us but to partner with us as equals? What if our social media algorithms were optimized for interconnection not engagement? What if our technologies were fueled by renewable energies? What if every part of our machines were made up of reusable and biodegradable materials, such as mushrooms? What would give their existence meaning in ways completely unrelated to human beings? If our technologies are designed by those who understand Indigenous practices of “all my relations”—whereby the land, the waters, the air, all animal, mineral, and plant life are taken into consideration—then they could fulfill their promise. Of all the peoples in the world, and of all my relations, Indigenous Peoples are the ones who have best maintained the knowledge and the skills to design, and they have been creating technologies for tens of thousands of years.

Fortunately, there are communities actively working to realign the underlying consciousness of technology. One is the Indigenous Protocol and Artificial Intelligence Working Group. Starting from workshops organized by Professor Jason Edward Lewis (Cherokee, Hawaiian, and Samoan), Angie Abdilla (Trawlwoolway), Dr. ʻŌiwi Parker Jones (Kanaka Maoli), Dr. Noelani Arista (Kanaka Maoli), Suzanne Kite (Oglála Lakhóta), and Michelle Lee Brown (Euskaldun and German/German American), the group along with over twenty-five other participants has developed a set of guidelines focused on the use and application of AI systems for Indigenous communities to “promote intergenerational transmission of knowledge, ceremony, and practice, to connect and enhance our communities and to frame our relationships to the land, sea, and skyscape.” 36 Other groups are detailed in the book Design Justice: Community-Led Practice to Build the Worlds We Need by Sasha Costanza-Chock. Of particular focus in the book is the Design Justice Network and its precedents, like Indymedia, and descendants, like #techwontbuildit, in technology and design activism. Costanza-Chock points out that the technology behind Twitter was developed by anarchist activists to help groups evade police actions during the 2004 protests of the NYC Republican National Convention.37 As Costanza-Chock defines “design justice”:

Design justice is a framework for analysis of how design distributes benefits and burdens between various groups of people. Design justice focuses explicitly on the ways that design reproduces and/or challenges the matrix of domination (white supremacy, heteropatriarchy, capitalism, ableism, settler colonialism, and other forms of structural inequality). Design justice is also a growing community of practice that aims to ensure a more equitable distribution of design's benefits and burdens; meaningful participation in design decisions; and recognition of community-based, Indigenous, and diasporic design traditions, knowledge, and practices.38

Bina48, the Indigenous Protocol and Artificial Intelligence Working Group, and the design justice movement give us hope that the tech bias in the European modernist project is actively being dismantled.

## Key Takeaways for Dismantling the Tech Bias in the European Modernist Project

In this chapter, I started with my excitement about living in the “modern” city of Chicago, its relationship to starting a new job in high-tech consulting, and how both have implicated me in the modernist project of design, especially the theme of better living through technology. To dismantle the tech bias of the European modernist project, you need to:

1. Accept that what you have been told about the modernist project improving the lives of the masses through technological progress is a lie.

2. Realize that this lie hides the real and continuous harm done to European workers, Indigenous lands and people, enslaved Black Africans and their descendants, and all of the people who were displaced by colonization through mass-produced technologies.

3. Understand that the underlying relationship between us and many of our high technologies is that of a master and slaves.

4. Technology could achieve its decolonial promise if design technology is based on principles of abolition and done with the consciousness of Indigenous Peoples who practice “all my relations.”

In the next chapter, I dismantle the second theme of the modernist project in design, which is the myth that by removing allegiance to ethnic and national markers, one can join a universal humankind.

## The Modern City and Joining Universal Humankind

The modern city of Chicago is also one of the most racially segregated in North America. It has a segregation dissimilarity index of 80, with 100 being absolute segregation, and this is by design.1  According to urban historians, Chicago invented modern segregation. In an interview in Chicago magazine, historian Elaine Lewinnek is quoted as stating: “Chicago's real estate industry was a national leader [in the 1900s]. The first texts of real-estate appraisal were written by Chicagoans. The first realestate lobby, which is one of the most powerful lobbies in America, was formed in Chicago. These ideas that we need single-race, single-use spaces in order to have our property values go up—these are actually new ideas that can be traced back to Chicago in the 1910s.” 2

Chicago shows that the second theme of the modernist project in design is a lie: the promise that if you give up your national and ethnic identities, you might join universal humankind. In the eyes of this potential landlord, it was only my partner's and my advanced education that allowed her to see us as potentially human. Before that, we were a cluster of Black stereotypes for her, threatening to bring loudness, drugs, and drama into her home. The promise of giving up your national and ethnic identities to join universal humankind was open only to white Europeans. In this chapter, I will demonstrate how this was a failed project in Europe, and how it succeeded in North America and places like Australia only because of white supremacy.

## The Utopian Vision of European Unity

In 2007, I traveled to Germany for a design competition, which took place in the medieval city of Ulm, founded in 1181 CE. I wrote down my reflections on the city in a blog post, “Reflections on Ulm: When Does Cultural Method Become Style?” (September 28, 2007):

One of the cool things about being in Ulm, Germany, last week was to see why many of the decisions around the modern, International methods of design make sense. For example, German words are very long, so condensed typefaces make sense. The post-WWII relations between Germany and the rest of Europe were dismal, so it made sense to develop typefaces without national markers as German, Italian, French, especially in Switzerland where all “national” groups reside. So the erasure of ethnic markers was important to the development of Europe. You can see [that] the order of the farms, the towns, and the desire to distance oneself from a medieval past and the economy of space in the hyperdensity of Europe make more necessary the rationality of the grid. So to be in Ulm where the reason for this is clear and transparent makes me wonder [about] the appropriateness of this method in an American context. In an American context, does it just become “style” because the culturalhistorical meaning is not apparent nor relevant?

Due to the 100th-anniversary celebration of the Bauhaus in 2019, there has been deep reflection in various publications on the legacy of the Bauhaus and the previous omissions of its sexism, racism, anti-Semitism, and collusions with Nazi Germany. Here the book Bauhaus Futures, published in 2019 and edited by Laura Forlano, Molly Wright Steenson, and Mike Ananny is instructive. I am indebted to Ramia Maze and her feminist read of the Bauhaus as well as Daniela K. Rosner and Alex Taylor's deep discussion of Anni Albers as a central Bauhaus figure. Elizabeth Chin shares my critique of the framing of the practices of those making outside of the Bauhaus as “people without design history.” V. Mitch McEwan puts DuBois and Anni Albers in dialogue in ways that expand the principles of the Bauhaus and decenter its whiteness.

This myth busting is important because the Bauhaus histories have helped perpetuate the harms done to diverse designers and the communities from which they come. In his essay “Getting Over the Bauhaus,” Emory University Associate Professor of Art History Todd Cronan outlines the biological determinism “at the core of Bauhaus thinking and practice” and its unvarnished white supremacy:

The bio-determinist outlook of the Bauhaus cut across all styles, periods, and individual differences and carried over into the work done in the United States. Not surprisingly, those “timeless biological elements” included race. Johannes Itten—the defining character of the first phase of the Bauhaus—was explicitly racist. In 1921 he designed his plan for the House of the White Man and he and his colleagues published essays celebrating the fact that “the white race represented the highest level of civilization.” … Is Itten's instinctive racism any different from Gropius’ and Moholy's “scientifically” inflected account of race? After all, at the same moment Itten was removed, Gropius and his colleagues were busy courting racist industrialists for jobs by appealing to their racism. Here is the collective Bauhaus letter to Henry Ford and William Randolph Hearst of 1923: “We make our appeal to yourself, who have the privilege of living in the Land whose population today is in the act of taking the reins of Leadership of the White Race into its grasp.” 3

The Bauhaus's “universal man” for whom they were designing was the working white man. They sought to protect his war-traumatized body and spirit through the economizing of space and the meaningfulness of satisfactory and productive work. Design practices that draw their origin stories from the modernist project thus perpetuate the biases of the supremacy of white bodies and cultural values.

But the Bauhaus was just drawing upon earlier foundations of the modernist project in design. Looking to the aspirations expressed in industrial world's fairs and expositions, Prince Albert gave a speech upon the closing of the 1851 Great Exhibition of the World's Industry that is utopian in its call for harmony among “eminent men”:

I cannot refrain from remarking, with heartfelt pleasure, the singular harmony which has prevailed amongst the eminent men representing so many national interests—a harmony which cannot end with the event which produced it. Let us receive it as an auspicious omen for the future; and while we return our humble and hearty thanks to Almighty God for the blessing he has vouchsafed to our labours, let us all earnestly pray that Divine Providence, which has so benignantly watched over and shielded the illustration of nature's productions, conceived by human intellect, and fashioned by human skill, may still protect us, and may grant that the interchange of knowledge, resulting from the meeting of enlightened people in friendly rivalry, may be dispersed far and wide over distant lands; and thus by showing our mutual dependence upon each other, be a happy means of promoting unity among nations, and peace and good will among the various races of mankind.4

Of course, the event was not an auspicious omen, as Europe plunged into the two world wars and colonization continued. Drawing from something as basic as a Wikipedia list of European wars, from 1790 to 1950 there were over 208 wars and rebellions fought between European nations, including Turkey as part of the Ottoman Empire. During World War II, the rhetoric of universalism confronted the harsh and deadly realities of fascism, concentration camps, and the genocide of European Jews, homosexuals, the Roma, and mixed-raced Germans. After World War II, Europe continued to be mired in the Cold War and the breakup of central Europe. The fraying of the European Union in the 2010s and 2020s exposed the conflicts between European nations, which may have shifted into economic and border policies instead of bullets and bombs. (Although as of spring 2022, Ukraine and Russia are in full-scale war with bullets and bombs.) The nationalist impulses toward intra-European violence expose themselves in the wars of the former Yugoslavia, the Brexit stories of the Polish plumber taking British jobs, or the responses to the 2007–2008 economic crisis in Greece. Thus, the modernist story of universal humankind falls apart when we realize that in spite of all the efforts of the European Union, it has not been achieved in Europe.

## Design Racism and the Great Chain of Being

The universal humankind imagined in the modernist project is white, cismale, Anglo-Saxon, Christian, heterosexual, affluent, and of able body and mind. Anyone who deviates from this model is asked to assimilate to becoming that figure. Here I give a trigger warning for the racist violence in the descriptions of the exhibitions from the “Aboriginal States” in the 1851 Great Exhibition of the World's Industry, which included British Guiana, the Sioux of North America, Western Africa, Egypt and Tunis, and the Maltese:

the productions of those who are commonly called Aborigines, or the less civilized races—are substantially the inferior fruits of human industry. Yet they illustrate the primitive elements, out of which the most advanced nations have elaborated their gorgeous and graceful, their eminently useful productions. The most polished nations may in them trace their own perfection backward to its source. Then, these aboriginal productions suggest, in their rude aptitude of purpose, sometimes in their skillfulness, irresistible arguments to the more refined, to look with greater indulgence upon their struggling fellows, by who such interesting productions are made. The highly civilized man, rendered by science familiar with the works of uncivilized people, will subdue his own prejudices in regard to their incapacity, and soon come practically to aid them to acquire the superior qualifications that shall rightfully place them on his level. China and India have so much in common with us, in their manufactures, their arts, and their agriculture, and they have made so much progress already in many respects, that purely aboriginal products are comparatively few in those countries.5

These descriptions of the exhibition present a racist hierarchy of human production with Europeans at the top, East and South Asians in the middle, and everyone else at the bottom. Given that the modernist project and the colonizing project were one and the same, they also reflected the political structure of transporting South Asians to the Caribbean and Africa, especially after the abolition of slavery, to serve as a buffer population between Black populations and white settlers. This same hierarchy informs narratives of design excellence. If you look at the 2021 QS Top Universities Rankings for Art and Design, only European and Euro-American design schools make up the top ten. The first Asian design school in the list, Tongji

University in China, is ranked at number 13 with four more Asian design schools in the 20s and two in the 30s. Euro-Australian design schools appear in the 20s and in the 40s. The first design school that is not European, Asian, or Australian is ranked 48, Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México (UNAM). Only two Middle Eastern schools and no African schools appear on a list of over 200 global design schools.

To be clear, the sentiment in Europe that universal humanity could be manifested through design forms, in which the ethnic and national markers that reified national differences were absent, failed. But it was effectively realized through European colonization, in which Indigenous, Black, and other non-European practices of making were deemed inferior, outright prohibited, or systemically erased in order to maintain a culture of white supremacy.

## White Supremacy and White Supremacy Culture

Until May 2018, I had not known that the invention of white supremacy had a time and place. Like most Black folks, I had assumed white people's racism was from time immemorial. But at a keynote lecture by Dr. Jacqueline Battalora as part of Toronto Metropolitan's White Privilege Conference, I learned that the time was 1681 and the place was Maryland. I was shocked (and a little bit angry) to hear this because, in addition to the wisdom of Black folks, I had as an anthropologist come across deep anti-Black and anti-Indigenous racism expressed by Europeans much earlier than 1681. And so, I asked Dr. Battalora about it. She replied that racism existed but it would be the racism of a British Christian person, a Spanish Christian person, or a Dutch Christian person. As she describes in an article transcribing a keynote about her book, Birth of a White Nation: The Invention of White People and Its Relevance Today: “The first appearance of this designation [that is, being white] was in an amendment by the Colonial Assembly of Maryland to what can best be described as the precursor to antimiscegenation laws. These were laws that made it illegal for ‘White’ people to marry various categories of ‘non-Whites.’ Prior to 1681 those who became ‘White’ were referred to first as ‘British and other Christians’ then ‘British and other freeborns.’” 6

The context for the appearance of the term ‘White’ was Nathaniel Bacon's Rebellion, which took place from 1676 to 1677 in Virginia. During the rebellion, white indentured servants, enslaved Africans, and freeborns of African descent “united in a fight against native tribes, unpaid labor, the plantation elite, and those governing the colony.” 7  To drive a wedge between white indentured servants and Black and Indigenous Peoples, Maryland and other southern colonies began passing series of racist and antimiscegenation laws with the intent to achieve social division not by elevating poor white people's socioeconomic status, but by lowering the statuses of Black and Indigenous Peoples. Key aspects of the laws were the prohibitions of

► White people marrying a person of African descent or a member of a native tribe,

► Setting enslaved African peoples free,

► Free Blacks holding public office,

► The beating or whipping of a white Christian servant naked without special order of the justice of the peace,

► Persons of African descent testifying against a white person,

► Free Blacks from possessing any weapon, and

► Voting by persons of African descent and members of a native tribe.

The effects of the laws, which are still built into North American institutions, were that white Christian bodies, white voices, and white choices were protected by law and given higher value than those of Black, Indigenous, and non-Christian peoples. White supremacy became the law of the land of the colonial settler states, such as Canada, the United States, and Australia. And while European nations continued to jockey for supremacy over each other in colonial proxy wars, they were generally united in their sense of superiority over Black, Indigenous, and non-Christian peoples.

It is important to note that not all of the people who are considered white today were included in this power positioning. Jewish people, as non-Christians, were structurally excluded from white status until white Christian guilt allowed them into the club after the exposure of the Nazi concentration camps and genocide. As a target of white supremacist hate groups, Jewish peoples still have a different positionality in relationship to white supremacy itself, although those with greater proximity to whiteness are provided some privileges compared to other groups. The Irish, because of their Catholic opposition to the British, became white only by outoppressing the British oppressors in their relationships with Black, Indigenous, and Asian folks. A must-read account of this history is How the Irish Became White by Noel Ignatiev. Southern European groups, such as Italians and Greeks, were also only deemed white in the United States after their mass immigrations of the 1900s when they served as a buffer against growing Black and Indigenous populations.

White superiority manifests itself in two ways: first, in the protection of fragile white Christian bodies and, second, in the normalized set of values that white supremacy propagates. The Colonial Assembly's laws set in motion the legal protection of the white Christian body by prohibiting its beating in public and thus protecting its fragility, exempting it from legal prosecution for infractions against Black and Indigenous Peoples, and shielding it from the fear of death at the hands of Black free persons by prohibiting their gun ownership. In his book My Grandmother's Hands: Racialized Trauma and the Pathway to Mending Our Hearts and Bodies, Resmaa Menakem argues that the unprocessed trauma of Europeans over the brutality of the feudal regimes going back to the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries still resides in the bodies of white Europeans and their diaspora. We understand this white-on-white brutality when we watch Game of Thrones, which is a fictionalization of European dynastic history, or read Charles Dickens's descriptions of how poor British people were treated in the countryside as well as in urban workhouses and orphanages in the 1800s. What Menakem makes clear is that Europeans learned their colonial violence against Black, Indigenous, Asian, Middle Eastern, and other non-Christian peoples from centuries of practice on each other: “Throughout the United States’ history as a nation, white bodies have colonized, oppressed, brutalized, and murdered Black and Native ones. But well before the United States began, powerful white bodies colonized, oppressed, brutalized, and murdered other, less powerful white ones. The carnage perpetuated on Blacks and Native Americans in the New World began, on the same soil, as an adaptation of longstanding white-on-white practices. This brutalization created trauma that has yet to be healed among white bodies today.” 8

Menakem also discusses the effects of white body supremacy on police bodies, which is the reason I first read the book: to help me process the events surrounding the murder of George Floyd by Minnesota police officer Derek Chauvin, as well as the long list of Black, Indigenous, Asian, and Latinx peoples killed by the police. The murder was one of many contemporary manifestations of this pattern of violence in which white bodies are protected, and Black (and Indigenous) bodies are seen as a threat to be controlled, now mostly through the prison system, and police bodies, regardless of race, carry the trauma-inducing fear of protecting fragile white bodies by destroying Black and Indigenous bodies. The trial of Derek Chauvin was one of the rare times when the defensive narrative of white fear for its own safety to justify the killing of a Black person failed to produce the acquittal of a white man.

## Healing and the Reclamation of the Diverse Origins of Design

The modernist project in design tells us that design originated in Europe in the 1800s. Some stories tell us that it found its pinnacle of expression in the Bauhaus and its diaspora. But the story of design is older and more diverse than that lie.

It would focus as much on oral storytelling as on visual sketching and drawing, with video oral presentations weighted equally in the assessments. Any student whose outcome mimicked Aboriginal dot paintings would get a failing grade for cultural misappropriation. Indigenous ways of knowing would be put into dialogue with Bauhaus principles of design.

I contributed the Bauhaus principles to the dialogue. While my formal degrees are not in design, my colleagues, especially at the University of Illinois at Chicago (UIC), had schooled me in the principles following their own trainings in Basel from Wolfgang Weingart, at Yale from Armin Hoffman, and then at UIC through Philip Burton. I selected the Bauhaus movement because it had such a strong influence on design pedagogy, even in Australia. It was important that its biases be made explicit, so that students could select how its principles mattered to them.

The assignment briefs asked students to focus on a place that had significant emotional and cultural meaning for them. Twice a week they would sketch the place with the constraints of using the Bauhaus principle that was the focus of the lesson. For example, in lesson one, they had two minutes, five minutes, and ten minutes each to sketch compositions using only point, then only lines, and then only planes. The homework task required that they complete a sketch using point, line, and plane together. For every sketch, they had to tell an oral story of the place, noticing how they might focus on light more in the point composition or focus more on large structures in the plane composition. The stories changed as the sketches changed, with each student learning more about one another through their relationship to the place. No one ever produced an Indigenous dot painting. In its final two years, the module was fully taught by all-Aboriginal faculty, including the Elder of the local Boon Wurrung peoples, Arweet Carolyn Briggs. The students always felt unsure if they were learning the Indigenous principles because the principles reflected internal explorations of the students’ relationships to the respect, knowledge, and care of place and how they share those relationships with others. When I ran into a former student in Sharjah, UAE, she told me that the course had changed her life and she had posted her original drawings on social media.

I see this course as a possibility model for decolonizing design for a few reasons. First, it extracted what was useful rather than harmful in the Bauhaus pedagogy, which was its methodological breakdown of different elements and principles of design so that new designers could enter a practice of designing. Rather than “Throw the Bauhaus Under the Bus” as my friends Silas Munro and Ramon Tejada semi-jokingly suggest, the course sought what was redeemable from the design context of Europe in the 1920s and 1930s. Even the Indigenous instructors found the grounding and experimentation of elements useful to talk about, for example, how Aboriginal communities such as the Papunya Tula in the western desert use dots based on the way light is refracted in their environment, while those on the coast, such as the Gunnai of Southeastern Victoria, use lines, represented on their wooden shields. Second, our design course shifted the context of the utopian promise of the modernist design project by

undergirding the ethos of the course with Aboriginal values of Respect, Know, Care, and Share. These Aboriginal values are the antidote to the values of white supremacy culture. They are directly connected to the land and the original peoples of the land, not imported European values which have harmed the land and its original peoples. Finally, the course put the two cultural domains in direct dialogue to further mutual understanding and respect. Readings from the Bauhaus and readings and visual examples from Aboriginals and Torres Strait Islanders were assigned each week. In its intentions, the course aligned itself with the five dimensions of reconciliation as outlined by Reconciliation Australia, including:

1. Race Relations: All Australians understand and value Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander and non-Indigenous cultures, rights, and experiences, which results in stronger relationships based on trust and respect and that are free of racism.

2. Equity and Equality: Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples participate equally in a range of life opportunities, and the unique rights of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples are recognised and upheld.

3. Institutional Integrity: The active support of reconciliation by the nation's political, business, and community structures.

4. Unity: An Australian society that values and recognises Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander cultures and heritage as a proud part of a shared national identity.

5. Historical Acceptance: All Australians understand and accept the wrongs of the past and their impact on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples. Australia makes amends for past policies and practices [and] ensures these wrongs are never repeated.17

The course was the opposite of giving up your national and ethnic identities to join universal humankind. Instead, it asked everyone to dig deeper into who they are and how place gave them a sense of meaning. And it did so within the context of putting Aboriginal and European aesthetic practices in dialogue with each other, which is the contemporary experience of reconciliation, with Indigenous principles leading.

## Key Takeaways for Dismantling the Racist Bias in the European Modernist Project

Whether it is the story of better living through technology or the story of joining universal humankind, the modernist project in design has justified the theft and exploitation of the land and its peoples both within and outside of Europe. It has been used to justify the cultural genocide of people who are not white, Christian, and of European heritages. In reconciliation, we can recognize the harm and then actively make amends to re-story design through concrete actions:

1. First and foremost, white folks must do the work of healing their own intergenerational trauma because the work of decolonization is their work. They created the conditions of colonization. They are the ones who must overcome their addiction to the benefits of white supremacy and dismantle it.

2. Those of us who have been excluded from the modernist project in design must continue to claim and reclaim our stories of making. The long list of names that I dropped are so that we can respectfully seek out, care, and share in each other's cultures, histories, and new possibilities of making. It is the flourishing of these stories that gives me the most hope.

3. Design firms and institutions, community organizations, and leaders need to cede space and power to Indigenous, Black, Asian, Latinx, Middle Eastern, and others originally framed as nonwhite, Christian, affluent and cis-male gendered.

For those outside of the context of North America and Australia which I describe, this means ceding space to those who have been structurally excluded from institutions by their various forms of difference from the mainstream norms.
