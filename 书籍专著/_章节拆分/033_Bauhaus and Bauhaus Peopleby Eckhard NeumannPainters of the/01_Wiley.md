For although one is ideological and the other antiideological, both are dedicated to using theatre, language itself, as a means of freeing the spectator to make his own decisions on the basis of his own feelings and thoughts. His major purpose is, after all, to cut through preconceptions about the nature and the effects of a writer's polemic aims on his work. Bauhaus and Bauhaus People is a collection of forty-eight reminiscences of the Bauhaus. Each essay is preceded by a rather prosaic biographical sketch of the author, and a number of interesting photographs rounds out this generally "those were the good old days" anthology. Some of the essays are worth serious consideration. Paul Citroen, for example, provides insight into the Mazdaznan cult which fluorished under Johannes Itten during the early years of the Bauhaus. "The Bauhaus Style-A Myth" by

Walter Dexel debunks the common view of the Bauhaus' ubiquitous impact in the 1920s. T. Lux Feininger points out some of the Bauhaus' pedagogical innovations. And Hannes Beckmann discusses the nature of Albers' approach to the Vorkurs. The remaining essays are crippled by their severe brevity, superficiality, and/or unwillingness to deal with highly complex issues. Often the author's bias is clear enough as, to take one example, when Lou Scheper writes that the Bauhaus was "so basically different from the other academies before and after" (p. 113), a view which Franciscono corrects in Walter Gropius and the Creation of the Bauhaus in Weimar in stating "the curriculum seems to have been little different outwardly from that of a conventional academy" (p. 163). But for the most part, the reader is left with a disturbing sense of issues being drastically simplified. Moreover, no effort is made by Neumann to indicate degrees of authenticity of the various authors as Franciscono does, for example, in his "warn[ing] against the extremely unreliable anecdotes of Lothar Schreyer" (p. 163, note 34). The concept of an anthology of recollections by the Bauhdusler is certainly laudable, but this book fails to provide it.

Equally disappointing is Roters' Painters of the Bauhaus. Individual chapters deal with Gropius, Feininger, Itten, Muche, Schreyer, Schlemmer, Klee, Kandinsky, Moholy-Nagy, Bayer, and Albers, with some minor figures handled with dispatch in the conclusion. The lack of an index symbolizes the overall unscholarly and confusing nature of this book. We can accept Roters' statement about Itten that "meditation lies at the core of his conception of artistic experience." But we are left hanging in air when Roters adds that Itten's "earlier works reveal their meaning most fully when they are observed with the conscious mind 'switched off' " (p. 52), since Roters never attempts to tell us what that meaning is, much less how to switch off our conscious minds. Or, to cite the chapter on Lionel Feininger, the artist's work and teaching at the Bauhaus receive remarkably scant attention, and there is no analysis of his relationship to other artists at this time. In the chapter on Schlemmer, Roters chooses to focus on the artist's ballets and puppets, but not on his paintings. All too often Roters substitutes quotations of other authors for his own analyses. In short, Roters has taken a number of artists who shared an association with the Bauhaus and proceeded to discuss their careers in the most general terms with little regard for their work at the Bauhaus itself.
