Great Britain was the leading power in the world politically and economically; it served more than any other country as a model for the young German Empire. The writings of British Arts and Crafts theorists, such as John Ruskin and William Morris, as well as knowledge of objects produced in the workshops of Morris, C. R. Ashbee, and other British designers, reached Germany and acted as a catalyst in this early phase of the movement, known as Jugendstil (Youth Style, after Munich's avant-garde journal Jugend). As the end of the nineteenth century approached, German design reform was driven by two pressing concerns: to create goods that could compete economically with other major European powers and to establish a greater sense of national identity following the country's unification of 1871. Britain was the country to learn from.'

Despite the admiration for Great Britain, the basic conditions for an Arts and Crafts culture in Germany differed in many respects. A major premise in Britain—the idealization of the Middle Ages—had little resonance in Germany. And while the English Arts and Crafts movement originated in large cities, such as London, Birmingham, and Manchester, its most profound associations were with nature, country life, and the country house, where "the simple life" was the paramount ideal. The reformist design culture of the era of Wilhelm II in Germany (1888—1918) was, in contrast, oriented more toward cities; beginning around 1896, reform experiments developed rapidly in regional urban centers throughout the newly unified German state.

From the beginning of the Arts and Crafts movement in England, its modern design culture was shaped by architecture and by the interplay of interior and exterior space. In 1859, for example, William Morris began plans for his home, the Red House, which he, the architect Philip Webb, and other friends designed and furnished. Conversely, the German Arts and Crafts movement gained momentum not from working architects but from artists who had formerly focused on painting, so the development of reforms in Germany took a different direction from those in Britain from the very start. Large-scale interior design projects in the modern style first became an artistic issue in Germany toward the end of the century, when the movement for a comprehensive reform of art and life inspired German Arts and Crafts innovators to begin designing and furnishing houses themselves.2

The role played by British publications in disseminating Arts and Crafts ideas in Germany can hardly be overestimated. Works such as Walter Crane's Claims of Decorative Art (1892) and The Basis of Design (1898), along with the monthly London journal The Studio (founded in 1893), were widely read and discussed. Although their influence on individual artists has never been investigated in detail, it's clear that many German Arts and Crafts designers relied on these and other British precedents.3

Contacts with the Arts and Crafts movement occurred on many levels of artistic activity, and they were even spurred by government intervention. In 1896 the German government appointed the architect Hermann Muthesius to its embassy in London as attaché for trade and industry, with the specific assignment of reporting on British architecture and decorative arts. During the seven years he spent in London, Muthesius proved to be an astute observer and investigator. He traveled extensively throughout the United Kingdom and established close relationships with many British architects, designers, and writers. Design reform ideas in Germany did not generate only from an advanced artistic consciousness; they were also promoted as a means of achieving economic supremacy. For example, in 1899 the sovereign ruler of the grand duchy of Hessen und bei Rhein, Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig, established an artists' colony on the Mathildenhöhe in Darmstadt with the hope that this undertaking would encourage production of high-quality decorative and applied arts throughout his state. An Anglophile, Ernst Ludwig had already introduced the Arts and Crafts to Germany in 1897 by commissioning Ashbee and Baille Scott to design two rooms at his Neues Palais in Darmstadt, which had been commissioned in 1864 by his grandmother Queen Victoria as a wedding present for his parents. Works in the new style not only had to be conceived and executed, they also had to be mediated to the public. Justus Brinckmann, director of the Museum fur Kunst und Gewerbe (Museum of Art and Industry) in Hamburg, aimed to shape the taste of German designers and critics by collecting and exhibiting works of art from the past and the present, including the e lence. Brinckmann followed the path of Morris, not by actually working as a designer himself but by setting up opportunities for the new style to flourish.

Enthusiasm for Britain peaked around the turn of the century. After 1900 the embrace of British models began to be challenged—a result of the growing self-confidence of the German Arts and Crafts scene. According to the judgment of several art critics, Britain belonged to the German bloc," thus enjoying greater respect than France, but at the same time it was increasingly regarded as an economic and political rival. Furthermore, the British belief that handwork was the sine qua non of creativity was not accepted with as much conviction in Germany. Whereas handcrafting was a central (if not always realized) ideal for the English reformers, utility and li important to German designers. The issue of hand or machine—since Ruskin, a topic of constant debate in Britain—was for Germany a pragmatic option in favor of the machine. Nonetheless, this attitude did not develop overnight. In the quest for new product quality, Germany, too, had to first make the breakthrough via handicraft. Analogous to British precedent, numerous workshops and communities that made unique or limited production objects were set up around 1900. The German designers, however, used the model of the workshop selectively—the path they chose quickly led to mass production, albeit filtered with the British ideals of integrity, utility, and high-quality design for everyday life.

An accelerated pace of economic growth after unification ensured that Germany achieved in a few years what had evolved in Britain over several decades; German Arts and Crafts proponents even went beyond the original model of artists' guilds by setting up increasing numbers of workshops with highly developed production technology. By 1907 Peter Behrens was appointed the artistic consultant of the giant electricity firm Allgemeine Elektricitäts-Gesellschaft (AEG) in Berlin, and the Deutsche Werkbund was established as an advocacy association for industry, designers, and artisans. The ideal of handmade beauty, luxury, and spiritual edification was giving way to design professionalism and technical invention. A new, industrially motivated model had developed out of the original goal of the unity of design and execution, with the aim that objects of whatever kind would be well designed, functional, inexpensive, and above all economic assets suitable for export. Although particular aspirations of the Arts and Crafts movement continued to be significant, Germany had sloughed off the British example and was developing its own new Kultur der Dinge (culture of things)."

ART AND LIFE The reform movement of the late nineteenth century aimed to change conditions considered obsolete and incapable of meeting the challenges of the age. The leaders of the new movement in Germany—including designers, artists, architects, patrons, critics, and businessmen—wanted not only to transform the appearance of the products they created but also to improve the taste of the entire population. Traditional academic art had proved unsuitable for realizing this aim, and the applied arts were embraced as the medium by which to convey a new, young feeling for life to the people, and to open their senses to the modern world.

One attempt to address this goal was made by the Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig, who in 1899 had sponsored the founding of the artists' colony at Darmstadt. Building upon his successful experience with prior commissions of British artists Ashbee and Baillie Scott, as well as the German artist Otto Eckmann, to design interiors for his palace at Darmstadt, Ernst Ludwig envisioned an artists' colony as a prime opportunity to cast a spotlight on the potential of his capital seat as a center of art, culture, and economic progress. Alexander Koch, the influential editor and publisher of two widely read journals of the decorative arts, Innendekoration and Deutsche Kunst und Dekoration, also played an important role in support of the colony. Between 1898 and 1899, the Grand Duke selected the first group of seven colony members: Peter Behrens, Rudolf Bosselt, Paul Bürck, Hans Christiansen, Ludwig Habich, Patriz Huber, and Joseph Maria Olbrich.3 On April 24, 1900, the foundation stone of the studio building, the Ernst Ludwig House, was laid on the Darmstadt Mathildenhöhe. Residences for the artists were built at the same time. All were designed by Olbrich, except for the Behrens House. The public opening took place with grand ceremony on May 15, 1901 (3.4). The applied arts displayed in the studio building and salesrooms, together with the artists' own houses, were presented as an exhibition called Ein Dokument deutscher Kunst (A Document of German Art). This was the first large-scale exhibition of German Jugendstil. The opening celebrations included a drama by Georg Fuchs and Peter Behrens, Das Zeichen (The Sign), which was performed with great solemnity, complete with a chorus in Greek robes, on the steps of the studio building.The central message of the play, and of the colony itself, was the emergence of a new era that would integrate art and life. As Ruskin had proclaimed, the artist should stamp his individuality on the whole of life. The reform of art was also a reform of life.

A plaque of gratitude executed by Rudolf Bosselt and dedicated by the artists to the Grand Duke embodies the dramatic tone of the event—a priestlike figure enveloped in a halo and swirling incense fumes bears a model of the studio building (3.5). Behrens proclaimed that "everything that belongs to life should be infused with beauty." To create beauty, and imbue even the most humble objects with it, was the utopian aim. Nowhere else had there been such a theatrical demonstration in the name of the new movement; this was a heady mix of spirituality, symbolism, and the applied arts. The opening events served as a form of consecration: a new national culture was to be built and the shackles of old beliefs abandoned.'

Paul Bürck's exhibition invitation, depicting an athletic young man with bare torso and a flowering sprig in his raised hand, represents these ideals (3.7). A sacred aura is also manifest in the exhibition poster designed by Peter Behrens (3.1). A female figure reaches up toward a crystal, the image of light and inspiration, which hovers above her between two ducal crowns.

One innovative aspect of the exhibition embodied the mission of unifying art and life—the opening of the members' houses, created as case studies of modern interior design, to the public. Thus it was not only a museum or an exhibition hall that provided the context for the new art and design products, but also the private houses of the artists. The visitors were given the opportunity to experience how the artists integrated art into their homes and into their lives.

Peter Behrens was the most successful of the group in achieving unity of the architecture and interiors. While not trained as an architect, Behrens convinced those in charge of the colony that he could design his own house. The dining room was central to his vision. Its white-painted furniture, tableware, and accessories all followed a basic geometric system of parallel linear arches and lancet ovals touching or overlapping one another (3.8 and 3.9). Even the table linen adhered to the same decorative principle, and the linear ornamentation on the table service and cutlery was repeated on the ceiling. No other room of the exhibition was worked out in such detail or expressed such exquisite beauty. In the music room, the furniture and doors were decorated with crystalline rhomboid patterns and with cast bronze and gilded ornament (3.10); the chair frames were stained black and inlaid with intarsia of wood.

Each room was designed as a Gesamtkunstwerk, a total work of art." After the cacophony of historicism, such rooms and houses created around 1900 reflected new ideals of clarity and unity. The artistry consisted of relating all objects to one another, in bringing the varied forms and media into harmony. An early signal in this direction had already been given by Henry Van de Velde's four rooms designed for Siegfried Bing's Paris gallery L'Art Nouveau, which Bing had presented at the Dresden exhibition of 1897. While the critics generally received these rooms favorably, they questioned the character of individual designs as showpieces rather than elements of a unified whole. More positively received were Richard Riemerschmid's music salon at Dresden (1899) and his room for an art lover at the Exposition Universelle in Paris (1900). In autumn 1902 the Wertheim department store in Berlin invited several artists, among them Baillie Scott, Behrens, and Huber, to design rooms

for an ideal home. The commission was further demonstration of efforts to improve design standards through the unity of art and life. In the department store, art was placed in the context of ordinary commercial life; anyone shy of entering a museum could feel free to wander, have a look at things, and be encouraged to place an order. Behrens again presented a dining room, this time in dark wood, with robust, angular forms (3.13). The sensitive, gentle curves of the Darmstadt dining room had given way to a geometric rusticity; applied decoration was avoided, and ornamentation was integrated into the furniture's construction (3.14 and 3.15). The ceiling lamp was particularly interesting (unfortunately no example seems to have survived); it differed from the otherwise predominant hoop lamps in its vertically arranged squares and rectangles suspended at various heights. Rectilinear shapes also defined the carpet decoration and the tablewares, their insistent rhythm, not unlike electric circuits, an indication of the increasing technical awareness in Behrens's vocabulary of forms (3.16-18). With this design, Behrens had transformed his style to one of objective geometry and rational utility. Thus he was on a direct route to his designs for the appliances, advertisements, factories, and workers' housing of the AEG, and to the application of the Gesamtkunstwerk in the creation of a corporate identity (3.19).

The new art developments in Germany around 1900 were characterized by numerous parallel expressions in regions across the country. While conditions varied from city to city, each of the endeavors sought to promote modern design. In Darmstadt, where the artists' colony had been established, Ernst Ludwig was truly idealistic about uniting art and life, but he was equally interested in exploiting the new design as an impetus to trade and industry in Hesse.2 Modern design could provide an opportunity for collaborations that would serve not only to improve taste and the quality

of production but also to boost economically weak parts of the province. Furniture, metalwork, stoneware, and earthenware—all were produced in the grand duchy.

Objects by Behrens, Christiansen (3.27), Huber, and Olbrich were made in workshops in Darmstadt and the surrounding areas: for example by Ludwig Alter, J. C. Boysen, Julius Glückert, and E. L. Other workshops were established within the colony during the subsequent period: in 1904, plans were initiated for Jakob Julius Scharvogel to move his ceramic manufactory from Munich, and in 1907 the Ernst Ludwig Press was founded as a private press modeled after Morris's Kelmscott Press, with Friedrich Wilhelm Kleukens as its director.2

At the turn of the century, Germany sought to assert itself as a major industrial and imperial power; simultaneously, it struggled to build a national identity following unification. One strategy to meet both needs involved reviving the flagging traditional earthenware and stoneware industries by hiring modern designers to update time-honored (but by now timeworn) forms and decoration. These efforts had the imprimatur of government support coupled with the enthusiastic participation of commercial companies, who saw their financial salvation in the hiring of modern designers. The factories in the region of Westerwald—in the eighteenth century the center for the manufacture of salt-glaze stoneware—had been eclipsed by the popularity of porcelain, a more refined material and one produced in other regions. These factories provided a paradigm of how Arts and Crafts ideals—local materials, integrity f production, utility—could be adapted to meet the desire to be both modern and German, qualities seen as necessary for the country's success in the new century. In 1900 the Westerwald Council asked Henry Van de Velde to provide designs for new stoneware vessels. While he incorporated the blue-gray body characteristic of the local vernacular, the decoration reflected his own individualistic style (3.32). For many other pieces, Van de Velde abandoned traditional colors entirely to work with copper reduction glazes (3.30).28

ART AND NATIONAL IDENTITY Germany, like many other European countries around 1900, was searching for its own national style and cultural identity. A look at the art literature of the turn of the century reveals the considerable attention paid to the topic. What is most striking is the ubiquitous assertion that design reform could create "healthy" German art and combat "signs of decay." This seems like the incantation of a magic spel, conjured up in the hope that the inner unity of the nation would be advanced by a unified concept of art. In nationalist circles, the country had always been perceived as a victim of outside influences. Now, leadership and resolution were called for, so that Germany might command its own fate. As a newly founded empire, Germany had to develop a unique culture, one that could match Britain and France. BEFORE THE ARTS AND CRAFTS: PROGRESSIVE ARCHITECTURE AND DECORATIVE ART IN VIENNA, C. 1860-C. 1895 In their study of the Arts and Crafts, Elizabeth Cumming and Wendy Kaplan identified the four principles of the movement as unity of the arts, joy in work, individual artistic expression, and emphasis on regional identity. Between about 1860 and the early 1890s, these ideas were only partly present in the progressive architecture and decorative art of Vienna. In some ways these decades anticipated the movement, and in other ways they did not.

The unity of the arts was mainly understood as a relationship between architecture, painting, and sculpture, rather than between the fine and applied arts, as William Morris, the leading figure of the Arts and Crafts movement in Britain, had recommended. Architecture was much more artistically advanced in Vienna than sculpture and painting, so the unity of the arts implied that painting and sculpture could reach the same artistic quality. Thus, Heinrich von Ferstel's Italian Renaissance design for the Museum für Kunst und Industrie (Museum of Art and Industry; now the MAK, Museum fur angewandte Kunst—Museum of Applied Arts) of 1868 was a model for the integration of these three fine arts. At the same time, the applied arts were not completely forgotten: the museum was also the first building in Vienna to achieve coherence in every detail, starting from the architectural proportions and materials right through to the design of the door handles. The moral dimension of the Arts and Crafts had a part to play in progressive Vienna, but it remained at a general, social level, unconnected with the personal notion of joy in work. THE DEVELOPMENT OF THE ARTS AND CRAFTS MOVEMENT IN VIENNA The ArtS and CraftS movement in Vienna developed rapidly in the second half of the 1890s, and the impulse for this came, in part at least, from Britain. The quiet penetration of Arts and Crafts ideas can be traced in the Museum of Art and Industry's library acquisitions. Before 1885 the library's British holdings included only technical works on art by Ruskin and two picture books by Walter Crane. In the late 1880s and early 1890s, under the directorship of von Falke, it bought two works by Ruskin that were seminal for the Arts and Crafts, The Stones of Venice and The Seven Lamps of Architecture. In the late 1890s and early 1900s, under the Anglophile director Arthur von Scala, the library purchased more books by Ruskin, picture books and Arts and Crafts essays by Walter Crane, and several books by William Morris in German translation, including his early lectures, Hopes and Fears for Art. In 190o it also bought two books printed and bound at Elbert Hubbard's Roycroft Press in East Aurora, New York, suggesting that Vienna was beginning to take note of the Arts and Crafts movement in the United States. This suggestion is strengthened by the fact that there were copies of the American magazine The Craftsman in the library of the Wiener Werkstätte, and also by the comment of the critic Hermann Bahr, made as early as 1898, that the United States played an important role in the development of Viennese Arts and Crafts, especially in the understanding and artistic treatment of materials. The new artistic aspirations of the movement became public in 1897, when the younger and more progressive artists from the Künstlerhaus left to form the Vienna Secession. The Secessionists were progressive in their modernism and in their internationalism. Over the entrance to their newly built exhibition rooms, they inscribed "Der Zeit ihre Kunst, Der Kunst ihre Freiheit" (To every age its art, to art its freedom), and they displayed work by artists from France, Germany, Belgium, and Britain. They were also progressive in embracing the decorative arts. The first manifestation of Arts and Crafts ideology in Viennese applied art can be seen in Josef Hoffmann's early furniture designs. Honesty of expression was embodied in the plain softwood boards, sawn so that their outlines also acted as ornament. Hoffmann designed the bedroom suite for the house of the painter Ernst Stöhr, a founding member of the Secession who was receptive to this kind of simplicity and averse to furnishings that expressed convention or prestige. The suite includes a cupboard (in this exhibition), a double bed, night tables, and a chest made of softwood formerly stained green (4.7). The combination of bed and chest is medieval in type and so is the plank construction, but there is no specific medieval source, and the plank construction itself has been transformed into a decorative motif. The idea of a useful object designed in an artistic spirit was also adopted in a competition to design table glass for an ordinary household, organized by the Museum of Art and Industry in 1899. The design by Koloman Moser that won the competition was executed in several decorative variants and successfully sold by the glass distribution firm E. Bakalowits & Söhne (4.9).

In the same year as the founding of the Secession, 1897, Arthur von Scala became director of the Museum of Art and Industry and the Kunstgewerbeschule. Von Scala was an Anglophile and a personal friend of the director of the South Kensington Museum in London.® In the tradition of English design reformers like Henry Cole and Owen Jones, he encouraged art teaching that was focused on draftsmanship and designing for industry; he did not fully support the Viennese avant-garde. Von Scala's direct influence on art education came to an end in 1899, when the Kunstgewerbeschule became independent under the directorship of the painter Felician Baron von Myrbach. In the same year Josef Hoffmann, then twenty-nine, and Koloman Moser, then thirty-one, joined the school. They were appointed professors in 1900. The arrival of von Myrbach, Hoffmann, and Moser was one of those strokes of luck that brings the right people together in the right place at the right time, giving them the opportunity to put their ideas into practice. Von Myrbach was not important as a painter or draftsman, but he understood the signs of the times. He carried out a thorough reform of the curriculum, setting up workshops and reorienting the school toward practical training, of the sort introduced into some British art schools in the 1890s.² The pottery workshop, started in 1899, was the first of many. A potter was employed and two wheels purchased for the ceramics class, which, until then, had only produced designs on paper (4.10 and 4.1). For the first time in the history of the school, the students were put directly in touch with materials. Design and execution were united once more. Through their creative teaching, Moser and Hoffmann fostered the independent, innovative ceramics style of the school; at the same time, they endeavored to introduce their students to the realities of business life, making contact with nationally important manufacturers who would execute and sell their designs. The manufacturers with whom students—as well as Moser and Hoffmann— collaborated included Johann Backhausen & Söhne for textiles (4.12); Johann Lötz Witwe glassworks (4.13); and the bentwood factory of Jakob & Josef Kohn and the Prag-Rudniker wicker factory for furniture (4.14).

THE WIENER WERKSTäTTE In 1903 Hoffmann, Moser, and the textile manufacturer and art patron Fritz Wärndorfer established a cooperative workshop in Vienna, the Wiener Werkstätte. Wärndorfer was to finance the project, and Hoffmann and Moser were to be artistic directors. They set up a metal workshop first, perhaps because they were concerned about the lack of contact between artists and metal craftsmen—there was no such workshop at the Kunstgewerbeschule (4.20). Later in 1903, they moved to larger workshops at Neustiftsgasse 32-34, where they also created departments for bookbinding, leatherwork, cabinetmaking, and lacquerwork, as well as an architectural office. Later still, departments for printing, fashion, and textiles were added. For glass and ceramics, they used their own designs or those of craft students, which were fabricated by outside manufacturers. Hoffmann and Moser must have been aware that their new role as businessmen gave them a chance to develop a convincing artistic enterprise, and they aimed to cover all the crafts necessary to create artistic unity in interior

decorating. They aimed, that is, to create a Gesamtkunstwerk, using their own workforce and workshops.

The idea of the Gesamtkunstwerk originated in the belief that humanity could be redeemed through art; therefore, the separation of art from life must be eliminated. Morris had first taken up this challenge in his attempt to restore the unity of fine and applied arts. The artists of the Secession responded to the challenge in their exhibition design and organization. For example, their fourteenth exhibition in 1902 was conceived as a Gesamtkunstwerk to showcase a single work of art—Max Klinger's monumental sculpture of Beethoven. The Kunstgewerbeschule professor and Ver Sacrum editor Alfred Roller created the mural on the wall behind the sculpture (his poster for the exhibition [4.21] was a detail of this mural); Gustav Klimt painted the famous Beethoven frieze in another room (4.22); and Hoffmann was the principal designer of the exhibition, which unified not only all the visual arts, but also art with music.

The most famous examples of Gesamtkunstwerk associated with the Wiener Werkstätte were the schemes for two large buildings—one public, the other private. The Purkersdorf Sanatorium (1904—5) was designed by Hoffmann, with interiors by Moser and Hoffmann. It combined white walls, clean-cut functional furniture, a dominant motif of geometric forms, and a light, clinical austerity (4.23 and 4.24). The other example, designed in 1905 and completed six years later, was the sumptuous family home of the Belgian banker Adolphe Stoclet in Brussels. Hoffmann served as the project's architect, and a whole host of Werkstätte craftsmen and Viennese decorative artists were involved in making the furniture, ceramic decorations, gold- and silverwork, embroidery, and stained glass. Four monumental figures in beaten copper stood on the tower, and, most sumptuous and original of all, a great mosaic frieze by Gustav Klimt adorned the dining room (4.25). This was the Wiener Werkstätte orchestra in full flood, with Hoffmann wielding the baton. But the idea was not confined to showpieces like these. The Werkstätte, however, was not prepared to revert entirely to the medieval practice of unity in design and execution. The craftsmen did not make their own designs but simply executed the designs of the artists working for the enterprise.

The classic designs of the early years of the Werkstätte, when Hoffmann and Moser were doing most of the designing, were mostly in metalwork, leather, and furniture. Although the commentary on these designs often focuses on the square as a decorative motif (it was used so frequently that it became almost a signature of Viennese Arts and Crafts), the quality of early Werkstätte designs often depends on a delicate and creative interplay of squares and circles.

Consider Hoffmann's Sitzmaschine of 1908, made by the bentwood firm of Jakob & Josef Kohn, an austere assembly of squares and rectangles on a large scale and in detail, which is, so to speak, qualified by the massive curve at the back, the balls at the joints, and the general softening of corners (4.32). In Moser's 1904 plant stand of perforated and painted metal, the circle is a controlling element of middle size—in the curves of the central stem and the circles of the plant holders—whereas the square is a tiny dynamic unit of the design, working at its own miniature level as a network of tiny voids that mysteriously make up a structure (4.33). A similar interplay can be seen in Hoffmann's designs for jewelry, in which the fat, curling leaves are gracefully contained by a fine, rectangular frame (4.34 and 4.35). In the early work of the Werkstätte, the square was a wellspring of invention, even though it seems so closed and formal. It had the same role in the designs of Mackintosh and Macdonald, despite their sensuousness.

These designs belong to those years when Hoffmann and Moser were the firm's main designers and Wärndorfer's backing was secure. All these things changed. The austere and substantial forms of the early work gave way to a playful and decorative style of fanciful objects, spriggy ornament, and allover patterns. The designers changed: Hoffmann was there for the whole life of the Werkstätte, subtly adapting his style, but Moser left in 1907; the new, decorative manner came in with Carl Otto Czeschka, who joined in 1905, and with Dagobert Peche in 1915. Peche was the principal source of decorative fantasy. A critic wrote, "In Josef Hoffmann's case everything rests firmly on the ground, in Peche's all is shooting upwards."35 In 1916 the Werkstätte set up the Künstlerwerkstätte (Artists' Workshop), which provided studio space and equipment for decorative artists and gave the Wiener Werkstätte itself an option on their work. This greatly increased the number of the firm's designers, now mostly women who had studied at the Kunstgewerbeschule. Their work was more various than before the war, but no less fanciful. And the backers changed. In 1914 Wärndorfer left Vienna for the United States under awkward circumstances. His place was taken by the banker Otto Primavesi, but the new situation was never stable. There were attempts in the 1920s to involve the Werkstätte with industry, but the artistic individualism of its prewar years was too strong for that, and it passed through a series of managers and financial crises in the 1920s, sustained less and less by its reputation. The workers were finally dismissed in 1932.

FORM OR CoNTENT? Given that the original purpose of the Wiener Werkstätte was to create modern, functional, and well-crafted objects for daily use, the question of how well it implemented its own credo must be addressed. Indisputably, the Werkstätte holds a position of international importance in the history of modern design. It is also well known that there were two important protagonists of the modern movement in Vienna at the turn of the century, and that they held opposing views. One was the Werkstätte's Hoffmann and the other was Adolf Loos.

Hoffmann stood for renewal through form: the world of design was to be made new, and modernity achieved, through the style of the Werkstätte's work. Loos, on the other hand, was an architect and cultural critic who concerned himself not with form but with content, not with the appearance of things but with the things themselves, with the way people live and use objects. He objected to the creation of new ornamentation for its own sake, and he was a critic of the culture as a whole." History has shown that Loos held the more profound and enduring view. Questions of content cannot, over time, be solved through the polishing of surfaces. The Werkstätte's superb handicraft production of objects designed by individual artists is incompatible with the needs of a modern society.

This is a lesson of history. In early-twentieth-century Vienna, however, the issue was not at all clear. What was clear was that the traditionally authoritarian, Catholic culture of the city made it very diffcult for any individual to express himself artistically, or even in the circumstances of daily life, and both Hoffmann and Loos experienced this. Vienna had the good fortune to bring forward two such gifted, controversial spirits at one time. Their different starting points offer two possibilities: to decree modernity through the artist's work, and especially through his control of the Gesamtkunstwerk; or to understand modernity as a social process in which the artist can play an enabling, but not a controlling, role. In this Viennese scenario, everyone must set his own priorities, must choose either form or content.

STATE PROMOTION OF THE CRAFTS, 1867-1897 The preservation and further development of Hungary's craft traditions were viewed as important state responsibilities, and in the decades following the Ausgleich an infrastructure was put in place that would shape a definable Arts and Crafts movement. Following the examples of London and Vienna, the National Museum of Decorative Arts was established in Budapest in 1872 "to gather the most precious remnants of the derativeart  the ps, and by sthem ateaching materil,  presrve andi ancient techniques in today's decorative arts..and further, through the education of mass taste, to create a larger public for these pieces of art." Under the auspices of this museum, the Magyar Iparmúvészeti Társulat (Hungarian Decorative Arts Association), its magazine Múvészi Ipar (Artistic Craft), and the Országos Magyar Királyi Iparmúvészeti Iskola (National School of Applied Arts) were established by 1885.

Outside Budapest the state encouraged the development of rural crafts, or home industries, as a means of countering deep-seated problems of agrarian decline and seasonal unemployment. Government ministries worked in tandem with local manufacturers and private organizations to arrest the falling away of skills and markets by providing training and equipment, assistance with the collection and modification of old patterns, price regulation, and improvements in retailing and distribution.

One of the earliest and most successful home-industry centers was run by Mrs. Gyarmathy (born Etelka Hory), who organized the production and sale of textiles embroidered by peasant women in the Kalotaszeg region of Transylvania (now part of Romania) (5.4). Already famous as a novelist and writer of short stories about rural life, she helped popularize the region, and by the 1890s she had agents in Berlin, Munich, Brussels, Paris, London, and New York. (It was through her London agent that Walter Crane was invited to Hungary in 190o.) The involvement of upper-class and aristocratic women in such enterprises conformed to a tradition of female philanthropic patronage that operated increasingly through an international network. As they became more successful, such organizations faced the perennial problem of steering between the commercial imperatives of the marketplace and the desire to preserve the integrity of the genuine folk culture on which the home industries drew.

Calls for a national style and fascination with vernacular culture had been accelerated by the prominent position given to crafts and peasant life in exhibitions that brought ethnographic research into the public domain. From the 1870s the Zsolnay company raised general awareness of regional patterns by improving the body and glaze of local ceramics marketed at national fairs." At the Budapest National Industrial Exhibition in 1885, the most popular pavilion was the Kalotaszeg House, reconstructed and furnished with traditional artifacts by Mrs. Gyarmathy and based on her research into rural traditions. Thereafter home-industry products from around the country were continually represented and marketed in the capital, initially through the new Museum of Commerce, and from 1895 to 1899 through a special bazaar, thus encouraging the rising taste for peasant-style crafts.² Paradoxically, as idealization of the country's folk culture became more prevalent among the urban bourgeoisie, conditions among the rural poor worsened. Bridging the growing socioeconomic divide between Hungary's urban and rural populations became a major issue that exponents of the Arts and Crafts movement sought to address both practically and symbolically.

The key event in the cultural landscape of Hungary at this time was the Millennial Celebration of 1896 commemorating the arrival of the Magyars in the Carpathian Basin one thousand years earlier. Not only indicating the political maturity and economic progress that Hungary had achieved as part of a major imperial power, the millennium celebrations (especially the exhibition that opened in May) also marked a critical moment in the crystallization of Hungarian nationhood. Due to its landlocked position at the crossroads between East and West, Hungary had long been a melting pot of different ethnic and religious groups. To meld and assimilate this heterogeneous collection of peoples into a recognizable nation entailed the "magyarization" of many minority groups, some of whom, like the Croats and the Slavs, were seeking political independence from Hungary, just as Hungary had done from Austria. Among the symbolic representations of a united autonomous realm with secure foundations was A Millenniumi Falu, a "village" organized by the Ethnographic Association. It brought together twenty-four model houses from different regions within Hungary, presenting a panorama of ethnicities, all harmonized within the framework of "Magyarorszag." Since the international exposition in Vienna (1873), the richness and diversity of Hungarian folk arts had attracted an international audience, though initially serving to reinforce rather than challenge the perception of "magyars" as a subject people within the Hapsburg Empire."² At the Paris Exposition Universelle in 1900, however, visitors could see how folk sources had been combined with sophisticated assimilation of progressive currents worldwide. At this and subsequent exhibitions prior to his untimely death in 1907, Pál Horti proved to be a particularly effective ambassador of his country's brand of modern design.3 His entrepreneurial experience in so many different areas of the decorative arts made him ideally suited to coordinate the Hungarian installation at the 1904 St. Louis world's fair, the Louisiana Purchase International Exhibition, for which he was awarded a gold medal and Diploma of Honor." In the Manufactures Building he created a modern version of a wooden Transylvanian country house and church with characteristic towers, fence, and Székely gate (5.18). The pavilion was a brilliant piece of stage-set propaganda, illustrating boh the survival of an ancient culture and its role as a source for modern art and craft. At the same time Horti was on an economic mission for the Hungarian government: he was to report on American decorative art manufacturing, especially in the furniture industry. The following year he was commissioned to design furniture and graphics for two American companies, Limbert  Grand Rapis nd Hollan Michigan, and the ho therafters nCincii, Ohio. Horti was attracted to these manufacturers as entrepreneurs purporting to operate businesses along Morrisian lines, particularly in the sense of promoting quality craftsmanship and improved working conditions for artisans, but combined with mechanized production methods and a modern approach to marketing and distribution. His desk and chair (5.19) were produced at the height of his professional career and international reputation. Designed by a Hungarian and manufactured in America, they were hybrid pieces that reflected progressive tendencies in both the United States and Hungary.

While showcasing the immense expansion and achievement of contemporary Hungarian design, these international exhibitions also highlighted a growing tension between the two types of modern craft activity—házipar (home industries) and iparmúvészet (artistic craft). The more traditional home industries attracted considerable popular interest, and the products, particularly the embroideries, sold in huge quantities to an international audience. By contrast, the artist-designers garnered more critical attention and were given greater official support. This pattern was established by about 1900, and it has continued to shape the analysis of the movement in general. In Hungary, as elsewhere, there has been a tendency to discuss the Arts and Crafts movement in terms of a relatively small number of individual artists, architect-designers, and intellectuals who were given special attention in art magazines of the period. This type o craft activity, iparmúvészet, has been emphasized at the expense of the more diffuse, traditional, and nonprofessional dimensions of the movement represented by the házipar. Yet the idea of creating a modern national style, rooted in folk culture, that would embody moral and community values was nurtured by the home industries. In the context of domestic and international exhibitions, both types of craft were presented as part of a continuum. ART INT0 LIFE, 1906-1914 Hungary's high-profile participation in exhibitions continued to attract international attention in the years leading up to the outbreak of World War I. At the same time, in the context of domestic policy, the Arts and Crafts became associated with ambitious programs of social reform and urban development, generating a more sustained and stylistically coherent engagement with the international Arts and Crafts. This final phase of the movement resonated most strongly with developments in Finland (a country linguistically close to Hungary), and with the work of English architect-designers C. R. Ashbee and M. H. Baillie Scott. Akseli Gallen-Kallela and Eliel Saarinen were protagonists of the Finnish movement who employed design as an agent of national identity. These Finnish leaders and their British counterparts, Ashbee and Baillie Scott, were all in direct contact with leading figures ofthe Hungarian Arts and Crafts during this period.3® Two centers of activity dominated—one at Gödölló, and the other around architect Károly Kós, the leading figure in a group known as the Fiatalok (Young Ones).37

The link between the two groups was their involvement in the recording and publication of folk arts, activities supported through Lippich. Many of the individuals were involved in the preparation of the monumental five-volume study A Magyar Nép Múvészete (The Art of the Hungarian People), published between 1907 and 1922. This experience brought a new understanding of the folk arts and how they might be used in contemporary architecture and design. With his design for the National Museum of Decorative Arts, Ödön Lechner had performed a major function by focusing attention on the issue of a "national language of form," but among his followers and admirers many were increasingly critical of the older architect's selective appropriation of vernacular motifs from published sources rather than from firsthand observation. Nostalgia for the unity and integrity of rural life inspired the establishment of the arts and crafts colony at Gödölló. Aladár Körosföi-Kriesch had settled there in 1901, and he was joined over time by others in search of a fulfilling, philosophically rich lifestyle. These like-minded artists and

The Gödolló artists tried their hands at a variety of media, producing stained glass (5.21), frescoes, ceramics, furniture, bookbinding, wallpaper design, embroidery, and most importantly, carpets and tapestries. Through immersion in such processes, and through exploration of Hungarian legends and ballads, they hoped to recapture the inner spirit of Hungary's medieval culture and folk arts. The quality of their oeuvre was inconsistent but, in common with the work of many Arts and Crafts groups, it was produced in a spirit of millenarian optimism that transcended mere aesthetics.

The search for totality of expression and a belief in the socially transformative, spiritual power of art were rooted in the concepts of collaboration and the Gesamtkunstwerk, the total work of art. In Budapest the architectural scene was enlivened by the precocious group of architectdesigners known as the Fiatalok (Young Ones), who emerged from the Technical University in 1907 and 1908. At their center was Károly Kós, a native of Transylvania, who worked briefly in Maróti's studio and collaborated with Wigand in Marosvásárhely. The group took up many of the themes addressed at Gödöllo, not least the shared belief in the ideal of a modern national style based on the village architecture of Transylvania. The town of Körösfo (after which Kriesch had named himself Körösföi) assumed iconic significance for the Fiatalok. As the critic Elemér Czakó observed in 1908, the distinctive form of the Körösf6 church was "engraved upon their spirits," as Mount Fujiyama was for Japanese artists. That year Lajos Kozma featured the church in his cover design for A Ház (The House), the new journal that served as the principal mouthpiece for the group's theories and designs (5.30). The magazine's coherent graphic identity, which indicated a clear debt to Wigand, emphasized the Fiatalok's adherence to ideals in book design that looked back to the Private Press movement initiated by William Morris's Kelmscott Press. In fact, Kós was experimenting with just this sort of activity alongside his architectural work, printinga serisf hand-lluminated texts basedon a combination of his ethnographicsudies and Transylvanian folk tales. Kós eventually set up his own printing press and guild at Crow Castle in Transylvania, which consciously emulated British models. His study Erdély Kövei (The Stones of Transylvania) was a tribute to Ruskin's Stones of Venice.

Fellowship, respect for the environment, simple living, and the integration of art into all aspects of life—these traditional values were being tested on a small scale at the Gödölló colony, but they were now harnessed to a more substantial program of social and municipal projects, which was facilitated by the end of a depression in the building trades and a change in the political climate. One of the key figures in this story was István Bárczy, the mayor of Budapest, who aimed to confirm the capital as a world city run by an enlightened municipal authority, a centerpiece for the nation-state.48 In planning housing projects, schools, facilities for leisure or for the poor, the authorities looked to foreign models, but they adapted these models to suit Hungarian needs and commissioned some of the most talented artists and architects available.

The commission for the seventeen pavilions went to Kós and Dezsö Zrumeczky, only two years after the completion of their studies. In the heart of stony Budapest, the two young architects set about creating a remarkable "village" for the animals, employing roughly hewn and carved timber above heavy rubble foundations, all based on vernacular prototypes (5.31). Further demonstration that this Transylvanian vernacular style was applicable to one of the most modern cities in Europe came the following year, when Kós won the commission to design housing for

The Wekerle Estate, developed between 1909 and 1926, would be the largest housing project built in the city during the first half of the twentieth century. In mixing city and country, the planners looked primarily to the British Garden City movement, but Kós based his actual designs for the main square on the German fortified towns of Transylvania, forming each of the main access points into covered walkways reminiscent of medieval gateways.

Bárczy's most ambitious and lasting project was a huge school-building program, part of his campaign to improve standards of literacy and general education. A major issue in Hungarian politics since the Ausgleich, a national education system was seen as an important element in "nation building." In the space of five years a remarkable series of fifty-five new schools was built throughout the city, the most outstanding of which was the Commercial School for Boys designed by Béla Lajta (5.33). Lajta had been collecting folk artifacts since 1900 and had direct experience of British architects Norman Shaw and M. H. Baillie Scott, whom he had met during an extended study and work tour of Britain and France. The façade of the school was austerely geometrical and functionalist, reflecting the commercial and industrial nature of the training provided within. In the detailing around the entrance, however, icons of modernity—such as a locomotive, a zeppelin, and an iron bridge—were combined with boldly stylized ornament derived from Lajta's study of folk arts, a decorative strategy that he carried through to the colorful ceramics, stenciling, stained glass, and other furnishings inside the building (5.32 and 5.34).

Compared to such high-end craft practices, relatively few attempts were made to engage with the industrial methods and character of the urban proletariat. Representations of work as a redemptive noble activity were invariably images of the solo woodworker, clad in an amalgam of peasant and medieval dress. In mural designs for a workers' club in Budapest, however, Maróti depicted groups of factory workers in modern clothing, universalized and dignified by allusions to Hungarian, classical, and Germanic myth (5.37). Largely funded by the family of Manfréd Weisz, a prominent industrial manufacturer, the project had been started in 1916, developed under the Bolshevik Republic of Councils in 1919 (after the war), and finally opened in 1920.

For many involved in the shaping of a Hungarian national consciousness through the arts and crafts, identity was not so much a matter of ethnicity or religion as a sense of being bound together by the abstract ideal of an enlightened, multiethnic state. The same style could be as applicable to a workers' club as to an art lover's house, to a Calvinist prayer school or Roman Catholic church as to a Jewish synagogue or tomb. The intellectual process of conceptualizing nationhood was nowhere clearer than in the way designers changed their names, deliberately identifying with an associative geography of the imagination. Around 1905 Béla Lajta, for example, had changed his surname from Leitesdorfer to reference the river that formed part of the border between Austria and Hungary. Similarly, Géza Rintel added Maróti to his surname, associating himself with Aranyos-Marót in northern Hungary (now Slovakia). Lajta and Maróti, like Kozma, Töry, Pogány, and many other design professionals involved with the Arts and Crafts in Hungary were all of Jewish extraction, exemplifying a significant facet of Budapest's intellectual and cultural life in the early twentieth century. For these individuals there was no conflict between their ethnic or religious identity and their intense patriotism.

By contrast, the craft activities of those who did not identify with the overarching ideal of Magyarorszag were officially relegated to the background. Many Slovaks in Upper Hungary, for example, aligned themselves with the Pan-Slavic movement rather than with Greater Hungary. Although reported outside Hungary, manifestations of the Slavic Arts and Crafts revival within Hungary, headed by Duan Jurkovic, were conspicuous by their absence from Budapest-centered publications, exhibitions, and collections. The simmering ethnic tensions that lay behind such omissions and exclusions were to erupt in the context of World War I.
