Whn consisted of four countries—England, Scotland, Wales, and the whole of Ireland, not just the northeastern counties. England was always the dominant partner, politically and culturally. An Act of Union between England and Scotland in 1707 had created the United Kingdom of Great Britain, which included Wales, by default. "Britain" and "British," in this essay as elsewhere, apply to this island union. Ireland, long treated as an English colony, had been brought within this system by another Act of Union in 1801, creating the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland.

The Arts and Crafts movement echoed these relationships. It started in England and flourished there perhaps more than anywhere else. England looms large in this story. In Scotland the movement reflected the ambiguities of the Union, sometimes seeming Scottish and sometimes British. In Wales it hardly took root at all, so Wales does not figure in this story. In Ireland it was definitely Irish, expressing a nationalist spirit. In this essay I have woven the stories of the Arts and Crafts in England, Scotland, and Ireland into a single narrative.

ART AND INDuSTRY In England and Scotland the Arts and Crafts movement was a response to industrialization. (In Ireland, which scarcely experienced industrialization in the nineteenth century, it had a different character, as we shall see.) From the mid-eighteenth century, the factory system, steam power, and complex machinery transformed Britain's productive capacity, bringing greater prosperity to a good part of British society. These changes were endorsed by the intellectual temper of the times, which valued reason, quantification, and the free play of economic forces. But Victorian culture was not monolithic, and voices were raised against the industrial spirit of the age.

One of those voices was that of art critic John Ruskin, in the second volume of his history of Venetian architecture, The Stones of Venice, published in 1853. Wanting to define the superiority of Gothic architecture, Ruskin contrasted the precise and perfect moldings of Greek buildings with the rough and lively carving of Gothic: skill and exactness versus feeling and humanity. Suddenly, his angry mind slipped to the present day and to the mechanical skills of British industry:

Men may be beaten, chained, tormented, yoked like cattle, slaughtered like summer flies, and yet remain in one sense, and the best sense, free. But to smother their souls. to blight and hew into rotting pollards the suckling branches of their human intelligence, to make the flesh and skin which, after the worm's work on it, is to see God, into leathern thongs to yoke machinery with—this is to be slavemasters indeed.'

So many thoughts are crowded into this terrible image of rationalized work. Yet it is also simple. Ruskin is thinking of poor, stumbling, feeling men:

Go forth again to gaze upon the old cathedral front, where you have smiled so often at the fantastic ignorance of the old sculptors: examine once more those ugly goblins, and formless monsters, and stern statues, anatomiless and rigid; but do not mock at them, for they are signs of the life and liberty of every workman who struck the stone.²

Ruskin's rhetoric created powerful antitheses—between Gothic and Greek, heart and mind, imagination and reason, medieval and modern, hand and machine, art and industry. These are the values of Romanticism set against those of utilitarianism.

Anti-industrialism was stronger in the Arts and Crafts movement in Britain than in other countries, and these antitheses were the stuff of it. For many Arts and Crafts people, "industry" meant a landscape of polluting factories, and "trade" meant profit triumphing over

beauty and workmanship. They often talked about "the machine" and, given Ruskin's writings, one might have expected them to be machine-haters. In fact, on this issue, they were both subtle and pragmatic. When they talked about "the machine," they were not thinking so much of particular machines as of the whole industrial and commercial system and the way it reduced making to a routine. As William Morris said, "It is not this or that tangible steel and brass machine which we want to et rid , but the great intangible machine  commercial tyranny, which oppreses the lives of ll of us." In their workshops they were undogmatic about machines, using them when it suited their needs, and not when it hindered them.

Ruskin set art and industry against each other. Some of his contemporaries hoped to unite the. Owen Jones, architecanddesiner;Richard Redgrave, painterturne administrato; and Henry Cole, civil servant, were design reformers, as we now call them. They believed that decorative art should follow rational principles: ornament should be conventionalized, patterns on flat surfaces should show only two dimensions. For them, carpets on which lilies and roses seemed to bloom in three-dimensional profusion were an offense against reason. They admired the nineteenth century's industrial achievements; their contribution would be to improve the taste of manufacturers and the public. This was the organizing, rationalizing spirit of the mid-nineteenth century applied todecorative art, and it would be hard toimagine a pointviewmore different from Ruskin's. The design reformers, however, were in tune with the age, and they directed the government's Department of Practical Art and its successor, the Department of Science and Art.

These departments controlled much British art education in the second half of the nineteenth century, and they created the collections of decorative art at the South Kensington Museum (now the Victoria and Albert). The influence of the design reformers' cultural empire, familiarly known as "South Kensington," could be felt throughout late-Victorian decorative art, including the Arts and Crafts. The heads of most Arts and Crafts people probably contained a healthily unresolved mixture of Ruskinism and design reform.

BEGINNINGs, 1860-1880 It is a delicate matter to trace beginnings. This section introduces architects and designers of the 1860s and 1870s who were interested in old-fashioned ways of making things or who believed that decorative art was as important as fine art. These were Arts and Crafts attitudes, but they did not appear as such, for the phrase "Arts and Crafts" had not been invented yet. These attitudes were also part of other movements, such as the Gothic Revival, which reintroduced medieval craft techniques, or the Aesthetic movement, which claimed a new, artistic seriousness for the decorative arts.

In 1861 William Morris and a group of artist-friends set themselves up as a firm of decorators, Morris, Marshall, Faulkner & Co. At first they chiefly made furniture and stained glass, often working for Gothic Revival architects (2.4). In the 1870s they began to produce more textiles and wallpapers, meeting an enthusiasm for the "artistic" furnishing of houses encouraged by the Aesthetic movement. This gave scope for Morris's special talent as a pattern designer (2.3). He drew inspiration from English garden and hedgerow flowers, and from old Italian, Persian, and Turkish textiles, with their formal patterns: freshness from nature, discipline from the past. William De Morgan trained as a painter in the 1860s, but then he met Morris, who encouraged him to turn to the decorative arts. In 1873 he set up a

pottery in Chelsea, where he and his craftsmen decorated pots and tiles with colored glazes. He was fascinated by luster, a metallic glaze made famous by the potters of medieval Islam, and Renaissance Spain and Italy (2.1). The technique had been more or less lost, but De Morgan experimented until he had re-created it. He was half artist and half scientist, and his appetite for the craft was an appetite for experiment and technique, the excited happiness of the maker in the workshop, waiting for the next batch of unpredictable lusterware to be fired.

There were two designers whose early careers and attitudes were particularly shaped by the movements of the 1860s and 1870s, and who were to play an important part in the Arts and Crafts. After an early success as a children's book illustrator, Walter Crane turned to designing wallpapers, textiles, and other products in the 1870s, becoming a leading designer of the Aesthetic movement. He also produced ambitious, symbolic paintings, which never had the success of his decorative work; people dubbed him "the Academician of the Nursery." Crane resisted the hierarchy that this implied: he saw fine arts and decorative arts as one project. This merging of categories was typical of the Aesthetic movement and would be carried forward into the Arts and Crafts under the banner of "the unity of art." Lewis F. Day began his career as a stained-glass artist, but in the 1870s he became a freelance designer specializing in wallpapers and textiles, and he was a consultant to several manufacturers. Two-dimensional decoration, particularly pattern design, was his forte, and he wrote dry, analytical textbooks on the subject. The spir o his work and ideas was close to that of the design reformers associated with South Kensington, and in the Arts and Crafts years he would champion design as applied ornament, and the designer'srole s collaborator withindustry.

Then there were two architects whose contrasting habits fed into the richness of the Arts and Crafts. Philip Webb, a good friend of Morris's, was an austere designer who saw his work as building" rather than "architecture." When he was designing, he thought a lot about the site and local building traditions. The massing of Four Gables was inspired by tower houses that policed the nearby Scottish border in medieval times. In peacetime, people lived in a low wing, but when in danger, they went up into the tower (2.6). Conversely, the Gothic Revival church architect J. D. Sedding offered the pleasures of decoration; he knew how painters, carvers, and metalworkers could enrich a building. Sedding loved the vividness and humanity of medieval wood carvings in Eglish parish churches, "life-revel," as he called them. In the 8s his love of decorative crafts manship became a program for the Arts and Crafts.

Toward the end of the 1870s, some of these attitudes found their way into words. Morris began to give occasional lectures on the decorative arts. He did not enjoy it, but he worked at it, hammering Ruskin's insights into ordinary sentences. Real art, he would say, "is the expression by man of his pleasure in labour." He would take his audience back to the Middle Ages and show them how medieval decorative arts were an enrichment of life: "Time was when the mystery and wonder of handicrafts were well acknowledged by the world, when imagination and fancy mingled with all things made by man; and in those days al handicraftsmen were artists, as we should now call them." This vision of the past was also a commentary on what was lacking in the present.

THE MOvEMENT TAKES SHAPE, 1880-1890 New workshops and studios were set up throughout the 1880s. Phoebe Traquair, an Edinburgh artist and mother of three young children, took up one technique after another, starting with embroidery in the late 1870s, adding calligraphy and illumination in the 1880s, then mural painting in 1885. In 1877 and 1878 W. A. S. Benson got to know Burne-Jones and Morris. At that time he was training to be an architect but was uneasy at the prospect of a deskbound profession. Morris and Burne-Jones encouraged him to start a workshop, as they had done almost twenty years earlier, and in 1880 he took that step. Benson loved tinkering with machinery, and by 1890 he was running what amounted to a factory in Hammersmith, equipped with lathes and stamping presses, producing vessels and light fttings in copper and brass (2.10). These were sold in his shop in London's West End, and he also issued catalogues, principally for the light fttings. Benson's work was very different from Traquair's: hers was handwork, while his depended a good deal on machinery. Both were part of the Arts and Crafts movement.

In about 1882 A. H. Mackmurdo, an architect, Selwyn Image, a designer and former clergyman, and Herbert Horne, an assistant to Mackmurdo, set up the Century Guild in London. This was an association of designers and craftsmen, rather than a single workshop, and furniture, metalwork, textiles, and interiors appeared under its name in the 1880s. The swirling forms of some of their designs, such as the Praise of the Soul textile, have been seen as precursors of the Art Nouveau style that developed in Europe in the 1890s (2.7). In 1883 T. J. Cobden-Sanderson, a neurotic London lawyer, took up bookbinding on the suggestion of Morris's wife, Jane, and the physical work helped to calm his soul. He became the leading British Arts and Crafts binder (2.8). In 1885 Christopher Whall, an unsuccessful painter, left London to live on a smallholding in Surrey with his wife. They kept chickens, a pig, and a cow. He wanted to make stained-glass windows, not just design them, and so he taught himself by trial and error, sharing his workshop with the cow (2.9). In 1888 C. R. Ashbee, an idealistic young architect, started a workshop in Whitechapel, the Guild of Handicraft, making metalwork and furniture (2.11—13). This was the Arts and Crafts as social reform, bringing pleasure in work to the poor of East London. In all these workshops, the currents of the Arts and Crafts flowed strongly—the idealism, the sense of exploration, the freshness of design, the healing and creative power of making things.

This was also the private side of the Arts and Crafts, the decorative artist in his studio, the craftsman in his workshop, negotiating daily with materials. It was the heart of the movement, where the tables were made, the books printed, the hopes and imaginings shaped. We have to listen carefully to hear this heart beating in the 1880s, for during this decade the noisy, public side of the movement came to the fore in the busy urban world of organizations and exhibitions. The Arts and Crafts developed alongside other movements of the decade, including a growing public concern with the social consequences of industrialization. The term "Industrial Revolution" was coined in 1880, and the early stages of socialism in Britain belong to this decade. The Arts and Crafts shared some of this spirit, and some of its leading figures, including Morris, Crane, and Ashbee, were socialists.

The earliest organization of the Arts and Crafts movement was the Art Workers' Guild, started as an association of painters, sculptors, architects, designers, and craftsmen early in 1884. The idea was that the members were all fundamentally artists and so could learn from each other.® Meeting regularly for a lecture or a demonstration, the guild forged loyalties among its members. Since it came to include most of the principal Arts and Crafts architects and designers in London (along with a number of lesser-known painters), it amounted to a representative body, but the members were reluctant to act in public or campaign for their beliefs.'" The guild hovered between the private and the public, speaking out occasionally on issues like the regulation of advertisements, or the building of bridges over the Thames, but it never acquired a public voice."

The second organization was the Home Arts and Industries Association, started late in 1884. Most of this essay is about the professional world of artists, architects, and designers. The association takes us to a quite different, mainly rural, world of craft as philanthropy, a world of village halls and ladies of the manor, of classes in simple wood carving and beaten metalwork. The idea was to keep the young men of the village out of the pub or away from the temptations of the local town, to revive local skills, or to provide local employment. These "home arts" classes were not dependent on the professional craft world. They developed independently and the association's job was to coordinate them. But some, like the Keswick School of Industrial Art, which started in the Lake District in 1884, were strong enough to become local industries (2.16). Gradually these stronger workshops merged with the professionals.

It is under the heading of philanthropy that Ireland makes its first appearance in this story. There was a great deal of craft activity in Ireland in the 188os, and almost all of it was philanthropic. This largely agricultural and poverty-stricken country had been governed by England for three centuries, and it was deeply divided between an Anglo-Irish Protestant ruling class and a Catholic peasantry Philanthropic craft was not new to Ireland, but it took on a new urgency in the 1880s. Alice Hart, an English philanthropist, set up the Donegal Industrial Fund in 1884 to create cottage industries in the famine-ravaged northwest. In the southwest, convents at Kinsale, County Cork, and Kenmare, County Kerry, started lace-making classes for local girls in the same year. In 1888 Florence Vere O'Brien, a talented Anglo-Irish designer, revived a lacemaking industry at Limerick (2.17). The urgency was probably due to the fact that the question o "home rule"—whether Ireland should have a degree of independence—was the most important single issue in British politics in the 1880s. Philanthropic craft appealed to both sides in this debate: as demonstrating the kindliness of British rule, and as fostering the economic life of a country that deserved independence. The Arts and Crafts movement was always closer to politics in Ireland than it was in Britain.

It is difficult to do justice to philanthropic crafts alongside the professional movement. They were a considerable phenomenon, involving thousands of people, and they had their own identity and purpose. But we do not know much about their history, except that many organizations and classes were set up in the 1880s and lasted well into the twentieth century. THE MOVEMENT AT ITS HEIGHT, 1890-1910 The two decades from about 1890 to about 1910 were the high flourishing time of the Arts and Crafts in the United Kingdom. No major events characterized these years, there were no turning points in the story, but there was a steady process of growth, both in the movement itself in its range of work and geographic extent, and in its influence on the world of architecture and the decorative arts.

New workshops continued to be established. In 1891 Morris set up the Kelmscott Press so that he could print books like the fifteenth-century ones he so much admired—strong, intensely physical things with the text standing clear and black on the handmade paper (2.21). In the 1880s Alexander Fisher had experimented with the then-disregarded technique of Limoges, or painted enamels, and in the early 1890s he began to teach it at the Finsbury Technical College in London. Soon Arts and Crafts metalwork and jewelry were glowing with the soft, metallic colors of enameling. In the late 1890s the Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings, a conservation pressure group started by Morris in 1877, began to send out young architects, under the guidance of Philip Webb, to help repair historic buildings. This was a real Arts and Crafts education, learning on the building site.'"4

In 1898 the young Edward Johnston looked at tenth-century manuscripts in the British Museum and decided that the whole art of formal writing lay in the use of a broad-nibbed pen. Arts and Crafts calligraphy, which had stumbled in previous decades, started on its course. Douglas Cockerell trained as a bookbinder with Cobden-Sanderson, set up his own workshop in 1898, and in 1904 joined forces with the newsagents W. H. Smith & Son in a trade enterprise. They produced fine bindings to Cockerell's personal standards, trade bindings to W. H. Smith's standards, and an improved trade binding Cockerell devised to show that inexpensive bindings could still be strong and pleasant to look at.'5

The society's exhibition in 1890 suffered a financial loss, and after that the exhibitions were held about every three years. The exhibitions of the 1890s (1893, 1896, and 1899) differed from the earlier ones in two ways. They were roughly the same size as the earlier exhibitions; the little workshops and studios were still dominant, rising from about a hundred in 1893 to about two hundred in 1899; and the trades remained stable at about thirty participating firms. But the little-known individuals who had been the second-largest element in earlier exhibitions disappeared almost completely. This striking change took place between 1890 and 1893. Nothing in the society's minutes explains it, but the organizing committee may have decided to do without the contribution of amateurs. Second, they differed in the character of the exhibits. There was still plenty of pattern and two-dimensional work, but it was joined now by simple, useful, threedimensional objects, brass and copper wares innocent of any decoration, plain oak tables and chairs, work similar to the sideboard by C. F. A. Voysey (2.22). The best-selling object in 1893 was a simple country rocking chair by Ernest Gimson

During these decades the movement spread throughout England, Scotland, and Ireland. In the 1880s it had been mainly concentrated in London, but in the first half of the 1890s it expanded into England's large industrial cities. Here it was generally sustained by the local art school, the city's governing elite, or the local trades. In Birmingham, where it was supported by all three, it took such a hold that it was still as strong in the 1920s and 1930s as it had been about 1900. In Manchester the School of Art appointed Walter Crane as director of design in 1893, and in 1896 artistically minded designers and managers from the city's trades set up a Northern Art Workers' Guild, where their discussions had a design-reforming tone.'® In Liverpool a special School of Architecture and Applied Art was set up at the city's new university in 1894, supported by the civic elite. By the early 1900s there were probably few English cities whose art school did not have some connection with the Arts and Crafts."It is strange that such a movement should thrive in the midst of industry, but it seems to have answered to a progressive spirit in these cities. A dedicatory plaque in the Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery reads, "By the gains of industry, we promote art."

In the 1890s Arts and Crafts people began to leave London for the country. The numbers were small, but their stories are some of the most memorable of the Arts and Crafts. In 1893 Ernest Gimson and Sidney and Ernest Barnsley went to live near Cirencester, among the wooded valleys and bare uplands of the south Cotswolds. The furniture and other work that they produced incorporated features from country woodwork, which celebrated the values they sought. The rail at the back of Gimson's sideboard derives from the sides of country wagons (2.23). In the mid-1890s Maud King and Godfrey Blount started textile workshops in Haslemere, Surrey, employing local women. Haslemere was not far from London by train, but this was a radical move. King and Blount believed that God had made the country but capitalism had horribly mismade the town. Essentially, they were waiting for the urban-industrial world to pass away. Ashbee's Guild of Handicraft was very successful in East London during the 1890s, but in 1902 he moved its workshops, about fifty men strong, to Chipping Campden in the north Cotswolds. Ashbee wanted a healthier environment for his workmen, but he perhaps did not think enough about how a complex city workshop might survive in the country (2.24). The early years of the guild in Chipping Campden were almost idyllic, but around 1905 the workshops were hit by declining sales and could not respond adequately. In 1908 the guild went into liquidation.

In Edinburgh Phoebe Traquair continued her joyful exploration of new kinds of work, taking up bookbinding in 1893, and enameling in 1901, in which she did her finest work (2.25). Full of enthusiasm for the Arts and Crafts, the young architect Robert Lorimer opened an office in Edinburgh in 1893. His first job was to repair the house and design a new garden at Earlshall, a sixteenth-century tower house in Fife, for R. W. Mackenzie, an eccentric businessman who drank only whisky and loved the old things Lorimer loved (2.27). The house was repaired in the spirit of the Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings; Lorimer designed simple cottage furniture for the kitchen, and Mackenzie added painted plates from the local pottery. Tall and gaunt, Scottish tower houses were often surrounded by walled gardens, and the original walls at Earlshall had survived. Within them, Lorimer created a sequence of outdoor rooms, sheltered from the bitter winds of the sea, a paradise for the mind. "He who loves his garden still keeps his Eden" is carved on the gateway.2" Earlshall is a very Scottish and a very Arts and Crafts place.

While the movement expanded geographically, it also acquired a greater presence in the world of the decorative arts. It penetrated the art schools, displacing design reform as the principal influence on art education. This was partly because its feeling for materials and the actual making of objects inspired better teaching, and partly because control of art schools passed in these years from a national body, the Department of Science and Art, to local authorities, who often believed that craft teaching would have more bearing on the improvement of local industries. We have already seen the close relations between the movement and provincial art schools. In 1896 the London County Council set up the Central School of Arts and Crafts to provide evening classes for artisans in the building and decorative arts trades (2.38). W. R. Lethaby, the sharpest mind in the movement, was headmaster from 1896 to 1911, and his staff included Douglas Cockerell, Christopher Whall, and Edward Johnston. The Central School became a powerhouse of the Arts and Crafts in the capital.

Arts and Crafts influence penetrated the press. Three of the new style of 1890s magazines, which printed photographs alongside text, were particular supporters of the Arts and Crafts movement. The first and most important was The Studio, starting in 1893. This monthly was the principal showcase for British Arts and Crafts for about twenty years and was widely read in Europe and the United States. The second was the monthly Architectural Review, which was edited from 1896 to 1900 by Henry Wilson, one of the most brilliant Arts and Crafts architects and designers. The third was the weekly Country Life. This was launched in 1898, but its Arts and Crafts allegiance only became clear in 1910, when Laurence Weaver was appointed architectural editor. Over the next seven years he produced more than two hundred articles on upper-middleclass houses in the country, the building type at which Arts and Crafts architects excelled.

And it penetrated further into the trades, beyond the manufacturers and retailers who r textiles since the 1870s, sold Arts and Crafts—influenced furniture, textiles, and pottery from about 1890, and metalwork from 1899 (2.39 and 2.40). About 1895 the young Ambrose Heal designed some plain oak furniture and put it in the showroom of the family firm, Heal & Son of Tottenham Court Road, London, specialists in bedding. The staff referred haughtily to Ambrose's "prison furnishings," but he gradually moved the firm in an Arts and Crafts direction (2.42).3 Goods from Liberty's and Heal's were not cheap, but they were usually cheaper than the equiva-Ient from Arts and Crafts workshops, and so made Arts and Crafts design more widely available. Arts and Crafts people often looked down on these shops. They cared more about production than about consumption and found most of their customers among the upper middle class. The English class system is relevant here, and there is a contrast with the United States, where the movement was seen as democratic, and cheapness as a virtue in Arts and Crafts goods.

Meanwhile, the tastes and workshop practices of the movement had been quietly changing. In the Arts and Crafts exhibitions of the 1900s (1903, 1906, and 1910), we begin to notice small and precious objects, hovering between the useful and the decorative. Phoebe Traquair's enameled metalwork, first exhibited in 1903, is typical. The principal influence was Henry Wilson's metalwork and jewelry, which was sculptural and encrusted with ornament (2.41). If the decorative surface was the leitmotif of the 1880s, and the simple object that of the 1890s, in the 1900s it was the decorative object. As if to underline this shift toward small-workshop virtuosity, from 1906 exhibits were listed under a single name; unless a separate executant was mentioned, it was assumed the designer and the maker were the same person.

The composition of exhibitors was changing too. The little workshops and studios maintained their dominance at about two hundred. The little-known individuals who had disappeared in the 1890s came back in strength, at about a hundred or more, only at this stage it was clear that they were art students; the movement was gathering strength from its penetration of the schools. And the trades maintained a steady level at about thirty in the exhibitions of 1903 and 1906. Then, in 1910, trade firms fell to about a dozen. Among those dozen were Jeffrey & Co., Longden & Co., and James Powell & Sons, old faithfuls who had shown in every exhibition. But something was happening: the precious objects at these shows, the designer-makers, the art students crowding in, the trade firms falling away—all these factors were related. The tense but creative relationship between the little workshops and the top end of the trade, between art and industry, was beginning to unravel.

THE QUALITIES OF ARTS AND CRAFTS OBJECTS We have come far enough in the story of the movement to take a general view of the objects that Arts and Crafts people made. The usual way of understanding such things is to look for common visual features and call them a style. But the illustrations of English, Scottish, and Irish work in this book do not show a single Arts and Crafts style. Newcomers to the Arts and Crafts find this difficult to understand. The truth is that it is not surprising, for what mattered in the Arts and Crafts was not what objects looked like but the spirit in which they were made. Arts and Crafts objects were distinguished not by style, but by qualities that correspond to common Arts and Crafts attitudes.

Many Arts and Crafts objects express a self-conscious simplicity. They convey a feeling that it was enough that a table or a dish should be just what it was, and that no amount of ornament or embellishment could add to its essential qualities. This was something subtler and deeper than mere visual simplicity or functionalism, more like a love of things. It was expressed in common Arts and Crafts practices: doing without ornament; relying on the texture of materials, the grain of wood, the thickness and bumpiness of handmade paper; and making much of construction, the obvious joints and heavy structures in furniture (2.43). There is no obvious English word for it, though the German word Sachlichkeit comes close, with its sense of what is there, objective, essential. Sache means "thing," and "thinginess" would be an awkward but expressive translation.

Alongside this slightly puritanical affection for the essence of things, there was a joyous and instinctive enthusiasm for the decorative arts and their historical traditions. This is a puzzling attitude, for it is almost the opposite of the first. The Arts and Crafts movement was a love affair with the "object-world" of the decorative arts, with the wealth of old work being gathered at the South Kensington Museum, the re-creation of old techniques, and the sheer pleasure of making. Think of Morris and his dyeing and weaving and printing; of his daughter May's embroideries (2.44); of William De Morgan and his luster glazes; of Alexander Fisher and his painted enamels. Think also of the prominence of purely decorative objects, stained-glass windows, embroideries, and jewels (2.45). This aspect of the Arts and Crafts is sometimes overlooked because it did not have any particular intellectual justification and did not provoke any talk of simplicity or design reform. Yet it was a large part of the movement. And it did not need any justification.

A profound, creative relationship with the past was a feature of Arts and Crafts work. It is difficult for a modern reader to understand how natural this relationship was, for the fourhundred-year-old European tradition of drawing inspiration from the past was broken in the twentieth century. Now we think it was somehow odd to look to the past, but it was normal then. Think of Edward Johnston and his tenth-century manuscripts, Christopher Whall and medieval glass, Robert Lorimer and his sixteenth-century tower houses. Edwin Lutyens's design for "The Hut" was inspired by the cottage vernacular of Surrey in the sixteenth, seventeenth, and eighteenth centuries, and it evoked all sorts of romantic historical associations with the countryside, peasant life, and Englishness (2.46).

There was also a profound, creative relationship to nature. The flowers and trees and birds that fly through M. H. Baille Scott's interiors and furniture designs are conventionalized in accordance with the decorative scheme, but they are somehow also vivid and real, the world of nature as a sustaining, beautiful place (2.47 and 2.48). This combination of convention and reality is most powerful, as we have seen, in Morris's textiles. Morris wrote, "You may be sure that any decoration is futile..when it does not remind you of something beyond itself, of something of which it is but a visible symbol." Arts and Crafts ornament was thus meant to carry the mind out to the countryside, just as the historical inspiration of Arts and Crafts work carries the mind back to the past. Nature and the past were the twin dreamworlds of the nineteenth-century Romantic imagination, the opposites of the city and the modern. Antimodernism runs deep in the Arts and Crafts.

ENDINGS, 1910 oNwARD In 1912 the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society ran into difficulties. The exhibtion that year took a loss and brought the society into the red. In the postmortem the committee was split between the older members and the not-so-old. The designer and metalworker Harold Stabler, then aged forty-one, led a subcommittee that reported in favor of the society going out into the marketplace and running a salesroom. The president, Walter Crane, and Christopher Whall and W. R. Lethaby, leaders of the movement then in their late fifties and sixties, opposed this, confirming the society's growing sense of craft as a small-workshop activity, isolated from manufacturing and the marketplace. It was impossible, Lethaby said, for the society to run a shop. In the end, nothing happened. In 1915 Stabler and others formed a r movement to bear on industrial production. This was not exactly a parting of the ways; Lethaby became the association's (slightly problematic) father figure. But now the values of the trades had their own voice. The society was left standing for craft, pure and simple.

Loss was the keynote of these years. It was not just the trades that fell away. It was also progressive architects, who had been important in the 1880s, but whose enthusiasms were now for the city, not the country, and for steel frames instead of local materials. It was journalists, too, who left. In 1911 the Architectural Review, which had once been a champion of the movement, referred to "the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society, now dead." Walter Crane wrote protesting that the society was alive, but the report was symptomatic.² For several decades the movement had achieved a presence in the public world of the metropolis, the press, and exhibitions. In the years around 1900 it was the principal progressive movement in British architecture and decorative art. A decade later, it was going out of fashion.

It is sometimes said that World War I brought the Arts and Crafts movement to an end, but in a strange way, the war gave it new life. In 1915 Crane died and the presidency of the troubled Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society passed to Henry Wilson, who had a radical vision for the crafts. He taught Arts and Crafts people to see opportunity in the social and educational reconstruction that would follow the war. In 1916 he organized the most remarkable Arts and Crafts exhibition ever: remarkable because it was held at the Royal Academy, the bastion of fine-art privilege; because Wilson's exhibition design gave a meaning and context to Arts and Crafts objects for the first time; and because he reaffirmed the original meaning of the movement (2.49). There was a room devoted to founders' work—Morris, Burne-Jones, Crane—of which Wilson wrote: "We still hold the ideas of a small group of men who formed this society eight and twenty years ago..ideas that are plain and simple and that lie at the root of life cannot grow old or become ridiculous like last year's clothing." For many people the horror of war made the English countryside seem heartrendingly beautiful. For Wilson it made the crafts seem all the more essential.

The Arts and Crafts continued quietly in the 1920s and 1930s. The public side of the movement had gone, but its heart survived in many little workshops, and its influence was still strong in art schools. Being out of fashion, it now lived more comfortably on the margins. It was easier to survive in the countryside: in the south Cotswolds so many artists and craftspeople had gathered around Gimson and the Barnsleys that the movement was if anything more populous than it had been before the war.³ It was also easier if you had a niche. Public ceremonies and presentations provided one kind of support for the crafts: Edward Johnston's brilliant career as a calligrapher arced over the war and into the 1930s, thanks partly to many public addresses he was asked to inscribe. His postwar work was arguably his finest (2.50). Conservation provided another: Douglas Cockerell found a niche in conservation bookbinding, and his workshop, continued by his son Sandy, closed down as recently as 1987.

This part of the story has no ending date: it is not a simple matter to lay the Arts and Crafts movement to rest, It has many endings. Sometimes it died with the people who made it live. Where the Arts and Crafts movement flourished, the names of Ruskin and Morris were invoked. The other chapters in this book explain these enthusiasms and their part in the development of Arts and Crafts work in different countries.

Those chapters also provide a larger view, a place from which we can look back on the movement in the United Kingdom. In this essay I have focused narrowly on the interplay between Ruskinism and design reform, and between the little workshops and the trade firms, because I think it helps to explain the story of the Arts and Crafts in the United Kingdom. If one takes a wider view and compares the movement in Britain with those in other industrializing countries, such as Germany and the United States, or in the countries of Scandinavia and Eastern Europe, which were not yet industrialized, the most obvious characteristic of the British movement seems to be its anti-industrialism. Part of the reason for this might be found in the British class system. The movement was allied to the British upper middle class. It was not just that it found its market among such wealthy people.
