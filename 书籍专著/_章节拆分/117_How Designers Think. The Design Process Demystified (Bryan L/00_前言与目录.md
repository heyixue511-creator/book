# Author's preface to the second edition

When this book was first written it represented the fruits of a journey into the nature of the design process which had already occupied the author for a little more than a decade. Some ten years later, at the time of writing the second edition, that journey continues to fascinate and to provide new challenges. The ability to create original artifacts which are simultaneously beautiful and useful is a great gift, and its study consequently quite absorbing. Throughout the history of the civilised world there have always been designers, but only relatively recently have they become so specialised and so separated from the making of their artifacts. Both designers and their work have acquired a much higher profile with the advent of mass visual media. Inevitably both those of us who teach designers and those who live in or use their designs have a vested interest in just how such creations come into being. Design research began seriously in the 196os as first designers themselves and educationalists began to look beyond the end products of design and to inquire more deeply into the process.

This book not only draws on this reasearch but also relies on the accumulated experience of the frustrations and rewards of teaching design over some twenty years. The author has taught students of architecture, planning and industrial and interior design, and this book is intended to help as an introduction to the skills they must acquire in the early years of their study. It does

not, however, teach a design method and is certainly not an instruction manual. Rather it attempts to show how varied and sophisticated are the mental skills which such designers need to employ. The reader is introduced to a variety of styles of thinking and to some techniques for forcing thought along desired channels. The book draws on the work of psychologists who have investigated design as well as the explanations and descriptions of some well known designers. Although most of this book concentrates on the designer as an individual we must recognise that designers cannot live in the isolation of the artist's garret. The modern designer works in and for society and often in teams. Today designers are increasingly using computers to help them through the escalating complexity of the problems they face. This book therefore concludes with chapters on the relationships both among designers and between them and others, and on the relationship they may have with artificial intelligence.

The central theme of this book however is that design is a skill which, for most of us, must be acquired and developed. There are, of course, "natural" designers just are there are artists, musicians, and sports people. However, such people are relatively rare, and most of us rely upon a more self conscious effort to develop what natural talent we may have into professionally useful skills. This book is intended to assist in that process.
