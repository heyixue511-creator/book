# 14 Designing with computers

The change from thinking about machine design to thinking about systems design is not a gradual evolution but a discontinuity; an abrupt change in basic philosophy comparable to the change which occurred when the engineer took over design problems from the blacksmith and carpenter.

W. T. Singleton, Man-Machine Systems

In 1968 one could read all existing literature in English on the subject of "artificial intelligence" within one month. Now it takes about six months.

Nicholas Negroponte (1975), Soft Architecture Machines

## Why use computers in design at all?

It may at first seem rather strange to include a chapter on designing with computers in a book about how designers think. We tend to view computers as rather limited in their ability to think. We may recognise some of the things they can do like calculate and sort information, but surely this is a long way from the essentially creative process of design with which this book is primarily concerned?

Computers can easily be portrayed as large, powerful, and even perhaps wilful, devices that are likely to get out of control. Along with their fellow invention, the robot, they feature in science fiction portraits of the future which are frighteningly inhuman and hostile. This sort of image is exemplified by HAL in Arthur C. Clarke's now famous story 2001, where a supposedly superintelligent computer fails to show the sort of values we would expect of a human, and thus we do not need to project ourselves into a speculative vision of the future to see examples of apparently wayward behaviour by computers. The absurdly wrong fuel bill sent out by computer to some defenceless and lonely pensioner does not seem fictional, but part of our real and current world.

What is of interest here in the context of this book is that such errors are much more likely to be the result of human error than of some machine malfunction. A typing error when entering data into a computer can cause problems enough, but such an error when entering a program can have horrific effects, the reason for which can be almost impossible to uncover. It is just no use therefore defending the computer by claiming human error, since it is in the very nature of computers that they can magnify the effects of simple human errors out of all proportion. A mistake in ordinary life is more often detected quickly and immediately rectified. Bugs in computer programs can lie untriggered for many years, only to wreak their havoc long after the author moved on to other things.

If computers are so dangerous and unfriendly in society at large, why should we try to apply them to the design process? Indeed, computer aided design has atttracted much criticism based on this generally uncomplimentary view of the computer in society. Some writers, for example Nigel Cross (1977) in his book The Automated Architect, appear to argue that computers are intrinsically harmful to the design process. Certainly many of these arguments need to be taken seriously, and raise issues of great concern to design theorists. Will designers feel intimidated and inhibited by the computer? Is the great speed of a computer likely to add more pressure and thus stress to the designer's condition? Will the reliance on numerical and quantitative data apparently demanded by computers distort the emphasis of the design process and thus serve only to create a huge number trap?

Whilst these are valid and useful questions with which we must deal, some of the criticism of computer aided design is rather less soundly based and relies more heavily on images and deep seated fears than on substance. The same author, Cross (1978) in a public exchange of views with Tom Maver, one of the British pioneers of research into CAD in architecture, criticises CAD on the basis that "the examples he (Maver) gives of successful CAD applications are for buildings such as district general hospitals, comprehensive schools and airport terminals. These are all apparently necesssary features of living in the modern worldbig, centralised, impersonal institutions." Cross's criticism here seems to be more one of lifestyle than of the nature of computing power. This linking of the computer with the notion of large centralised and institutionalised societies seems misleading on two counts. Firstly, we shall make reference later in this chapter to work supervised by Maver which allowed nursery school teachers to participate in the design of their workplace. Secondly, although historically computers were large and institutionally owned, they are now very much part of the domestic scene as a result of the technology push which brought them into the mass market.

Of course computer enthusiasts are no less guilty of this image building. Negroponte (197o) seems just as sure of his ground when he tells us that his "interest is simply to preface and encourage a machine intelligence that stimulates a design for the good life and will allow for a full set of self-improving methods. We are talking about a symbiosis that is a cohabitation of two intelligent species." Heady stuff indeed, which just happens to sneak in the rather dramatic assumptions that machines can be intelligent and considered as a species. However, the very technology push which is so clear in Negroponte's intentions and of which Cross seems so suspicious, has brought computers into the design studio where we would ignore them at our peril. Just as not all features of the human intellect are always helpful to the designer so it is with computers. In fact computers, like people, have strengths and weaknesses. For this very reason we must study them as possible cooperating partners in design to see if Negroponte's productive symbiosis can really be achieved.

## An historical perspective

Since we are actually living through the computing revolution right now, our historical perspective is likely to be lacking in the perception which comes from hindsight. It is much easier to be wise when looking back at the great industrial revolution, and at the changes it brought in terms of technology and the separation of designing from making, and to the growth of the design professions. However, look back we must in order to develop a shared view of the impact and importance of computers to the designer. As the second edition of this book is being written, there have been massive changes in the human/computer relationship even since the first edition just less than a decade ago. One of the most significant aspects of this issue lies not in the actual technical advances but in the extent to which they have penetrated society. During the last decade, the author's local city council promised to put a computer in every school. We have had at least one year in which the toy retailers announced that computers were the largest selling type of Christmas gift. Thus we have a generation of children who are growing up with computers, not as futuristic monsters, but as educational tools and toys, and as a part of everyday life.

There can be no question that this poses a substantial problem in the development of professional practice. By their very nature, professions are hierarchical and defensive. Those who run our design practices, in this regard at least, had a quite different education to that of the next generation of designers. The design schools in universities and polytechnics are beginning to admit students from this new generation and face a challenge for which they are ill-equipped. Many of these schools have struggled to persuade their funding authorities of the need to install computer aided design facilities, and this is slowly happening. What, in general, these schools lack however, is the pool of human skill and expertise in teaching designers to work with the new technology. We are thus still a long way from developing a mature and considered approach to the introduction of computers into design, and are probably still in the "technology push" rather than "market pull" phase of this revolution.

## Computers as machines

Computers in reality cannot be considered in isolation save perhaps in technical studies of their design and manufacture. In use computers only form parts of systems, which usually include not only software but also human beings. It is this wider study of systems to which we must progress here; however, let us first examine them as machines.

We cannot devote more than a small amount of space here to computer hardware, but a brief description is necessary before we can imagine how our thoughts might be aided by such devices. In essence, although these days technically computers are very sophisticated, they remain simple in concept. In functional terms the hardware can be seen to consist of only a few major elements, each of which have temptingly anthropomorphic possibilities. The data storage media which remember, the central processor which appears to think, and the peripheral devices which appear to see and write or draw, are all that need concern us here.

Computers are widely held to be in their fourth generation with the so-called "fifth generation" computer under development. Here it is necessary to recognise that if the fifth generation arrives as is forecast it will be as the result of fundamentally different types of advance to those which enabled its ancestors.

The first generation of machines recognisable as computers emerged in the mid 194os. These machines were dependent on valves and were consequently vast in size, though tiny in capacity compared with even a domestic computer of today. They were unreliable and needed to be looked after by a whole team of technicians and engineeers. It was the invention of the transistor which was to replace the valve that signalled the second generation. The second generation of mid 195os computers used this much more compact, reliable technology to create machines, which although still large, at least had a practically useful capacity.

The integrated circuit enabled manufacturers of computers to build complete circuits on a single device and this gave rise to the third even more compact and powerful generation of computers in the mid 196os. It was at this point that sensible research into computer aided design could begin, although of course these machines were the sort of large institutionally owned computers of the kind that could not practically be used in most design studios.

The large scale integrated circuitry of the early 1970s enabled another simultaneous reduction in size and increase in power. The new "chips" of the fourth generation of computers were now small enough to allow the microcomputer to be mass produced for the domestic and business markets. Thus although the fourth generation was the result of an advance of a similar technical nature to those which had gone before, it had significantly more impact on society and consequently on our potential relationship to computers as "thinking" partners.

Of particular significance to designers is the parallel growth in computer graphics devices. The spread of microcomputers has opened up a sufficiently large worldwide market to enable the mass production of cheap devices on which it is possible to generate graphics of a quality which interests designers. This process continues, particularly with regard to what the computer world calls "hard copy" devices such as plotters and printers. Electrostatic plotters and laser printers are already leaving their more mechanical pen plotters and dot matrix printers in the shade. Speed, line and colour quality, all seem to be increasing to meet the critical demands of the design draughting market.

## The information revolution

The apparently relentless advance of computer hardware is really only part of a much wider revolution in society which concerns the whole business of information technology. Other parallel advances in information handling, communication and reprographic machines, such as copiers, facsimile devices, television and video recording, all have their effect on our changing view of information. Western society as it is now organised is totally dependent on this revolution. Although this book relies on the traditional printed page to communicate these ideas from author to reader, this is supported by advanced information technology at every stage. The author used a computer with word processing software to write the original ideas at one end of the chain, and the stock of printed books and orders is managed by the publisher's computer system at the other end.

Information now ranks with the other great concepts of science such as energy, matter, etc, and is recognised as an essential requirement for man to exist in this and future societies (Lewin 1982)

This new appreciation of information as a fundamentally important concept has been accompanied by advances in our understanding of the hitherto largely ignored phenomena of information processing and communication. The computer as a complete working entity is as dependent on information science as it is on pure technical innovation. The way in which we program computers and conceive of how they might be persuaded to help us think has advanced enormously in recent years. The so called Fifth Generation Computer Project represents an attempt to use all this understanding to produce a radically new kind of computer.

All four previous generations of computer have essentially been data processing and calculating machines which have simultaneously become more compact, reliable and powerful at each step. The programmer of such computers has had to use a language far removed from our everyday human communication and to think out the way in which the computer would solve a problem. The philosophy behind the fifth generation computer is that it should hold sufficient knowledge to be able to understand the nature of a problem and then solve it. This would allow us to communicate with computers in a natural language such as English, even perhaps in its spoken form. Eventually we may be able to show such a computer drawings or models which it could understand. Such a computer sounds as if it might indeed be a useful partner in the design studio!

Fifth generation computers will have to depend on developments of both hardware and software which have largely taken place since the fourth generation computer was first created. Such computers will inevitably rely upon parallel processing. In the conventional computer only one task is performed at once, and the computer performs apparently very complex tasks simply by working extremely fast. Quite ordinary computers are now able to complete several million instructions each second. However, devices such as the transputer now allow us to build computers which have many processors capable of working simultaneously. To understand the implications of this for design we can imagine such a computer trying to draw a perspective in which all the lines were dealt with at once instead of, as at the present, in sequence. Such an advance would thus allow us actually to move around a designed object in real time, perhaps adjusting the design as we did so. In turn the parallel processor will depend on the latest development in the line of chips, the very large scale integrated (VLSI) circuit, to hold the vast quantities of data required.

Programming techniques have advanced significantly in recent years to provide us with quite new kinds of language. These new languages such as PROLOG and LISP are declarative rather than procedural as were earlier languages such as FORTRAN or BASIC. Such languages are more suited to the expression of knowledge in the form of facts and rules, than to the expression of mathematical procedures. They enable us to make computers apparently "think" quite differently. In essence they provide computers with an inferential capability previously almost impossible to imagine. It is generally these declarative languages which are used for the development of the so called "expert" system. Whilst the older procedural languages are frequently used to simulate mechanical or physical processes, here the intention is that the computer should behave as if it possessed human expertise. The expert system can hold knowledge about a limited situation, search through that knowledge, select and ask the most revealing questions, draw conclusions and even explain why it has arrived at those conclusions. Thus it is that expert systems can already perform such tasks as medical diagnosis or advise on legal matters. Design orientated applications of these ideas have generally been limited to systems for checking designs against legislative requirements, offering planning application guidance, choosing trees suitable for certain site conditions and so on.

## The computer in the design office

Just how does this concept of information relate to the act of designing and how might we understand the changes that might take place when we introduce computers into the process? In an attempt to begin answering this important question it is necessary to analyse the roles the computer could play in the design practice, and then to review some attempts at realising these roles in the form of computer aided design.

We have seen that computers can manipulate data which represents both text and graphics and can produce both written and drawn output. We also know that computers can calculate and, with the newer programming techniques, can make inferences from a knowledge base. If these skills are applied to the tasks performed in the design office, it seems reasonable to classify the computer's role in terms of its potential degree of involvement in the design process itself. In fact, computers can be seen to play at least four roles in the design office (Lawson 1981). We can call these the Management Role, the Information Processing Role, the Solution Evaluation Role and the Solution Generation Role.

In the Management Role the computer is helping the designer to run the business. It may involve electronic mail, keeping diaries and warning when procedures need to be followed, managing staff timesheets and paying staff salaries, or simply acting as a word processor. In this role the computer is thus supporting not the central, or radical, role of the design office, but the support roles. It is a tool more for the secretary and office manager than for the designer. The computer can be seen in this role in many types of professional office, not just the design practice, and, although interesting in its own right, such a role need not really concern us here.

In the Information Processing Role, the computer is used to hold and reproduce data usually either in the form of drawings or written documents. In the design office such a use of the computer attempts to automate the task of preparing such documents, whether they be schedules, bills of quantity, design drawings, or production drawings. Such a role can be played by a computer with more or less standard software such as word processing systems and two dimensional drafting systems. Although this does not directly impinge on the design process, such a role shows two major changes from the management role. The computer is now concerned with the information about the design itself rather than the running of the office. In turn this implies that designers themselves may be using the computer and that what they see may indeed change their thinking about the design.

However, it is when we move on to the Solution Evaluation Role that the computer really begins to impact on the design process and to influence the thoughts of the designer. Here the computer is being used in some way to model a proposed design, and to generate output which helps to evaluate the design. In the design fields with which this book is primarily concerned, this will almost certainly imply that some form of three dimensional modelling software is in use. Now instead of creating drawings on the computer screen, the designer describes, through some appropriate language, the three dimensional form of a design to the computer. In turn the computer may produce any number of drawings such as perspectives, axonometrics, or even plans and elevations. Here the computer is being used to help the designer "see" what a design would actually look like, and in this sense it is automating the process of building a physical model. However we must not be fooled by this analogy, for there are some fundamental differences between working with physical and computer models. These differences have to do both with the way we manipulate such models and the kinds of information they can hold. This we will return to in due course, but first let us continue to map out the ground a little.

Finally we can envisage the computer in a Solution Generation Role, in which the computer actually suggests either part or all of a design solution. Our understanding of the nature of computers would tend to suggest that such techniques are more likely to be successful at the objective and closed end of the spectrum of design activity. Although history bears this out, recent developments in knowledge engineering and artificial intelligence now suggest that such ideas could in future spread, to impact on the much more subjective design fields. Clearly this sort of involvement brings the computer right into the very heart of the design process itself, and is therefore much more likely to be controversial than our first role which kept the computer to a more remote management role.

## Early attempts at solution generation

Not surprisingly, computer aided design made its first real impact in those areas of design which are fairly dependent on numerical techniques. For example Logcher et al (1967) had developed their Integrated Civil Engineering System (ICES) and incorporated a designer-oriented language for structural engineers (STRUDL) long before comparably sophisticated architectural applications had been contemplated. On the same basis the computer soon invaded other areas of design amenable to numerical techniques such as the aerodynamic shaping of aircraft and car bodies. In architecture, the sizing and organisation of lift systems, and the design of waiting areas for queues in airports were amongst the earliest problems to be tackled.

In fact, by far the most important contribution of computer aided design remains in the fields of electronic and mechanical engineering. There seem to be several reasons why this should be. Certainly, these are rather closed ended design tasks with clearly defined goals and criteria of success, but there is more to it than that. In these fields the ratio of the cost of the object being designed to the cost of the design process is small, since most electronic circuits and mechanical devices will be mass produced. In urban design, architecture or interior design the reverse is true. Such design processes form a very small proportion of the budget for single original creations, and there is consequently no tradition of investing in efficient methods of design. It is also the case that although computer graphics are required for the design of electronic circuits for example, they only need to be symbolic rather than formal representations. Colour could be used to indicate wiring layers or electrical functions, for which a limited palette of crude hues would suffice. By contrast the interior designer requires many subtle shades to represent the actual appearance of paints, textiles and textured materials. Thus there was a multiple barrier to introducing computer graphics into architecture or interior design. The software was going to be much more difficult to write and the hardware more sophisticated. Thus the higher costs would be incurred in the very field of design which has traditionally low design process budgets.

When computers were applied to architecture then the idea was seen as controversial. Surprisingly, the early attempts were often made to cast the computer in the solution generation role. One of the most notable of these was the BAIDl program published by Boyd Auger (1972). This highly specialised piece of software was intended to help solve the problem of arranging housing layouts to satisfy the then applicable constraints for privacy and for daylight and sunlight. The rules governed the extent to which overlooking would be permitted and the amount of clear sky and of direct sunlight which could be experienced in the main rooms of the house. Auger demonstrated his program by creating a design for the small but intriguingly shaped island of Isla Dino off the Calabrian coast of Italy. Auger's program randomly generated locations for houses and then tested each location against accepted criteria for privacy, daylight and sunlight. Firstly, the Scottish Building Regulations rule requiring a minimum 18.3 metres between any directly opposing window facades, secondly a minimum $\textbf { I } \%$ daylight factor at each facade, and thirdly, a minimum 2 hours of sunlight per day over ten months on the living room facade. The intention here was to use the computer to generate ideas by making many runs of the program and allowing the designer to select, combine and modify parts of these solutions.

Another highly influential early attempt at solution generation was Whitehead and Eldars(1964) program to plan buildings so as to minimise the distances travelled by their occupants. The authors demonstrated the effectiveness of their technique by designing a hospital operating theatre suite with the program. They argued that the time wasted by hospital staff moving around the building could be costed by relating it to the salaries of the various groups such as surgeons and nurses. The input to the program is thus a series of numbers representing the expected number of journeys made between each pair of spaces for each group of hospital staff over a unit period of time. These figures are weighted by the relative salaries and the program then seeks to minimise the distances between the pairs of rooms generating the greatest circulation. In fact the program proceeds simply by first positioning the room with the largest overall circulation figure and then adds the room which most relates to $\mathbf { i t } ,$ continuing on to position each successive room so as to minimise the circulation to all previously positioned rooms.

Such a technique is open to many criticisms, of which three seem quite important. Firstly, it does not actually guarantee an optimal solution. Cross $\left( { \bf { I 9 7 7 } } \right)$ showed that some designers were able to produce solutions to the Whitehead and Eldars design problem which achieved lower circulation costs than could their computer program. (It is, however, worth noting that the average performance of Cross's human designers was worse than the computer.)

The second major criticism of the Whitehead and Eldars approach is of a type which can frequently be made about early attempts at computerised decision making software, and that is to do with the rather restrictive nature of the required input. The data we have about the potential movement of people about a building is, in reality, rather less precise than it appears, once it has been entered into the Whitehead and Eldars program. The pattern of use of a building is actually quite difficult to predict as accurately as is required to fill in the interaction matrix, which forms the input to the program. People do, to some extent at least, respond to their environment and change their behaviour accordingly. This is currently one of the obstacles faced by computerised motor car navigation systems which help drivers get through crowded cities. We learn to take short cuts, or to change our pattern of journeys so as to make our own life more convenient. Unless a building is to be identical to one already constructed therefore, the data for Whitehead and Elders program is unlikely to be an accurate prediction of the way the building would actually be used. To be fair to the computer, criticism could equally well be applied to a human designer's efforts should he attempt to design to minimise circulation cost. Perhaps though the computer tends to hide this "number trap" from us and create an unconscious "cost benefit" mentality.

The third criticism is perhaps the most serious for the proponents of computer aided design, and that concerns the way in which such a technique impinges upon the designer's own thought processes. After the Whitehead and Eldars type of program has run, the designer is left with a kind of quasi-optimal solution in terms of circulation cost. Of course there are many more issues which influence planning, some of which are more quantifiable than others. Capital and maintenance costs tend to be minimised by reducing the perimeter wall. The external constraints, which include such problems as access, particularly of service vehicles, privacy, lighting, solar gain and so on, all contribute to the final decisions on the disposition of spaces. The practical constraints of construction and structure and the distribution of services all suggest building configurations which might not be sympathetic to the solution derived by the computer. The crux of this difficulty is that the computer has removed just one set of internal client/user generated set of constraints, namely human circulation, and ignored all other complexities of the problem. The designer is therefore now faced with the task of modifying the computer solution to take account of all other requirements. In this sense the human is seen as responding to the computer domination of the process. Whether or not such a relationship is helpful or conducive to good design is by no means clear. Whether buildings designed in such a way would in any general sense be recognised as better is even more doubtful.

There is no real evidence of designers actually choosing to work with either the Auger or the Whitehead and Eldars program. Neither program was actually made available in a commercial form on a computer which could sensibly be used in a design office. Such a package could easily be achieved today however, so this continued lack of enthusiasm does indeed seem to suggest that designers do not find such systems of great value. Perhaps this comes from the dominant role which the computer plays in this form of human/computer interaction. Our earlier chapter on design strategies showed how varied the attack on a problem can be. A technique which determines that the human must respond to an idea generated by a computer about a rather limited subset of the problem would not seem to offer much promise.

## Early solution evaluation ideas

One way of reversing this dominance is to cast the computer in the role of evaluator of the human idea. A whole series of programs were developed at the ABACUS unit at Strathclyde University which followed this principle. The SPACES suite of programs begins with numerical inputs not dissimilar to those required by Whitehead and Eldars. However these programs were intended to help design Scottish schools, and the inputs were therefore in the form of timetables showing the numbers of pupils studying subjects in classes of various sizes. The computer then generated the schedule of accommodation and produced a "bubble diagram" in which the diameters of the bubbles represented floor areas of rooms and the distances between the bubbles represented their desired proximity.

However, SPACES allows the user to go on to interact with the computer by moving the bubbles around and changing their shape. Thus the designer could begin to develop a series of actual rooms which could be arranged in a "building-like" fashion. During this phase the computer assisted by continuing to make feedback available to the designer on how well the current design was performing. This was achieved by displaying an overall circulation "efficiency score" and indicating which pairs of spaces were too far apart or unnecessarily close. Thus the designer here is able to incorporate his own thinking on issues other than circulation and to allow the emerging design to reflect them whilst not losing track of circulation efficiency.

ABACUS continued to develop programs of this kind, but generalised rather than restricted to the design of a particular building type. The GOAL and PARTIAL programs allowed a variety of performance feedback including simple estimates of capital cost and energy consumption. In 1977 Robert Aish reported a most interesting set of experiments using these systems to design an eighty place nursery school. What makes these experiments so fascinating was the inclusion of nursery school headteachers amongst the subjects. These headteachers had no experience either of architectural design or of computing, but could perform this task using the programs provided. Thus the computer had been used to support the design process by providing some basic draughting and evaluative facilities. As Aish puts it, the "participant can produce a graphically accurate drawing of his design idea while the calculative aspects of the program perform a value free tutorial role and help him to bring his design within cost and performance limits". Encouragingly the results of the study showed that when the teachers were involved in using the program then the designs were judged (by other teachers) as more satisfactory interpretations of user needs. Again, this is not to say these were "better" buildings in any general sense, but it is interesting to see that computer aided design can at least offer the promise of designs which accurately satisfy client/user constraints.

## Adhoc or integrated systems?

If we accept this principle that computers can indeed help in the solution evaluation role then the problem becomes one of how best to design such a CAD system. There are several aspects to this problem which continue to bedevil system designers. In essence, most of these problems revolve around the twin issues of the interface design and the intelligence of the system.

Figure 4.2 in the chapter on the components of design problems mapped out some aspects of window design. It is quite possible to conceive of computer programs which would deal with many of the boxes in this diagram. The physics of the situation in terms of heat, light and sound can certainly be modelled on a computer, and we have just seen Boyd Auger's program which also included a measure of privacy. Computer perspectives can be drawn very much more quickly and accurately than by hand to give reliable impressions of the extent of views through windows from inside rooms. The time taken to perform even the most rudimentary calculations of heat loss or daylighting by hand often proves just too big a disincentive for designers actually to use the tools created for them by scientists. The author has found that students of architecture hardly ever reported the use of daylight prediction methods in the offices which they attend during their period of practical experience, although all are taught to use such techniques at university.

The computer can perform such calculations so rapidly that, in theory at least, the feedback obtained could be used effortlessly by the designer to modify a poor solution. However, this presupposes two conditions before the computer could help the designer think in this way. Firstly, the computer must be programmed with a series of routines for calculations, for example heat loss and daylighting, and where appropriate, criteria of success, for example building regulation requirements. Secondly, the computer must be able to receive information about individual designs and allow for the easy modification of that information.

On the whole the first requirement can easily be satisfied for some aspects of design, and can often be achieved with relatively small amounts of computer power. However the input of the geometrical and specificational data about a design is rather more problematic. Much of the information about a design, particularly the geometrical information, will be common to many programs. For example the positions, sizes and shapes of walls, floors, roofs and windows will be needed to calculate heat loss or daylight. Some information, however, particularly the more esoteric specificational data, for example the light reflectance of wall surfaces, will be required only by one specialised program. Thus we have two basic ways of making this necessary information available to the computer. We can either run entirely separate, or ad hoc, programs each requiring some geometrical and specificational information to be input as needed, or we can first describe the design in general to the computer and then run whatever programs we choose, possibly adding very specific minor items of data each time. This distinction between the ad hoc and integrated ways of using computers in architectural design was first articulated by Lansdown in 1 969, and there remain advantages to both even now.

If we know in advance which evaluative studies are to be conducted and they are limited in number, then the ad hoc approach might be simpler. It could be done on cheaper computers and with programs which requested only the minimum amount of information, which could be made quite easy to use. There are now many examples of simple ad hoc programs in the architectural field which will run on small personal computers: heat loss calculations, condensation and insulation calculations, sizing simple structural members and so on. Inevitably many of these programs involve the input of numerical data which the user must first obtain by measuring the design. For example, simple heat loss calculations may impose a considerable demand for measuring the areas of all the surfaces in a building. There are thus two dangers here. Firstly the measurements may be wrong and the mistake go unnoticed since the computer does the sums. Secondly the large amount of re-measuring work needed may prove too large a disincentive to exploring design changes.

If however, we wish to be free to conduct any number of studies and to relate them to each other, possibly with repeated runs, then the integrated approach might be more sensible. However it will almost certainly require much more computing power and is likely to be much more difficult to use. This approach though, fits much more sympathetically into the design process. There are two major related reasons for this. Firstly the computer takes on the measurement as well as the calculation role, removing the risk or error. This means that each individual evaluation study makes virtually no demands on the designer other than initiating the appropriate program. Here, by contrast, the designer's time has been spent earlier in the process by describing the design to the computer. Secondly relationships and trade offs can be studied with, for example, heat loss set against solar gain and lighting considerations. Unfortunately these benefits are bought at quite a price. The success of this approach depends upon how easy it is to describe a design to the computer and then adjust that design. Inevitably this requires both more expensive computers and the development of new skills in operating a sophisticated CAD system.

## Computer models

The integrated approach depends upon the existence of a computer model of the design and on a method of describing that design to the computer. These two related problems are worthy of our attention since they fundamentally affect the way a designer may think about a design which is developing in the computer model.

The model itself is never value free, but inevitably represents the prejudices and preconceptions of those who designed its data structure. For example, computer models can be organised to represent either surfaces or solids into which holes may be carved. A landscape architect would almost certainly want a surface modeller whilst a sculptor needs a solid modeller. Unfortunately, neither type of model is entirely satisfactory for architecture and interior design. We shall return to this problem a little later. The degree of computation required tends to escalate dramatically as we increase the geometrical complexity of the model. Simple cuboid forms are more easily handled than irregular polyhedral forms, which in turn are very much more easily computed than curved forms.

Restricted geometry models are obviously a case of the computing tail wagging the design dog! However even simple cuboid models can have a role in that they are not only very easy to compute for the system designer, but also simple to describe and manipulate for the designer/user. For example the author developed an extremely limited and simple model for use as an educational tool on the first ever commercial personal computer. This program was intended to teach first year architecture students how daylight, solar gain and heat loss interact in the design of walls and windows. The student draws a single rectangular room on the screen and inserts windows. The computer will in turn report the heat loss and solar gain and daylight distribution. Now the interaction between these three environmental factors is a complex one. The student however is able to develop an understanding of this interaction by changing the number, size, shape and position of windows, the orientation, size and shape of the room, and the thermal properties of the building components. Such a program is far too restricted by its model for general use, but may still play a useful educational role.

The difficulty is, however, that in practice the computer system designer simply does not know what degree of geometrical flexibility the designer will want. If he provides too much, then the program may be unnecessarily difficult to use, if he provides too little he will restrict the designer's freedom. One way of overcoming these difficulties is to work with a structural and constructional system, which has itself been explicitly defined. Many of the early attempts to produce computer building modelling systems relied upon this approach. In the UK the HARNESS package for hospital design, the West Sussex County Architects system, the CEDAR system of the Government Property Services Agency and the OXSYS system of the Oxford Regional Hospital Board (later to become BDS from ARC) all followed this pattern. To a greater or lesser extent such systems rely upon the use of prescribed building components which are not only limited in number but also in jointing possibilities and in plan and section configuration. Other systems such as RUCAPS rely on grid planning and floor level layering and dimensioning in an attempt to contain the problem.

## The problem of the interface

It would seem that a method of describing a design to the computer must be devised which is no more time consuming than the traditional methods of drawing or model making. Actually this may not necessarily be $\mathbf { s o } ,$ for the integrated computer model can in turn be used to generate the production drawings and schedules which otherwise often consume much more time than the early design phases.

There are also some benefits which are incidental to this discussion but which can be very important in the design office. Architectural working drawings in particular are notoriously difficult to coordinate. The process of drawing production take so long that many changes to the design are likely after the first sheet is drawn. Ensuring that all changes are properly included on all drawings is extremely difficult. Studies by the Building Research Establishment (Crawshaw, 1976) have shown that failure to coordinate drawings is one of the major causes of on-site contractual problems creating delay and additional cost.

So it may be that designers will be prepared to use a system which, provided it can save production drawing time and ensure drawing coordination, takes a little longer to use. However this requirement to generate production drawings places even further demands on the integrated system. A detailed study of this problem must be left to books on computer aided design. What is of interest to us here is the manner in which the designer is thinking at early and later stages of design. Researchers at the GABLE unit at Sheffield University have been working on these issues over many years under the author's direction and have developed CAD systems for architects to use from design through to working drawing production. In particular this work has shown how architects need to think about their buildings in several overlapping ways. At the sketch design stage the ideas may well be in terms of spaces, as shown in the ABACUS studies described earlier. However at some point the building also becomes a collection of elements such as walls, floors and roofs. As we have seen in the chapter on design strategies such changes do not necessarily follow in a logical sequence adhered to by all designers.

Lawson and Riley (1982) published two papers reporting work which showed how these problems could be overcome by providing enough intelligence in the computer program to enable it to "read" architectural plans. In this technique the designer simply drew lines on the computer screen which were known to represent certain building elements such as walls and windows. The computer then analysed these drawings to compute a model of the building which could be examined in several ways. Firstly the building could be seen as a collection of building components, secondly as a series of floor levels or elevations, and finally as a collection of spaces. In fact the computer automatically built a model of the inside of every space in the building and of the external skin. The designer could then use this model to draw views of the building, evaluate environmental performance or generate production drawings. A refined version of these pro-

## The metaphor of the interface

Clearly such systems require new skills from the designer to those already acquired on the traditional drawing board. The designer now needs to interact with a three dimensional performing model of his design which is in the computer whilst still thinking about the design in his head. Usually this will have to be done by way of two dimensional devices such as screens, digitisers, mice and the like. It has become normal for computer software to employ some sort of metaphor in the design of the interface between man and machine. Most computers still have a key called "Carriage Return" to represent the old physical action of pulling the lever on a manual typewriter which returned the carriage to its extreme right position. Word processors now extend the metaphor to include "tab stops", and even the idea of "cut and paste" for editing blocks of text. Apple Macintosh introduced the idea of the "desktop" to personal computing where the user can even delete files by moving an iconic representation into a screen picture of a waste bin, or to shuffle papers around just as one might on a real desk.

In computer aided design these metaphors are not quite so easily developed. However many systems now share at least some metaphors. The idea of overlaying is a common notion in CAD systems and refers back to the registering of transparent drawings on a drafting table in such a way that various different kinds of information could be read together. Van Norman (1986) has claimed that such metaphors are central to the success of CAD systems. He has suggested that the metaphor needs to satisfy four basic conditions to be successful. The physical task used as a metaphor must closely resemble the task assisted by the program. The user of the program should already possess expertise at the physical task. It should be possible to employ the metaphor throughout the program, and the metaphor should be capable of being stretched where there are no physical parallels.

Metaphors in architectural draughting systems tend to rely on the traditional drawing board. Layered sheets drawn on by pens using tee squares and setsquares, and sometimes even compasses and dividers, are common. In graphic design the metaphors rely on the ideas of painting, and use such ideas as paintboxes, brushes and spray guns. In engineering design an assembly of parts metaphor is more common. Here the designer will create form by colliding primitives such as spheres, cones and cubes, possibly using lathe terminology for turning and shaping items.

One of the problems here is that none of these metaphors really meets the need for architectural design, as opposed to purely draughting. The three dimensional model suggests that we should abandon the two dimensional drawing board and paintbox metaphors, but the engineering component metaphor is hardly appropriate either. Van Norman suggests a modelshop metaphor in which we represent the tools we might have used to create a physical architectural model. This seems inadequate too since such models rarely show structure or constructional detail, usually being made of different materials to the real building.

It remains an interesting question as to how dependent we really are on such psychological props. There are some signs that youngsters who have grown up using computers as toys find the ideas of computer models and transformation quite natural without the need of physical metaphors. Perhaps one day we shall not need such metaphors. Indeed, there are increasing problems these days with the extending of metaphors in CAD to deal with the many processes unique to the computer.

## Designer and computer

Although attempts have been made to make computers simulate human thought, the natural characteristics of computers and people seem unmistakeably distinct. The differences between the performance of man and machine were first highlighted by the American psychologist Paul Fitts, famous for his work on what he called "human performance theory" (Fitts 1951). The Fitts List, as it became known, shows how machines are characterised as fast, accurate, consistent, and precise by comparison with humans. However they are also inflexible, unimaginative, and can be tedious to program. Humans whilst being slow and error prone, are flexible, imaginative, good at inductive rather than deductive reasoning, but can be inconsistent. Of course humans can get bored or feel unwell and perform badly, however they are usually aware of this and can adapt. By comparison computers can fail dramatically without warning.

The designers of systems involving both men and machines have thus attempted to combine the attributes of people and machines to the best overall effect. Often such man-machine systems are required to make decisions in order to control some process or operation. The flight deck of a modern aircraft or the control room of a nuclear power station are both examples of the nerve centre of a system which can no more be fully automated than it could survive entirely without computers. For their part the computers are rapid, precise, reliable and eternally vigilant, while the human ability to make balanced and integrated judgements of unpredictable situations is undeniably vital.

Clearly designing is not amenable to techniques of total automation, and as far as we can see computers will not design in the sense in which we use the word throughout this book. However, we have seen in this chapter that recent developments are creating a new generation of computers which are capable of behaving in ways which much more closely resemble human characteristics than Fitts envisaged in the early 195os. The view of computers as restricted and predictable dates back to their early generations. Byron's daughter is reported to have condemned Babbage's mechanical precursor of the electronic computer as having "no pretensions to originate anything". She maintained that such a device could only do as it was instructed. Now whilst this assertion is in principle true, the modern computer with the benefit of its enormous power and speed relying on modern artificial intelligence software can generate results which surprise us. As Turing, one of the most influential early writers on artificial intelligence, put it, the restrictive view of the computer assumes that "as soon as a fact is presented to a mind, all consequences of that fact spring into the mind simultaneously with it" (Turing 1950). Nothing could be further from the truth, or design would be easy indeed. As we observed in an earlier chapter, creativity remains one of the most intractable areas of psychology. No-one is able to guarantee that given a set of facts, he will be able to deduce all the consequences, or to imagine all the implications. Increasingly we must expect computers to help us not just with calculations and facts but with inferences and suggestions.

This may indeed begin to happen once we have learnt to link together the now traditional computer aided design system and the newer intelligent knowledge based system. Progress in this difficult field is likely to be slow as we learn to engineer knowledge in the new ways this will demand (Lansdown 1982). Donald Hoey (1987) has shown that it is possible to use these ideas to offer architectural criticism of designs held in a CAD system by reading the geometrical data into an expert system provided with knowledge about design criteria. For his demonstration Hoey encapsulated the writings of the Russian constructivist El Lissitzky in such a knowledge base. El Lissitzky expressed a design philosophy of spatial characteristics not just through his drawn work but also in extensive written publications. Hoey's program was therefore able to behave like a critic well versed in El Lissitzky's views and to comment on whether a design followed his various principles of spatial organisation. At the other end of the spectrum, research into artificial intelligence is leading to systems which can recognize three dimensional form using binocular vision. There now seems a real possibility that eventually such systems will be able to create models in CAD systems of existing objects which can be captured on video. This brings closer the possibility of a genuine three dimensional interaction between designer and computer. Whether such ideas will ever be developed to the point of such systems becoming useful "thinking" and designing partners only time will tell.

It seems reasonable to view the design of a computer aided design system as a man-machine decision making system. Much has been written about the design of such systems, and most authors in some way base their methodology on the Fitts' list approach. Singleton (1974) for example, shows a complete process in which the objectives of the system and thus its functions are first defined and then allocated to humans or machines on the basis of man-machine capabilities. The real difficulty with using this approach towards computer aided design is that we understand so little of the process as practised by man that it is by no means easy to define functions in the manner that Singleton intended. As we saw in earlier chapters, design problems are rarely initially explicit, and thus even the designer is unsure of the goals. How then can we define these goals, except in a very general way, even before the designer begins?

Whilst computer aided design remains shrouded by so many unanswered questions there seems little doubt that work will continue. The existing research inertia and the promise of so many potential advantages to be gained from harnessing the power of advanced information processing are sufficient to motivate many to try to answer some of the questions. Whether the apparent advantages of computer aided design will materialise or remain a mirage is as yet unclear. However the attempt, in turn, seems likely to bring an increased understanding of how designers think, and for that alone it should be encouraged.

Only connect

This book is really a collection of contradictions. It sets out to describe how designers think, but inevitably concludes that we know rather less about the subject than we would like. It tries to define design, and yet recognises that no one definition is likely to be universally acceptable and yet remain useful. It seeks a methodology and concludes that this remains a highly individual matter. However, hopefully the book will have achieved at least three things for its reader. Firstly it should have convinced you that design is an infuriatingly frustrating, but superbly challenging, and thoroughly rewarding activity. From this it follows that the design process must be studied and not just the end products of that process, since clearly the process influences the product. Secondly, the book should have demonstrated that design is indeed a skill which can be learnt and practised, and we all have it in us to develop those skills. Finally it should be clear that the design problem has a unique set of qualities which distinguishes it from other fields of human endeavour such as science, art, law, medicine or politics. Whilst the author strongly believes design can be taught, he does not believe this implies that a single process can be taught. It seems each of us must find our own methodology and learn how to adapt it to new problems and to working in new situations perhaps with other designers or computers.

It is impossible to write a value free book on such a matter, and inevitably this represents an inadequate attempt. Since the first edition was published, Rowe (1987), using Attoe's (1978) terminology, has suggested that positions taken up by writers in this field can be seen as somewhere on a continuum from "doctrinaire positions" to "categorical systems". On the whole the doctrinaire positions seem to be taken up by designers and depend on aphorisms such as the famous translated utterance of Mies van de Rohe "less is more" or Louis Sullivan's equally terse "form follows function". Categorical systems are rather less certain and prescriptive, but rather offer frameworks within which knowledge about the subject can be located. In this sense Rowe would presumably regard the model of design problems which forms a theme threading through this book as a categorical system.

If that is so, then the author would not be unhappy unless it should be thought that he sees this model as representing some absolute truth, for he does not. It is simply a way of looking at the problem which has seemed useful over the years to a number of people who have used it, sometimes to develop their design skills, and sometimes to advance their ideas about the nature of design thinking. If alternatively it suggests different categorical structures to others, then it will also have served its purpose. This is neither the first nor the last book to take this line, with one of the more recent being Heath's (1984) attempt to categorise architecture into "commodity buildings", "systems buildings" and "symbolic buildings", which he casts into a problem space having dimensions of "symbolism", "information" and "noise". There are probably as many such categorical systems as there are authors with the stamina to work them into books.

Design is a difficult enough matter without making it more complex, and it is to be hoped that this book, whilst it may reveal complexity, has not obscured. Conferences about design research are rarely attended by those who are recognised as the leading designers of their day, and many practising designers have little respect for some design researchers. Regrettably there may appear to be a tradition of obfuscation amongst some authors in the field. Sometimes this is simply because they have identified a very small problem which they think is important, and to solve that problem they have brought techniques to bear which in turn rely upon sophisticated theories. The reader who is tempted to be critical and dismissive of such efforts should perhaps remember that a client may find some of the doodles which are made by designers just as difficult to relate to the big problem seen from his perspective.

There are now so many backgrounds from which researchers into design can and do come; cognitive psychology, information science, philosophy, sociology, linguistics, art and of course design itself. It is not therefore surprising that it is difficult to understand the research which might emerge from such disparate minds. So where does design research go from here? In fact there are really only a few basic approaches that can be taken to design research. We can construct artificial experiments under controlled conditions, we can observe what designers do, we can interview designers or read what they say, and we can simply think about it. Examples of all of these approaches have been discussed in this book, but each can be found wanting.

All experiments of course are artificial, but in this case it is extremely difficult to create conditions which are an accurate model of the real world. Consequently those experiments which are devised tend to deal with very limited aspects or phases of the process. Nevertheless some basic ideas such as those developed by Lawson and Rowe can be taken on by others. Indeed Lawson's early ideas on the intermingling of analysis and synthesis, which came from the laboratory, inspired the work of Darke which has conducted in the field.

Simply watching designers means relying on their externalisations, either in the form of drawings or words. Regrettably neither is always coherent unless we also know what is going on in their heads, and as soon as we ask them that we have destroyed the very situation we are observing. Of course there are ways round this, for example recording design group conversations as Agabani did, but the situation remains artificial and the data requires considerable interpretation.

We can interview and analyse the writings of designers as Darke and Suckle have done, but the writings of designers are misleading in their own way too. Most designers seem at their best when designing and not when writing. Those that do have a way with words often use that ability to "sell" their ideas rather than explain their methods. What is perhaps worse, when they do choose to describe their process, we and probably they, are not sure to what extent this is accurate reporting as opposed to posthoc rationalisation.

Simply thinking about design as Broadbent has done is probably the most difficult approach of all since it depends so very much upon personal interpretation and can be so vulnerable to preconception. Such ideas themselves of course cannot really be tested, but they may provoke others in the empirical tradition to try to devise experiments to test their theories.

Not one of these approaches then, offers the complete answer; and the uncomfortable truth is that we must carry on pressing ahead, hoping that we can all understand just enough of each other's ideas to piece them together into some grander notions which illuminate the scene. In the meantime designers will continue to think, sometimes by themselves, sometimes with others, and sometimes with computers. Just occasionally their results will excite and inspire us and we will continue to wonder how it is done. Perhaps we should hope never fully to understand the way designers think, for it is exactly because the designer does not know what he will think next which makes design such a challenging and satisfying occupation ...
