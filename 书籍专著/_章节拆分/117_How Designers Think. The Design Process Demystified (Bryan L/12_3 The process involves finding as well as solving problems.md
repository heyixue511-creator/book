## 3 The process involves finding as well as solving problems

It is clear from our analysis of the nature of design problems that the designer must inevitably expend considerable energy in identifying the problems confronting him. It is central to modern thinking about design that problems and solutions are seen as emerging together rather than one following logically upon the other. The process is thus less linear than implied by many of the maps discussed in chapter 3 but rather more argumentative. That is, both problem and solution become clearer as the process goes on. We have also seen in chapter 6 how the designer himself is actually expected to contribute problems as well as solutions. Since neither finding problems nor producing solutions can be seen as predominantly logical activities we must expect the design process to demand the highest levels of creative thinking. We shall discuss creativity as a phenomenon and how it may be promoted in the next section.
