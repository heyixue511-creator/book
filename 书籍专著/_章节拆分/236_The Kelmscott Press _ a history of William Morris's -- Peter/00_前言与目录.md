With this brisk introduction William Morris began writing on 11 November 1895

a brief explanation for an American admirer of how and why he had established the most famous of all private presses, the Kelmscott Press. The essay first appeared in Modern Art, a Boston magazine, in 1896 and was later reprinted, under the title A Note by William Morris on his Aims in Founding the Kelmscott Press, as the last publication of the Press in the spring of 1898. Morris's formulation of his purposes was characteristically terse: 'it was the essence of my undertaking', he wrote, 'to produce books which it would be a pleasure to look upon as pieces of printing and arrangement of type.' The rest of the essay was devoted to a discussion of paper, type-design, spaces between words and lines, margins, and ornaments.

Morris's Note is an undeniably important document. As one of his few public statements about the Kelmscott Press, it has been quoted frequently since 1898 in histories of the Press; indeed, in 1924 Bernard H. Newdigate went so far as to suggest that the Note was one of the three books that every student of English book-production ought to read.' Yet one might argue that the essay is cryptic, even evasive, leaving a host of questions unanswered. How did Morris develop an interest in book-design and production? Why did he turn to fifteenthcentury German and Italian books for his models? What was the relationship between the Kelmscott Press books and the best work of other nineteenth-century English printers? How exactly did Morris rediscover some of the fundamental principles of fine printing? How did he find the craftsmen to carry out those principles for him?

We need the answers to these and other questions, for there is nowadays general agreement — even among those who dislike his style of typography - that Morris is one of the most important printers of the modern era and that the establishment of the Kelmscott Press was an event of major cultural significance. Throughout northern Europe and the English-speaking world, particularly in the

United States, the impact of Morris's typographical theories and of the Kelmscott books theselve was pervav. Histoians are stil ung about the natureof that influence (whether, in particular, it was always a good or enlightening influence), but no one disputes that the forces set in motion at the Kelmscott Press eventually spread round the globe.

Fron a merely quantitative point of view, the achievement of the Kelnscott Press does not seem especially impressive. Between 1891 and 1898 it produced fifty-two books and a set of specimen pages of another book that was never conpleted. The books were designed by Mor a ing about 3o0 copies. All were ornamented with initials and borders by Morris, and some were illustrated, usually by Edward Burne-Jones. So why are these few Kelmscott books famous everywhere today, and why have they profoundly affected twentieth-century theories of book-design?

One explanation of their potent appeal lies in the fact that Morris's ideas, like his ornamented books, are richly suggestive. When he appears to be speaking about typographical matters, he is in reality examining how we ought to live.'If I were asked to say what is at once the most important production of Art and the thing most to be longed for, I should answer, A beautiful House; and if I were further asked to name the production next in importance and the thing next to be longed for, I should answer, A beautiful Book,' he wrote in an unfinished essay. 'To enjoy good houses and good books in self-respect and decent comfort, seems to me to be the pleasurable end towards which all societies of human beings ought now to struggle." That is a most characteristic passage by Morris: ot content with treating typography as 'a minor art' (to quote Stanley Morison's phrase),3 Morris insists upon enlarging the context and seeing the beautiful book as an essential part of his attempt to redeem the late-Victorian world from dreary mechanical ugliness. Pugin's pictures, in turn, evoke Carlyle's Past and Present (1843), the very title of which vibrates with the fervour of Victorian moral idealism, and its implied contrast, like Pugin's, between a lost Golden Age and the bleak world of nineteenth-century England may help us to grasp the passionate intensity of Morris's rejection of the present. We may perhaps wish to protest that Pugin, Carlyle, Ruskin, Morris, and others of their outlook idealized the Middle Ages and ignored the material benefits of our mechanized civilization; yet we fail to do them justice unless we recognize that the Gothic Revival was not ultimately an attempt to resuscitate a period style of architecture or art but rather a morally powerful protest against the psychological and social disintegration produced by the modern world.

The Kelmscott Press was, in effect, the final phase of the Gothic Revival. Burne-Jones compared the Kelmscott Chaucer (1896) to a cathedral; by the same analogy, the smaller books can be regarded as parish churches, each faithfully rendered in quasi-nedieval style. But such a sweeping rejection of contemporary life brought with it apparently insoluble problens. How, for example, could one reproduce the general appearance of a fifteenth-century book and still escape the dangers of miere imitation? By turning one's back on contemporary printing machinery, how could one inprove the appearance of the ordinary book? In reverting to methods of hand-production, how could one print books that readers with modest incomes might afford?

HE history of the printed book may seem, at first glance, to be a perfect confirmation of Morris's view that the older crafts had steadily deteriorated in quality since the Middle Ages. 'Printing', as Richard Durnford, Bishop of Chichester, put it trenchantly, is an art which beginning perfect, ever afterwards declined."

Ana wno, comparing tne typical massproduced volume of the nineteenth century - nastily printed in greyish ink on shoddy, acidic paper - with the Mainz Psalter or Gutenberg's Bible, could doubt that Morris and the Bishop were right?

Yet the story of the decay of printing is a good deal more complex than Morris ever acknowledged. For him, it was all quite simple: the earliest printed books were still the best because they were produced at a time when the spirit of medieval craftsmanship was strong, but, with the advent of false historicism and a weakness for pseudo-classical ornament in the sixteenth century, printing, like architecture, began to decay. Only in the second half of the eighteenth century, according to Morris, were the consequences of this typographical dry rot fully revealed in the 'sweltering hideousness of the Bodoni letter, the most illegible type that was ever cut'. Bodoni and Didot, even more relentlessly than Baskerville (whose achievements Morris certainly underestimated), carried the development of the 'modern' typeface to its logical conclusion. 'It was reserved for the founders of the later eighteenth century', wrote Morris, 'to produce letters which are positively ugly, and which, it may be added, are dazzling and unpleasant to the eye owing to the clumsy thickening and vulgar thinning of the lines.  The nineteenth century's further contributions to this melancholy tale were increased mechanization and cheaper paper, ink, and binding materials. When he reflected upon the contemporary printing scene, Morris found a small source of optimism only in the growing popularity of Caslon's old-face type and its imitations.

Because of the wide influence of Morris's typographical writings, this sweeping indictment of nineteenth-century printing became accepted dogma for several decades following his death in 1896. There came in time the inevitable reaction against his oversimplifications and his doctrinaire hatred of the modern world. Writing in an American typographicaljournal, the Dolphin, in 1938, Peter Beilenson remarked with obvious irony that 'there had developed the general opinion that between the early great classical period of Didot, Bodoni, and Bulmer, and the late arts-and-crafts period of Morris, the printing of the century was one continuous sacrifice on the altars of Machinery and Mainmon'. But there were other old-style printers, les frequently remembered today perhaps than the Chiswick Press but famous in their own time. The firm of George Falkner and Sons in Manchester, for example, were celebrated for the large collection of woodcuts, decorated initials, and head-and tailpieces with which they ornamented pseudoantique books. Falkner productions were invariably described by reviewers or the firm itself as quaint' — i.e. self-consciously antiquarian in favour. (It is significant that Morris never uses the word 'quaint' when writing about his own variety of old-style printing.) The Falkners had set up a wooden hand-press in 'the quaint Caxtonian Printing Offices in Old Manchester', and their printer's mark, designed by Walter Crane, showed a pressman at work on a hand-press. Printers like Falkner and Sons issued a good deal of ephemera as well as books: their 'Old Style Calendar for 1882', for instance, contained 'many delightful and entertaining particulars, very commodious indeed and profytable, worth the labour of your reading.
