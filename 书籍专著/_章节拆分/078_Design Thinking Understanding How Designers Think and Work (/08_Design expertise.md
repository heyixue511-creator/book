# Design expertise

At the beginning of this book, in Chapter 1, I set out some of theways that the activity of designing can be investigated, and in the intervening chapters I have reported and discussed some of my own studies and related these to the work of other researchers. In the main, my approach has been one of empirical investigation, based on reported experience or observation and analysis. In explaining and relating these various studies, I have tried to build an evidence-based understanding of how designers think and work, and the nature of expert performance in design. In this final chapter I will consider design expertise more generally, its development from novice to expert, and suggest that the developed cognitive skills of design thinking can be regarded as a particular form of intelligence.

## Experts and novices

Many of the classic studies of expertise have been based on examples of game-playing (such as chess) or on comparisons of experts versus novices in solving routine problems (e.g. in puzzle solving). These studies are generally examples of solving what are called well-defined problems, whereas designers characteristically deal with ill-defined problems, where there is no definitive, complete formulation of the problem and no single or verifiably correct solution. Some of the standard results from the classic studies of expertise do not match with results from studies of expertise in creative domains such as design. For example, creative experts might reformulate the given task so that it is more problematic – that is deliberately treat it as ill-defined – which is contrary to the assumption that experts will generally solve a problem in the ‘easiest’ way or certainly with more ease than novices. In some ways, therefore, experts in creative fields treat problems as ‘harder’ problems than do novices. We saw examples of that in the discussion of design ability in Chapter 1 and in the case studies of Gordon Murray and Kenneth Grange: examples of designers going beyond the brief or ‘given’ problem.

Experts will often see a given problem as more complex than do novices, partly because of their greater experience. In studies of problem-solving, novice behaviour is usually associated with a ‘depth-first’ approach, which means that the novice identifies a problem aspect and immediately begins exploring its solution in depth. In designing, this results in partial sub-solutions that may be difficult to reconcile together into a satisfactory overall solution. The strategies of experts, however, are usually regarded as exhibiting predominantly breadth-first approaches, that is starting with a broad problem exploration and developing partial sub-solutions together in parallel. In designing, this is reflected in expert designers’ considering broader contextual aspects of the design problem before moving on to aspects of more detail.

Some depth-first and breadth-first aspects were observed between the behaviour of novice and experienced designers in studies by Saeema Ahmed and her colleagues. They found clear differences between the behaviours of new graduate entrants to the engineering profession and much more experienced design engineers. The novices used ‘trial and error’ techniques of generating and implementing a design modification, evaluating it, then generating another and so on through many iterations. Experienced engineers were observed to make a preliminary evaluation of their tentative decisions before implementing them and making a final evaluation. They used the foresight they had gained from experience to consider whether it seemed worthwhile to move further into the implementation stage of a design decision.

However, a more complex view has gradually emerged as more studies are made of expert designer behaviour. We have already seen how designers can exhibit opportunistic behaviour in pursuing interesting conjectures in more depth before returning to a broader strategy. This was contextualized further by Linden Ball and his colleagues, who studied the strategies of both hardware and software designers and suggested that ‘experts will often tend to mix both breadth-first and depth-first solution development strategies . . . the preferred strategy of expert designers is a top-down, breadth-first one, but experts will switch to depth-first design to deal strategically with situations where their knowledge is stretched. Thus, depthfirst design is a response to factors such as problem complexity and design uncertainty.’ This suggests that, as well as spotting promising opportunities for solution generation, even expert designers will run up against unfamiliar problems and will have to make some initial in-depth exploration of partial solution concepts in order to assess their viability.

## From novice to expert

We are all familiar with the concepts of the novice learner and the expert performer, and aware that something happens in the development from one to the other. A novice undergoes training and education in their chosen field and then at some later point becomes an expert.

The development of expertise usually seems to pass through different phases (Figure 8.1). In all fields, the accumulation of experience is a vital part of the transformation from novice to expert. For some people, the expert level of achievement is where they remain, perhaps with some continued moderate improvement before reaching their peak and beginning their decline. A few manage to go beyond the level of their peers into a further stage of development, reaching outstanding levels of achievement and mastery. At the other end of the scale of maturity, at a young age, all of us are introduced to a variety of human activities, whether it be making music, playing football, drawing pictures or writing stories. We all improve a little, but some, as noted earlier, will begin to practice with a dedication – and probably a joy or even obsession – that sets them apart.

Moving from one level of expertise to another is not necessarily a steady progression. It is not simply a matter of knowing more and learning to work more quickly or smoothly. The changes in level involve learning to work in different ways. This shift to working in a different way can develop almost unnoticed by the learner but can mean shifts in level of attention that we all experience in learning a skill, as the fundamental practices become performed less consciously and flow more easily.

There has been much more of a history of studies on understanding expertise and expert performance in fields and contexts other than design, including chess, music, science and sports. From these studies, there is a general view that expertise can only be developed over time as a person matures. Usually, there comes a point when a peak of performance is reached and then a seemingly inevitable decline begins. This performance peak will be reached at different ages in different domains: for physical sports, it may be around the age of middle-twenties, whereas in cognitive activities it may not be until much later in life; in the sciences, people seem to produce their best work in their thirties, whilst in the arts it may be in their forties. Some outstanding individuals seem to defy the general pattern of development-peak-decline and continue producing great work well into later years.

A general aspect that seems agreed from studies of expertise is that it requires a minimum period of practice and sustained involvement before performance reaches a recognized expert level of achievement – at least ten years from first involvement. This is a matter not simply of experience or exposure to the field of endeavour but of dedicated application. One of the key factors in the acquisition of expertise is believed to be deliberate, sustained, guided practice.

The psychologist Anders Ericsson, who is an expert in the study of expertise, has suggested that ‘The attained level of performance of many types of experts, such as musicians, chess players and athletes, is closely related to their accumulated amount of deliberate practice’. Usually, a young person may display a certain aptitude or interest, and parents or teachers then encourage and guide their development. But without the dedicated application of the individual, levels of performance will remain modest. Again, according to Ericsson,

Superior expert performance is primarily acquired .  .  . Many thousands of hours of deliberate practice and training are necessary to reach the highest levels of performance . . . Most international masters emphasise the role of motivation, concentration, and the willingness to work hard on improving performance . . . The masters seem to consider inborn capacities and innate talent as relatively unimportant.

So in all fields it seems that the key to the development of expertise is practice, practice, practice.

## Development of expertise

As we have seen, many studies of expert designers suggest that from initial scoping of the given problem they move on to early solution conjectures and use these conjectures as a way of exploring and defining the problem and solution together. This solution-focused nature of designing was first noted by Bryan Lawson in comparisons he made of the problem-solving behaviours of scientists and architects. Lawson set the same problem-solving exercise for students of science and architecture and found that senior students could be distinguished as adopting predominantly either problem-focused or solution-focused strategies. The science students attempted to discover the underlying structure, or rules of the problem in order to solve it (problem-focused), whereas the architects made solution attempts and then sought to improve them (solution-focused). These differences were noticeable amongst senior students but not amongst first-years, so it appeared that the architects had learned their solution-focused strategy during their education as an effective strategy for resolving the kinds of problems that they had been set. They had learned to think like designers.

Education in design has long-established practices that are assumed to help the progression from novice to expert, although in general they are not based on evaluated evidence. There is still rather limited understanding of the differences between novice and expert performance in design, and how to help students move from one to the other, but there has been some progress.

For example, Henri Christiaans found, from protocol studies of industrial design students, that junior students did not seek a lot of information on the project they were given, and tended to ‘solve a simple problem’, being unaware of a lot of potential criteria, difficulties and possibilities. These are characteristics of a novice. Amongst senior students he found that they could be divided into two types. Although they all gathered more project information than junior students, for one type the activity of gathering information was too dominant, and sometimes just a substitute activity for actually moving on to design work. The more successful type, in terms of the quality of their solutions, gathered rather less information but considered it more carefully and gave the impression of consciously building up a broader view of the problem.

Very similar conclusions were reached by Cynthia Atman and her colleagues, who found from protocol studies of engineering students that, amongst first-year students ‘those who spent a large proportion of their time defining the problem did not produce quality designs’. As with the industrial design students, it seemed that the novice engineering students just solved simple interpretations of the problem or became stuck in problem definition and did not progress satisfactorily into further stages of the design process. However, amongst senior students Atman did find that attention to initial ‘problem scoping’ (i.e. ‘adequately setting up the problem before analysis begins’, including gathering a larger amount and wider range of problem-related information) did result in better designs. In general, developing greater expertise means developing a broader and more complex understanding of what has to be achieved, and this seems to be the case in design: more experience leads to more sophisticated problem scoping and defining.

## Developing design thinking

Cynthia Atman has led a significant programme of studies into the nature of design expertise and its development through education. She and her colleagues focused on recording and assessing the design processes of engineering students, through a series of laboratory-type protocol studies of design projects in different years of study. They also made complementary studies of the processes of experienced professional designers working on the same projects as were set for the students. They recorded the sequences of different activities that were employed by the designer throughout a design process, from problem definition to idea generation, through modelling and evaluating proposals, to communication of a final design. For each designer, the pattern of activities was portrayed in standardized timeline plots of their individual design processes. These timeline plots help to visualize and convey the process of designing. Figure 8.2 shows example design process timeline plots for students and expert designers tackling a playground design project. Each entry along the horizontal left to right timeline indicates the designer’s focus of attention across the rows of different types of activity.

A significant difference that can be observed between the patterns of activities is that experienced designers, as they progress through a design project, transition back and forth across a broad range of activities throughout the whole process. There is a general overall progression from problem definition and gathering data to deciding on and communicating a final proposal, with the majority of the designer’s attention being given to modelling ideas for solutions, but their attention is also constant across all the different activities, with frequent switching between them. Atman called this a ‘cascade pattern’ that is typical of experienced designers. It shows a ‘cascade’ of the designer’s attention, generally from top-left to lower-right in the charts, with periods of consistent attention to certain activities but with a continual breadth of attention across all the various activities of a design process. It is not a simple, direct flow from problem definition through each of the other activities to communication of a final proposal.

It emerged from Atman’s studies that the development of these patterns of more experienced design behaviour can be assisted through education, developing as students progress from their first to final years of study. Figure 8.3 shows the differences in the design process timelines for one student, tackling the same two problems as a first-year and as a graduating senior student. The problems were of different levels of complexity: a relatively simple ‘Design a pingpong ball launcher’ and a more complicated ‘Design a street crossing system’. For each problem exercise the timelines clearly show that, as a senior, the student not only spent more time on the problems but also exhibited the richer, ‘cascade’ pattern of activities. In general, in comparison with the first-years, senior students tend to gather more information across a broader set of issues, consider more alternative solutions and transition more frequently between types of design activities.

Atman and her colleagues also compared timeline design process patterns against the quality of the resulting designs. Using a children’s playground design problem, they assessed the quality of the designs on aspects such as whether the initial constraints of the problem were met, they satisfied playground standards for specific elements of the proposed design and met expected experience outcomes for the users. Figure 8.4 shows some example timeline patterns for senior students, ranked by the assessed quality of the resulting designs. Quality assessment scores range from low (at the top), to middling, and high (at the bottom). The results show that higher solution quality scores correlate with designers using a greater range of activities and a greater intensity of transitions between activities, with attention being paid across all types of activity throughout the design process. Better designers exhibit richer process behaviours.

These aspects of expert behaviour represent a further development of the changes already evident between first-year and senior students. So the evidence from Atman’s extensive studies in the development of expertise is that students do develop along a path towards expert behaviours, growing in their capacity to identify and integrate relevant factors, and to perform more complex design processes, and that development continues with more experience. Atman and her colleagues have fed their observations and analyses of design process behaviours back into education, to both students and tutors, and found that the timelines evidence encourages a greater awareness of design expertise and its development, and has a positive educational effect.

## Expertise in teamwork

Atman and her colleagues also found the rich, cascade process pattern occurring in team design as well as in individual designers. In analysing the activity of the experienced design team working on a digital pen project that was introduced in Chapter 2 to illustrate analogical thinking, she and her colleagues generated a timeline plot covering the joint discussion of all seven team members. Figure 8.5 shows the team’s timeline, over phases or episodes characterized by the researchers as developing changes in the team’s focus. The session began with a brief introductory preamble in which the team leader reviewed the given design problem with the team; the first design episode focused on brainstorming ideas; the second episode shifted to focusing on a particular solution type; and the final episode was concerned with developing details of the proposed solution. The timeline plot shows how the team transitioned regularly and frequently between the various types of activities throughout the process, similar to expert individual designers, with a gradual progression from problem definition into generating ideas and on to modelling and technical feasibility issues.
