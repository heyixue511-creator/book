# How designers work

n the previous two chapters we have looked quite closely into the Idetails of how designers actually tackle a design problem. This was made possible through the use of laboratory ‘protocol study’ experiments that were in many senses far removed from the reality of everyday design practice. In this chapter, I will develop a broader summary and comparison of these two experimental studies, and try to draw evidence and conclusions about the way designers work when confronted with a design task. The fact that these two experimental studies have been the subjects of detailed analysis by several researchers (in an international conference on Analysing Design Activity) will help to make the comparisons and summaries, but first we must acknowledge the problems and limitations of such studies.

It was clear in some of Victor Scheinman’s ‘think aloud’ statements that he felt constrained in the experimental situation; for example, he referred to what he would do in a normal situation, such as call an expert friend, but which wasn’t possible to do in the experimental situation. (He did manage to go ‘outside’ of the experimental situation and do something that surprised the experimenters, when he telephoned for advice from a company making similar products.) However, there were other, more subtle clues within the recordings of the experiment that indicated that we cannot rely totally on ‘think-aloud’ statements as records of mental processes. Some researchers have pointed out that non-verbal thought seems to be intrinsic to design thinking and that the forced verbalization of protocol studies must interfere with non-verbal thought processes.

It was also clear that there were many points in Victor’s stream of comments where his verbalizations stopped for several seconds. On one occasion, he had to be prompted by the experimenter to resume his thinking aloud. Commenting on Victor’s silent moments, Peter Lloyd and his colleagues suggested that ‘it seems obvious that in these short periods of silence he is thinking deeply about something’, and that these silent periods ‘seem to be points of value where decisions are made, but we have no idea of the thinking behind these decisions’.

So we have to be careful about drawing very strong conclusions about deep cognitive processes from such experiments, in which a designer works alone and tries to verbalize their thinking. The second experiment, in which the three-person team of designers worked more naturally, although still within tight experimental constraints, obviously also contains many gaps in the individual thought processes, when a team member is silent, where again we can make little assumption about what the individual was thinking. However, there are lots of useful observations that can be made from such experiments as these, and in fact both these experiments have been analysed quite considerably and in depth by a number of independent researchers.

## Creative working

We have seen previously, in Chapter 1 and the earlier case studies, that experienced, successful designers have ways of working that enable them to reach creative insights and make promising conjectures that drive the design process forward. Whilst maintaining a deep involvement with details within a particular project, they also have a way of keeping up an apparently higher level of awareness for spotting constructive opportunities that may emerge during the process. In Victor Scheinman’s design work there were several points at which he clearly recognized breakthroughs or significant concept generation points, using expressions such as ‘Ah-ha!’ and ‘Good idea!’ These expressions indicate that he feels he is ‘on to something’, that a way forward is opening up.

Ömer Akin and Chengtah Lin analysed Victor’s work and identified a number of what they called ‘novel design decisions’: that is, non-routine decisions that ‘turn out to be critical for the progress of the entire design’. Whilst there was a large number of ‘routine’ design decisions in Victor’s protocol, Akin and Lin identified just ten novel design decisions (NDDs), which were characterized by three features: an NDD resolves a problem or bottleneck, it does not follow from previous assumptions and the designer identifies the NDD as an important feature of the overall design. A particular example is the emergence of Victor’s decision to base his design on a triangular frame, when he said: ‘It would be nice if I could, for instance, run these rods up here to some point and therefore create a triangle, this would give me great stiffness – good idea!’

Akin and Lin sorted Victor’s working activities into three categories: examining (such as reading information, inspecting the available bike or backpack), thinking (whether verbalized or silent) and drawing. They then went on to record the frequency with which Victor switched between these activities and found an interesting correlation between the frequency of switching and the occurrence of NDDs. In almost all cases, they found Victor was alternating between the three activity modes in rapid succession at the point of reaching a novel design decision. Akin and Lin were rightly cautious about drawing any inference that rapid succession between different activities actually causes or produces NDDs. Their observation was only that the two seem to go together. They concluded only that ‘Our data suggest that designers explore their domain of ideas in a variety of activity modes when they go beyond routine decisions and achieve design breakthroughs’, and that ‘The designer appears to be decidedly engaged in all three major activities at the time of the NDD’. What their analysis suggests is that a designer’s level of engagement rises, and they work across a wider span of attention to different facets of the problem and solution, as they sense the achievement of a critical, creative point in the process.

Some other studies have also noted the apparent importance of frequent shifts of attention or between activity modes in influencing either the creativity or overall quality of the design concepts produced. For example, in protocol studies of industrial design students, Henri Christiaans segmented the students’ activities into the three modes of gathering information, sketching and reflecting. He suggested that the students who were more successful in producing creative design concepts were those who showed evidence of rapid alternation between the activity modes. Also, Cindy Atman and her colleagues, from studies of engineering design students, found that the overall quality of the design concepts that the students generated was related to evidence of more fluid and rapid transitions occurring between different activities in the design process, such as gathering information, generating ideas and modelling. We will look at these findings in more depth in Chapter 8.

Of course, there can be no suggestion of causality; simply flitting more quickly between activity types is not necessarily going to help a designer to be more creative. It seems rather that creative working is associated with a high level of concentration and engagement, especially at points when a designer senses a critical moment in the progress of the project.

## Team working

Design work is rarely a matter of a single individual working alone, as in the protocol study of Victor Scheinman. A lot of designing involves collaboration with other people, such as specialist consultants, clients or potential customers for a new product, and most design work takes place in a collaborative studio environment with and alongside colleagues. In a study of graphic designers, for example, Dianne Murray recorded the very social nature of design in practice and emphasized the openness and richness of activities within a design studio: ‘Briefing sessions take place in the studio in clear sight and sound of everyone. Work in progress is left on drawing boards; discarded sketches, photocopies, printouts and transparencies are left lying around on desks or on the light box.’ She commented that ‘Design is not hidden, it is constructed in public so other people can read it, and accepting commentary on it from somebody else is part of a tradition they embody’.

So a valuable aspect of the teamwork study here is that it enables us to observe how designers work together with one another. We have seen in that experiment how the three designers successfully worked together collectively, whilst tending to play somewhat different roles. Some researchers who analysed this experiment noted the different styles and approaches that were shown by the three, and especially the differences between Kerry’s and John’s approaches. Particularly in the early part of the session, Kerry tended to want to gather specific information and to engage with the available hardware and some of its details, whereas John tended to want to step back from details, work at a more general level of the overall process and gain a broad view of the problem.

The different approaches of Kerry and John were summarized by Margot Brereton and her colleagues as wanting to pin down a solution (Kerry) and wanting to preserve ambiguity (John) (Table 7.1). Having two such complementary emphases within a team can be good, but of course it has to be managed to avoid conflict and achieve a constructive outcome. Brereton pointed out that, to resolve or avoid conflict, ‘The designers masterfully invoke the support of neutral parties such as common sense, higher principles or theories, and expert or standard practices to support their opinions. These serve to depersonalise the debate, in addition to being means of persuasion and explanation of rationale.’ These experienced designers seem to have had some effective strategies for working together that built on each other’s preferences for ways of approaching the design task. Brereton concluded that ‘the collaboration is successful because the group is well balanced in their roles and manages their negotiation well. Kerry seeks to pin down solution alternatives, John seeks to preserve ambiguity and characterize the ongoing process, and Ivan keeps the solution progress on track and acts as an arbitrator between John and Kerry’ (Table 7.2).

Despite the differences between the individual designers, analyses of the experiment have shown that their individual contributions to the session, whilst different in content, were quite similar in terms of quantity. Whilst John produced more solution variant ideas, equal to those of the other two together, all three contributed equally in analysis comments on solution variants, and John and Kerry each contributed exactly the same number of decisions in choosing between solution variants (Ivan rather less). Ivan, as we might now expect, made many more comments on organizing the process, in contrast to Kerry who made very few such comments.

An individual designer working alone has no one to collaborate with, so how does the work of an individual designer compare with that of a team? These two experiments, using the same design task, allow us to make such a comparison, and the question was explicitly addressed in an analysis by Gabriela Goldschmidt. She pointed out that the cognitive tasks within design can be shared out between team members, whereas a designer working on their own has to produce the full range of tasks of inquiring, formulating, suggesting, evaluating, modifying and so on. She compared several key sequences of activity between Victor and the team, and found some significant similarities, with Victor’s activities often ‘shadowing’ those of the team. She observed that

He oscillates between overviews and technical details, between functional aspects of the design product and issues related to human factors. He thinks of features, product identity and aesthetics along with stiffness, strength and ease of production.

Team members do the same, but they can let a colleague answer a question they raise, or pick up someone else’s line of thought and build on it. The single designer has only him or herself to rely on, and he or she must act as a team and give all the answers while also asking all the questions.

These comments indicate some of the complexity that there is in the task of designing. Goldschmidt’s conclusion is that Victor implicitly ‘plays’ several roles and acts as ‘a team of one’, as in this extract from his think-aloud comments about adding some plastic clips to his rack design that would grip and hold the backpack frame: ‘Why do we want clips? Because we want to take advantage of the fact we’re using an external-frame backpack. An internal frame can’t use clips.’ Here, she suggests, Victor1 asks, Victor2 answers and Victor3 gives the design rationale. In this, there is an echo of designers ‘talking to themselves’ implicitly through their drawings and in their scribbled comments. In both Victor’s and the team processes there are also echoes and perhaps a confirmation of Donald Schön’s characterization of design as ‘a reflective conversation with the situation’.

## Team leadership

Some of the researchers who analysed the team’s work regarded John as acting in a ‘team leader’ role, partly because he seemed to be the ‘ideas person’ but also because he would make comments intended to focus and direct the conversation. Ivan also showed leadership in organizing the process by acting as whiteboard manager and timekeeper. We do not know what relationships or roles the team members may have brought with them from their normal work environment (they were all members of the same large design office), so there might have been some built-in hierarchies within the team. In some situations the leader might be pre-assigned to that role, or it might emerge informally through the course of a teamwork project. However, most real-world design teams do have some form of hierarchy, particularly where one member is clearly identified as being the team leader. That person is usually charged with responsibility for achieving a satisfactory outcome and steering the progress towards that outcome.

Leadership is not just a matter of being a dominant personality, or ‘having the most to say’. Andy Dong made a very detailed analysis of the conversational patterns of this team and others in similar experiments, and suggested that the constructive influence of a member in a design team is due to the way they interact with the others and articulate shared concepts. He observed that, ‘while John is a productive individual idea generator, he also builds upon Ivan’s and Kerry’s ideas’. For example, Dong refers to points in the conversation where John ‘accumulates the discourse’ and characterizes a shared concept at that point, such as ‘maybe it’s a pack conversion kit’ or ‘maybe it’s a little vacuum-formed tray’. To ‘accumulate the discourse’ means not just to sum up but to make a constructive contribution that focuses the previous discussion onto a type of solution concept and suggests a way forward. Dong referred to this as ‘deriving a representation of the team’s shared language that frames the design concept’.

However, in successful team design work, this constructive approach is not due to just one member of the team. Dong also looked at other teams and found that, in the more successful teams, the whole pattern of their conversation was more focused. A successful team’s discussion showed more ‘coherent’ development, in which the team members built upon each other’s contributions, progressing in a coherent form of conversation. The evidence for this lay in measuring how closely team members’ contributions to the discussion followed on from previous contributions, rather than diverging from them.

The importance and the nature of the role played by a team leader were identified by Chih-Chun Chen and colleagues in a study of a team of experienced designers in a real-world, cross-cultural (Europe/ China) automotive accessories design project. Their conclusion was that ‘the role of a leader in collaborative design can be vital to team success’. This success is based on how the team leader controls team progress by coordinating team discussions, helping the team to build up shared understandings of the problem situation and to collaborate in accumulating and progressing solution ideas. They suggested that team leadership shapes collaborative designing through two principal mechanisms: guiding the team’s design process strategy and ensuring effective communication within the team. A key aspect of effective communication is the role of the leader as a ‘communication hub’, receiving ideas and opinions from team members and relaying them back to the speaker and the rest of the team for clarification, exploration and confirmation. For this to be effective, team members need to feel comfortable in sharing their comments and proposals; when a team is working well members do not respond negatively to each other’s comments and proposals but by proposing re-interpretations or constructive alternatives.

From their study of teamwork in medical product design, mentioned in Chapter 4 with respect to co-evolution, Stefan Wiltschnig and his colleagues also emphasized the role of the team leader in initiating constructive, co-evolutionary creative design episodes. This was particularly so with regard to making changes in the initial problem requirements that subsequently led to solution proposals. These initiatives were often purposefully introduced by the team leader. Witschnig reported that ‘many design conversations were initiated by the team leader mentioning or amending a design requirement. Other members of the design team would then contribute useful ideas, either by way of developing the interpretation of the requirement or by way of generating and evaluating solution possibilities.’

An example of this was an episode initiated by team leader K and progressed by team member M. They are discussing product features for the ease and safety of use in a type of ostomy bag, which is worn by the user to hold fluid draining from their body:

K: What else have we got .  .  . we’ve talked about whether we could do something to avoid making it impossible to fold it this way. But that’s a problem for

M: The ones who’d like to – yes, it’s a problem for those who want to fold it that way while emptying. If you could make something so it was not so high up

K: So you couldn’t fold it

Here, team leader K initiates a discussion on how the user might mishandle the bag and how the fastening of the bag might be designed to prevent that. Team member M confirms the problem and suggests not a specific idea but a generic approach of making it ‘not so high up’, and K confirms the implicit intention of making the mishandling impossible. Over the next few minutes the team pursued a string of related solution suggestions involving changing the bag closure in several ways to overcome the folding issue, such as incorporating colour coding or adding text, or changing the user instruction manual.

The researchers commented that ‘These episodes did not seem to involve great leaps in creative discovery regarding how the formulation of the problem should change, but rather entailed minor increments being introduced by the team leader in order to change the focus to another part of the design problem space.’ However, they added that the team leader’s role ‘seems to be vital for the achievement of organisational goals relating to the delivery of high-quality, innovative design solutions’. In initiating and coordinating changes of focus the team leader is performing acts of metacognition – which means thinking about one’s own thinking or reflecting on the team’s process and progress and making changes as necessary. Individual designers working alone also perform these acts of metacognition in working as a ‘team of one’.

## Design process

Experienced designers know that it is possible to go on almost forever gathering information and data about a design problem, but that they have to move on to generating solution proposals, which in themselves begin to indicate what is relevant information. In a design project it is often not at all clear what ‘the problem’ is; it may have been only loosely defined by the client, many constraints and criteria may be undefined and everyone involved in the project may know that goals may be redefined during the project. In designing, ‘problems’ are often defined in relation to ideas for their ‘solution’, and designers do not typically proceed by first attempting to define their problems rigorously, which they know may be impossible. Of course, this can mean that important information is overlooked or might be discovered only very late in the design process, causing disruption and delay. For these reasons, there have been attempts to set out formal, metacognitive models of an ideal design process and suggestions for methodologies or structured approaches that should lead designers efficiently towards a good solution.

It is therefore interesting that, in our team design experiment, the team did set out a model for their process and tried to stick to it. Their model process consisted of the following stages:

Some analysts have looked at the actual process that the team (and Victor) followed. For example, Joachim Günther and colleagues analysed the individual statements in the team’s conversations into three types, concerned with (1) clarifying the task, (2) searching for concepts and (3) fixing the concept. Charts of their analyses of the team’s and Victor’s processes are given in Figure 7.1. We can see that the three types of activity became considerably mixed together and inter-related. For the team, throughout the first half of the session there was a lot of iteration between ‘clarifying the task’ and ‘searching for concepts’. What the team was actually doing through this period was generating lots of partial concepts and then considering the implications of those concepts. In that way they were exploring the problem through possible solutions. They did not spend very long on their initial task of ‘quantify the problem’ in isolation from generation of concepts, but after about twenty minutes they began to consider specific aspects of solution concepts and then (thanks to Ivan) organized this into searches for ‘joining concepts’, divided into joining ‘pack to rack’ and ‘rack to bike’. The chart in Figure 7.1 (top) shows clearly that ‘searching for concepts’ dominated most of the team’s process, but came to a fairly quick stop soon after they identified the ‘tray’ concept, at around eighty minutes. Overall, they followed the activities that they had set out in their model process but not in a strict sequence of separate activities.

It might seem surprising that ‘clarifying the task’ is an activity that recurs throughout so much of the session, as it did in the team’s process. But it is quite normal in the design process that there is an ongoing interactive, co-evolutionary exploration and development of both the problem and the solution. A pattern similar to this team’s activity pattern has been found in other studies and is not a-typical. For example, Vinod Goel found, in protocol studies of architectural design, that ‘problem structuring’ could constitute as much as 30 per cent of the overall task time, and although it was concentrated near the beginning of the task it could recur constantly until almost the very end of the session.

The chart of Victor’s process in Figure 7.1 (bottom) shows more clear-cut separation of activities, especially in the first half-hour, in which he concentrated on ‘clarifying the task’ by reading the brief and the evaluation report on the previous prototype solution, and telephoning a company that makes similar products. Victor spent some time in familiarizing himself with the task, including considering his own personal experiences of riding with a backpack as well as collecting information from others. A concern with user issues was more evident in Victor’s process than it was in that of the team. He ‘internalized’ the problem information, rather than making externalized lists (perhaps because he did not have to share and agree it with anyone else), decided on a preferred mounting position for the rack and implicitly on its material (tubular aluminium) and began a process of designing by drawing – making sketches of the bicycle and evolving a structure that met his user requirements for stability and stiffness (and the client’s requirement for uniqueness). In fact, much of his design activity was similar to that of the team, in that he made progress by proposing a partial solution concept and then analysing its strengths and weaknesses.

Victor commented at one point that ‘scheduling my time on these things, that’s the hard part’, and he frequently commented on the time constraint he was working under. Organizing a design process is quite a difficult task, because there has to be allowance for freeflowing activity within an overall schedule of things to be done within a time limit. The team’s design process seemed quite complex; it was free-flowing, but it was also quite controlled. Brereton et al. concluded that ‘the designers are continuously engaged in multiple activities at different levels. Although they focus in on issues, they continuously monitor the progress of the solution from the point of view of various requirements and solution alternatives. They reflect on their course of action, monitoring and modifying their process.’

The differences, such as they are, between the design processes of Victor and the team seem to be more to do with the differences between individual and team working processes. Victor was ‘a team of one’ and he was not subject to the pressures and constraints of working with other people, such as we considered in the analysis of teamwork in the previous chapter.

## Structure versus spontaneity

Some other studies have identified that designers usually start with a structured plan for their design process, as our team did, but frequently slip from the plan into opportunistic pursuit of issues or partial solutions that catch the designer’s attention, again as our team did. It has been argued that opportunistic behaviour, often derogatorily regarded elsewhere, is appropriate behaviour for designers because it allows the flexibility to deal with design problems and the ‘opportunities’ that emerge in the design process. For example, Willemien Visser made a study of an experienced mechanical engineering designer who claimed to be following a structured approach, but Visser found frequent deviations from this plan. She noted that ‘The engineer had a hierarchically structured plan for his activity, but he used it in an opportunistic way. He used it only as long as it was profitable from the point of view of cognitive cost. If more economical cognitive actions arose, he abandoned it.’ Thus Visser regarded reducing ‘cognitive cost’ – that is the mental load of maintaining a principled, structured approach – as a major reason for abandoning planned actions and instead, for example, delving into pursuing a partial solution at a relatively early stage of the process.

Raymonde Guindon also emphasized the opportunistic nature of design activities, which was evident in her protocol studies of three experienced software system designers. Guindon stressed that ‘designers frequently deviate from a top-down approach. These results cannot be accounted for by a model of the design process where problem specification and understanding precedes solution development and where the design solution is elaborated at successively greater levels of detail in a top-down manner.’ Guindon observed the interleaving of problem specification with solution development, ‘drifting’ through partial solution development, and jumps into exploring suddenly recognized partial solutions, which were categorized as major causes of ‘opportunistic solution development’. However, rather than regarding this opportunism as a weak feature of design behaviour, Guindon suggested it might be inevitable, because ‘These deviations are not special cases due to bad design habits or performance breakdowns but are, rather, a natural consequence of the ill-structuredness of problems in the early stages of design’. Like Visser, Guindon also referred to ‘cognitive cost’ as one possible explanation for such behaviour: ‘Designers find it advantageous to follow a train of thought temporarily, thus arriving at partial solutions at little cognitive cost.’

Clearly, whether it’s a single designer or a design team they have to monitor and adjust their own design process as they move through a project; they must occasionally take a step back from the activities they are directly engaged in to reflect on their progress. Dagny Valgeirsdottir and Balder Onarheim studied a team working in automotive design and observed that ‘Process awareness is a cognitive creativity skill that individuals in a design team use to facilitate a creative process’. They identified the operation of this metacognitive skill occurring where someone in the team uses phrases such as ‘we need to’, ‘we should’, ‘I think we’ or words related to process awareness such as ‘insight’, ‘focus’, ‘fixated’ or ‘incubation’. In the short account earlier of the problem of users potentially mishandling an ostomy bag, the team leader initiated a change of focus with the phrase ‘so what have we got?’, suggesting a summarizing and progress review point in the process.

## Collaborative designing

It seems especially hard in teamwork to follow a tightly prescribed process because individual members may have individual preferences for ways of working. There is often a natural flow to design processes that can run counter to a prescription for what should happen when. The team process in collaborative designing therefore requires particular attention to how natural conversation can be guided into a constructive, productive and creative process. One means of helping to do this is to externalize aspects of the conversation into a shared public space.

We saw in our team design experiment how they used a whiteboard for listing process steps, problem structures and solution ideas. The whiteboard is a means of externalizing and recording thoughts, and also supports moments of reflection when the team pauses and considers what they have on the whiteboard. Through this kind of externalizing, a design team is able to share and deal with complex structures and relationships that are very difficult or impossible to hold solely within the brain. The use of sticky notes, either on a whiteboard or some other flat surface, has now become almost ubiquitous in team processes of all kinds for this kind of externalization. In design teamwork, they have joined other common supportive media for designing such as sketching, modelling and prototyping.

The use of sticky notes in collaborative design has been studied to identify how they support particular aspects of design thinking.

Linden Ball and his colleagues pointed out that ‘sticky notes can be used quickly, easily and informally to generate many potentially relevant – albeit somewhat disconnected – ideas without designers needing to possess full insight into where the generated information might be leading. In a short amount of time a blank workspace can thus become populated with a multitude of visually distinct ideas’. They argued that ‘to disregard the humble sticky note as a key support material in creative design is to ignore a whole sphere of cognition that underpins effective real-world design practice’.

Ball suggested that the properties of sticky notes make them not just an easy means of recording and structuring ideas, but that they are inherently supportive of design thinking in collaborative processes. The visual and adjustable display of sticky notes supports perceptions and other cognitive design processes in identifying and modifying conceptual relations amongst the parts to form wholes. Ball proposed that the use of sticky notes makes it possible for a design team ‘(i) to consider the parts as “variables” in a system, thereby enabling the exchange of parts, while retaining the structural whole; (ii) to restructure the whole representation into new representations by re-configuring the parts; and (iii) to explore and establish dynamically new possible structural links to existing concepts, thereby gradually and generatively “building-up” or advancing a conceptual whole with new emergent properties’. That outline might be regarded as offering a neat summary not only of collaborative designing processes but also of design thinking more generally. Like drawing and sketching in design (as I commented in Chapter 4), sticky notes permit and encourage simultaneous, non-hierarchical participation in collaborative designing, using a common representation.

Frequently, collaborative designing includes designers working with a wide range of participants or stakeholders, including end-users of the proposed design result. This involves different attitudes towards and patterns of design activity that have to be accommodated within the design process. In user-centred workshops in communication design for sustainable cleaning in childcare centres and for asthma management and awareness, Simone Taffe found that non-designer participants were happy to engage in idea generation but reluctant to evaluate the collected ideas. For example, ‘the childcare staff were reluctant to place evaluation dots on the cleaning moodboard as they did not want to criticise their co-workers’ ideas or have any form of disagreement about their co-created posters’. She also found that a ranking method often used by communication designers was resisted by the asthma staff because

the words to be sorted presented a mix of negative and positive issues. Seeing printed cards displaying the negative aspects of [their own] work practices created an immediate barrier for the asthma staff, preventing them from participating in the card ranking activity. The designer technique of ranking was perceived as confrontational whereas discussion was more conducive to forming a consensus towards good ideas.

Collaborative designing is therefore clearly a social process and so progress can be influenced by normal issues of social interaction, such as reluctance to criticize others in a group. Externalizing aspects of the process, through the use of whiteboards, idea cards, sticky notes and other modelling, is a way of opening up the design process and making social interaction cooperative and creative. However, collaborative designing has to be carefully managed to create a successful process of social and creative interaction that enhances group knowledge and skill.

## Creative designing

In making an analysis of our backpack-carrier team design experiment, David Radcliffe spotted a weakness in the team’s process that constrained their creativity. They decomposed the overall problem into sub-problems of joining ‘pack to rack’ and ‘rack to bike’, as shown in Ivan’s whiteboard lists of the various concepts (Figure 6.4). At the core of their problem structuring was the concept of a ‘rack’. But the design brief that the team was given asked for ‘a carrying/fastening device’ – something much more open than directly specifying ‘a rack’. This particular interpretation severely limited much of the team’s exploration of the problem.

Radcliffe noticed that this decomposition of the problem made it difficult for the team to consider solution concepts that arose naturally in the conversation but that did not fit the agreed decomposition. At separate times both Kerry and John commented on the fact that the existing external frame of the backpack was in itself a structure something like a rack. Kerry commented that ‘We’ve already got that frame on the pack; it would be nice if we can take advantage of that’. A little later, John commented that ‘there’s a kind of other class of solutions outside of our design problem [structure], and that’s that you could somehow use the external frame and wouldn’t need the rack’. Just a little later again, Kerry formulated a solution concept for a simple device, suggesting that ‘maybe the attachment is kind of a leg that attaches right to the external frame’. Radcliffe notes that, ‘Ivan records the concept on the whiteboard as “legs on frame” under the “pack to rack” heading . . . There is no explicit acknowledgement that this concept falls outside the pack-to-rack/rack-to-bike dichotomy. In terms of the recorded work of the team, listing concepts under suitably decomposed headings takes precedence over capturing less structured or controllable emergence of design ideas.’

A ‘no-rack’ concept clearly does not fit anywhere within the team’s problem decomposition. Although the team went on to consider some more ideas for such a ‘no-rack’ concept, eventually it was lost as they returned to their more orderly sequence of planned activities. This loss of a potentially radical, creative solution was precisely due to the team creating, and sticking to, a specific structuring of the problem. In collaborative and co-designing, which often has a broad brief and can include heterogeneous teams comprising not only designers but also clients, customers and other specialists, leaving the problem structure as open as possible for as long as possible can help to avoid premature fixation and the unnecessary restriction of solution types.

David Radcliffe also concluded from his analysis that the team’s progress to a solution concept demonstrated that the emergence of creative ideas cannot be constrained to a particular place or sequence within a design process. He pointed out, ‘It is clear that design ideas emerge where they will in the continuum of the design conversation . . . Ideation cannot be constrained to occur only during the prescribed time for this activity as dictated by notions of due process and proper sequence of phases in design.’ To provide a specific emphasis, Radcliffe pointed out that the team’s decisive ‘tray’ concept was generated during what was supposed to be the evaluation phase to ‘select a concept’.

## The creative bridge

There seemed to be the classic notion of a ‘creative leap’ in the team’s design activity when John proposed that ‘maybe it’s like a little vacuum-formed tray’, and this tray concept became key to developing the team’s final design proposal. Did the tray idea just come ‘out of the blue’ or was it an ‘accumulation’ of previous ideas and contributions? It was certainly the first instance of the use of the word ‘tray’, but some possibly related concepts that had been mentioned earlier included references to plastic as a possible material and flat, solid forms for the rack device. In fact, nearly twenty minutes earlier than he first expressed the tray idea, John had referred to a similar kind of rack idea that he recalled: ‘Actually a friend of mine suggested, a couple of years ago, a product that he would do, an injection-moulded rack that would kind of like fold down.’ And Ivan immediately responded with recalling a similar bicycle luggage device that he remembered: ‘It’s like a little rack that was flat, it had these panels . . . but these panels were solid, it had little wheels . .  . and it would come off and then it would be like a little trailer.’ Ivan went on to explain how it worked when folded up on the back of a bicycle by over-drawing it on a sketch that Kerry had made (silently) much earlier of something very like a ‘tray’ on the back of a bike (Figure 7.2).

So ideas related to the device as a tray-like, flat sheet, probably of plastic, had been suggested earlier, both explicitly by John and Ivan and implicitly by Kerry in her sketch. The significant difference seems to be the overt expression by John of this concept as a ‘tray’ – that is a flat surface with a raised lip around its circumference. The strength of the ‘tray’ concept seems to be that it identified and summarized an easily recognizable, appropriate solution form, in a way that was significantly different from the earlier concepts of a ‘flat’, ‘folded’, ‘panel’. We can see that it drew upon those earlier concepts that, in retrospect, seem very similar, but that lacked the apparently critical feature of ‘containment’ that a tray has. Its generation was perhaps aided by the immediately prior consideration of a more extreme form of containment, a bag. Like the bag, the tray concept seemed to focus on one particular problem (containing the backpack’s straps) as the most significant consideration, but it was quickly elaborated to satisfy a range of other problems and functions. John’s expression of the concept was also timely – it came at the point when the team was trying to ‘select a concept’ and move on from problem exploration and concept generation to final design work.

It might seem that John’s apparently sudden expression of the ‘tray’ concept is an example of a ‘creative leap’. However, our analysis has shown that the expression of the concept actually ‘accumulates’ a lot of prior concepts, examples and discussions within the team. It is more like the key frame of ‘keep newspapers separate’ that occurred for the litter bin designers in Chapter 4. Creative designing is not necessarily the making of a sudden ‘leap’ from the problem space into the solution space, from requirements to solution, but is the formulation of an apposite proposal. Once the proposal is made, it is seen to be an apposite response to the given, explored and framed problem situation. It is an abductively reasoned conclusion that creates a resolution of the design requirements on the one hand and the design structure of a potential new product on the other, a matching problem-solution pair. The apparent sudden illumination that can occur in creative designing is therefore more like constructing a ‘creative bridge’ between the problem space and the solution space than like taking a ‘creative leap’ from one to the other.

## Summary

Designing is usually a social process of teamwork, with members playing different roles; a designer working alone has to internally play the different roles. The leadership influence of a member in a team arises from how they interact with the others, articulating shared understanding of the problem and accumulating and progressing solution idea concepts. A successful team’s discussion shows coherent development, with the team members building upon each other’s contributions. Process awareness is a cognitive creativity skill that facilitates progress. In initiating and coordinating the process, a team leader is performing acts of metacognition. A design team may set up a planned procedure, but they recognize that this is a framework that will be adapted as the work progresses. Externalizing aspects of the process, through the use of sticky notes, drawings and other modelling, are ways of opening up the design process and making social interaction cooperative and creative. When a key concept is voiced other members of the team recognize it as important and apposite, combining a resolution of the design requirements on the one hand and the structure of a potential design solution on the other: a proposal of a matching problem-solution pair. The apparent sudden illumination is more like constructing a creative bridge between the problem space and the solution space than like taking a creative leap from one to the other.
