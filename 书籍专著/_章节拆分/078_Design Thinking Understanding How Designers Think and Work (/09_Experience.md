# Experience

What we have seen throughout this book is that designing is a form of skilled behaviour. It has also become clear that, as with other skills, successful behaviours are something that can be learned. The performance of a skilled practitioner, whether musician, footballer or designer, appears to flow seamlessly, adapting their performance to the circumstances without faltering; but the learning of any skill relies on controlled practice and the development of underlying technique. It is not simply a matter of possessing ‘talent’ but comes as the result of a dedicated application to and practice in a chosen field, and the accumulation of experience.

Peter Lloyd and Peter Scott found from protocol studies of architects and engineering designers that, for both, their approach appeared to be related to the amount and type of their previous experience. They found that more experienced designers used more of a conjecturing, solution-focused approach, more of what they called ‘generative’ (productive or abductive) reasoning, in contrast to the deductive reasoning employed more by less-experienced designers. They concluded that, in particular, ‘It is specific experience of the problem type that enables designers to adopt a conjectural approach to designing, that of framing or perceiving design problems in terms of relevant solutions’.

Clearly, part of the development of expertise lies in the accumulation of experience. Something that distinguishes experts from novices is that the experts have been exposed to a large number of examples of the problems and solutions that occur in their domain. It is a matter not just of copying from examples but of learning from them. A key competency of an expert is the mental ability to stand back from the specifics of the accumulated knowledge of precedents and form more abstract conceptualizations pertinent to their domain of expertise.

Experts are believed to be able to store and access information in larger ‘chunks’ than novices and to recognize underlying principles, rather than focusing on the surface features of problems. This kind of behaviour classically occurs in chess playing, where chess masters are able to survey chess board positions and recognize patterns, and strategies for resolving them, whereas novices cannot see beyond the next one or two moves.

Bryan Lawson has drawn parallels between expertise in chess and design, and suggested that design masters also recognize patterns in problem situations and draw upon knowledge of precedents that they have abstracted into solution principles, or ‘schemata’ as Lawson calls them. A typical design schema for an architect might be a way of organizing internal space, such as around an atrium, or for an industrial designer might be grouping together different functional parts of a product. Lawson also suggested that, like chess masters, design masters have repertoires of ‘gambits’, or ways of proceeding, of entering into and opening up the problem situation. A typical gambit might be like that of Kenneth Grange (Chapter 3), starting a new project by ‘trying to sort out just the functionality, just the handling of it, and by-and-large out of that comes a direction’.

In order to develop skilled expertise it seems that a novice needs lots and lots of practice, guided by good teachers. The novice designer also needs exposure to good examples of expert work in the domain, and needs to learn to perceive and retain these examples in terms of their underlying schemata or organizing principles. Like learning a language, it is a matter of immersion and internalizing different levels of understanding and achievement.

Just as design problems are ‘ill-defined’, so expert designers appear to be ‘ill-behaved’ problem solvers. Rather than the conventional behaviours of solvers of well-defined problems, designers are solution-focused, not problem-focused. This change of focus appears to be a feature of design expertise that develops with education and experience in designing. In particular, experience in a specific type of design domain enables designers to move quickly to formulating a problem frame and proposing a solution conjecture. Successful, experienced designers are repeatedly found to be proactive in problem framing, actively imposing a view of the problem and directing the search for solution conjectures.

To cope with the uncertainty of dealing with ill-defined problems, a designer has to have the self-confidence to define, redefine, challenge and change the problem as given, in collaboration with the clients and stakeholders and in the light of solutions that emerge in the very process of designing. People who prefer the certainty of structured, well-defined problems are unlikely to appreciate the delight of being a designer.

## Design intelligence

What I have attempted to show throughout this book is that design ability is a multi-faceted skill. More than that, I have tried to show that there are particular, designerly ways of thinking and working that identify designing as equivalent with other highly regarded forms of cognitive skill. In fact, it seems possible to make a reasonable claim that design ability is a form of natural intelligence, one of the kind that the psychologist Howard Gardner identified. Gardner’s view is that there is not just one form of intelligence (as conventionally identified in forms of ‘intelligence tests’), but several, relatively autonomous human intellectual competencies, including linguistic, musical, mathematical, bodily and spatial intelligences. It seems reasonable that we might add design intelligence to those.

The understanding of design expertise suggests that a designer has a high level of intelligence used to plan, review, reflect on, adapt and, above all, create novel solutions. We have seen many aspects of this design intelligence in this book. For example, we have seen that good designers have a way of thinking that involves operating across different levels of detail in a project, from high-level systemic goals to low-level physical principles. Rather than solving merely ‘the problem as given’ they apply their intelligence to the wider context and suggest imaginative, apposite solutions that resolve conflicts and uncertainties. They have cognitive skills of problem framing, of gathering and structuring problem data and creating coherent patterns from the data that indicate ways of resolving the issues and suggest possible solution concepts. Design intelligence involves an intense, reflective interaction with models and representations of problems and solutions, and an ability to shift easily and rapidly between concrete representations and abstract thought, between doing and thinking. Good designers also apply constructive thinking not only in their individual work but also in collaboration in teamwork.

## The designing brain

The nature of design intelligence becomes particularly and tragically highlighted in rare cases where it is impaired through neurological damage to the brain, such as through a stroke. One of these cases was reported by cognitive scientists Vinod Goel and Jordan Grafman, who studied an architect who had had a seizure, associated with a tumour in his right prefrontal cortex, a region at the front of the brain that is associated with high-level cognitive functions. Before his attack, this person had practised successfully as an architect but was unable to do so afterwards. In an experiment, Goel and Grafman compared his post-seizure design ability with that of a ‘control’ participant, another architect with similar education and design experience, on being given a relatively simple task of re-designing a laboratory space. The sequences of design sketches that the two architects produced are shown in Figures 8.6 and 8.7. Each began by making a survey drawing of the existing laboratory and its furniture. The healthy control participant then produced a coherent series of sketches, beginning with abstracted considerations of circulation and organization, then developing proposals and refining the preferred one. The neurological patient produced three separate, basic and incomplete proposals, finishing with a ‘final proposal’ that was still inadequate and incomplete.

The differences in the thinking processes of the two people become clear in graphs of the amount of time each devoted to different cognitive activities, as revealed by their ‘think aloud’ comments made during the experiments. These are shown in Figure 8.8. The control participant focused initially on problem structuring, with periodical returns to that throughout the process. He then moved on to preliminary design and to refinement and detailing. His graph clearly shows a complex but controlled pattern of activities, with overlap and rapid transitions between activities. In contrast, the neurologically damaged patient spent a huge amount of time on attempting problem structuring and only small amounts of time on preliminary design and refinement.

The patient understood the task and even observed that ‘this is a very simple problem’. His sophisticated architectural knowledge base was still intact and he used it quite skilfully during the problem structuring phase. However, the patient’s problemsolving behaviour differed from the control’s behaviour in the following ways: (1) he was unable to make the transition from problem structuring to problem solving; (2) as a result preliminary design did not start until two-thirds of the way into the session; (3) the preliminary design phase was minimal and erratic, consisting of three independently generated fragments; (4) there was no progression or lateral development of these fragments; (5) there was no carry-over of abstract information into the preliminary design or later phases; and (6) the patient did not make it to the detailing phase. In short, the patient simply could not perform the relatively simple design task.

So in this unhappy case we can see exposed some of the considerable complexity that there is in normal design thinking, and evidence that the human brain has high-level cognitive functions that control or process activities that are essential aspects of design ability and that contribute to design thinking as a form of intelligence.

Neurological studies of brain activity have identified specific areas of the brain as being active during design thinking. Katerina Alexiou and her colleagues conducted such a study using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) of the brains of volunteers who performed two similar tasks that were presented in different ways: one corresponding to a well-defined problem-solving task and the other corresponding to a typically ill-defined design task. The tasks were to plan a simple domestic room layout using a given set of furniture. In the problem-solving version, the task was given as: Arrange the furniture so that: the bed is in the corner of the room, with its head to the west – the wardrobe is next to the door and next to the bed – the desk is under the window. In the designing version, it was given as: Arrange the furniture so that: the room is functional – the room is comfortable – it has at least a bed, a wardrobe and a desk. The problem-solving version is constrained and a correct solution can be formulated; the designing version is open-ended and qualitative and can have many satisfactory solutions. The results indicated that designing and problem-solving each involve cognitive functions associated with distinct brain networks and that designing draws on a more extensive network of brain areas in the right prefrontal cortex. The prefrontal cortex is involved in complex cognitive behaviours including planning and decision making.

The two hemispheres of the brain, right and left, have different cognitive specialisms. The neural networks specific to designing appear to lie more within the right rather than the left hemisphere of the brain. Neuroscience studies tend to confirm that the right hemisphere is more specialized in spatial and constructional tasks, in aesthetic perception and emotions. The left hemisphere is more specialized in language abilities and verbal reasoning. Damage to the left hemisphere often results in the loss of some speech functions, whereas damage to the right hemisphere, as we have seen, can result, amongst other things, in the loss of design ability.

A view of design thinking as a form of intelligence does not necessarily mean that some people ‘have it’ and some people do not. Design ability is something that everyone has, to some extent, because it is embedded in our brains as a natural cognitive function. Although everyone can design, like other forms of intelligence and ability design intelligence may be possessed, or may be manifested in performance, at higher levels by some people than by others. And like other forms of intelligence and ability, design intelligence is not simply a given ‘talent’ or ‘gift’ but can be trained and developed. Otherwise, what would be the point of having design schools?

## Summary

Results from studies of expertise in creative domains such as design do not always match with results from standard studies of expertise; expert designers exhibit different strategies from conventional problem-solving. The development of expertise happens over time as a person learns and matures, and usually passes through different levels of attainment. Moving from one level to another is not necessarily a steady progression: the changes in level involve internalizing different ways of working. Developing greater expertise generally means developing a broader and more complex understanding of goals, which is related to the amount and type of previous experience. Typically, experienced designers traverse across a broad range of activities throughout the whole design process. Achieving skilled patterns of design behaviour can be assisted through education and guided practice. An expert designer can be regarded as having a high level of a particular design intelligence. As with other cognitive abilities, aspects of design cognition are located in specific areas of the brain and can be impaired through neurological damage to the brain.

## Glossary

abduction/abductive As well as the two more commonly understood forms of logical reasoning – deduction and induction – there is another form known as abduction. Abductive reasoning leads to a plausible or satisfactory, but not certain, conclusion from a set of observations or problematic situation. It provides a reasonable and defensible suggestion that something may be, but not that it definitely is or must be, an appropriate conclusion. It is the rhetorical, persuasive logic of design. See pages 11, 26–7.

co-evolution Both the understanding of the problematic situation and proposals for its solution co-evolve during the process of designing. Designers look for structures within the problem space that prompt complementary structures within the solution space. Iteration between the two search spaces of problem and solution proceeds in a process of co-evolution, progressively refining together both the formulation of the problem and a matching proposal for its solution. See pages 92–5.

emergence Emergence occurs in designing when patterns or properties that were not explicitly included or intended are perceived in a solution conjecture. This perception can then lead to improving or developing the solution. The recognition of emergent solution features can also lead to reconsidering the problem, the co-emergence of new issues or the re-definition of requirements and the re-interpretation of the problem. See pages 12, 89.

fixation A weakness in design behaviour can occur where details of known previous design solutions are inappropriately, and perhaps unconsciously, carried over into new solutions. This fixation effect can lead to inhibiting creative thought and to restricting the search space for appropriate solutions. See pages 67–70.

framing Framing is a key aspect of design expertise and the central feature of reflective practice. It involves forming a viewpoint that structures the loose complexity of a problematic situation and thereby establishes a constructive coherence within the problem space. The framed viewpoint provides a perspective that also sets some boundaries on the solution space and promotes and guides the search for solution conjectures. See pages 24, 88–91.

metacognition Cognition, commonly referred to as ‘thinking’, includes many aspects of intellectual functioning such as perception, reasoning and problem-solving. Metacognition is a higher level of awareness of one’s own thought processes and an understanding of the patterns behind them: thinking about thinking or knowing about knowing. Designers use metacognition when they reflect on their ways of acting and thinking during a design process, assess their progress and choose a particular strategy or procedure to follow. See page 142.

opportunism An apparent weakness in design behaviour has been termed opportunistic behaviour. This is observed as deviations from a structured plan or methodical process into the pursuit of issues or partial solutions that catch the designer’s attention, or as ‘drifting’ through a sequence of partial solution developments. The cognitive cost of maintaining a very broad focus may be a reason for these behaviours. It may also be a consequence of working with the ill-structuredness of design problems and should not necessarily be equated with poor design behaviour but accepted as a natural characteristic of experienced design behaviour. See pages 116, 145–6.

reflective practice The theory of reflective practice proposes a model of skilled professional activity around processes of naming, framing, moving and reflecting, which iteratively move towards problem understanding and solution development. Naming identifies things of interest; framing guides subsequent activity by providing a way to perceive and shape the problem; moves generate potential solutions suggested by the frame; reflecting on the appropriateness and contextual implications of the moves assesses their validity and/or identifies more things of interest. See pages 21–2, 89.

representations Tentative design solution conjectures are expressed in various representations, including not only sketches and drawings but also physical prototypes and computer models. They support the uncertain, exploratory nature of conceptual design activity by externalizing and facilitating design cognition, and they store and communicate the designer’s thinking. Representations aid the generation and evaluation of solution concepts, the identification of what needs to be known about a developing concept and the recognition of emergent features and properties. See page 13.
