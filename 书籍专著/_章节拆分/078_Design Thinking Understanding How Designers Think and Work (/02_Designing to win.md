# Designing to win

Our first case study is of an outstanding designer who has had a long and distinguished record as a highly successful and highly innovative designer in a highly competitive environment, that of Formula One racing car design.

Gordon Murray emigrated to Britain as a young, graduate engineer, from his home in South Africa where he had designed, built and raced his own car in club events. He joined the Brabham Formula One racing car team as a designer-draughtsman and was rapidly promoted to chief designer. For twelve years he carried the major responsibility for the design of a series of innovative and frequently successful racing cars. Brabham cars designed by Gordon Murray and driven by Nelson Piquet won two World Championships. In 1987 Gordon Murray then moved to the McLaren Formula One team as technical director. Through the four race seasons from 1988 to 1991, McLaren cars designed by Murray and his team won three World Championships with driver Ayrton Senna and a fourth with driver Alain Prost. In total, Gordon Murray’s cars won fifty-one Grands Prix races.

Gordon Murray then became technical director of McLaren Cars Limited, an off-shoot of the Formula One team, and became responsible for the design and development of a completely new, road-going ‘supercar’ – the McLaren F1, designed on the same rigorous principles as a Formula One car. As well as many technical innovations, the F1 featured a novel seating arrangement, with the driver positioned centrally and two passenger seats beside but slightly behind the driver, in an ‘arrowhead’ configuration (Figure 2.1).

After leaving McLaren, Gordon Murray established his own automotive design consultancy and has extended his career into a wide variety of vehicle forms, ranging from high-performance sports cars to small, efficient city cars, autonomous vehicles and a flatpacked truck for developing countries.

## Background: Formula One designing

Formula One racing car design is significantly different from almost every other kind of design domain. Gordon Murray likens it to war. Engineering and technological development in wartime is the closest analogy to Formula One he can think of, with resources – human, financial and technical – being poured into the design and construction of machines that must have, and maintain, a vital performance edge over those of the opposition. Throughout the nine-month Formula One season there is a battle to be fought on a different field every other week, with a new campaign starting again every year.

This constant competitive atmosphere creates tremendous pressure, particularly on someone in the position that Gordon Murray was in for many years at Brabham of being totally in control of, and responsible for, the complete technical operation: designing, constructing, testing, racing and organizing, throughout the year, and from one year to the next. With the calendar of Grand Prix races fixed in advance, there is also constant time pressure, with no possibility of missing a race or practice session.

Throughout a Formula One racing season there is constant work to make continuous improvements and adjustments to the current car design. These may be responses to obvious shortcomings, such as component failures, or to assessments of the car’s performance and handling, either in general or in relation to certain features of certain race circuits. The performance improvements aimed for may seem incredibly tiny by other standards – perhaps one-tenth of a second per lap – but they can make the difference between winning or losing the race. And at the same time, every other team is also making constant improvements, so the situation is never static.

## Radical innovation

Beyond continually making improvements during the season, there is a limit to what can be achieved with any car design before a jump has to be made to basically a new design. As Gordon Murray says, ‘Given the situation and the pressure at any one time, you do get to a brick wall. I mean, you’re doing all these normal modifications, you know you can’t go any quicker, you need to make the step forward.’ Typically, such a step forward happens during the short close-season, when every team seeks to start the coming new season with an advantage over its rivals.

In many instances, and for most teams, this will be a new version of the previous season’s car; perhaps a new chassis, new suspension or a new engine to be accommodated; perhaps a change in regulations to be met. For Gordon Murray it would often mean trying a ‘step forward’, a radical new concept. In the midst of the pressure, the fervour, the panic, he ‘used to get breakthroughs, I mean I used to get like suddenly a mental block’s lifted’. These breakthroughs would come as a sudden illumination: ‘I know it’s a cliché, but I really did have a lot of good ideas in the bath!’ But such illuminations came, again in classical form, after long periods of immersion in, and preoccupation with the problem.

In Formula One there are always strict design requirements to be met: the technical and sporting regulations, which minutely and precisely specify the physical and operational limits within which the teams must compete, and which can change from season to season. Gordon Murray regards the constraints (the regulations) of racing car design, along with its intense pressure and competition, as fundamental to the necessity to innovate. Outside of racing car design he thinks that there may not be enough pressure on the designer, nor tight enough regulations, nor strong enough competition, for such radical, innovative design thinking as in Formula One.

Another crucial factor is the necessity and the motivation to carry through the bright idea into detailed implementation. Again, intense pressure, even in the close-season, ensures that ideas that look certain to be winners will be pushed through to detailed implementation with the same fervour as in the racing season. Other possibly good ideas are discarded on a rapid evaluation of their implications for a car’s weight, performance or handling. In Formula One design, it is a matter not just of having ideas but of really implementing ideas that are going to improve performance, of having to ‘do it’, as Gordon says, ‘You have the idea, but you have to do it, and that’s what cuts the bullshit out.’

## Self-adjusting suspension

As one example of radical innovation, Gordon Murray refers to the development at Brabham of a novel suspension system. At the time, the then Formula One governing body, FISA, became concerned to reduce the designed-in ‘ground effect’ on the cars. This effect had been pioneered on the Lotus team’s cars; smooth underbodies lying very close to the ground, side skirts right down to the ground and careful aerodynamic design provided a low-pressure under-car area which increased the car’s grip on the track surface. This meant much higher cornering speeds were possible, to the extent that some people became worried about the heavy g-force effects that were being imposed on the drivers, which might cause them to loose consciousness. FISA decided to ban the ‘sliding’ skirts that kept the body-ground gap closed but allowed fixed skirts and set a minimum underbody ground clearance of 6 centimetres. For Gordon Murray this sudden change in regulations was a stimulus to innovation. His first World Championship-winning car, he said,

came absolutely from the regulation change. You sit there and you read the regulations and think, how we are going to do it? How the hell can we get ground-effect back? What [the regulations] said was ‘At all times the car will have a 6 centimetre gap between the bodywork and the ground . . . and there can be no driver-operated device to change that gap’. And everybody looked at it, and built cars with 6 centimetre gaps . . . and I looked at it and I thought, if that 6 centimetre gap could be a 1 centimetre gap I could double the ground effect on the car; and it’s going to go down to a 1 centimetre gap at some point, like [under braking] at the end of the straight. So if I can make a physical thing . . . that drives the car down on its own, and holds the car down on its own without any mechanical aid or button or electrics or anything, it’s legal. So in three months we developed a hydro-pneumatic suspension system.

Gordon Murray’s thinking on this, and he says it came as a sudden illumination, was that the authorities had to accept that at some points during a race, any car’s ground clearance is going to be less than the 6 centimetres minimum simply because of the body dipping under braking, or rolling in corners and so on. Knowing that any driveroperated, mechanical device to alter the ground clearance was illegal, he focused on the physical forces, the ‘bits of nature’, that act on a car in motion. The downforce from air pressure on a moving car could, if the car was appropriately designed aerodynamically, push the car down over its whole length and width. The design challenge, therefore, was to let the natural downforce push the car down at speed, and then somehow to keep it down as it travelled around the circuit, but then allow the car to return to 6 centimetres clearance at standstill.

The ingenious solution used a hydro-pneumatic suspension system for the first time in Formula One, with struts at each wheel connected to hydraulic fluid reservoirs. As the car went faster, the natural downforce of airflow pushed the body lower on its suspension and the fluid in each suspension strut was pushed out into the reservoirs. The trick then was to find a way of letting the fluid return to the suspension struts only very slowly when the car slowed down. By the end of the first lap, the car body would be lower and would stay low, but on slowing down and stopping at the end of the race, the fluid would return from the reservoirs into the suspension struts, and the body would rise to the required 6 centimetres ground clearance.

Gordon Murray found how to achieve the required result in the technology of micro-filters, used mainly in the medical industry.

They were using these organic micro-filters which let the fluid through themselves but very, very, very, slowly. And we built the world’s tiniest throttle valve with one of these filters in it, and a tiny little pin – we were using drills that you couldn’t even see! We went and quickly developed what size hole we needed, so that it took a lap to push the fluid through these little holes – all naturally with the downforce – pushed the fluid into the reservoirs and the car was stuck on the ground, running with its skirts virtually touching the ground. And because it took so long for the fluid to get back through the same valves and filters, it held the car down there, and after the race you have the slowing down lap . . . and the car just slowly came back up. Nothing to do with the driver at all, just physical forces. And we went to the first race in Argentina and just blew everybody into the weeds, just totally; and everybody went bananas!

Other teams protested that the Brabham cars must have been fitted with a driver-operated device. It was obvious that the cars were lower during racing than they were in the pits, but of course the scrutineers could find no illegal device. Under pressure from the other teams, the authorities pointed out that the Brabham cars were clearly lower than 6 centimetres when out on the circuit, which contravened the regulations, but Gordon countered that, at various points, so was every other car. To curb the protests, he suggested to the authorities that every car could have its underbody painted, and at the end of the race every car which showed that at some point the underbody had rubbed the ground should be disqualified; and of course, the other teams would not accept this. Some other teams experimented haphazardly with variable suspension systems, but, very frustratingly for Gordon, just a few races later in the season, FISA reversed its stance and allowed driver-operated devices for controlling suspension height.

The Brabham self-adjusting suspension system was an example of an innovation initiated by a change in regulations which forced Gordon Murray’s thinking on how to retain the ground effect. It is an example of radical design innovation, through thinking from first principles about the effects of natural forces and having the motivation to follow through from a basic idea into finely detailed implementation.

## Pit stops

Another example of radical innovation by Gordon Murray was the Brabham team’s introduction of planned pit stops for refuelling during a race, before this became normal practice (and was eventually ruled out from the 2010 season, although still allowed for tyre changes). This was not so much an innovation in the car design per se but reflects more of a systems approach to the overall goal of winning each race. At that time it was not normal to have pit stops as regular, planned parts of the race routine and team strategy. Pit stops were for emergencies such as changing a punctured or badly worn tyre. For Gordon Murray, the innovation of introducing planned pit stops was part of an overall strategy arising from taking his thinking back to a basic issue – how to make the car lighter. The lighter the car, the faster it is in accelerating and decelerating.

Gordon says his mind was ‘banging away’ at this issue for a long time. He looked at the regulations and realized that there was nothing in them about when you could put fuel into the car. So the idea dawned of running the car with only a little over half the normal, full-race fuel load and including a pit stop for refuelling. But that was only the starting point for a thorough investigation of the implications of such an idea and of working through the detailed implementation.

The first thing to do was to evaluate the implications of the idea. A pit stop took a lot of time; not only was there the actual stopped time of the car at the pits, but there was also the time lost in decelerating and driving into the pits and also in accelerating away again on tyres that take a couple of laps to heat up to optimum operating temperature. Formula One pit stops eventually became refined down to an incredibly quick norm of about six seconds actual stopped time, in which time over 100 litres of fuel were taken on and all four wheels changed. The total racing time lost was perhaps some twenty seconds. Gordon calculated that if the total racing time lost by a pit stop was less than twenty-six seconds, there could be sufficient advantage gained elsewhere to make it worthwhile.

There were many factors that came into calculating the advantage. As well as the weight reduction, half-size fuel tanks also have an advantage over full-size ones in that the car’s weight distribution is lower, and is more constant throughout the race, and the roll-couple on corners is lower, allowing faster cornering. The complicated choices of harder or softer tyre compounds also become a critical factor, because a lighter car can run on softer compounds which also improve cornering speeds. Even the psychology of racing came into it, because a car with obvious advantages in the early part of a race could lead other competing drivers into pushing their cars harder, or into taking more risks. For all of the objective, measurable factors, fine calculations were made, leading to the conclusion that a pit stop had to lose less than twenty-six seconds of racing time to be worthwhile.

At that time, a quick pit stop for tyre changes took about fifteen seconds of actual stopped time. Gordon Murray calculated that he had to get this down to about ten seconds and to reduce as much as possible the slowing-down and warming-up times. And so, ‘the innovation process continues, because you’ve got all these new things that nobody’s done before that you have to come up with’. An extraordinary development programme had to be undertaken in an incredibly short time.

From having the first idea to having a pit-stop car running and doing a test was three or four weeks, and that’s all the time that you have. So you would take each individual thing and tackle it. Say, OK, how can we get thirtyfive gallons into the car in ten seconds? The only way you’re ever going to do it is using pressure, and then you have a crash programme to develop a system . . . That’s what is great about race car design, because even though you’ve had the big idea – the ‘light bulb’ thing, which is fun – the real fun is actually taking these individual things, that nobody’s every done before, and in no time at all try and think of a way of designing them. And not only think of a way of doing them, but drawing the bits, having them made and testing them.

Within three weeks, they had thought of, designed, made and tested a pressure-fed refuelling system. To improve pit-stop procedures, Gordon hired a film crew to film the team practising pit stops and then played back the film, stopping it to identify difficulties and errors, and devising ways to improve the procedures. The new systems, the improvements and the training of the pit team got the actual stopped time down to under the target of ten seconds. One ‘big killer’ remained:

When you put new tyres on they were cold, and it always took two laps to get back up to speed, and the time you lost in those two laps killed the whole thing. So then I thought, well I know the tyres start working at seventy degrees centigrade . . . so we designed an oven, a wooden oven with a gas-fired heater, and we heated the tyres up, and ten seconds before the car was coming in we opened the oven door, whipped the tyres out, put them on, and the guy was instantly quick. Now every Grand Prix team has tyre heating; that’s where it started.

Pit stops to change all four wheels and tyres with varying hard or soft compounds (with a stopped time of just a couple of seconds) are now a key factor in race strategy for every Formula One team.

The example of the introduction of the ‘pit-stop car’ illustrates how a radical innovation was driven by the competitive urge to find a significant advantage within the constraints of the regulations; how the basic creative idea had to be evaluated on precise calculations; how a total systems approach was adopted; and how implementation had to be carried through to fine levels of detail.

## Designing from first principles

As well as the competitive pressure and the tight constraints, Gordon Murray emphasizes that an important factor in innovative design is reconsidering the problem situation from first principles; he stresses the need to ‘keep looking back at fundamental physical principles’. His innovative suspension system was based on such fundamental physical principles as the natural air-pressure downforce on a fastmoving car. The McLaren F1 sports car was also conceived according to the principles of Formula One design – principles such as lightness of materials, rigour of construction and optimization of performance. These principles were proved when some GTR racing versions of the F1 were produced; at their first outing, in the 1995 Le Mans twentyfour-hours race, McLaren F1s came first, third, fourth and fifth.

The unconventional ‘arrowhead’ seating layout (see Figure 2.1) also came from first principles. It is based on the racing principle of placing the driver at the centre of the car and is something he has used again in other car designs. It was something that Gordon Murray had had in mind for many years, as evidenced by a sketch for a car design with the same layout in one of his student notebooks, some twenty-five years earlier. The seating layout is a very visible example of how he is prepared always to think anew about any aspect of the car that he is designing.

## Steering column

As a less-visible example of his approach to designing from first principles, Gordon Murray refers to a small, and perhaps seemingly insignificant, part of the McLaren F1, the steering column. ‘Conventionally, it would have been, right, steering columns are typically three-quarter-inch solid steel bars.’ He explained how this conventional solution arises because the column not only has to carry torsional forces from the resistance to the turning wheels but also bending loads from the driver leaning and pulling on it whilst getting in and out of the car. It also has conventional points of support, is mounted in rubber bushes to reduce noise and is encased in a plastic housing for reasons of appearance and convenience. But it does not provide the sort of direct steering feel that a racing driver needs and that feeling was supposed to be one of the principles embodied in the design of the McLaren F1.

So Gordon decided to start by separating the needs to carry both torque and bending loads. Whatever the form of the steering column itself, it still needs a cover to house electrical cables and to mount switches, ‘so if you’ve got to have that anyway, why not use the insect principle where the skeleton’s on the outside, and make that the structure that takes all the bending forces?’ This thinking led to the design of the steering column itself as an aluminium tube of just 1 millimetre wall thickness, possible because it is only taking torque and not tension or compression. The support point is closer behind the steering wheel rather than down at the dashboard, and the system is now much lighter but stronger than a conventional solution and also has the right racing feel (Figure 2.2). The design process stemmed from considering first principles – separating the torque and bending loads – and from an imaginative breakthrough – using the housing cover for structural purposes as well as appearance and practicality.

Gordon Murray insists on keeping experience ‘at the back of your mind, not the front’ and to work from first principles when designing. For instance, in designing a standard component such as a suspension wishbone, he says, ‘It’s all too easy, and the longer you’re in design the easier it is to say, I know all about wishbones, this is how it’s going to look because that’s what wishbones look like.’ But if you want to take a step forward, if you’re looking for ways of making it much better and much lighter, then you have to go right back to first principles. He says it is like always designing things as though for the first time, rather than the n-th time.

## Case note: Analogical thinking

n his design of the McLaren F1 steering column, Gordon Murray Iused an analogy with the exoskeleton structure that some insects have. The use of analogical thinking is common in designing, with biological analogies often being used to inspire creative thinking (something like Philippe Starck’s analogical inspiration of the form of a squid for his lemon squeezer) or translated into technical applications.

Analogical thinking in design has been studied quite extensively. For example, Martin Stacey and his colleagues looked at idea creation by analogical transfer in a study of a team of designers who were generating ideas for a new product: a ‘digital pen’ using novel print-head technology. The envisaged pen might be a children’s toy or perhaps a craft or art tool. Some initial issues that were identified from an existing basic engineering prototype included keeping the print-head level and constraining the way it contacts potential printing media.

Here is an extract of the team’s discussion of ideas on how to control the print head and keep it flat on a variable surface:

Jack: A sledge manages to keep level by having quite a wide base and then a main force in the middle, unlike a set of skis which are quite narrow and you go up on an edge when you’re turning. A sledge is quite broad and you have the weight right in the middle so they manage to keep both runners on the snow.

Alan: So would you potentially see some guiders down the side of it?

Jack: Well, I guess the easiest way to keep the pen at a right angle would be to have a set of stabilizers on it, like a bicycle.

Chad: I was thinking of maybe like a flat base with a sort of universal joint like a windsurf mast. The face is quite big but it stays flat, but the bit you hold on to can be at different angles.

The analogies brought into play here are far removed from the immediate context of the design project, going from a sledge to children’s bicycle stabilizers to a windsurf mast. In this example they are all to do with controlling and guiding a flat object moving across a variable and low-friction surface. At other times, closer analogies might be used – at one point the team was discussing the unusually broad head of this pen:

Chad: So it’s more like a paintbrush isn’t it, like a DIY paintbrush Tom: It’s more like a roller, yeah

Analogies are used in design thinking not only as inspiration for a specific solution idea but also as concise ways to suggest an underlying principle that might then lead to a range of solution ideas. As well as solution generation they are also used in problem identification and formulation and to explain or elaborate in communicating a concept.

## Integrated design

Gordon Murray is known principally as a racing car designer. But for many years he nursed a very different concept of what a car could be: a small, cheap, efficient city car. After he left McLaren he began development of this very different kind of car in his own design company. The resulting concept was announced as the T.25 city car. The T.25 is a very practical urban-use vehicle that can carry a driver with two passengers or with a large amount of luggage or shopping; it is light and agile, with good performance and low fuel consumption, and occupies one-third of the road or parking space of a conventional car.

For this car Gordon Murray also developed an innovative manufacturing process, which is as important to the project as the innovative automotive design concept. This integrated thinking was essential to his approach to designing a low-cost solution, as he explained:

You need something that is small, light, radical, efficient, and it has to be cheap, because one thing you can’t do is build the world’s most sophisticated little city car that will save the world and then say to people it costs £20,000, because you won’t sell many – if you don’t sell enough it won’t make a difference, and the way you reduce the cost of producing a car and therefore the retail price is by first of all reducing the part count, the number of parts that go into the car, and that you do by design and by conceptual design.

So for Gordon Murray conceptual design, detail design and manufacturing are all tightly bound together.

## City car

Gordon Murray’s motivation for designing the T.25 city car was not just the economic and environmental advantages it might offer but also a desire to design an efficient, small car that would be attractive and fun to drive and would radically impact traffic problems such as congestion. His aim was to develop not just a new type of car but a new concept of personal transport.

Just as in his racing car design work, the city car is also designed from first principles. This is clear in some of Gordon’s earliest sketches for the car, in which he pays special attention to the suspension system (Figure 2.3). The suspension system is an important feature in early considerations because a tall, narrow car such as the T.25 tends to roll sideways on corners and that has to be countered in the suspension system. He also explained that, for economy, ‘I want to make a suspension with the least number of components and moving parts; this has only three parts in the whole suspension system’. The same kind of thinking applied also, for example, to the ‘frog-eye’ style of headlight design.

Even a radical concept such as the easy access, lift-up opening canopy, rather than side doors, came from the same approach.

Going from seven openings to five makes a car a lot cheaper and more rigid and lighter; going from five to three and from three to two is magic, and going down to one you can’t get any better – you’ve got to get into the car. So that sort of stuff you think about from the beginning, and that applies to lights, mirrors, instruments, wheels, brakes, everything.

After producing many dozens of small sketches of details and concepts over several years (Figure 2.4), and experimenting with performance, using different engines in some existing small cars, the design work took a major step forward when Gordon and his team built a very simple, full-size mock-up of the car, using wire and cardboard (Figure 2.5). The final version of the city car (Figure 2.6) bore a striking resemblance to that simple mock-up. The mock-up was also a useful design tool, as it began to suggest some new possibilities. For example, Gordon’s earlier idea for the seating arrangement was for two people sitting in tandem, the passenger behind the driver. But when the mock-up was built, ‘we discovered that you could, quite easily with the same width of motor car, for short journeys, you could take three people in it’, using a similar seating layout as in the McLaren F1. Another cost-saving idea also arose from exploring the mock-up, when one of the team realized that the rear-located engine could be accessed from inside the car, so avoiding the need for external access. Gordon explained:

At one stage, we were looking at having to have a boot lid to access the engine, until one of the guys said, Come and look at this, if you stand in the car you can open what is effectively your boot floor from the inside of the car, you can get to the oil and water and stuff from there – you fold up the seats, open the hatch in the floor and you are there.

The T.25 had a 660 cc petrol engine, but a T.27 electric version was developed as well. Like Gordon Murray’s Formula One cars and the McLaren F1, both the T.25 and T.27 were also winners – but for energy economy rather than speed. In 2010 the T.25 won the Royal Automobile Club’s Future Car Challenge, with a 97 miles per gallon fuel consumption over the 60-mile course from Brighton to London, and the next year the T.27 won the electric car class and best overall, with an energy consumption of just 7 kilowatt-hours, equivalent to 340 miles per gallon. In the city car design we can see that Gordon Murray applied the same kind of design thinking as in his racing car designs. There is a breadth of approach towards an overall, high-level goal, complemented by close attention applied across all levels right down to fine details, and all interlocking into the overall concept.

## Case note: Modelling

C imple models and mock-ups such as the wire and cardboard model of Gordon Murray’s city car are often used in exploring solution ideas. For example, in early development of a new model of vacuum cleaner within the Dyson company, the team built a simple cardboard model that attempted to combine the best ideas that had emerged from earlier individual activity and group discussions (Figure 2.7). The model is largely made from corrugated cardboard, with a thin sheet of clear plastic bent around to give an impression of the transparent dust-cyclone casing. It represents some almostfixed decisions about how big the motor might be and how big the dust collection cylinder might be. It helps in answering questions such as: are the wheels the right size, how is access to the filter arranged, where will the cable retract to? It also facilitates and enhances communication within the team, which can begin to debate subtle changes to configurations and identify strengths and weaknesses.

There is a deliberate roughness and incompleteness in the model. Allowing parts of the design to be vague or unresolved has advantages: it allows the maker and the viewers to focus their attention on those elements that are under discussion or are more or less resolved, and by only vaguely modelling components the designer can build in some ambiguity. Team members might view this ambiguity in many different and positive ways, resulting in a number of interpretations and ideas from the model.

As David Mlynski, the design engineer who built the model said, ‘When I’m modelling in card I’m still exploring the problem. It’s part of my creativity. Sometimes I can draw my ideas and I use this to clarify my own thinking and to share ideas with others. But at other times I’m thinking and making at the same time. I couldn’t ask someone to make the model for me because I’m designing it as I’m making it.’

## Learning from failure

For Gordon Murray, it is the pressure of competitive design and the necessity to follow through ideas into rigorous implementation that results in successful innovation. He suspects that many people have bright ideas, but that they lack the experience and the motivation to carry them through to fruition: ‘They have this great idea and then they lose interest.’

However, he also admits that not all his racing car design innovations have been successes; he has had a share of failures, too, and is willing to learn from the experience. One of his significant failures was the Brabham ‘surface cooling’ car. This radical concept was meant to be several steps forward at once: reduction in weight, improvement in driver safety and what was meant to be a long-term technical advantage over the opposition. His imaginative idea of surface cooling was to do away with normal radiators for cooling the engine, and instead to pass the water and oil through surface heat exchangers built integrally into the monocoque structure; the ‘skin’ of the car was therefore both structure and radiator.

There were innumerable detailed implementation problems with the surface cooling features, and trials soon showed that surface cooling was not going to work. So a revamped version of the previous season’s car had to be rushed out for the new season. However, Gordon was also working on another radical innovation, related not only to cooling the engine but also to increasing the car’s ground effect. Introduced in the middle of the season, this new version became known as the ‘fan car’ because it was fitted at the rear with a large fan, primarily for cooling the engine but which also happened to extract large amounts of air from underneath the car and therefore enhance the downforce. Driven by Niki Lauda, the car easily won its first race, but, as with the attempt to increase ground effect with the hydro-pneumatic suspension system, it faced immediate protests from the other teams.

The use of the fan was controversial because although engine cooling fans were legal, devices for increasing downforce were not. Gordon’s argument was that ‘If we don’t have the fan then the engine will overheat’. However, the protests were so strong and influential that the Brabham team decided not to use the fan car again after its first race. It wasn’t exactly a failure, but Gordon learned from the experience of the fan car, and much later, in 2020, introduced a more sophisticated version of the concept in the design of his new sports car, the Gordon Murray Automotive T.50 (Figure 2.8). This supercar is claimed to have the most advanced aerodynamics of any road car, offering an ultra-smooth body shape and marking a new development in ground-effect innovations through active management of the underbody airflow, controlled by its rear-mounted fan – the same feature that had been introduced years before in the ‘fan car’.

Another innovative design that failed was the last one Gordon Murray designed for Brabham, before he moved to McLaren. This car was designed to be as low as possible, with the driver almost lying flat. It involved also putting the engine into a reclined position, and this was the feature that proved not to work. Gordon said, ‘We just could not get the lay-down engine to scavenge the oil properly, and we kept losing a lot of horsepower.’ But he learned from the failure, and two years later at McLaren he was able to develop the same concept more successfully:

I did a lay-down McLaren, just like at Brabham but with a Honda engine that worked, and we won fifteen out of the sixteen races. So you do have things that don’t work; but in that case it wasn’t the idea that didn’t work, I just ran out of time and money, and I took on much too much in a very short period to get it to work properly.

Despite having experienced some failures and disappointments, Gordon Murray learns from failures and retains a commitment to holding on to good ideas, and re-purposing them when an opportunity arises.

## Design process and working methods

Although Gordon Murray carried immense personal responsibility for the design work of his racing cars, and of course for his city car, for which he founded his own company, inevitably it involved a lot of teamwork. Clearly he has been successful in inspiring others to work with him. He likes to involve team members in the design problems and for that reason prefers to recruit all-rounders to his team: ‘I never have engineers that aren’t designers.’ He also likes to work collectively, standing around a drawing board or a mock-up discussing problems and trying ideas. For the McLaren F1 design, he installed a five-metre long drawing board in the design office, so that the car could be drawn full size, and several team members could gather round to contribute. He likes to have people around ‘to bounce ideas off’, and prefers to give them enough freedom and responsibility to feel that they are really making a worthwhile contribution to the team. His personal motivation manifests itself in a dedication which he also expects to find in the other members of his design team; he expects to appoint people who ‘if you cut them, they bleed motor oil’.

Gordon’s personal design process is based on starting with a quick sketch of a whole idea, which is then developed through many different refinements. He said that,

I do a quick sketch of the whole idea, and then if there’s one bit that looks good, instead of rubbing other bits out, I’d put that bit to one side; I’d do it again and expand on the good bit, and drop out the bad bit, and keep doing it, doing it; and end up with all these sketches, and eventually you end up throwing ninety percent of these away.

He also talks to himself – or rather, writes notes to himself on the sketches, notes such as ‘rubbish’, ‘too heavy’ or ‘move it this way 30 mm’. Eventually he gets to the stage of more formal, orthographic drawings, but still drawing annotated plans, elevations and sections all together, ‘Until at the end of the day the guys at Brabham used to call them “Gordon’s Wonder Plots”, because they used to say “It’s a wonder anybody could see what’s in them”!’
