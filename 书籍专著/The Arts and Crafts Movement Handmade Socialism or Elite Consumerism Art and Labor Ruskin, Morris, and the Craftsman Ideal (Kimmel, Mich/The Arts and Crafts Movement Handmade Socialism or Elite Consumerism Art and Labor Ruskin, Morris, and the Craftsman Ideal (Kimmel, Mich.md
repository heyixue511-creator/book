Like sociology, the Arts and Crafts moveme emerged from the nineteenth-century antimode ist critique of the human consequences of capitalist industrialization. Like sociology, th movement stressed the degradation of labor and the debasement of the products of the mecha nized factory, and railed against an unrespo sive centralizing state that was more concern about protecting property rights than abou human welfare. And like the best sociologist the Arts and Crafts theorists evidenced a sensitivity to the contradictions of capitali production. Recall Tocqueville, for exampl describing Manchester in 1835 (1958, p. 96):

Few took comfort in the transiti from a culture of production to a consum culture, or in the trebling of salaried, white-co lar workers and the halving of independe farmers or self-employed men between 1860 and 1910. But unlike Toennies, Sumner, or Giddings, among others, the Arts and Craf linked their critique of industrial capitalism to a socialist political agenda. And it was th explicitly political orientation that marked th division between the English and the America wings of the movement. For Ashbee, the return to traditional crafts in the guilds was personal as well as political.
