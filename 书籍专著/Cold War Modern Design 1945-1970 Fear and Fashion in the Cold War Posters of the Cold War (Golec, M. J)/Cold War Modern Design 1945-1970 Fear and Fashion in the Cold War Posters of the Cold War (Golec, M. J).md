Cold War Modern: Design 1945 – 1970

David Crowley and Jane Pavitt (eds.), V&A Publishing, 2008. 336 pp., 50 illus, 200 col. illus, cloth, £35.00. ISBN: 1-851-77543-9

Fear and Fashion in the Cold War

Jane Pavitt , V&A Publishing, 2008. 128 pp., 100 col. illus, paper, £19.99. ISBN: 978-1-85177-544-6

Posters of the Cold War

David Crowley , V&A Publishing, 2008. 112 pp., paper, £14.99. ISBN: 978-1-85177-545-3

Writing in the catalogue that accompanied the exhibition ‘ Cold War Modern: Design 1945 – 1970 ’ (25 September 2008 to 11 January 2009) at London’s Victoria and Albert Museum, David Crowley and Jane Pavitt propose, ‘ Design was not a marginal aspect of the Cold War but central — both materially and theoretically — to the competition over the future ’ (p. 14). Drawing design into the fi eld of ideological confl ict between East and West, editors Crowley and Pavitt and eleven contributors reattach design during the cold war to its historical and political origins. At the centre, design contributed to the aggressive staging of cold war modernity, beginning in 1945 and following the negotiations between the USA and the Soviet Union (and their proxies) that continued into the 1970s. Design was either created in support of the utopian aspirations or destructive drives of one or other super power or it was a critical response to the alignment and hegemony of power. Both the editors and contributors to this exemplary resource expertly interrogate the dynamics of the social, the political, the cultural and the sexual, all as constructed and organized experiences with cold war modern design.

Prismatic in its approach to the material, environmental and imaginative impact of cold war modernity, the catalogue presents multiple and diverse views of as many instances of design as ‘ agents of social, moral and technical progress ’ (p. 15). Copiously illustrated, the book is divided into eight chapters, each of which contains from one to three sections. Crowley and Pavitt collaborate on the Chapter 1 ‘ Introduction ’ and on Chapter 6 ‘ The Hi-Tech Cold War ’ . They split the remaining chapters between themselves, and sections with contributors. The fact that Crowley and Pavitt each write lengthy sections for each chapter is a defi ning characteristic of this volume. The consistent return to both views, or to one or the other’s position, interspersed with the differing voices of the various contributors, provides a textual overlapping and refracting that greatly contributes to the critical outcomes of the catalogue. The addition of Crowley’s Posters of the

Cold War and Pavitt’s Fear and Fashion in the Cold War as related but stand-alone volumes furthers the critical and historicizing ambitions of the exhibition and catalogue. Attempts to demystify the spell of design’s reifi cation require such an approach to the critique of cold war ideologies and to an analysis of cold war materialities. Such a prismatic approach takes seriously that the two are not mutually exclusive — ideology made material and material made ideological. Thus, the centrality of design — the thing as object and meaning — to cold war modernism is established and reinforced through the many lenses of historical description.

For the historian of design, striking the right balance between context and design is tricky business. The problem was best stated by Walter Benjamin, who, in his ‘ Author as Producer ’ , queried, ‘ Rather than ask, “ What is the attitude of a work to the relations of production of its time? ” I should like to ask, “ What is its position in them? ” ’ 1 For the history of design, the methodological distinction is crucial since an answer to the former question can too easily lead to a kind of contextual history where design is merely an extension of policies, events and systems of belief. From the attitudinal perspective, design perpetually exists outside a context that frames its meanings. What passes for history, then, is a practice of placing design in its proper context so as to commune better with its meanings (and not its materialities). Benjamin’s latter question, however, motivates a more robust analysis; one that is no less relational in its character but that is invested in confronting design as always already in context. Whether the historian of design can make out the ‘ position ’ of design is another story altogether. But, an historical account of design’s positionality might start with the tautological assertion that a description of design is a description of its context. Thus, a history is excavated, ‘ furrowed ’ , as Foucault would say, of the materialities that work at it from within. 2 From the positional perspective, design is seen as a composite of multiple frames, networks or what followers of Foucault call ‘ discourses ’ ; likewise, design is a frame or network itself. The fact that the historian may not be able to reduce an historical account of design to design in context or design as context is what constitutes the interpenetrating complexities of the subject, something historians of design should not shy away from.

While Actor Network Theorists such as Michel Callon and Bruno Latour have been arguing for just this approach to the analysis of social relations between human beings and non-humans, 3 the best example of a positional approach to the history of design is offered by the Lacanian critical theorist Slavoj Zizek. On multiple occasions, Zizek describes a tripartite system of Western ideology that materializes in the concrete structure of an everyday object — the toilet. Generally speaking there exists three types of toilet design — the German, the French and the Anglo-American. In each case, Zizek correlates the design of the toilet to the Weltanschauung of its user. Zizek writes, ‘ In a traditional German toilet, the hole into which shit disappears after we fl ush is right at the front, so that shit is fi rst laid out for us to sniff and inspect for traces of illness. In the typical French toilet, on the contrary, the hole is at the back, i.e. shit is supposed to disappear as quickly as possible. Finally, the American (Anglo-Saxon) toilet presents a synthesis, a mediation between these opposites: the toilet basin is full of water, so that the shit fl oats in it, visible, but not to be inspected ’ . 4 Zizek goes on the categorize each instance of plumbing: an instance of romantic – philosophical contemplation in the case of Germany; an instance of revolutionary zeal in the case of France and an instance of pragmatic outlook in the case of America. What is crucial for the history of design in this example is that it is not that the structure of the object refl ects or is an extension of a world view but that, as Zizek relentlessly points out in vulgar detail, a toilet is both object and ideology in one. The object and its framing take on a perverse and interrelated character where the design of an ordinary plumbing fi xture irreducibly responds to and facilitates comportment to one’s own excrement. (Perhaps, this is what Thorstein Veblen meant when he argued that the engineering class was the only revolutionary class?) 5

To be fair, there are no toilets in Cold War Modern, or, at least, none that I could fi nd while working through the sections of the catalogue. What is apparent to varying degrees throughout, however, is the positionality of object and ideology manifest as ‘ cold war modern ’ . As a whole, Cold War Modern identifi es mundane design — the everyday — and reveals its extraordinary ability to endorse, coerce, seduce and critique. Thus, kitchens are battlegrounds in Crowley and Pavitt’s ‘ Introduction ’ , furnishings and appliances are forms of propaganda in Castillo’s ‘ Marshall Plan Modernism in Divided Germany ’ and in Reid’s ‘ “ Our Kitchen is Just as Good ” : Soviet Responses to the American National Exhibition in Moscow, 1959 ’ , fallout shelters are homes in Pavitt’s ‘ The Bomb in the Brain ’ and the earth is a spaceship in Crowley’s ‘ Looking Down on Spaceship Earth: Cold War Landscapes ’ . Some exhibitions were excuses for spreading American style Democracy, as detailed in Castillo and Reid and in Pavitt’s ‘ Design and the Democratic Ideal ’ . Other exhibitions were celebrations of technological utopias, as in Harwood’s ‘ Imagining the Computer: Eliot Noyes, the Eames and the IBM Pavilion ’ and in Goodyear’s ‘ Expo 70 as Watershed: The Politics of American Art and Technology ’ . In Murawska-Muthesius ’ ‘ Modernism between Peace and Freedom ’ , Picasso’s artistic formalism embodied freedom, and in Crowley’s ‘ In the Image of Revolution ’ , design was protest and a form of resistance.

Much that is great about Cold War Modern is its introduction of histories of Eastern cold war rhetoric and discourse to an English-speaking audience. Both the editors and contributors very well represent the former Soviet Union, East Germany, China, Poland and Czechoslovakia, among others. Through descriptions of design, readers gain some insight into the ‘ bureaucratic castle worlds ’ and ‘ citadel cultures ’ of the cold war. 6 What is most refreshing is how well the catalogue documents cold war events such as the American National Exhibition in Moscow, reconsidered from the Eastern perspective. Reid’s section on the exhibition shows that, despite some positive responses to the American exhibits, the Soviet public was largely critical of both the quality of design and manufacture and the promises made for the emancipation of the housewife. Reid confronts the fact that there is no way to distinguish design from context because it takes dominion everywhere.

While the ‘ attitude ’ of a work of design or an exhibition of products might have been socialist, imperialist, capitalist, progressive, totalitarian or democratic, the unifying character of design in the cold war is its modernity — its allegiance to the rational, the empirical, the bureaucratic and the organizational. As Crowley asks when discussing ‘ Thaw Modernism ’ in Poland, Czechoslovakia and the Soviet Union, ‘ Whilst these designs were evidently modern, how socialist were they? In form or materials, there was little to distinguish Eastern Bloc design from its Western counterparts ’ (p. 145). But this does not mean that socio-political context determines the difference between form and material. No matter how perceptually indiscernible, the position of design is both in and as context. Even in cases where, as Crowley describes the waning status of ‘ Thaw Modernism ’ , production could not keep pace with rhetoric, the gap indicates the place or position of design in, and as, offi cial rhetoric. ‘ Not only were the hopes of producing a rational world based on the principles of function and utility evaporating: the ambition of turning state socialism itself into a human design product was also being dashed ’ (p. 151). The odd materiality of design discourse was likewise manifest in the West, where Pavitt observes, ‘ In the US, magazines such as Life would match articles on the newest gadgetry for streamlined kitchens with descriptions of how to provision one’s domestic fallout shelter ’ (p. 103). The image of material abundance countered the image of total devastation in an uncomfortable diptych of cold war affl uence, the intersecting coordinates of which are marked by design.

For all its positive attributes, Cold War Modern does suffer somewhat from its ambition. The prismatic approach to the topic and the ensuing organization of the texts lead to sometimes distracting redundancies. Specifi cally, I refer to instances where historical events such as the US Marshall Plan and the MoMA ‘ Good Design ’ exhibition are discussed in both Castillo and Pavitt. This is a minor issue of style, but it does indicate areas where an overemphasis of historical context can result at best in a sense that design is being placed within an historical context but can at worst crowd design out of the picture. One way around this stylistic problem would have been to allow the contributors more room to explore the specifi cities of their topics and to reduce the length of the editors ’ chapter introductions. Instead, readers get a lot of introduction and general historical context and not enough focus on design. The same can be said for Pavitt’s Fear and Fashion in the Cold War and Crowley’s Posters of the Cold War. Both are excellent surveys, of cold war fashion in the former and cold war graphic design in the latter. But, in the authors ’ efforts to ensure proper contextual framings, they repeat some material from each other’s books and from the Cold War Modern book.

I do not want to belabour this point. Every chapter and section in all three books contributes greatly to the relevancy of the history of design as an area of critical scholarship. No doubt historians of the cold war will learn a great deal from this catalogue, as will designers and policymakers. For my money, the approaches taken in Cold War Modern offer lessons best learned in the fraught terrain of ideological confl icts. Indeed, what is the position of design in the material and discursive production of its time?
