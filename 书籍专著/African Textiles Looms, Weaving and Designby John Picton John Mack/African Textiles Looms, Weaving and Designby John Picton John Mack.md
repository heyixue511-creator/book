The juxtaposition in this single volume of descriptions of beating bark with a mallet over a wooden anvil and of beating woven cloth in the same way to "iron" it has caused me to wonder if the beating process might have carried over from the earlier medium (bark) to the later (woven cloth) in West Africa and the Congo.

Picton and Mack describe traditional weaving from the entire continent of Africa and neighboring Madagascar, in contrast to most recent who have focused on sub-Saharan African textiles, considering North Africa as a cultural affiliate of the Middle East. Madagascar has simply been left out of the picture altogether in their publications. In taking all of Africa as the locus of their Picton and Mack were faced with a staggering diversity of data on loom types, weave processes, and decorative techniques, which had to be organized and presented in a comprehensible fashion. This they accomplished by concentrating on technical processes rather than by exploring avenues of research such as historical relationships between weave traditions. While there are incomments on such as the economic importance of weaving, the status of weavers, and the use of woven fabrics, the focus is definitely on technology The stated aim is "to provide a comprehensive view of the ways and means of textile manufactur and design in Africa" (p.16). To include design as a part of their description is logical since, in the case of woven fabrics, design is almost a by-product of technology, being determined by the nature and color of raw materials, the weave processes used in their manipulation and the methods of decorating the finished pieces.

Looms and basic weaves are very simply described Emery's terminology (Emery 1%966). Understanding them should not be a problem for nonweavers. African textiles, in point of fact, are not particu larly complicated structurally For purposes of description African looms are grouped into two classes, those employing a single-heddle device for controlling warps, and those hava double heddle. This is a of Roth's earlier classification, which established five types (Roth 1917:150). Under the present the on looms must include information on textiles woven on the Nigerian woman's vertical loom, the Berber loom, the mat loom of the Congo Basin, and horizontal looms staked out on the ground, as found in North Africa, the Sudan, Cameroon and (formerly) East Africa. (Remember, the focus is on technology, not historical between these forms.) The chapter on the double-heddle loom need not include such a variety - it mentions their presence in northeast Africa, and among urban North African Arabs, but describes in some detail only West African men's strip looms and their products

Even with the focus on and design, it would not be possible to describe fully the variety of African textiles, and the author have selected for detailed coverage the weaving of a few societies whose work has been well documented field studies. Thus, for example, wool processing and weaving among the Ayt Haddidu (Gabus 1955) is described as representative of Berber in general, and weaving at Ilorin and Iseyin as typical of Yoruba men's weave.

The authors do us a service by considering the rather complicated picture of weaving on the island of Madagascar in a separate chap ter. As they state, quite correctly, such a chapter may "be useful to an English-speaking audience because much of the available informa tion is locked up in French publications that are not readily accessible" (p.135)

The final four chapters deal with applied decorative techniques. Two of them, "Pattern-dyeing" and "Drawn, Painted, Printed and Stencilled Patterns," contain data available to American readers in several current publications. The most recent of these, Claire Polakoff's Into Indigo, apparently appeared too late for inclusion in the bibliog raphy The final two chapters, "Applique and Related Techniques" and "Embroidery," do contain much of interest which will be new to students of African textiles in this The discussion of the relationship of applique in the Sudan and West Africa to banners and tunics of the Mahdist movement is particu larly intriguing, and it is good to have a summary of Heathcote's material on Hausa embroidery

There is little in this volume to elicit adverse criticism. The authors have managed a tour de force in covering so much material in so slender a volume, and doing it with such clarity I would like to have seen all fabrics illustrated with warps in vertical position or, failing that, with the warp directio clearly indicated by an arrow or some such device. It seems for a fabric to be described in the text as warp striped and pictured in a way most readers will see as weft striped. I would also have printed Figures 6 and 7 with the unwoven section of warps at the top of the plates, as they would have been on the loom itself. These are certainly very minor criticisms. The book should be read and enjoyed by everyone interested in African art or the textile arts in general.

The foreword to this lavishly illustrated book claims that it is "a first synthetical attempt to describe the terracottas of the ancient civiliza tions of Mali, Nigeria (Nok, Ife, Benin), Ghana (Akan), Chad and Cameroon (Sao)." Such an ambitious undertaking has long been a dream West African historians! Mr. de Grunne's book, unfortunately, is not as synthetic as terracotta researchers might hope. It should be described more accuratel as an exhibition catalogue of African terracotta sculpture from what appear to be several Belgium and French collections.

The is divided into four dent sections based on geographical areas; the most extensive is on Mali, an area of particular interest to the author. A geographical and historical summary precedes the stylistic classifications for each region. Catalogue photos are accompanied by specific data - material, dimensions, date when known, and previous publication references A short annotation is often included with the illustrations. The location or present owner ship of most pieces illustrated is not given, presumably for the anonymity of private donors/collector

The omissions and inconsistencies in this book are too numerous to list Itis initially evident to the reader that the text was organized to accommodate specific terracott objects, not the objects needed to illustrate a comprehensive history of West African terracotta sculpture as the title and foreword imply The Nigerian chapter, for instance, illustrates only nine figures, but the section on Mali is one of the most complete reviews yet published. While style categories are primar ily identified by location, the author create new categories when an object can not be readily attributed to a documented area style. Several of these categories, as in the Akan chapter, are based on as few as one to five pieces and comprise such nebulous divisions as "the semi-closed eye-lid" or the "naturali tic" styles.

While the text is usually a of published individual area studies, broad generalizations and opinions are frequentl without documentation. Such as "a scholar of the Ghana Museum who saw . . ." or "the travellers' accounts have provided us with . . ." only make the reader wonder exactly whom the author is citing. When discussing two categories of Akan funerary terracotta objects, de Grunne says that the "heads and statuettes which constitute the second category are the exclusive apanage of royal clan members" Such an oversimplification creates a false statement, and in this instance it is contradicted by the author's own opinion a few pages later when he says that "all the heads in refined were restricted to and were artists, while the simpler heads were destined to ordinary people and were sculpted by potters" (p. 157). With no further documentation or definition, value judgments such as this one are no more than questionable assumptions.
